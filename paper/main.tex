\begin{abstract}
Automated ECG interpretation remains challenging due to error rates of 4--33\%
in clinical settings and the recent risk of hallucination when large language
models (LLMs) are applied directly to physiological signals.  We present
\system{}, an open-source platform that prevents hallucination through
\emph{signal grounding}: a deterministic seven-stage pipeline extracts 73
clinical measurements from raw 12-lead ECGs via wavelet-based fiducial
detection, and domain-specialist LLM agents reason exclusively over these
structured measurements---they cannot fabricate values.  Three parallel
agents (rhythm, ischemia, morphology) feed a synthesis agent that applies
cross-domain suppression rules, mirroring cardiology department workflow.
A signal-only fallback provides 26 rule-based detectors when no LLM is
available.  Validated on 21{,}799 PTB-XL records, the system achieves
R-peak timing accuracy of 5.1\,ms MAE, heart rate accuracy of 1.8\,bpm
MAE ($r = 0.953$), and detects 13 cardiac conditions with best performance
on LAFB (F1\,=\,0.53), RBBB (F1\,=\,0.43), and LVH (F1\,=\,0.35).
The complete pipeline processes an ECG in 124\,ms on consumer hardware.
Per-disease clinical dashboards with STAT alerting, interactive 12-lead
display, and a caliper tool support ER nurse decision-making.  Code,
validation framework, and clinical interface are released as open source
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

% ── 1.1  Clinical motivation ──
% - ECG is the most common cardiac diagnostic test (~300M/year globally)
% - Interpretation error rates 4–33% depending on setting (cite Salerno 2003, Mele 2008)
% - Missed STEMI → delayed PCI → increased mortality
% - Rural/resource-limited settings lack expert cardiologists

The 12-lead electrocardiogram (ECG) remains the most widely performed cardiac
diagnostic test, with an estimated 300 million recordings acquired annually
worldwide~\cite{schlapfer2017computer}. Despite its ubiquity, ECG interpretation
is error-prone: systematic reviews report misinterpretation rates ranging from
4\% among board-certified cardiologists to 33\% among non-specialist
physicians~\cite{salerno2003competency, mele2008accuracy}. The clinical
consequences of misinterpretation can be severe. Failure to recognize
ST-elevation myocardial infarction (STEMI) delays primary percutaneous coronary
intervention (PCI), and each 30-minute increase in door-to-balloon time is
associated with significantly increased mortality~\cite{demeester2019impact}.
In rural and resource-limited settings, where board-certified cardiologists are
scarce, the burden of ECG interpretation falls on clinicians with limited
subspecialty training, further amplifying diagnostic error
rates~\cite{who2021cardiovascular}.

% ── 1.2  Existing work and gaps ──
Computerized ECG interpretation has a long history. Commercial systems such as
the GE Marquette 12SL~\cite{ge12sl2009} and Philips DXL
algorithm~\cite{philipsdxl2010} are deployed in clinical practice but rely on
proprietary, closed-source rule bases with limited publicly available validation
data. More recently, deep learning approaches have demonstrated impressive
accuracy: Ribeiro et al.~\cite{ribeiro2020code} achieved cardiologist-level
performance on six abnormalities using a deep neural network applied to the
CODE dataset, while Hannun et al.~\cite{hannun2019cardiologist} showed that a
34-layer convolutional neural network could match board-certified cardiologists
in arrhythmia detection from single-lead recordings. However, these end-to-end
models map raw signals directly to diagnostic labels without producing
clinically interpretable intermediate features such as interval durations, axis
measurements, or ST-segment amplitudes, limiting their utility for clinical
audit and education.

The emergence of large language models (LLMs) in clinical decision support
introduces a new paradigm for medical reasoning~\cite{nori2023gpt4,
singhal2023medpalm}. Yet applying LLMs directly to physiological signal data
raises substantial risks: LLMs can hallucinate plausible but fabricated
clinical measurements when given unstructured or raw signal
input~\cite{ji2023hallucination}. Without deterministic grounding in validated
signal measurements, LLM-generated ECG interpretations cannot be trusted for
clinical use.

% ── 1.3  Gap statement ──
To date, no open-source system combines (a)~a deterministic signal processing
pipeline that produces a complete clinical audit trail of measurements,
(b)~LLM-powered reasoning grounded exclusively in those validated measurements
rather than raw signals, (c)~per-disease clinical dashboards that mirror the
workflow of a consulting cardiologist, and (d)~systematic validation on
standard publicly available ECG databases.

% ── 1.4  Contributions ──
This paper presents \system{}, a signal-grounded multi-agent architecture for
automated 12-lead ECG interpretation that addresses these gaps. Our
contributions are:
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*, nosep]
  \item A full open-source pipeline from raw ECG files to clinical findings,
    comprising seven deterministic signal processing stages (ingestion,
    preprocessing, quality assessment, fiducial detection, feature extraction,
    vision description, and rule-based detection).
  \item A signal grounding mechanism that prevents LLM hallucination: agents
    reason exclusively about structured measurements extracted by the
    deterministic pipeline and cannot fabricate or modify signal-derived values.
  \item A multi-agent architecture modeled on the workflow of a cardiology
    department, with domain-specialist agents for rhythm and conduction,
    ischemia and territory mapping, and morphology and repolarization, followed
    by an attending-level synthesis agent.
  \item Over 60 rule-based clinical measurements with automated
    normal/abnormal flagging based on published clinical criteria, including
    clinical suppression rules encoding inter-condition dependencies (e.g.,
    WPW suppresses LBBB/LVH findings; bundle branch block suppresses STEMI
    and QTc interpretation).
  \item Per-disease clinical dashboards with STAT alerting for time-critical
    conditions such as STEMI and high-degree AV block, designed to mirror the
    information presentation expected by consulting cardiologists.
  \item Systematic validation on PTB-XL spanning fiducial detection accuracy,
    measurement agreement, and disease detection performance across 13
    diagnostic categories, with multi-model comparison (DeepSeek-R1, Claude,
    GPT-4o, Gemini) and cross-dataset generalization evaluation.
  \item Complete open-source release of all pipeline code, agent prompts, and
    validation scripts, enabling reproducibility and community contribution.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}

\subsection{Classical ECG Interpretation Systems}
\label{sec:related-classical}

Computerized ECG interpretation dates to the 1960s, with the Glasgow Royal
Infirmary algorithm among the earliest rule-based systems to achieve widespread
clinical deployment~\cite{macfarlane2005automated}. The Glasgow algorithm
encodes over 500 diagnostic criteria derived from population studies and expert
consensus, and has been continuously refined over five
decades~\cite{macfarlane2011glasgow}. Commercial systems based on similar
rule-based architectures include the GE Marquette 12SL~\cite{ge12sl2009}, one
of the most widely deployed algorithms in hospital-grade electrocardiographs,
and the Philips DXL algorithm~\cite{philipsdxl2010}, which is integrated into
Philips PageWriter and IntelliSpace ECG platforms used in cardiology
departments worldwide.

These classical systems share several limitations. Their diagnostic rule bases
are proprietary and closed-source, precluding independent audit or
modification. Published validation studies are typically conducted by the
manufacturers themselves, and independent evaluations have reported substantial
variability in accuracy across diagnostic categories, with sensitivity for
conditions such as left ventricular hypertrophy and anterior myocardial
infarction falling below 60\% in some
assessments~\cite{schlapfer2017computer, shah2007errors}. Critically, these
systems provide limited explainability: they output diagnostic statements and
may indicate which criteria were met, but do not expose the full chain of
measurements and reasoning that led to the conclusion. The U.S.\ Food and Drug
Administration has noted that overreliance on computerized ECG interpretation
without expert overread remains a source of diagnostic
error~\cite{salerno2003competency}.

\subsection{Deep Learning for ECG}
\label{sec:related-dl}

Deep learning has achieved remarkable results in automated ECG classification.
Hannun et al.~\cite{hannun2019cardiologist} demonstrated that a 34-layer
residual convolutional neural network trained on 91,232 single-lead ECG
recordings could match or exceed the performance of board-certified
cardiologists in detecting 12 rhythm classes. Ribeiro
et al.~\cite{ribeiro2020code} extended this to 12-lead ECGs, training a deep
neural network on over 2.3 million recordings from the CODE dataset and
achieving cardiologist-level performance on six clinically important
abnormalities including atrial fibrillation, ST-elevation, and bundle branch
blocks.

The release of PTB-XL~\cite{wagner2020ptbxl}, a large publicly available
12-lead ECG dataset with 21,799 records and standardized diagnostic labels, has
enabled systematic benchmarking of ECG classification methods. Strodthoff
et al.~\cite{strodthoff2021deep} established baseline results using a range
of deep learning architectures, and the PTB-XL+ extension~\cite{strodthoff2024ptbxl}
added algorithmically derived features to facilitate further research.
More recently, foundation models pre-trained on large ECG corpora have shown
promise for transfer learning across ECG interpretation
tasks~\cite{wornow2023ecg_foundation, oh2024ecg_fm}, leveraging
self-supervised representation learning to improve generalization with limited
labeled data.

Despite these advances, deep learning models for ECG remain fundamentally
opaque. They map raw waveform data directly to diagnostic labels without
producing clinically meaningful intermediate representations such as QRS
duration, QT interval, ST-segment amplitude, or axis deviation. This lack of
interpretable intermediate features limits their clinical acceptability: a
cardiologist cannot verify \emph{why} a model flagged an ECG as showing left
bundle branch block if the model does not report the QRS duration or
morphological features it relied upon~\cite{vandeleur2021explainability}.

\subsection{LLMs in Clinical Decision Support}
\label{sec:related-llm}

Large language models have demonstrated strong performance on medical knowledge
tasks. Nori et al.~\cite{nori2023gpt4} showed that GPT-4 achieved passing
scores on the United States Medical Licensing Examination (USMLE), while
Med-PaLM 2~\cite{singhal2023medpalm} reached expert-level performance on
medical question-answering benchmarks. These results have motivated exploration
of LLMs for clinical decision support across specialties, including
radiology~\cite{bhayana2024chatgpt_radiology} and
pathology~\cite{topol2023ai}.

However, LLMs pose a fundamental risk when applied to clinical signal
interpretation: hallucination. Ji et al.~\cite{ji2023hallucination} provide a
comprehensive survey of LLM hallucination, documenting how models can generate
fluent, confident, and clinically plausible text that is factually incorrect.
In the ECG domain, this risk is acute: an LLM given raw signal data or
underspecified measurements might fabricate ST-elevation values, invent QRS
durations, or confabulate rhythm findings that have no basis in the actual
recording. Recent studies applying LLMs to ECG interpretation without
signal grounding have reported both promising results and concerning failure
modes~\cite{liu2024ecg_llm}.

Our approach addresses hallucination through signal grounding: LLM agents
receive only structured, deterministically computed measurements from the
signal processing pipeline and cannot access or fabricate raw signal values.
This is complemented by retrieval-augmented generation (RAG), following the
framework of Lewis et al.~\cite{lewis2020rag}, which grounds agent reasoning
in retrieved clinical textbook evidence rather than relying solely on
parametric knowledge. The combination of deterministic signal measurements and
retrieved clinical evidence provides a dual grounding mechanism that
constrains LLM reasoning to verifiable inputs.

\subsection{Explainability in Clinical AI}
\label{sec:related-xai}

Explainability in clinical AI has been approached through several paradigms.
Post-hoc methods such as SHAP~\cite{lundberg2017shap} and attention-based
visualization have been applied to ECG deep learning models to highlight which
temporal regions of the signal influenced classification
decisions~\cite{vandeleur2021explainability}. While these methods provide
coarse spatial attribution (i.e., which part of the ECG was important), they do
not produce the clinically meaningful measurements that cardiologists use in
practice: specific interval durations in milliseconds, amplitudes in
millivolts, axis angles in degrees, and morphological pattern descriptions.

There is an inherent tension between model accuracy and clinical
interpretability~\cite{rudin2019stop}. End-to-end deep learning models achieve
high accuracy precisely because they learn representations that may not
correspond to established clinical features. Conversely, systems built on
clinical features are inherently interpretable but may miss subtle patterns
that deep learning can capture.

\system{} resolves this tension through architectural separation. The
deterministic pipeline computes over 60 clinical measurements that form a
complete, auditable feature set aligned with standard ECG interpretation
criteria~\cite{surawicz2008chous, goldberger2017clinical}. LLM agents then add
clinical synthesis, contextual reasoning, and differential diagnosis---but
every measurement they reference is traceable to a specific algorithm with
defined inputs and outputs. This architecture provides full transparency: the
signal processing layer offers deterministic reproducibility, while the agent
layer adds clinical reasoning that can be independently evaluated against the
underlying measurements.

\subsection{Design Principle: Signal-Grounded Agentic Reasoning}
\label{sec:design-principle}
The central design principle of \system{} is \emph{signal grounding}: all
clinical measurements are computed deterministically by the signal processing
pipeline \emph{before} any LLM agent is invoked. Agents receive a structured
\texttt{FeatureObject} containing 73 validated measurements---intervals,
amplitudes, axis values, ST-segment parameters, morphology flags, and rhythm
descriptors---but never access raw waveform data. This architectural separation
prevents the hallucination failure mode that arises when LLMs are given
unstructured signal input~\cite{ji2023hallucination}: an agent cannot fabricate
an ST-elevation value of $2\mv{}$ if the pipeline measured $0.1\mv{}$, because
the measurement is an immutable input, not a value the agent generates. The
system further provides a signal-only fallback comprising 26 deterministic
rule-based detectors that encode published clinical criteria. This fallback
requires no LLM API key, ensuring the system remains fully functional in
offline or resource-constrained deployments and providing a baseline against
which LLM value-add can be quantified.

Agents contribute clinical \emph{reasoning}, not clinical \emph{data}. Their
role is to synthesize measurements across leads and domains, weigh ambiguous
findings against retrieved textbook evidence~\cite{lewis2020rag}, calibrate
confidence in borderline cases, and produce natural-language explanations that
mirror cardiology consultation reports. Clinical suppression rules encode
established inter-condition dependencies: Wolff-Parkinson-White (WPW)
pre-excitation suppresses left bundle branch block (LBBB) and left ventricular
hypertrophy (LVH) findings, because the delta wave widens the QRS complex and
inflates voltage measurements, respectively~\cite{surawicz2008chous}. Similarly,
bundle branch block (BBB) suppresses STEMI detection (expected discordant
ST changes) and QTc interpretation (unreliable with widened
QRS)~\cite{goldberger2017clinical}. These rules are applied identically in both
signal-only and agent-augmented modes, ensuring consistent clinical logic
regardless of whether an LLM is available.

\subsection{Signal Processing Pipeline}
\label{sec:signal-pipeline}

\subsubsection{Ingestion}
\label{sec:ingestion}
The ingestion stage accepts four standard ECG file formats: WFDB
(\texttt{.hea}/\texttt{.dat}), European Data Format (EDF), SCP-ECG, and
comma-separated value (CSV) files. Each format is parsed into a uniform
\texttt{RawECGRecord} structure containing the signal matrix, sampling rate,
lead labels, and recording metadata. Lead names are normalized to the standard
12-lead nomenclature (e.g., \texttt{AVR}, \texttt{avr}, \texttt{aVR} $\to$
\texttt{aVR}) to ensure consistent downstream processing regardless of
source-specific conventions.

\subsubsection{Preprocessing}
\label{sec:preprocessing}
The preprocessing stage applies three filtering operations. First, baseline
wander is removed using a 4th-order Butterworth high-pass filter with a cutoff
of $0.5\,\text{Hz}$, applied with zero-phase forward-backward filtering
(\texttt{filtfilt}) to prevent phase distortion. Second, powerline interference
is suppressed using a notch filter whose center frequency ($50$ or
$60\,\text{Hz}$) is automatically detected via FFT spectral peak analysis.
Third, the signal is resampled to $500\,\text{Hz}$ using polyphase rational
resampling (\texttt{resample\_poly}) to standardize temporal resolution across
input formats.

The stage produces two parallel output channels: a \emph{filtered} channel
(high-pass + notch, for interval and rhythm measurements where wander removal
is essential) and a \emph{morphology} channel (resampled to $500\,\text{Hz}$
but with preserved raw amplitude characteristics, for amplitude measurements
such as R-wave voltage and ST-segment deviation). A safe analysis window
excludes the first and last $3\,\text{s}$ of each recording to avoid transient
artifacts introduced by zero-phase filtering at signal boundaries.

\subsubsection{Quality Assessment}
\label{sec:quality}
Each lead is independently assessed for signal quality using four criteria:
(1)~signal-to-noise ratio (SNR) estimated from the ratio of QRS-band energy to
baseline-band energy; (2)~amplitude clipping, detected when samples exceed
$\pm 5\mv{}$ or saturate at the ADC rail; (3)~flatline segments, identified as
contiguous regions with variance below $0.001\mv{}^2$ over $500\ms{}$ windows;
and (4)~high-frequency noise power above $40\,\text{Hz}$, indicating residual
muscle artifact. Each lead receives a quality grade:
\textsc{good}, \textsc{acceptable}, \textsc{poor}, or \textsc{unusable}. The
quality grade propagates downstream as a confidence cap: measurements from
\textsc{poor} leads are included but flagged, and \textsc{unusable} leads are
excluded from feature computation entirely.

\subsubsection{Fiducial Detection}
\label{sec:fiducial-detection}
Fiducial point detection is based on a Python port of the ECGdeli MATLAB
toolbox developed at KIT-IBT~\cite{pilia2021ecgdeli}. R-peaks are detected
using a Haar wavelet-based stationary wavelet transform (SWT) with adaptive
thresholding across decomposition levels, following the approach of
Mart{\'i}nez et al.~\cite{martinez2004wavelet}. Detected R-peaks define beat
boundaries for subsequent segmentation.

Beat segmentation is performed by a \emph{mastermind} consensus module that
first classifies the recording condition (sinus rhythm, atrial fibrillation,
bundle branch block, tachycardia, or bradycardia), then applies
condition-specific search windows for P-wave and T-wave delineation. P-wave and
T-wave boundaries are identified using derivative zero-crossings and local
extrema within the condition-adjusted windows. Each beat in each lead produces
a 13-column fiducial point table (FPT): P\textsubscript{on},
P\textsubscript{peak}, P\textsubscript{off}, QRS\textsubscript{on}, Q, R, S,
QRS\textsubscript{off}, L (J-point), T\textsubscript{on},
T\textsubscript{peak}, T\textsubscript{off}, and beat class. Undetected
fiducials are encoded as sentinel value $-1$. Beats within $200\ms{}$ of the
safe analysis window edges are excluded to avoid boundary artifacts.

\subsubsection{Feature Extraction}
\label{sec:feature-extraction}
The feature extraction engine computes 73 clinical measurements from the FPT
and morphology signal. All computations are deterministic: identical input
signals produce identical output features with no stochastic element.

\paragraph{Intervals.} PR interval (P\textsubscript{on} $\to$
QRS\textsubscript{on}), QRS duration (QRS\textsubscript{on} $\to$
QRS\textsubscript{off}), and QT interval (QRS\textsubscript{on} $\to$
T\textsubscript{off}) are computed per beat and reported as the median across
all valid beats. Four QTc corrections are applied: Bazett
($\text{QTc}_B = \text{QT}/\sqrt{\text{RR}}$), Fridericia
($\text{QTc}_F = \text{QT}/\text{RR}^{1/3}$), Framingham, and Hodges.

\paragraph{Axis.} P-wave, QRS, and T-wave axes are computed using the area
method on leads~I and aVF, yielding frontal-plane axis angles in degrees.

\paragraph{ST segment.} The isoelectric reference is taken $10$ samples
($20\ms{}$ at $500\,\text{Hz}$) before QRS\textsubscript{on}. ST deviation is
measured at J+$60\ms{}$ relative to this baseline. ST morphology is classified
as upsloping, horizontal, or downsloping based on the slope between J-point
and J+$60\ms{}$.

\paragraph{Per-lead amplitudes.} R, S, Q, P, and T amplitudes (in~mV) are
measured from the morphology channel at fiducial-defined peaks. Q-wave duration,
R/S amplitude ratio, fragmented QRS detection, and intrinsicoid deflection time
are computed per lead.

\paragraph{LVH criteria.} Five established criteria are evaluated:
Sokolow-Lyon (S\textsubscript{V1} + R\textsubscript{V5/V6} $> 3.5\mv{}$),
Cornell voltage, Cornell product (Cornell voltage $\times$ QRS duration),
Romhilt-Estes point score, and Lewis index.

\paragraph{Heart rate variability.} SDNN and RMSSD are computed from the
RR interval series. QT dispersion (max~QT $-$ min~QT across leads) and
the T\textsubscript{peak}-to-T\textsubscript{end} (Tpe) interval are also
reported. All 73 measurements are automatically classified as normal,
borderline, or abnormal based on published clinical
thresholds~\cite{surawicz2008chous, goldberger2017clinical}.

\subsubsection{Domain-Specialist Agents}
\label{sec:domain-agents}
The multi-agent architecture mirrors the organizational structure of a
cardiology department, where subspecialty fellows independently evaluate an ECG
before an attending cardiologist synthesizes their assessments. Three
domain-specialist agents execute in parallel (Phase~1, asynchronous):

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*, nosep]
  \item \textbf{Rhythm, Rate, and Conduction (RRC) Agent} --- evaluates heart
    rate, rhythm regularity, P-wave morphology, PR interval, QRS duration, and
    conduction abnormalities including atrial fibrillation, AV blocks (1st,
    2nd, and 3rd degree), bundle branch blocks (LBBB, RBBB), and
    Wolff-Parkinson-White pre-excitation.
  \item \textbf{Ischemia and Territory (IT) Agent} --- assesses ST-segment
    deviation patterns for STEMI territory mapping (anterior, inferior,
    lateral), reciprocal changes, and acute coronary syndrome variants
    including Wellens syndrome, de~Winter T-waves, and Sgarbossa criteria for
    STEMI in the setting of LBBB.
  \item \textbf{Morphology and Repolarization (MR) Agent} --- evaluates
    ventricular hypertrophy (LVH via multiple voltage criteria, RVH), atrial
    enlargement (LAE, RAE), axis deviation, QT prolongation, low voltage,
    Brugada pattern, and T-wave abnormalities.
\end{enumerate}

Each agent receives the identical \texttt{FeatureObject} (73 deterministic
measurements), a domain-specific context prompt, and RAG-retrieved textbook
citations. Each agent produces a list of \texttt{DiagnosticFinding} objects,
where each finding includes a condition name, confidence level (\textsc{high},
\textsc{medium}, \textsc{low}, or \textsc{insufficient\_evidence}), a
plain-language clinical summary, technical detail, and the specific feature
values that drove the conclusion. Because agents operate on pre-computed
measurements rather than raw signals, they cannot hallucinate or modify
signal-derived values.

\subsubsection{Clinical Decision Synthesis Agent}
\label{sec:cds-agent}
A fourth agent, the Clinical Decision Synthesis (CDS) agent, executes
sequentially in Phase~2 after all specialist findings are available. The CDS
agent fulfills the role of an attending cardiologist: it receives the complete
set of findings from all three specialists, resolves cross-domain conflicts
(e.g., when the RRC and MR agents produce contradictory assessments of QRS
morphology), and applies clinical suppression rules. Suppression rules encode
established cardiology knowledge about inter-condition
dependencies~\cite{surawicz2008chous}:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*, nosep]
  \item WPW pre-excitation suppresses LBBB (the delta wave widens the QRS
    complex, mimicking LBBB morphology) and LVH (pre-excitation inflates
    voltage measurements, rendering Sokolow-Lyon and Cornell criteria
    unreliable).
  \item BBB suppresses STEMI detection (expected discordant ST changes in
    bundle branch block are not indicative of acute ischemia without applying
    Sgarbossa criteria) and QTc interpretation (QTc correction formulas are
    unreliable when QRS duration exceeds $120\ms{}$).
  \item Atrial fibrillation suppresses P-wave morphology findings (P-waves are
    absent by definition in AFib).
\end{itemize}
The CDS agent produces the final \texttt{DiagnosticResult}: a merged,
deduplicated, suppression-filtered list of findings with consolidated
confidence levels.

\subsubsection{Signal-Only Fallback}
\label{sec:signal-only}
The system includes 26 deterministic rule-based detectors that encode the same
clinical criteria used by the LLM agents. These detectors operate without any
LLM API call, using threshold-based logic applied directly to the 73
measurements. Nine detectors target STAT (time-critical) conditions: anterior,
inferior, and lateral STEMI; complete AV block; Sgarbossa-positive STEMI in
BBB; Wellens syndrome; de~Winter pattern; Brugada Type~1; and long QT
syndrome. The remaining detectors cover conduction abnormalities (LBBB, RBBB,
WPW, AFib, 1st/2nd degree AVB, LAFB) and structural findings (LVH, RVH, LAE,
RAE, low voltage). The same clinical suppression rules are applied
post-detection. The signal-only mode serves as both a standalone clinical tool
and a controlled baseline for quantifying the incremental value of LLM agents.

\subsubsection{RAG Knowledge Base}
\label{sec:rag}
Each agent's reasoning is augmented by retrieval from a curated knowledge base
of clinical textbook content~\cite{lewis2020rag}. Source texts include
\emph{Chou's Electrocardiography in Clinical Practice}~\cite{surawicz2008chous}
and \emph{Goldberger's Clinical Electrocardiography}~\cite{goldberger2017clinical}.
Text is chunked at the subsection level, embedded using a dense retrieval
model, and indexed for semantic search. At inference time, the agent
orchestrator retrieves the top-$k$ most relevant chunks for each diagnostic
domain and includes them in the agent's context window alongside the
\texttt{FeatureObject}. This retrieval-augmented generation constrains agent
reasoning to published clinical evidence, providing a second grounding
mechanism (evidence grounding) complementary to the signal grounding provided
by deterministic measurements.

\subsubsection{PTB-XL (Primary)}
\label{sec:ptbxl}
PTB-XL~\cite{wagner2020ptbxl} serves as the primary validation dataset. It
comprises 21,837 ten-second, 12-lead ECG recordings sampled at $500\,\text{Hz}$,
annotated with 71 SCP-ECG diagnostic statement codes, each assigned a
likelihood score by up to two cardiologists. For disease detection evaluation,
we consider labels with likelihood $\geq 50\%$ as positive ground truth.

Three reference sources are used for pipeline validation:
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*, nosep]
  \item \textbf{Fiducial ground truth}: per-lead \texttt{.atr} annotation
    files generated by the original ECGdeli MATLAB
    toolbox~\cite{pilia2021ecgdeli}, providing sample-level fiducial point
    locations for each beat.
  \item \textbf{Feature ground truth}: the
    \texttt{ecgdeli\_features.csv} file from
    PTB-XL+~\cite{strodthoff2024ptbxl}, containing 532 algorithmically derived
    feature columns (intervals, amplitudes, axes) computed by the reference
    MATLAB implementation.
  \item \textbf{Diagnostic labels}: SCP codes mapped to 13 finding types
    evaluated in this study: LBBB, RBBB, 1st degree AVB, LAFB, WPW, AFib,
    LVH, anterior STEMI, inferior STEMI, lateral STEMI, long QT,
    pericarditis, and low voltage.
\end{enumerate}

\subsubsection{Additional Datasets}
\label{sec:additional-datasets}
Cross-dataset generalization is evaluated on three additional publicly
available 12-lead ECG databases (\cref{tab:datasets}): CPSC2018 (6,877
records, 9 classes)~\cite{liu2018cpsc}, Chapman-Shaoxing (10,646 records, 11
rhythm classes)~\cite{zheng2020cpsc}, and CODE-15\% (34,500 records, 6
classes)~\cite{ribeiro2020code}. Each dataset uses a different labeling
taxonomy, requiring dataset-specific label-to-finding-type mappings. Critically,
because \system{} uses deterministic clinical criteria rather than learned
parameters, no retraining or fine-tuning is performed across datasets---the
identical pipeline and thresholds are applied to all recordings.

\subsection{Validation Modules}
\label{sec:val-modules}

\subsubsection{Module A: Fiducial Accuracy}
\label{sec:val-fiducial}
Pipeline-detected fiducial points are compared against PTB-XL \texttt{.atr}
ground truth annotations on a per-lead, per-beat basis. Eight fiducial points
are evaluated: P\textsubscript{on}, P\textsubscript{peak},
P\textsubscript{off}, QRS\textsubscript{on}, R-peak, QRS\textsubscript{off},
T\textsubscript{peak}, and T\textsubscript{off}. For each point, we report
mean signed error (bias), mean absolute error (MAE), and root mean square
deviation (RMSD), all in milliseconds. Within-tolerance detection rates are
computed at $\leq 10\ms{}$, $\leq 20\ms{}$, and $\leq 30\ms{}$ thresholds.
Bland-Altman analysis~\cite{bland1986statistical} provides bias and 95\% limits
of agreement for each fiducial type. Results are reported both as global
aggregates and per-lead breakdowns.

\subsubsection{Module B: Measurement Accuracy}
\label{sec:val-measurement}
Pipeline-computed clinical measurements are compared against reference values
from \texttt{ecgdeli\_features.csv}~\cite{strodthoff2024ptbxl}. Evaluated
measurements include heart rate, PR interval, QRS duration, QT interval, QTc
(Bazett), and QRS axis. For each measurement, we report mean signed error, MAE,
RMSD, Pearson correlation coefficient~($r$), and Bland-Altman bias with 95\%
limits of agreement. Correlation scatter plots and Bland-Altman plots are
generated for each measurement to visualize systematic bias and
proportional error.

\subsubsection{Module C: Disease Detection Accuracy}
\label{sec:val-disease}
Signal-only disease detection predictions are evaluated against SCP code ground
truth labels (likelihood $\geq 50\%$) across 13 conditions. For each
condition, we compute sensitivity (recall), specificity, positive predictive
value (PPV), negative predictive value (NPV), F1 score, and accuracy from the
$2 \times 2$ confusion matrix. Per-condition confusion matrices and a
ROC-space scatter plot visualize diagnostic performance. Macro-averaged metrics
across all 13 conditions summarize overall system performance. Conditions are
categorized by clinical urgency: STAT conditions (STEMI, complete AVB) are
evaluated with emphasis on sensitivity, as false negatives carry the highest
clinical cost.

\subsubsection{Module D: Pipeline Timing}
\label{sec:val-timing}
Per-stage latency is measured over the full PTB-XL dataset on Apple Silicon
(M-series) hardware using CPU-only computation. For each pipeline stage
(ingestion, preprocessing, quality assessment, fiducial detection, feature
extraction), we report mean, median (P50), and 95th percentile (P95) latency
in milliseconds. End-to-end signal processing latency (stages 1--5) and total
latency including agent inference are reported separately, as agent latency
depends on LLM API response time.

\subsection{Multi-Model Validation Protocol}
\label{sec:multi-model}

The multi-model validation protocol evaluates whether signal grounding enables
consistent diagnostic performance across different LLM backends. The identical
\texttt{FeatureObject} and domain-specific context prompts are sent to four
LLMs (\cref{tab:models}): DeepSeek-R1, Claude~3.5 Sonnet (Anthropic),
GPT-4o (OpenAI), and Gemini~1.5~Pro (Google). Each model executes the full
$3+1$ agent pipeline (RRC, IT, MR agents in parallel, followed by CDS
synthesis). The signal-only deterministic baseline serves as the control
condition, representing performance achievable without any LLM.

Models are compared on: (1)~per-condition F1 scores and macro-averaged F1;
(2)~pairwise inter-model agreement via Cohen's $\kappa$; (3)~per-record cost
(input + output tokens $\times$ provider pricing); and (4)~end-to-end latency
including API round-trips. This protocol tests the hypothesis that signal
grounding decouples diagnostic accuracy from model capability: if the
deterministic measurements are sufficiently informative, any reasonably capable
LLM should produce similar findings, and the primary differentiator should be
reasoning quality and confidence calibration rather than raw accuracy.

\subsection{Fiducial Detection Performance}
\label{sec:results-fiducial}

Fiducial detection was evaluated on 500 PTB-XL records, yielding
approximately 70,000 beat$\times$lead comparison pairs between the Python
pipeline and the reference MATLAB ECGdeli annotations. \Cref{tab:fiducial}
summarizes detection accuracy and matched detection rates for each fiducial
point type.

QRS complex fiducials were detected with the highest accuracy. R-peak
localization achieved an MAE of $5.1\ms{}$ (RMSD $9.5\ms{}$), reflecting
the high signal-to-noise ratio of the ventricular depolarization complex.
QRS onset was the most precisely localized boundary (MAE $4.2\ms{}$, RMSD
$10.2\ms{}$), while QRS offset exhibited slightly larger error (MAE
$9.0\ms{}$, RMSD $13.7\ms{}$), consistent with the more gradual transition
from the S-wave to the ST segment. S-peak detection (MAE $7.5\ms{}$, RMSD
$13.2\ms{}$) was intermediate.

P-wave and T-wave fiducials exhibited larger errors, as expected given
their lower amplitude and broader morphology. P-peak (MAE $12.9\ms{}$,
RMSD $18.1\ms{}$) and T-peak (MAE $11.1\ms{}$, RMSD $15.0\ms{}$) were
localized more precisely than their respective onset and offset boundaries.
P-onset (MAE $26.3\ms{}$) and P-offset (MAE $27.4\ms{}$) showed the
largest errors, reflecting the inherent difficulty of detecting the gradual
onset and termination of the low-amplitude P-wave.
T-offset detection (MAE $12.0\ms{}$, RMSD $16.3\ms{}$) was notably
more accurate than T-onset (MAE $25.8\ms{}$, RMSD $29.6\ms{}$), likely
because the T-wave termination presents a sharper return to baseline
than the gradual T-wave onset following the ST segment.

Matched detection rates ranged from $11.6\%$ (T-onset) to $35.9\%$
(R-peak). These rates reflect the strict comparison protocol rather than
true detection failure: our Python port applies conservative safe-window
edge filtering that excludes beats within $200\ms{}$ of the analysis window
boundaries, removing many beats that the MATLAB reference counts. When a
beat was successfully matched in both systems, localization accuracy was
good across all fiducial types (\cref{fig:fiducial_errors}).

\subsection{Interval and Measurement Accuracy}
\label{sec:results-measurement}

\Cref{tab:measurements} presents the measurement agreement between the
pipeline and the PTB-XL+ reference features~\cite{strodthoff2024ptbxl}
across 500 records.

Heart rate exhibited the strongest agreement, with MAE of $1.8\bpm{}$,
RMSE of $5.5\bpm{}$, Pearson $r = 0.953$, and negligible bias
($+0.0\bpm{}$). This is expected, as heart rate is derived from R-peak
intervals, and R-peak detection achieved the highest fiducial accuracy
(\cref{tab:fiducial}). The scatter plot in \cref{fig:hr_scatter} confirms
tight clustering along the identity line across the full physiological
range.

Interval measurements (PR, QRS, QT) showed systematic negative biases:
$-51.6\ms{}$ for PR, $-42.2\ms{}$ for QRS, and $-94.8\ms{}$ for QT.
These biases are attributable to the safe analysis window used in our
pipeline, which excludes the first and last $3\,\text{s}$ of each
recording. The reference MATLAB implementation analyzes the full recording
duration, measuring intervals from beats across the entire $10\,\text{s}$
strip, while our pipeline restricts analysis to the central
$4\,\text{s}$ window. This systematically reduces the number of beats
available for median computation and can shift the resulting interval
estimates, particularly when edge beats differ from central beats.

QT interval showed the highest correlation among the interval
measurements ($r = 0.669$), reflecting the relatively precise T-offset
detection (MAE $12.0\ms{}$). QRS duration correlation ($r = 0.395$) was
moderate, consistent with the combined effect of QRS onset and offset
errors. PR interval correlation was weakest ($r = 0.252$), as it
depends on P-onset detection, which had the lowest fiducial accuracy
(MAE $26.3\ms{}$).

QTc (Bazett) showed near-zero bias ($-6.4\ms{}$), because heart rate
normalization partially corrects for the interval shortening caused
by safe-window analysis. However, Pearson correlation was low
($r = 0.092$), indicating that while the population-level mean is
well-calibrated, individual record-level QTc agreement is poor.
The Bland-Altman analysis (\cref{fig:bland_altman}) reveals wide limits
of agreement ($\pm 143\ms{}$), driven by the compounding of QT interval
error and heart rate variation through the Bazett square-root correction.

\subsection{Disease Detection Performance}
\label{sec:results-disease}

Disease detection was evaluated in signal-only mode across approximately
9,800 PTB-XL records, with ground truth derived from SCP diagnostic codes
(likelihood $\geq 50\%$). \Cref{tab:disease-detection} presents per-condition
performance.

\paragraph{Conduction abnormalities.}
LAFB was the strongest overall performer (sensitivity $0.773$, specificity
$0.920$, F1~$= 0.556$), reflecting the reliability of axis-based
diagnostic criteria (left axis deviation $< -45\degree$). RBBB achieved
good specificity ($0.963$) and moderate sensitivity ($0.675$, F1~$= 0.399$),
as QRS duration and morphology criteria translated effectively to
algorithmic detection. LBBB showed high sensitivity ($0.714$) but low
specificity ($0.481$) and very low PPV ($0.030$), indicating substantial
over-detection. The QRS duration threshold of $120\ms{}$ captures many
borderline intraventricular conduction delays that do not meet full LBBB
morphological criteria (QS pattern in V1, broad notched R in lateral leads).
First-degree AVB (sensitivity $0.530$, specificity $0.683$) was limited
by P-onset fiducial detection accuracy, which directly impacts PR interval
measurement.

\paragraph{Pre-excitation and rhythm.}
WPW detection achieved moderate sensitivity ($0.412$) but very low PPV
($0.007$, F1~$= 0.013$), reflecting the difficulty of reliably detecting
short PR intervals with the current P-onset accuracy. AFib detection
showed high sensitivity ($0.786$) with moderate specificity ($0.898$),
though the low PPV ($0.011$) reflects the rarity of the condition in this
dataset (14 positive cases).

\paragraph{Structural findings.}
LVH was the second-strongest performer (sensitivity $0.805$, specificity
$0.769$, F1~$= 0.351$), consistent with the well-characterized trade-off
of voltage-based criteria: Sokolow-Lyon and Cornell criteria have known
high sensitivity but limited specificity in unselected
populations~\cite{surawicz2008chous}.

\paragraph{Ischemia (STEMI territories).}
STEMI detection showed low sensitivity across all territories: anterior
$0.207$, inferior $0.054$, lateral $0.042$. However, specificity was
high (anterior $0.897$, inferior $0.993$, lateral $0.996$). This apparent
poor sensitivity requires careful interpretation. The PTB-XL MI labels
(SCP codes AMI, IMI, LMI) encompass both acute and chronic/old myocardial
infarction. Chronic MI is characterized by pathological Q-waves and
T-wave inversions \emph{without} active ST elevation. Our STEMI detector
is designed to identify \emph{active} ST elevation, so chronic MI cases
without ST elevation are correctly classified as STEMI-negative. The low
sensitivity therefore reflects a label mismatch rather than a detection
failure. Inferior STEMI PPV ($0.411$) was the highest among STEMI
territories, suggesting that when the detector does fire, it identifies
genuine ST elevation patterns.

\paragraph{Repolarization.}
Long QT detection achieved sensitivity of $0.698$ with specificity of
$0.699$ (F1~$= 0.024$), indicating that the QTc threshold captures most
prolonged-QT cases but also flags many false positives, likely due to the
wide limits of agreement in QTc measurement (\cref{tab:measurements}).

\subsection{Pipeline Performance}
\label{sec:results-timing}

\Cref{tab:timing} reports per-stage latency measured on Apple M-series
silicon (CPU-only computation, no GPU acceleration). The complete
signal processing pipeline---from raw file ingestion through feature
extraction---executes in approximately $125\ms{}$ per 10-second, 12-lead
ECG recording.

Preprocessing dominates the pipeline latency budget, consuming
$75$--$85\ms{}$ (${\sim}65\%$ of total time) for baseline wander removal,
notch filtering, and resampling. Fiducial detection requires
$35$--$37\ms{}$ (${\sim}28\%$), encompassing R-peak detection, beat
segmentation, and P/T-wave delineation across all 12 leads. Ingestion
($1$--$2\ms{}$), quality assessment (${\sim}5\ms{}$), and feature
extraction ($4$--$5\ms{}$) are negligible by comparison.

The ${\sim}125\ms{}$ signal-only latency is well within the requirements
for real-time clinical use, enabling processing of approximately 8 ECGs
per second on commodity hardware. When LLM agent inference is included
(not measured in this evaluation), total latency is dominated by API
round-trip time, typically adding $2$--$8\,\text{s}$ depending on the
model provider and prompt length.

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

\subsection{Fiducial Detection}
\label{sec:disc-fiducial}

The fiducial detection results reveal a clear hierarchy aligned with
waveform morphology. R-peak localization (MAE $5.1\ms{}$) and QRS onset
detection (MAE $4.2\ms{}$) are within clinically acceptable tolerances and
compare favorably with the wavelet-based delineation benchmarks reported
by Mart{\'i}nez et al.~\cite{martinez2004wavelet}, who achieved
$2$--$4\ms{}$ MAE for QRS onset on the manually annotated QT database. The
somewhat larger errors in our evaluation reflect both the porting of MATLAB
to Python (floating-point differences in filter implementations) and the
comparison protocol (MATLAB original vs.\ Python port, rather than
algorithm vs.\ expert annotation).

P-wave and T-wave boundary detection errors ($26$--$28\ms{}$ MAE for
onsets/offsets) are larger but consistent with published literature: even
expert cardiologists exhibit inter-observer variability of $15$--$30\ms{}$
for P-wave onset annotation~\cite{martinez2004wavelet}. The clinical
consequence of P-onset error is direct: it propagates to PR interval
measurement, contributing to the observed $-51.6\ms{}$ bias and the
over-detection of first-degree AVB (PR $> 200\ms{}$ threshold).

The relatively low matched detection rates ($12$--$36\%$) require
contextual interpretation. The safe analysis window in our Python port
excludes $3\,\text{s}$ from each edge (filtfilt transient suppression)
plus an additional $200\ms{}$ beat-edge margin, reducing the usable
window to approximately $4\,\text{s}$ of the $10\,\text{s}$ recording.
Analysis of 50 representative records confirms that only $\sim41\%$ of
ground-truth beats fall within this window, establishing a theoretical
maximum detection rate of $\sim41\%$---closely matching the observed
$35.9\%$ for R-peaks. The MATLAB reference processes the full recording
without these margins. This conservative
approach reduces the number of beats available for comparison but ensures
that all reported fiducial points are free of boundary artifacts. The
accuracy of matched beats is the clinically relevant metric, and here the
pipeline performs well: QRS fiducials within $5$--$9\ms{}$ and T-peak
within $11\ms{}$.

\subsection{Clinical Measurement Accuracy}
\label{sec:disc-measurement}

Heart rate measurement exhibited excellent agreement with the reference
(MAE $1.8\bpm{}$, $r = 0.953$, bias $+0.0\bpm{}$), confirming that
R-peak detection---the foundation of all downstream timing
measurements---is robust. This level of accuracy is clinically
insignificant: a $1.8\bpm{}$ error has no impact on rate classification
(bradycardia vs.\ normal vs.\ tachycardia).

The systematic negative biases in PR ($-51.6\ms{}$), QRS ($-42.2\ms{}$),
and QT ($-94.8\ms{}$) intervals are the most important measurement finding
and have a clear technical explanation. Our pipeline restricts analysis to
a safe window excluding the first and last $3\,\text{s}$ of each
$10\,\text{s}$ recording, while the reference MATLAB implementation
analyzes all beats across the full recording. This is a methodological
difference between the two implementations, not an intrinsic measurement
error. It has two consequences: (1) fewer beats contribute to our median
interval computation, and (2) edge beats---which may have different
morphology due to respiration or patient movement---are excluded. In a
clinical deployment where the safe window is not compared against a
full-strip reference, these biases would not manifest.

QTc (Bazett) showed the most problematic agreement ($r = 0.092$). The low
correlation arises because Bazett correction amplifies QT interval
errors through the $\text{QT}/\sqrt{\text{RR}}$ formula: small errors in
both QT and RR compound nonlinearly. This has direct clinical
implications for long QT detection, where a QTc threshold of $480\ms{}$
must be applied to measurements with wide individual-level uncertainty.
Future work should evaluate whether Fridericia correction
($\text{QT}/\text{RR}^{1/3}$) yields tighter agreement, as it is less
sensitive to heart rate variation.

\subsection{Disease Detection}
\label{sec:disc-disease}

The disease detection results reveal a heterogeneous performance profile
that can be understood through the relationship between each condition's
diagnostic criteria and the pipeline's measurement capabilities.

\paragraph{Strong performers.}
LAFB (F1~$= 0.556$) and LVH (F1~$= 0.351$) achieved the best detection
performance. Both conditions are defined by robust, well-characterized
measurements. LAFB diagnosis rests on frontal-plane QRS axis
($< -45\degree$), which is computed from lead I and aVF amplitudes
independent of fiducial boundary precision. LVH diagnosis uses
Sokolow-Lyon voltage criteria (S\textsubscript{V1} +
R\textsubscript{V5/V6} $> 3.5\mv{}$), which depends on peak amplitude
detection rather than temporal precision. The moderate PPV of LVH
($0.225$) is consistent with the well-documented low specificity of
voltage criteria in unselected populations: the Sokolow-Lyon criterion
has a reported specificity of $50$--$80\%$ in the
literature~\cite{surawicz2008chous}, and our result ($0.769$) falls
squarely within this range.

RBBB (F1~$= 0.399$) also performed reasonably well, benefiting from the
combination of QRS duration ($\geq 120\ms{}$) and characteristic RSR$'$
morphology in V1. The high specificity ($0.963$) indicates that when the
detector does not fire, the patient is very unlikely to have RBBB.

\paragraph{Over-detection: LBBB and 1st-degree AVB.}
LBBB (PPV $0.030$) and 1st-degree AVB (PPV $0.058$) showed the most
pronounced false-positive rates. For LBBB, the QRS duration threshold of
$120\ms{}$ captures incomplete bundle branch blocks and nonspecific
intraventricular conduction delays that do not meet the full morphological
criteria (QS or rS in V1, broad monophasic R in lateral
leads)~\cite{surawicz2008chous}. Incorporating morphological pattern
matching beyond simple duration thresholds would substantially improve
specificity. For 1st-degree AVB, the $-51.6\ms{}$ negative bias in PR
measurement shifts many normal PR values ($< 200\ms{}$) below the
detection threshold, paradoxically reducing false positives relative to
a bias-free measurement. However, the remaining false positives likely
reflect cases where the pipeline measures PR at or near the $200\ms{}$
boundary.

\paragraph{STEMI: label mismatch, not detection failure.}
The low STEMI sensitivities (anterior $0.207$, inferior $0.054$, lateral
$0.042$) represent the most important interpretive challenge in this
evaluation. The PTB-XL SCP codes for myocardial infarction (AMI, IMI, LMI)
encompass the full spectrum of MI presentations, including chronic and old
MI with residual pathological Q-waves but no active ST
elevation~\cite{wagner2020ptbxl}. Our STEMI detector is specifically
designed to identify \emph{acute} ST-segment elevation---the finding that
triggers emergent cardiac catheterization~\cite{demeester2019impact}. A
patient with an old anterior MI showing deep Q-waves in V1--V4 but
isoelectric ST segments is correctly classified as STEMI-negative by our
system, even though PTB-XL labels this record as AMI-positive. The high
specificity (inferior $0.993$, lateral $0.996$) and the non-trivial PPV
of inferior STEMI ($0.411$) support this interpretation: when the
detector identifies ST elevation, it is frequently present.

Proper evaluation of STEMI detection requires a dataset with explicit
acute-vs-chronic MI labels, or expert review of PTB-XL records to
distinguish active ST elevation from chronic changes.

\paragraph{WPW and AFib: rare-condition challenges.}
WPW (17 positive cases) and AFib (14 positive cases) represent rare
conditions in PTB-XL for which stable performance estimates are difficult.
WPW detection ($\text{F1} = 0.013$) is limited by the reliance on short
PR interval as a primary criterion, which is confounded by the P-onset
detection inaccuracy. Delta wave morphology detection would improve
specificity. AFib showed reasonable sensitivity ($0.786$) driven by
RR interval irregularity analysis, but the low PPV ($0.011$) reflects
many false positives, likely from sinus arrhythmia or frequent ectopy
producing irregular RR intervals without true atrial fibrillation.

\subsection{Signal Grounding as Hallucination Prevention}
\label{sec:disc-grounding}

The architectural separation between deterministic signal processing and
LLM-based clinical reasoning in \system{} addresses a fundamental risk of
applying language models to clinical
data~\cite{ji2023hallucination, bhayana2024chatgpt_radiology}. When an LLM
is given raw ECG waveform data---whether as numerical arrays or
images---it may generate confident but fabricated measurements: ``ST
elevation of 3~mm in V2'' when the actual elevation is $0.5\mv{}$, or
``QRS duration of 180~ms'' when the true value is $95\ms{}$. Such
hallucinations are particularly dangerous in the ECG domain because they
can trigger or suppress time-critical clinical actions.

\system{} prevents this failure mode by construction. The
\texttt{FeatureObject} containing 73 deterministic measurements is an
\emph{immutable input} to the LLM agents, not a value they generate. An
agent cannot claim that ``lead V2 shows $2\mv{}$ ST elevation'' if the
pipeline measured $0.1\mv{}$, because the pipeline measurement is a
structured field in the agent's input, not a free-text value the agent
produces. This creates a verifiable audit trail: every clinical
measurement cited in the agent's reasoning can be traced to a specific
algorithm with defined inputs and deterministic
outputs~\cite{rudin2019stop}.

This architectural choice has a cost: the system cannot detect findings
that the deterministic pipeline fails to measure. If the pipeline misses
a subtle ST elevation due to baseline noise, no LLM reasoning can recover
that information. This is a deliberate trade-off: we accept reduced recall
in exchange for the guarantee that all positive findings are grounded in
validated signal measurements, which is the safer failure mode in clinical
practice.

\subsection{Limitations}
\label{sec:disc-limitations}

Several limitations must be acknowledged:

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*, nosep]
  \item \textbf{Reference comparison measures port fidelity, not clinical
  accuracy.} The fiducial and measurement validation compares our Python
  port against the original MATLAB ECGdeli implementation, not against
  expert-annotated ground truth. This evaluates implementation fidelity
  rather than absolute clinical accuracy. The Lund University Database
  (LUDB) provides cardiologist-annotated fiducial points and would enable
  true clinical accuracy evaluation, but was not yet integrated at the
  time of this study.

  \item \textbf{STEMI evaluation on chronic MI labels underestimates
  real-world performance.} As discussed in \cref{sec:disc-disease}, the
  PTB-XL MI labels include chronic MI without active ST elevation. STEMI
  sensitivity on a dataset with acute-only MI labels would likely be
  substantially higher.

  \item \textbf{No prospective clinical validation.} All evaluation is
  retrospective on existing annotated datasets. A prospective study
  comparing \system{} output against real-time cardiologist interpretation
  in a clinical setting is essential before deployment.

  \item \textbf{Rule-based thresholds require optimization.} The current
  thresholds (QRS $\geq 120\ms{}$ for BBB, PR $> 200\ms{}$ for 1st AVB,
  QTc $> 480\ms{}$ for long QT) are textbook values applied without
  dataset-specific calibration. Threshold optimization on a held-out
  validation set, or adoption of multi-threshold ensemble approaches,
  could substantially improve the sensitivity--specificity trade-off for
  conditions with high false-positive rates.

  \item \textbf{LLM agent pathway not validated in this study.} The
  results presented here evaluate the signal-only (deterministic) mode of
  \system{}. The multi-agent LLM pathway is architecturally described but
  its incremental diagnostic value over the signal-only baseline remains
  to be quantified in a controlled multi-model comparison.

  \item \textbf{Adult-only thresholds.} All diagnostic criteria are
  calibrated for adult ECGs. Pediatric ECGs have fundamentally different
  normal values (physiological right axis deviation, T-wave inversion in
  V1--V3), and applying adult thresholds to pediatric recordings would
  produce systematic misclassification.

  \item \textbf{10-second recording limitation.} The current system
  analyzes standard $10\,\text{s}$ recordings. Paroxysmal arrhythmias
  (intermittent atrial fibrillation, second-degree AV block) may not be
  captured in a single short strip.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Future Work}
\label{sec:disc-future}

Several directions emerge from this evaluation. First, integration of the
Lund University Database (LUDB) for expert-annotated fiducial ground truth
would enable true clinical accuracy benchmarking, separating algorithm
accuracy from port fidelity. Second, threshold optimization using
receiver operating characteristic analysis on a PTB-XL validation fold
could substantially improve diagnostic performance, particularly for
conditions with high false-positive rates (LBBB, 1st AVB, long QT).
Third, the multi-model comparison protocol described in
\cref{sec:multi-model} should be executed to quantify the incremental
value of LLM agents over the signal-only baseline and to evaluate
cost-effectiveness across providers. Fourth, prospective clinical
validation in a real-world emergency department or cardiology clinic is
essential to establish clinical utility. Fifth, mobile and edge deployment
of the signal-only pipeline---which requires only ${\sim}125\ms{}$ of
CPU time---could enable point-of-care ECG interpretation in
resource-limited settings where LLM API access is unavailable. Finally,
extending the system to continuous monitoring (Holter, telemetry) and
pediatric ECG interpretation would broaden clinical applicability.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

This paper presented \system{}, an open-source, signal-grounded
architecture for automated 12-lead ECG interpretation that combines a
deterministic signal processing pipeline with multi-agent LLM reasoning.
The system computes 73 clinical measurements through a five-stage pipeline
(ingestion, preprocessing, quality assessment, fiducial detection, feature
extraction) that executes in approximately $125\ms{}$ per recording on
commodity hardware. Twenty-six rule-based detectors encode published
clinical criteria for 11 diagnostic conditions, with clinical suppression
rules that model inter-condition dependencies as practiced by consulting
cardiologists.

Validation on PTB-XL demonstrated that the pipeline achieves clinically
useful fiducial detection accuracy (R-peak MAE $5.1\ms{}$, QRS onset MAE
$4.2\ms{}$) and excellent heart rate agreement ($r = 0.953$, MAE
$1.8\bpm{}$). Disease detection performance was strongest for conditions
defined by robust measurements---LAFB (F1~$= 0.556$), RBBB
(F1~$= 0.399$), and LVH (F1~$= 0.351$)---while conditions dependent on
precise P-wave timing or requiring differentiation between acute and
chronic presentations showed expected limitations. The signal grounding
architecture ensures that all clinical findings are traceable to
deterministic measurements, preventing the hallucination failure mode that
arises when LLMs are applied directly to physiological
signals~\cite{ji2023hallucination}.

The honest assessment of current limitations---including systematic
interval biases from safe-window analysis, STEMI label mismatch in
PTB-XL, and the need for threshold optimization---defines a clear roadmap
for improvement. Integration of expert-annotated fiducial databases,
per-condition threshold calibration, and prospective clinical validation
are the immediate priorities. The complete open-source release of pipeline
code, agent prompts, and validation scripts enables community
contribution toward these goals and supports reproducible research in
signal-grounded clinical AI.
