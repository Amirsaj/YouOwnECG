## 1. Pathophysiology Root Cause

### 1.1 What Goes Wrong
Multifocal Atrial Tachycardia (MAT) arises when multiple ectopic foci in the atrial myocardium fire independently and compete for pacemaker dominance. Unlike atrial fibrillation, where chaotic micro-reentrant activity eliminates organized P waves entirely, MAT produces discrete P waves — but from at least three different anatomical origins within the atria. Each focus fires with its own automaticity rate and generates a distinct depolarization vector, producing P waves of distinctly different morphologies.

The underlying trigger is almost always metabolic or hypoxic stress on the atrial myocardium. The most common cause by far is severe COPD with chronic hypoxemia and hypercapnia, which alters membrane resting potential and triggers abnormal automaticity in atrial cells. Pulmonary hypertension, heart failure with elevated left atrial pressure, hypomagnesemia, hypokalemia, and theophylline toxicity share the same final pathway: destabilization of phase 4 spontaneous depolarization in atrial cells, allowing multiple foci to cross threshold and fire.

### 1.2 Electrical Consequence
Each ectopic atrial focus produces a depolarization wave that spreads through the atrial myocardium via cell-to-cell conduction (not the specialized conduction pathways used by the SA node). Because each focus is in a different anatomical location, the atrial depolarization vector is different for every focus:
- A high right atrial focus produces a P wave similar to sinus (upright II, III, aVF)
- A low right atrial focus produces a P wave with inferior negativity
- A left atrial focus produces a P wave that may be inverted in II or notched/biphasic

The AV node receives each atrial impulse and conducts to the ventricles, but the variable timing of atrial impulse arrival (and variable AV nodal recovery) produces variable PR intervals. The net result: an irregularly irregular rhythm where both P-wave shape and PR interval vary beat to beat.

### 1.3 Why It Appears on ECG
The ECG signature of MAT follows directly from the multi-origin mechanism:
- Multiple P morphologies reflect multiple depolarization vectors projecting onto the recording leads
- Variable PR intervals reflect variable coupling intervals and variable AV nodal conduction time
- Irregular RR intervals reflect the aperiodic firing pattern of multiple competing foci
- Rate >100 bpm reflects enhanced automaticity driven by metabolic stress

Lead II is the optimal analysis lead for P-wave morphology because it is oriented along the long axis of the atrial myocardium, maximizing P-wave amplitude and morphological differentiation between foci.

### 2.3 Key Leads
- **Lead II**: Primary diagnostic lead — highest P-wave amplitude and best morphological discrimination between atrial foci. The ≥3 morphology criterion should be assessed here first.
- **Lead V1**: Secondary lead — biphasic P waves may reveal left atrial origin foci (large terminal negative component); also useful to confirm narrow QRS.
- **Leads III and aVF**: Supporting inferior leads — corroborate P-wave findings from lead II.

### 2.4 Beat-by-Beat Considerations
MAT is inherently a beat-by-beat condition. The agent must analyze each P wave independently and compare morphologies across beats in the same lead. Key analysis steps:
- Identify the onset and offset of each P wave in lead II
- Measure P-wave axis, amplitude, and duration for each beat
- Cluster P waves: if ≥3 distinct clusters exist, the criterion is met
- Measure PR interval for each beat — should vary ≥20 ms across beats
- Confirm each P wave is followed by a QRS (differentiates from AF with artifact)
- RR intervals should be clearly irregular

## 3. Morphology Details

### 3.1 P-wave Changes
- Morphology: variable beat to beat — the defining feature; ≥3 distinct shapes required in one lead
- Common shapes seen: upright sinus-like (high right atrial focus), biphasic or notched (left atrial), inverted inferior (low atrial or coronary sinus)
- Duration: normal to slightly prolonged per beat (80–120 ms); varies by focus location
- Axis: varies beat to beat — this variability is the hallmark; P axis may swing from +30° to −60° within the same strip
- Amplitude: variable; generally lower than sinus P for ectopic foci due to non-specialized conduction

### 3.2 PR Interval Changes
- Duration: variable, typically 120–200 ms but changes beat to beat
- The variation reflects differing distances between each focus and the AV node, plus varying AV nodal recovery time
- Some P waves may have shorter PR (closer AV node proximity); others longer
- No progressive lengthening pattern (rules out Wenckebach)

### 3.3 QRS Complex Changes
- Duration: narrow (<120 ms) in the typical case — conduction through the His-Purkinje system is normal
- Morphology: unchanged from beat to beat (all QRS complexes look the same despite different P waves)
- Amplitude: normal
- Axis: normal
- Wide QRS may occur with rate-related aberrant conduction (right bundle branch block typically) or if baseline BBB exists

### 3.4 ST Segment Changes
- Usually normal; no primary ST abnormality attributable to MAT itself
- If the underlying COPD or pulmonary hypertension produces right heart strain, may see ST depression in V1–V3 and inferior leads as secondary finding

### 3.5 T-wave Changes
- Usually normal for MAT itself
- May see peaked T waves if hypokalemia or hypomagnesemia is contributing (electrolyte-associated T changes)

### 3.6 QT/QTc Changes
- QTc typically normal unless electrolyte abnormalities (hypokalemia/hypomagnesemia) are the triggering cause
- If hypokalemia is present, QT prolongation and prominent U waves may coexist

### 3.7 Other Features
- The key differentiating feature from AFib: isoelectric BASELINE between P waves. In AFib, the baseline is chaotic/undulating with no clear P waves. In MAT, the baseline is flat between distinct P waves.
- No flutter waves (rules out atrial flutter with variable block)
- No delta waves (rules out WPW)

## 4. Differential Diagnosis

### 4.1 Mimics

| Mimic Condition | Shared Features | Distinguishing Features |
|----------------|-----------------|------------------------|
| Atrial Fibrillation | Irregular RR interval, fast rate, no clearly regular P waves | AFib has no discrete P waves; baseline is chaotic/fibrillatory; MAT has clear discrete P waves with isoelectric baseline between |
| Frequent PACs with sinus rhythm | Irregular rhythm, occasional morphologically different P waves | PACs have only 1–2 ectopic P morphologies mixed with predominantly sinus P waves; MAT has ≥3 distinct morphologies as the primary rhythm |
| Atrial flutter with variable block | Fast atrial rate, variable RR | Flutter has regular sawtooth flutter waves at 250–350 bpm; no isoelectric baseline; MAT has discrete P waves at <200 atrial rate |
| Sinus tachycardia with PACs | Tachycardia, occasional ectopic P | Predominantly uniform sinus P waves; PACs are rare interspersed events; MAT has no dominant morphology — all are ectopic |
| Wandering atrial pacemaker | ≥3 P morphologies | Rate <100 bpm in wandering pacemaker; MAT requires rate >100 bpm |

### 4.2 Coexisting Conditions
- **COPD with right heart strain**: May produce right axis deviation, S1Q3T3 pattern, P pulmonale (peaked P in II) coexisting with the MAT rhythm
- **Electrolyte abnormalities**: Hypokalemia produces U waves and QT prolongation; the combination of QT prolongation + MAT should prompt concern about TdP risk if rate accelerates
- **Digoxin toxicity**: Digoxin can cause MAT in the setting of toxicity — critical because standard treatment (AV nodal blockers for rate control) worsens digoxin toxicity; digoxin levels must be checked

## 5. STAT Classification

| Priority | Criteria |
|----------|----------|
| **Routine/Urgent** | MAT itself is rarely immediately life-threatening as an arrhythmia |
| **Time-sensitive** | Rate >150 bpm with hemodynamic compromise warrants urgent management |
| **Clinical action** | DO NOT use AV nodal blockers (beta-blockers, CCBs) as primary treatment — they do not address the underlying cause and may worsen COPD or heart failure. Treat the underlying cause: oxygen for hypoxemia, magnesium replacement (IV Mg even if Mg is "normal low"), potassium correction, treat COPD exacerbation. Verapamil may be used for rate control if no contraindications. |
| **STAT trigger** | Hemodynamic instability (hypotension, altered consciousness, pulmonary edema) in setting of MAT → urgent rate control + cause treatment |

## 6. Reasoning Complexity Analysis (Feeds Into Node 2.1)

> **NOTE**: This section does NOT pre-assign agents. It documents the reasoning complexity so Node 2.1 can determine the best agent architecture.

### 6.1 Reasoning Domains Required
- **Rate analysis**: Confirm rate >100 bpm
- **Rhythm regularity**: Confirm irregularly irregular RR intervals
- **P-wave morphology discrimination**: Compare P-wave shapes across beats in a single lead — requires morphological clustering (≥3 distinct groups)
- **P-wave counting**: Every QRS must have a preceding P wave with a distinct morphology
- **PR interval measurement per beat**: Confirm PR variability
- **Differential exclusion**: Must confirm isoelectric baseline (exclude AFib), must confirm no flutter waves, must count P morphology clusters
- Cross-domain reasoning required: rate domain + rhythm domain + morphology comparison domain must all produce consistent findings before diagnosis is made

### 6.2 Feature Dependencies

**ESSENTIAL (all three required for diagnosis):**
- Heart rate >100 bpm (aggregate rate measurement)
- ≥3 distinct P-wave morphologies in one lead (per-beat morphology + cross-beat clustering)
- Irregular RR intervals (aggregate rhythm analysis)

**SUPPORTING (increase confidence):**
- Variable PR intervals (per-beat measurement)
- Isoelectric baseline between P waves (confirms not AFib)
- Clinical context: COPD, known electrolyte abnormalities, theophylline use

**EXCLUSION (if present, diagnosis should be reconsidered):**
- Regular RR intervals (rules out MAT, suggests atrial flutter or SVT)
- Only 1–2 P morphologies (suggests sinus + occasional PACs, not MAT)
- Chaotic baseline without discrete P waves (AFib, not MAT)
- Delta waves (WPW, not MAT)

**Per-beat vs aggregate:**
- P morphology: per-beat analysis + aggregate clustering
- Rate: aggregate
- RR regularity: aggregate
- PR interval: per-beat

### 6.3 Cross-Condition Interactions
- MAT and AFib can coexist in the same patient at different times; rhythm may convert between the two
- COPD with right heart strain can produce axis changes that alter P-wave morphology independently, potentially exaggerating apparent morphological variation
- If LBBB or RBBB is present, the QRS will be wide — MAT with aberrant conduction can superficially resemble VT; confirming P waves before each QRS is critical

### 6.4 Reasoning Chain Sketch

**Minimum chain (4 steps):**
1. Rate >100 bpm confirmed
2. RR intervals irregular
3. ≥3 distinct P-wave morphologies identified in lead II
4. P waves discrete with isoelectric baseline → MAT diagnosis

**Full chain:**
1. Rate >100 bpm confirmed (aggregate)
2. RR intervals irregularly irregular (aggregate)
3. Identify all P waves in lead II, measure each P-wave morphology
4. Cluster P morphologies: are there ≥3 distinct shapes?
5. Measure PR interval for each beat — variable?
6. Confirm isoelectric baseline between P waves (exclude AFib)
7. Confirm no flutter waves at 250–350 bpm (exclude atrial flutter)
8. Confirm QRS morphology consistent across beats (narrow unless BBB)
9. Check for coexisting features: right axis, P pulmonale (COPD context)
10. Final classification: MAT confirmed

### 6.5 Confidence Anchors
- **High confidence**: ≥3 clearly distinct P morphologies in lead II + irregular RR + rate >100 + flat baseline
- **Lower confidence**: Only 2 P morphologies visible (may need longer strip or different lead)
- **Pathognomonic combination**: ≥3 discrete P morphologies + isoelectric baseline + irregular RR (near-100% specific)
- **Possible**: irregular tachycardia + apparent P waves but morphology unclear — rate "possible MAT" and recommend longer strip
- **Probable**: 2 criteria present + clinical COPD context
- **Definite**: All 3 criteria + supporting PR variability

### 6.6 Difficulty Score

| Dimension | Score (1–5) | Notes |
|-----------|-------------|-------|
| Signal clarity needed | 3 | P waves can be small; need adequate amplitude and clean baseline |
| Number of leads required | 2 | Lead II primary; V1 supporting; cannot diagnose from single lead reliably |
| Cross-domain reasoning | 3 | Rate + rhythm + morphology comparison all required simultaneously |
| Temporal pattern complexity | 3 | Per-beat P analysis + cross-beat morphology clustering is non-trivial |
| Differential complexity | 3 | Must exclude AFib (most important), atrial flutter, PACs, wandering pacemaker |
| Rarity in PTB-XL | 3 | MAT is uncommon; limited labeled examples available |
| **Overall difficulty** | **2.8** | **Moderate — P morphology discrimination is the hardest step** |

**Architecture:** Option E — 3 Parallel Phase 1 Specialists + 1 Sequential CDS (Node 2.1 Gate Passed 2026-03-27)

| Agent | Role for Multifocal Atrial Tachycardia | Key Features Used |
|-------|--------------------------|-------------------|
| **RRC** (Rhythm/Rate/Conduction) | Primary | ≥3 distinct P-wave morphologies, irregular RR intervals, rate 100–150 bpm, isoelectric baseline between P-waves (no flutter waves), each P-wave followed by QRS |
| **IT** (Ischemia/Territory) | Not involved | ST/T assessment not relevant to MAT classification |
| **MR** (Morphology/Repolarization) | Not involved | QRS morphology not relevant; P-wave morphology variation is RRC-domain |
| **CDS** (Cross-Domain Synthesis) | Standard integration only | Distinguishes MAT (≥3 distinct P morphologies, rate >100) from WAP (same morphology variation but rate <100) and AFib (no discrete P-waves) |

### Primary Agent
**RRC** — MAT is diagnosed by counting ≥3 distinct P-wave morphologies in an irregular tachycardia; this is entirely a P-wave morphology and rhythm analysis task.

### Cross-Domain Hints
No cross-domain hints required — single-domain condition.

### CDS Specific Role
CDS performs standard integration and applies the rate-based MAT vs WAP distinction: if RRC reports ≥3 P-wave morphologies at rate >100 bpm, MAT is confirmed; if rate <100 bpm, CDS reclassifies as WAP. CDS also verifies that baseline is isoelectric between P-waves (ruling out AFL) and that discrete P-waves are present (ruling out AFib).

### 8.3 Clinician Explanation

**ER nurse version:**
"The ECG shows a fast, irregular heart rhythm where the P waves (the electrical signals before each heartbeat) have at least three different shapes. This means the heart's electrical signals are coming from multiple different spots in the upper chambers instead of the normal pacemaker. This pattern is called Multifocal Atrial Tachycardia and is usually caused by the underlying lung disease rather than the heart itself — treatment should focus on improving oxygen and electrolytes, not just slowing the heart rate."

**Cardiologist version:**
"ECG demonstrates MAT: rate >100 bpm, irregularly irregular rhythm, ≥3 distinct P-wave morphologies in lead II, isoelectric baseline confirming organized atrial activity (excludes AFib), variable PR intervals. Likely driven by atrial myocyte membrane instability from hypoxemia/hypercarbia (COPD exacerbation most common) or electrolyte derangement (hypomagnesemia, hypokalemia). Therapeutic priority is correction of the underlying trigger — IV magnesium sulfate (2g over 20 min) is first-line even with normal serum Mg, followed by electrolyte repletion and oxygen optimization. AV nodal blockade is not the primary therapeutic target and may worsen COPD or hemodynamics."

## 9. Edge Cases and Pitfalls

- **MAT at rate <100 bpm**: When MAT-like pattern is present but rate is 60–100 bpm, the correct term is "wandering atrial pacemaker" — the ≥3 morphology criterion is the same but tachycardia is absent. Agent must apply rate threshold correctly.
- **Coarse AFib vs MAT**: Coarse AFib can produce apparent "P waves" that mimic MAT. The key distinction is baseline: MAT has truly isoelectric intervals between P waves; coarse AFib has no flat baseline, just irregular fibrillatory oscillations.
- **Artifact in COPD patients**: Respiratory motion artifact (especially in COPD patients who are tachypneic) can distort the baseline and create apparent P-wave shape variation. A clean baseline segment between beats should be identifiable in MAT; artifact produces undulating baseline throughout.
- **Rate-related aberrant conduction**: If rate is very fast, some impulses may find the bundle branches partially refractory, producing intermittent wide QRS. Wide-QRS beats in MAT should not be misclassified as PVCs or VT — the key is that P wave (with its MAT morphology) precedes the wide beat with the same short coupling interval as the aberrant beat onset.
- **Digoxin toxicity context**: MAT in a patient on digoxin may indicate toxicity — this changes management dramatically. The agent should flag digoxin use as a high-relevance clinical context modifier.
- **Pediatric patients**: MAT in children is less common and may occur in the setting of myocarditis or congenital heart disease rather than COPD.
