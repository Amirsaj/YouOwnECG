## 1. Pathophysiology Root Cause

### 1.1 Cellular Mechanism — Therapeutic Effect
Digoxin inhibits the Na/K-ATPase pump on cardiac myocytes. This is the mechanism of its therapeutic action:
1. Na/K-ATPase inhibition → intracellular Na+ accumulates
2. Elevated intracellular Na+ reduces the Na+ gradient driving the Na/Ca2+ exchanger (NCX)
3. NCX extrudes less Ca2+ → intracellular Ca2+ rises
4. Increased intracellular Ca2+ → enhanced contractility (positive inotropy)

At the AV node, digoxin enhances vagal tone (parasympathomimetic effect) → slowed AV nodal conduction → rate control in atrial fibrillation. This is the primary clinical use.

### 1.2 ECG of Therapeutic Digoxin Effect (NOT toxicity)
The therapeutic effect produces characteristic ECG changes that are expected and normal in a patient on digoxin:
- **"Salvador Dalí mustache" (scooped ST depression):** The signature finding. Concave (scooped) downward ST depression with rapid upstroke of the T wave. Best seen in lateral leads (V4-V6, I, aVL) and inferior leads (II, III, aVF). This is from digoxin's direct effect on phase 2 repolarization — enhanced Ca2+ shortens and distorts the plateau.
- **Shortened QT interval:** Digoxin shortens the action potential plateau → QTc is shortened (similar to hypercalcemia mechanism)
- **T-wave flattening or inversion:** Follows the scooped ST
- **PR prolongation:** From AV nodal slowing; typically 200–240 ms; expected therapeutic effect
- **Atrial rate slowing:** In AF, ventricular rate is controlled

These changes are NORMAL in a patient on digoxin and do NOT indicate toxicity.

### 1.3 Cellular Mechanism — Toxicity
At toxic digoxin levels, Na/K-ATPase is overwhelmed:
- Massive intracellular Ca2+ overload → spontaneous Ca2+ release from sarcoplasmic reticulum → delayed afterdepolarizations (DADs) → triggered arrhythmias
- Simultaneously, excessive vagal enhancement → profound AV nodal block
- The paradox: enhanced automaticity (ectopic beats, tachyarrhythmias) + suppressed conduction (AV block) occurring simultaneously

### 1.4 Risk Factors for Toxicity at Therapeutic Levels
- **Hypokalemia:** K+ and digoxin compete for the same Na/K-ATPase binding site. Low K+ → more digoxin binding → toxicity at therapeutic levels
- **Hypomagnesemia:** Impairs Na/K-ATPase → potentiates digoxin effect
- **Hypercalcemia:** Additive Ca2+ overload
- **Hypothyroidism:** Reduces renal digoxin clearance → elevated levels
- **Renal insufficiency:** Digoxin is renally cleared; AKI or CKD → accumulation
- **Drug interactions:** Amiodarone, verapamil, quinidine all increase digoxin levels

## 2. ECG Presentation — Lead by Lead

### 2.1 Diagnostic Criteria

| Criterion | Finding | Therapeutic Effect vs. Toxicity |
|---|---|---|
| Scooped ST depression | Concave downsloping ST with rapid T-wave upstroke | THERAPEUTIC — expected finding |
| Short QT | QTc shortened | THERAPEUTIC |
| PR prolongation | 200–240 ms | THERAPEUTIC (AV nodal slowing) |
| T-wave inversion | Lateral leads | THERAPEUTIC |
| PVC bigeminy/trigeminy | Alternating sinus beat + PVC | TOXICITY — most common initial sign |
| PAT with block | Atrial tachycardia + AV block | TOXICITY — pathognomonic |
| Junctional tachycardia | Regular narrow-complex tachycardia | TOXICITY |
| Bidirectional VT | Alternating QRS axis beat-to-beat | TOXICITY — near-pathognomonic |
| Complete AV block | No conduction; escape rhythm | TOXICITY — severe |
| AF regularization | AF with regular R-R intervals | TOXICITY — junctional tachycardia + complete AV block |

### 2.3 Key Leads
**V4-V6** most clearly show the scooped ST depression of the therapeutic digoxin effect. The concavity and rapid T-wave upstroke are most visible in lateral precordial leads.

**All leads** are relevant for toxicity arrhythmia assessment — rhythm analysis is the primary task.

### 2.4 Beat-by-Beat Considerations
- **PVC bigeminy:** Every other beat is a PVC — most common early toxicity rhythm. Recognizing the bigeminal pattern is critical.
- **PAT with block:** Regular atrial tachycardia (rate 150–250 bpm) with AV block (2:1 or higher) → ventricular rate slower. A regular atrial tachycardia with AV block in a patient on digoxin = toxicity until proven otherwise.
- **Bidirectional VT:** QRS morphology alternates beat-to-beat between two morphologies (two different axes), typically RBBB + left axis alternating with RBBB + right axis. Each beat has a different QRS vector. This is from alternating ectopic foci in the left and right fascicles. Near-pathognomonic for digoxin toxicity (also seen in severe hypokalemia and catecholaminergic polymorphic VT — CPVT).
- **AF regularization:** A hallmark scenario — patient with known AF on digoxin whose ECG shows REGULAR R-R intervals. AF should be irregular. Regular AF = not true AF → junctional tachycardia has taken over + complete AV block (no AF conducted to ventricles).

## 3. Morphology Details

### 3.1 The "Salvador Dalí Mustache" — Scooped ST Depression
The most recognizable ECG pattern in cardiology. Characteristics:
- **Concave (scooped) downward ST depression:** The ST segment sags below the baseline with a curved, concave shape — like a hammock or a spoon bowl
- **Rapid T-wave upstroke:** The T wave rises sharply back to baseline from the nadir of the ST sag
- **Asymmetric:** ST depression is gradual onset, rapid return
- **No horizontal flat ST:** Distinguishes from ischemic ST depression which is flat or downsloping
- **No reciprocal changes:** Unlike STEMI, digoxin effect is diffuse and does not have reciprocal elevation

Seen in: V4-V6 (most prominent), I, aVL, II, aVF (less prominent).

### 3.2 Shortened QT
Digoxin shortens the QT interval by abbreviating the action potential duration (enhanced Ca2+ loading shortens phase 2). This is the same mechanism as hypercalcemia. Shortened QTc in a patient on digoxin is expected and confirms digoxin effect.

### 3.3 PR Prolongation
First-degree AV block is an expected therapeutic consequence of AV nodal slowing. PR 200–280 ms. PR >280 ms or second-degree AV block raises concern for toxicity.

### 3.4 Bidirectional VT Morphology
Beat-to-beat alternation of QRS axis. Each beat points in a different direction:
- Beat 1: QRS with inferior axis (positive in II, III, aVF)
- Beat 2: QRS with superior axis (negative in II, III, aVF, positive in aVR)
The QRS morphology may be RBBB pattern in both beats (alternating fascicular exit), or the morphologies may be distinctly different.

### 3.5 PAT with Block Morphology
- P waves visible, regular, rapid (atrial rate 150–250 bpm)
- P-wave morphology may differ from sinus (ectopic atrial origin)
- AV block (2:1, 3:1, or variable) — ventricular rate is slower than atrial rate
- Regular ventricular rate despite AV block (regular block ratio)
- The combination of atrial tachycardia + AV block = pathognomonic for digoxin toxicity

## 4. Differential Diagnosis

### 4.1 Mimics

| Condition | Overlapping Feature | Distinguishing Feature |
|---|---|---|
| Subendocardial ischemia | ST depression | Ischemic: flat/downsloping, territory-specific, troponin rise; digoxin: scooped, diffuse |
| LVH strain | Lateral ST-T changes | LVH: voltage criteria met; asymmetric T inversion; no scooping |
| Hypokalemia | T-wave changes | Hypokalemia: U waves, QU prolongation; no scooped ST |
| Hypothyroidism | Sinus bradycardia, T changes | Hypothyroid: low voltage, flat T waves, no scooping |
| Catecholaminergic polymorphic VT (CPVT) | Bidirectional VT | CPVT: exercise-triggered; no digoxin; different clinical context |
| WPW with AF | Irregular wide-complex tachycardia | WPW: delta waves, very rapid irregular rate; not PAT with block |

### 4.2 Coexisting Conditions (Toxicity Precipitants)
- **Hypokalemia:** Potentiates toxicity at therapeutic levels — the most common precipitant
- **Hypomagnesemia:** Same mechanism; check with hypokalemia
- **Renal insufficiency (AKI/CKD):** Reduced digoxin clearance → accumulation
- **Dehydration:** Reduced renal perfusion → decreased digoxin clearance
- **Amiodarone:** Reduces digoxin clearance by ~50%; classic drug interaction
- **Verapamil/diltiazem:** Reduce renal and non-renal digoxin clearance

## 5. STAT Classification

| Status | ECG Finding | Clinical Action |
|---|---|---|
| Therapeutic effect | Scooped ST, short QT, PR prolongation, rate-controlled AF | No action; document as expected finding |
| Concerning for toxicity | PVC bigeminy, first episode | Hold digoxin, check level and electrolytes |
| Toxicity | PAT with block, frequent multifocal PVCs, junctional tachycardia | STAT — digoxin level, electrolytes, hold dose, monitoring |
| Severe toxicity | Bidirectional VT, complete AV block, AF regularization | STAT — digoxin immune Fab (Digibind), cardiology consult |
| Life-threatening | Sustained VT, VF, cardiac arrest | STAT — Digibind, defibrillation, avoid calcium (paradoxically worsens) |

## 6. Reasoning Complexity Analysis

### 6.1 Dual-State Diagnosis Challenge
Digoxin is unique in cardiology: the same drug at therapeutic levels produces expected ECG changes (therapeutic effect), and at toxic levels produces life-threatening arrhythmias. The clinician must distinguish therapeutic ECG changes from toxic changes — a nuanced task that requires clinical context.

### 6.2 The AF Regularization Trap
The most dangerous diagnostic pitfall: a patient with known AF on digoxin whose ECG shows REGULAR intervals. The treating team may incorrectly assume the patient has "converted" to sinus rhythm. In fact, junctional tachycardia + complete AV block = regular narrow-complex rhythm despite the underlying AF. This presentation requires immediate recognition.

### 6.3 Bidirectional VT — Rare but Important
Bidirectional VT is rare and digoxin toxicity is the primary cause in adults. Recognition requires understanding beat-to-beat axis alternation. The differential includes CPVT (in younger patients without digoxin) and severe hypokalemia.

### 6.4 PAT with Block — Pathognomonic
Paroxysmal atrial tachycardia with AV block is near-pathognomonic for digoxin toxicity. Learning to identify the combination of a fast atrial rate with slower ventricular rate from a regular block is the key skill.

### 6.5 Calcium Contraindication
IV calcium is contraindicated in digoxin toxicity (it worsens intracellular Ca2+ overload and can precipitate VF). This is a high-stakes clinical rule that must be embedded in the reasoning system.

CDS plays a critical arbitration role for digoxin effect: IT will detect ST depression in the lateral leads and flag a potential ischemia signal, while MR will identify the scooped reverse-tick morphology as pathognomonic for digoxin effect. CDS generates a final output distinguishing digoxin effect from digoxin toxicity based on the combined cross-domain picture.

## 7. RAG Knowledge Requirements

### 7.1 Essential Knowledge Chunks
- Digoxin mechanism: Na/K-ATPase inhibition → intracellular Ca2+ increase → inotropy
- Therapeutic ECG changes: scooped ST, short QT, PR prolongation
- Toxic ECG arrhythmias: PVC bigeminy, PAT with block, junctional tachycardia, bidirectional VT, complete AV block
- AF regularization as toxicity sign
- Toxicity precipitants: hypokalemia (most common), renal failure, drug interactions (amiodarone, verapamil)
- Treatment: digoxin immune Fab (Digibind); calcium contraindicated
- Digoxin level: therapeutic 0.5–2.0 ng/mL; draw >6 hours after dose for accurate level

### 7.2 Reference Values
- Therapeutic digoxin level: 0.5–2.0 ng/mL
- Toxic level: typically >2.0 ng/mL, but toxicity can occur at lower levels with hypokalemia
- PR prolongation threshold for concern: >280 ms or second-degree AV block
- Digibind dosing: number of vials = serum digoxin level (ng/mL) × weight (kg) / 100

## 9. Edge Cases and Pitfalls

1. **Scooped ST depression misread as ischemia:** The digoxin ST depression is concave (scooped) while ischemic ST depression is flat or downsloping. In a patient with chest pain on digoxin, the scooped ST does not exclude ischemia but is a confounding pattern. Troponin measurement is essential; the ECG alone cannot confirm ischemia.

2. **AF regularization misidentified as sinus conversion:** In a patient on digoxin for AF who appears to convert to regular rhythm, the clinician must consider digoxin toxicity (junctional tachycardia + complete AV block) before assuming successful cardioversion. Key sign: P waves are absent or fibrillatory baseline is still present despite regular ventricular rate.

3. **Hypokalemia + digoxin = toxicity at therapeutic levels:** The most common clinical scenario of digoxin toxicity in clinical practice is a patient on digoxin who develops hypokalemia (from diuretic use). Digoxin levels may be "therapeutic" but toxicity occurs because K+ depletion increases Na/K-ATPase binding of digoxin.

4. **Do NOT give calcium in digoxin toxicity:** IV calcium is contraindicated. It worsens intracellular Ca2+ overload. Historical reports of "stone heart" (irreversible contracture) from calcium administration in digoxin overdose. If hypocalcemia is also present and severe, use extreme caution and only give calcium after Digibind.

5. **Bidirectional VT vs. CPVT vs. artifact:** Bidirectional VT can be misread as artifact or as two different rhythms alternating. Careful inspection of all 12 leads simultaneously reveals the regular R-R interval with alternating axis. CPVT occurs in young patients during exercise without digoxin use — context is key.

6. **Digoxin level timing:** Digoxin has a long distribution phase. A level drawn within 6 hours of the dose will be falsely elevated. Levels drawn at <6 hours post-dose should not be interpreted as reflecting steady-state tissue distribution. Clinical toxicity findings (arrhythmias) are more reliable than early levels.
