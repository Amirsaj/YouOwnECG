## 1. Pathophysiology Root Cause

### 1.1 Cellular Mechanism
Low extracellular calcium reduces Ca2+ influx through L-type voltage-gated channels (ICaL) during phase 2 of the cardiac action potential. With less inward Ca2+ current, the phase 2 plateau requires a longer duration to complete — the cell compensates by maintaining the plateau phase for an extended time before phase 3 repolarization can occur.

The result on the ECG is a prolonged ST segment — the isoelectric component of the action potential plateau. The key distinction from other causes of QT prolongation is that in hypocalcemia, the T wave itself retains normal morphology. It is the isoelectric ST segment that elongates, not the T wave. This is the most clinically important distinguishing feature.

### 1.2 Ionic Current Changes
- **ICaL (L-type Ca2+ channel):** Reduced inward Ca2+ current → phase 2 plateau maintained longer to achieve repolarization threshold
- **Phase 3 repolarization (IKr, IKs):** Normal outward K+ current; activated normally once the plateau phase reaches threshold
- **Net result:** QT prolongation with preserved T wave morphology; ST segment isoelectric prolongation is the primary change
- **Neuromuscular effects:** Low Ca2+ reduces membrane stabilization → increased neuronal and muscle excitability → Chvostek sign, Trousseau sign, tetany, seizures (separate from cardiac effects)

### 1.3 Etiology
- **Hypoparathyroidism:** Most common cause; post-thyroid/parathyroid surgery is the most common clinical setting; also autoimmune, genetic
- **Hypomagnesemia:** Impairs PTH secretion and peripheral PTH action → secondary hypocalcemia; must always be checked and corrected
- **Vitamin D deficiency/malabsorption:** Reduced intestinal Ca2+ absorption
- **Pancreatitis (acute):** Saponification — free fatty acids from pancreatic lipase bind Ca2+ in fat necrosis deposits
- **Massive blood transfusion (citrate toxicity):** Citrate anticoagulant chelates ionized Ca2+ → transient hypocalcemia during large-volume transfusion
- **Rhabdomyolysis:** Ca2+ deposits into damaged muscle during acute phase
- **Hyperphosphatemia (renal failure):** Phosphate binds Ca2+ → calcium-phosphate deposits → reduced ionized Ca2+

## 2. ECG Presentation — Lead by Lead

### 2.1 Diagnostic Criteria

| Criterion | Finding | Threshold / Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Prolonged QTc | QTc >450 ms (male), >460 ms (female) | Required finding |
| Elongated ST segment | Isoelectric ST segment visibly prolonged | Key distinguishing feature |
| T wave morphology | Normal — symmetric, upright | Preserved T wave = hypocalcemia; abnormal T = LQT |
| T wave duration | Normal | Not prolonged |
| TdP risk | Present if QTc >500 ms | Same threshold as other causes of long QT |
| QRS complex | Normal width | Unchanged |
| P wave | Normal | Unchanged |

### 2.2 Lead-by-Lead Manifestation

| Lead | Typical Finding | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| I | QT prolongation, normal T | Lateral assessment |
| II | QT prolongation, elongated ST | Best lead for QT measurement |
| III | Variable | Less reliable |
| aVR | Reciprocal | Normal |
| aVL | QT prolongation | Lateral |
| aVF | QT prolongation, elongated ST | Inferior assessment |
| V1 | QT prolongation | May show T wave flattening in severe disease |
| V2 | QT prolongation | Elongated isoelectric ST visible |
| V3 | QT prolongation | Elongated isoelectric ST visible |
| V4 | QT prolongation, elongated ST | Good visualization |
| V5 | QT prolongation, elongated ST | PRIMARY measurement lead |
| V6 | QT prolongation, elongated ST | Lateral |

### 2.3 Key Leads
**Lead II and V5** are primary QT measurement leads. The elongated ST segment is most clearly visible in mid-precordial leads (V3-V5) where the ST isoelectric segment can be compared against the preceding TP baseline.

The critical visual assessment: after the QRS, there is an unusually long flat isoelectric segment before the T wave begins. This flat segment is the prolonged phase 2 plateau.

### 2.4 Beat-by-Beat Considerations
- QT prolongation is uniform across all beats (electrolyte disorder is systemic)
- Rate variability does not significantly change the appearance; QTc correction is essential
- PVC-on-T risk increases as QTc exceeds 500 ms
- T wave alternans (beat-to-beat T wave amplitude variation) is a sign of severe electrical instability and TdP risk; rare but important

## 3. Morphology Details

### 3.1 ST Segment — The Defining Feature
The ST segment in hypocalcemia is isoelectric and prolonged. It appears as an extended flat line between the end of the QRS and the beginning of the T wave. This is in contrast to:
- ST elevation (ischemia, pericarditis, Brugada, early repolarization)
- ST depression (subendocardial ischemia, digoxin effect, hypokalemia)
- Normal ST (short flat segment, T wave begins promptly)

Visual estimation: if the ST segment appears to be as long as or longer than the T wave itself, suspect hypocalcemia.

### 3.2 T Wave
The T wave is preserved in morphology. It is:
- Upright in leads where it should be upright (I, II, V4-V6)
- Normal height
- Normal symmetric shape

This preserved T wave is the defining distinguishing feature from congenital Long QT syndrome, where T waves are notched, biphasic, asymmetric, or show other characteristic abnormalities.

### 3.3 QTc Measurement
- Measure from the beginning of the QRS to the end of the T wave (where T wave meets the isoelectric baseline)
- The elongated ST segment appears before the T wave — this is included in the QT measurement
- Bazett correction: QTc = QT / √(RR interval in seconds)
- Normal QTc: ≤450 ms (male), ≤460 ms (female)
- TdP risk threshold: QTc >500 ms

### 3.4 QRS Complex
- Normal width and morphology
- Unchanged from baseline

### 3.5 P Wave and PR Interval
- Normal
- The cardiac conduction system is not primarily affected at mild-moderate calcium levels

### 3.6 Rhythm
- Sinus rhythm maintained in most cases
- PVCs may occur in severe hypocalcemia
- TdP: polymorphic VT with rotating axis, initiated by PVC-on-T
- VF: uncommon but documented in extreme hypocalcemia

### 3.7 T Wave Alternans
A subtle finding in severe hypocalcemia: beat-to-beat variation in T wave amplitude (electrical alternans of the T wave). This reflects heterogeneous repolarization — some cells taking longer to repolarize than others, creating spatial gradients that manifest as alternating T wave heights. This is a sign of imminent TdP risk.

## 4. Differential Diagnosis

### 4.1 Mimics

| Condition | Overlapping Feature | Distinguishing Feature |
|---|---|---|
| Congenital Long QT (LQT1) | Prolonged QTc | Abnormal T wave morphology (broad-based T); genetic; normal Ca2+ |
| Congenital Long QT (LQT2) | Prolonged QTc | Notched or biphasic T waves; genetic |
| Congenital Long QT (LQT3) | Prolonged QTc | Late-peaking T with isoelectric ST — most similar but different T wave shape |
| Drug-induced Long QT | Prolonged QTc | History of QT-prolonging drug; variable T wave changes; normal Ca2+ |
| Hypomagnesemia | Prolonged QTc | U waves present; T wave flattening; Mg2+ low |
| Hypokalemia | Prolonged QTc (QU) | U waves; T-U fusion; K+ low |
| Ischemia | ST changes | ST depression or elevation; territory-specific; troponin rise |

### 4.2 Coexisting Conditions
- **Hypomagnesemia:** Impairs PTH secretion → perpetuates hypocalcemia; must be corrected simultaneously; also causes independent QT prolongation
- **Hypoparathyroidism (post-surgical):** Acute post-thyroidectomy hypocalcemia → sudden QTc prolongation in ICU/floor monitoring
- **Pancreatitis:** Hypocalcemia in pancreatitis correlates with severity; also contributes to hemodynamic instability
- **Massive transfusion:** Citrate-induced hypocalcemia occurs during rapid transfusion; often unrecognized; treatable with IV calcium gluconate
- **Alkalosis:** Alkalosis reduces ionized Ca2+ by increasing Ca2+-albumin binding (without changing total Ca2+); hyperventilation → hypocalcemia symptoms with normal total Ca2+

## 5. STAT Classification

| Severity | Ca2+ Level (ionized) | QTc | Symptoms | Action |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Mild | 0.9–1.15 mmol/L | Mildly prolonged | Perioral tingling, Chvostek/Trousseau | Oral supplementation |
| Moderate | 0.7–0.9 mmol/L | QTc 460–500 ms | Muscle cramps, tetany | IV calcium gluconate, monitor |
| Severe | <0.7 mmol/L | QTc >500 ms | Seizures, laryngospasm, carpopedal spasm | STAT — IV calcium bolus, continuous infusion |
| Critical | Any with TdP | Polymorphic VT | Hemodynamic compromise | Defibrillation if sustained; IV Mg2+ + calcium |

## 6. Reasoning Complexity Analysis

### 6.1 Pattern Recognition Complexity
Low-moderate. The ECG change (long flat ST segment with normal T wave) is visually distinctive once the clinician knows what to look for. The main challenge is recognizing the ST segment prolongation rather than simply noting a "long QT."

### 6.2 Measurement Challenges
QTc measurement is standard; the main pitfall is failing to check whether the QT prolongation is from ST elongation (hypocalcemia) or T wave morphology change (LQT syndrome, drug effects). This distinction drives the clinical workup.

### 6.3 Clinical Context Importance
The etiology context is critical: post-thyroid surgery patient with long QT → hypocalcemia first. Massive transfusion patient with long QT → citrate toxicity. ICU patient with pancreatitis and long QT → check ionized Ca2+. The ECG finding prompts the lab check.

### 6.4 Distinction from LQT Syndrome
This is the most important intellectual task. The rule: **elongated ST segment with normal T wave morphology = hypocalcemia** (or LQT3 — which can appear similar but has a very late-onset T wave). **Abnormal T wave (peaked, notched, biphasic, large) = LQT1/LQT2/drug-induced**. Calcium level confirms.

### 6.5 Treatment-ECG Feedback
IV calcium gluconate normalizes QTc within 30–60 minutes in acute hypocalcemia. Serial ECG monitoring confirms response. Failure to normalize QTc after calcium repletion → check Mg2+ level.

### 6.6 Difficulty Score

| Dimension | Score (1–5) | Rationale |
|---|---|---|
| Pattern recognition | 2 | Elongated flat ST is visually distinctive |
| Measurement precision | 2 | Standard QTc measurement; clear T wave morphology |
| Differential diagnosis | 2 | LQT syndrome is the main mimic; distinguishable by T wave |
| Multi-condition interaction | 2 | Hypomagnesemia coexistence important |
| Severity stratification | 2 | Ionized Ca2+ drives stratification; QTc confirms |
| Treatment implication | 2 | IV calcium; Mg2+ check; straightforward |
| Arrhythmia recognition | 2 | TdP recognition standard |
| **Overall** | **2.0** | **Low-moderate complexity** |

## 6A. Agent Assignment

**Architecture:** Option E — 3 Parallel Phase 1 Specialists + 1 Sequential CDS (Node 2.1 Gate Passed 2026-03-27)

| Agent | Role for Hypocalcemia | Key Features Used |
|-------|--------------------------|-------------------|
| **RRC** (Rhythm/Rate/Conduction) | Supporting | QT/QTc measurement (markedly prolonged); RR interval for rate correction |
| **IT** (Ischemia/Territory) | Not involved | No ischemic ST or T-wave changes; ST prolongation is flat/isoelectric not elevated |
| **MR** (Morphology/Repolarization) | Primary | Markedly prolonged QTc due to prolonged flat ST segment (phase 2 elongation), normal T-wave morphology (T-wave itself not prolonged), absence of prominent U-waves |
| **CDS** (Cross-Domain Synthesis) | Standard integration only | Integrates prolonged QTc (RRC) + elongated flat ST segment with normal T-wave (MR) → hypocalcemia pattern; distinguishes from long QT (prolonged T-wave) and hypokalemia (U-waves) |

### Primary Agent
**MR** — The hallmark of hypocalcemia is a prolonged flat ST segment with normal T-wave morphology, which is a repolarization pattern within MR's domain.

### Cross-Domain Hints
- MR → RRC: `cross_domain_hint: "QT markedly prolonged due to elongated ST segment; T-wave morphology normal — confirm RRC QTc measurement reflects ST prolongation, not T-wave prolongation"`

### CDS Specific Role
CDS performs standard integration for hypocalcemia, applying the key distinction within the prolonged QT differential: hypocalcemia prolongs the ST segment (phase 2) while leaving T-wave morphology intact, whereas long QT syndromes (congenital or drug-induced) prolong the T-wave itself. Hypokalemia is further differentiated by the presence of U-waves and T-wave flattening. CDS uses MR's characterization of which component of the QT interval is elongated to generate the specific diagnosis and flag TdP risk when QTc exceeds 500ms.

## 9. Edge Cases and Pitfalls

1. **Post-thyroid surgery hypocalcemia:** This is the most common acute clinical scenario. Parathyroid glands may be inadvertently removed or devascularized. Hypocalcemia can develop within 24–48 hours and progress rapidly. ECG monitoring post-thyroidectomy is warranted.

2. **Ionized vs. total calcium:** In critically ill patients with hypoalbuminemia, total calcium is falsely low while ionized calcium may be normal — and vice versa (alkalosis from mechanical ventilation reduces ionized Ca2+ with normal total Ca2+). Always check ionized calcium in the ICU.

3. **LQT3 vs. hypocalcemia:** LQT3 (SCN5A mutation) prolongs QT with an apparently normal or flat ST segment and a late-peaking T wave. This can closely resemble hypocalcemia. The T wave onset in LQT3 is delayed (very late after QRS) while the T wave in hypocalcemia begins at a normal time but follows a very long flat ST segment. Lab confirmation is required.

4. **Citrate toxicity during massive transfusion:** Each unit of pRBC contains citrate anticoagulant. During massive transfusion (>10 units), citrate accumulates and chelates ionized Ca2+. This is transient and rapidly reversible with IV calcium, but may cause arrhythmias during the transfusion itself. Prophylactic calcium gluconate is recommended during massive transfusion protocols.

5. **Hyperventilation-induced tetany:** Respiratory alkalosis reduces ionized Ca2+ by increasing albumin-Ca2+ binding. Panicked patients who hyperventilate may develop perioral tingling, carpopedal spasm, and potentially arrhythmias from ionized hypocalcemia with normal total calcium.

6. **Magnesium first:** In any hypocalcemia with refractory response to IV calcium, hypomagnesemia is the culprit. Mg2+ is required for PTH secretion. Without Mg2+ correction, calcium levels will not normalize regardless of how much calcium is given.
