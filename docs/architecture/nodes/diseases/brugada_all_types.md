## 1. Pathophysiology Root Cause

### 1.1 Molecular Basis
Brugada Syndrome (BrS) is an inherited channelopathy with autosomal dominant inheritance and incomplete penetrance. The core mechanism is reduced inward sodium current (INa) in the right ventricular outflow tract (RVOT) epicardium. The primary gene is SCN5A, encoding the cardiac voltage-gated sodium channel Nav1.5 — loss-of-function mutations identified in 20–30% of clinically diagnosed cases. Additional susceptibility genes include CACNA1C (L-type Ca²⁺ channel), CACNB2 (Ca²⁺ channel β-subunit), SCN1B, GPD1L, KCNE3, and others, all converging on reduced depolarizing current in the RVOT region.

### 1.2 Electrophysiological Basis of the ECG Pattern
The RVOT epicardium has prominent transient outward potassium current (Ito), which creates a notch in phase 1 of the action potential. Normally, the inward plateau current (INa, ICa) overcomes Ito and maintains the phase 2 plateau. When INa is reduced (SCN5A loss-of-function, fever, sodium channel blockers, vagal predominance), the Ito notch deepens until phase 2 collapses entirely in the RVOT epicardium. The endocardium maintains its plateau. This epicardial-endocardial voltage gradient during phase 2 (the "phase 2 re-entry" mechanism) generates the ST elevation visible in V1–V3.

The ST elevation therefore represents a phase 2 loss of dome in the RVOT epicardium, not ischemia or infarction. This explains why the pattern is dynamic — conditions that further reduce INa (fever >38.5°C, sodium channel blockers, vagal tone at rest/sleep) unmask or worsen the pattern.

### 1.3 Clinical Phenotype
- Male predominance (~8:1 male-to-female ratio; testosterone upregulates Ito)
- VF events typically during rest or sleep (high vagal tone reduces INa further)
- Average age of first VF event: 40s, but can be any age including pediatric
- Fever is a potent trigger: acute febrile illness can precipitate VF in previously undiagnosed patients
- Structurally normal heart on echo/MRI in the majority
- Spontaneous Type 1 pattern = higher risk than drug-induced Type 1

## 2. ECG Presentation — Lead by Lead

### 2.1 Diagnostic Criteria (2025 AHA/ESC Guidelines)

| Pattern | ST Morphology | Amplitude | T Wave | Diagnostic Status |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Type 1 (Coved) | Downsloping ST from J point; concave-down or straight descent | ≥2 mm ST elevation in ≥1 of V1–V3 | Inverted (negative) | DIAGNOSTIC for Brugada Pattern |
| Type 2 (Saddle-back) | Elevated ST with concave (upward) morphology; ST remains elevated ≥0.5 mm at nadir | ≥2 mm J-point elevation | Positive or biphasic | NOT diagnostic alone; requires provocation test |
| Type 3 (historical) | Saddle-back with <1 mm J-point or ST elevation | <2 mm total | Positive | No longer used as separate classification in 2023 ESC update |

**Key rule:** Type 1 pattern in ≥1 of V1–V3 (at standard OR high lead placement) = Brugada ECG Pattern. Clinical syndrome requires Type 1 + documented VF/VT, syncope, nocturnal agonal breathing, or family history.

High lead placement: V1–V2 placed in the 2nd intercostal space (one space higher than standard). This increases sensitivity for coved pattern detection by ~25% because the RVOT is anatomically superior.

| Lead | Type 1 Finding | Type 2 Finding | Notes |

| I | Normal | Normal | Peripheral leads uninvolved |
| II | Normal or minor changes | Normal | Not a diagnostic lead |
| III | Normal | Normal | |
| aVR | May show reciprocal changes | Normal | |

### 2.3 Key Leads
- **V1 and V2 (standard or high placement)**: The only leads diagnostically relevant. Brugada pattern is a right precordial phenomenon reflecting RVOT depolarization.
- **High V1–V2 placement (2nd ICS)**: Increases coved pattern visibility when standard placement is negative or shows only Type 2. Should be performed in any suspected case.
- **V3**: Often transitional; less specific. ST elevation in V4–V6 should trigger competing diagnoses (STEMI, LBBB, pericarditis).

### 2.4 Beat-by-Beat Considerations
- Pattern may be absent on any given ECG (concealed BrS) — single normal ECG does not rule out Brugada Syndrome
- Circadian variation: pattern often more prominent at night (increased vagal tone)
- Fever effect: pattern may appear or worsen acutely during febrile illness; always obtain ECG in BrS patients presenting with fever
- Drug-induced: IV ajmaline, flecainide, or procainamide can convert Type 2 → Type 1 (provocation test in controlled setting)
- Spontaneous variation: Type 1 → Type 2 → normal on sequential ECGs in same patient is characteristic of BrS

## 3. Morphology Details

### 3.1 Coved Morphology (Type 1)
The classic Type 1 coved pattern is defined by:
- J wave or ST segment elevated ≥2 mm at the J point in V1 (or V2)
- Downsloping ST segment: the ST starts elevated and descends toward the baseline in a straight or convex-down manner
- The ST slope is negative — it does not rise after the J point
- Terminal T-wave inversion: the T wave is negative, completing the "coved" appearance (like an inverted arch)
- The "r' " in V1 that precedes the coved ST elevation may mimic an incomplete RBBB morphology

### 3.2 Saddle-Back Morphology (Type 2)
- J point elevated ≥2 mm
- ST segment is concave (bowing upward), creating a "saddle" shape
- T wave is positive or biphasic
- The ST segment does not descend to baseline before the T wave — it remains elevated ≥0.5 mm
- Visually: the ECG trace goes up (J point), dips slightly (saddle nadir), then rises again into a positive T wave

### 3.3 RBBB Overlap
True RBBB creates ST elevation in V1–V2 via a different mechanism (delayed RV depolarization). The RBBB ST elevation is saddle-back in morphology, not coved. The T wave in RBBB is typically inverted in V1 as a secondary change, but the overall RSR' morphology and broad S waves in lateral leads (I, V5–V6) confirm RBBB. Brugada Type 1 does not require broad S waves in lateral leads. When both coexist, coved ST elevation in V1–V2 with wide QRS still satisfies Brugada ECG criteria.

### 3.4 QRS Duration
May be mildly prolonged in V1 due to delayed RVOT conduction, but full RBBB pattern not required for diagnosis. QRS in limb leads typically normal.

### 3.5 PR Interval
PR prolongation (first-degree AV block) seen in ~20% of BrS patients. SCN5A loss-of-function also reduces nodal and His-Purkinje conduction velocity.

### 3.6 QTc
Usually normal in Brugada Syndrome. Prolonged QTc suggests coexisting long QT or alternative diagnosis. Very short QTc suggests possible BrS-SQTS overlap (rare).

### 3.7 P-Wave
Normal in most cases. Occasionally atrial conduction prolonged; AF may coexist as SCN5A affects atrial channels.

## 4. Differential Diagnosis

### 4.1 Mimics

| Condition | Distinguishing Feature |
|---|---|
| Incomplete RBBB | Saddle-back ST in V1; wide S wave in I and V5–V6; no fever/drug precipitant; stable morphology |
| True RBBB | RSR' in V1, broad S in lateral leads, secondary T inversions V1–V3 — but NOT coved morphology |
| STEMI (V1–V2 territory) | Regional ST elevation with reciprocal changes; evolves; Q waves develop; troponin rises |
| Pericarditis | Diffuse ST elevation, PR depression, no inverted T in V1 |
| Right ventricular hypertrophy | Dominant R in V1; right axis deviation; no coved morphology |
| Hyperkalemia | Wide QRS; peaked T waves; P-wave loss; serum K+ confirms |
| ARVC | T inversions V1–V4 with epsilon waves; RV structural abnormality on echo/MRI |
| Pulmonary embolism | S1Q3T3; sinus tachycardia; right axis deviation; RV strain pattern |
| Normal variant ST elevation | Concave upward morphology; no J-wave notch; stable; asymptomatic young male |
| Aortic dissection (rare) | ECG may show ST changes; clinical presentation dominant |

### 4.2 Coexisting Conditions
- **SCN5A overlap syndromes**: Same SCN5A loss-of-function can manifest as BrS, LQTS3, sick sinus syndrome, or progressive cardiac conduction disease — phenotypic overlap exists
- **AF**: SCN5A effects on atria; AF documented in 5–20% of BrS patients
- **Fever-precipitated VF**: Not true coexistence but critical recognition — any patient with BrS developing fever requires hospitalization for monitoring

## 6. Reasoning Complexity Analysis

### 6.1 Reasoning Domains Required
- Precise morphology classification: coved vs saddle-back distinction is the critical binary decision
- Lead placement awareness: standard vs high-placement V1–V2 affects sensitivity
- Pattern dynamics: concealed pattern, fever effect, drug-induced conversion
- RBBB differentiation: structural RBBB vs RVOT conduction delay
- Risk stratification: spontaneous vs drug-induced, symptom status, family history
- Drug knowledge: sodium channel blockers as both diagnostic tools and provocateurs

### 6.2 Feature Dependencies
- Coved morphology detection requires accurate measurement of ST slope direction (downsloping = Type 1; concave/upsloping = Type 2)
- J-point amplitude ≥2 mm threshold must be measured from isoelectric baseline
- High-lead placement information may not be encoded in standard ECG metadata — requires clinical documentation
- Fever history requires integration of vital signs data outside ECG

### 6.3 Cross-Condition Interactions
- RBBB + BrS: assess coved pattern independently of RBBB morphology; both can coexist
- BrS + SQTS: rare, both SCN5A and KCNH2 involved in reported overlap cases
- BrS + early repolarization: inferolateral ER coexisting with BrS pattern — separate classification
- Ajmaline/flecainide administration: drug-provocation ECG shows Type 1 as diagnostic endpoint

### 6.4 Reasoning Chain Sketch
1. Detect ST elevation in V1 and/or V2 (standard placement)
2. Classify morphology: downsloping + inverted T = Type 1 (coved); concave + positive T = Type 2 (saddle-back)
3. If Type 1: confirm amplitude ≥2 mm; apply Brugada ECG pattern diagnosis
4. If Type 2: flag as suspicious; recommend high-lead placement repeat; do not diagnose without provocation
5. Check for RBBB: if present, assess whether coved morphology is superimposed on RBBB pattern
6. Integrate clinical: fever, syncope, family history, SCN5A mutation status
7. Assign STAT level based on pattern type + clinical context
8. If Type 1 + any symptoms → STAT escalation

### 6.5 Confidence Anchors
- Type 1 coved in V1 with ≥2 mm elevation + inverted T: high confidence
- Type 2 saddle-back only: always low confidence for diagnosis — requires provocation
- RBBB pattern in V1 with saddle-back ST: low confidence for BrS, high confidence for RBBB
- Fever + Type 1: high confidence for BrS unmasking by fever

### 6.6 Difficulty Score

| Dimension | Score (1–5) | Rationale |
|---|---|---|
| Morphology Recognition | 4 | Coved vs saddle-back distinction subtle; RBBB overlap challenging |
| Interval Measurement | 2 | J-point amplitude measurement; no complex intervals |
| Differential Diagnosis | 4 | Multiple right precordial ST elevation mimics |
| Clinical Integration | 4 | Spontaneous vs drug-induced; fever; symptoms; family history |
| Rarity / Prior Probability | 3 | Uncommon but not rare; ~1:2000 population |
| Arrhythmia Risk Stratification | 4 | Spontaneous Type 1 vs drug-induced Type 1 risk difference significant |
| **Overall** | **3.8** | Primarily morphology classification and context integration |

## 6A. Agent Assignment

**Architecture:** Option E — 3 Parallel Phase 1 Specialists + 1 Sequential CDS (Node 2.1 Gate Passed 2026-03-27)

| Agent | Role for Brugada Syndrome (All Types) | Key Features Used |
|-------|--------------------------|-------------------|
| **RRC** (Rhythm/Rate/Conduction) | Supporting | Arrhythmia context: VF history, documented VT, syncope; rate and rhythm assessment; first-degree AV block (associated with BrS in some patients) |
| **IT** (Ischemia/Territory) | Supporting | ST elevation in V1-V2 detection — must not classify Brugada coved/saddle-back ST pattern as anterior STEMI |
| **MR** (Morphology/Repolarization) | Primary | Type 1 coved morphology (≥2mm J-wave + descending ST + inverted T in V1-V2), Type 2 saddle-back (≥2mm J-wave + elevated ST ≥1mm + positive/biphasic T), Type 3 (<2mm J-wave, <1mm ST elevation); morphology classification in V1-V2 (or V1-V2 in 2nd/3rd ICS position) |
| **CDS** (Cross-Domain Synthesis) | Required — CRITICAL: resolves Brugada coved ST vs anterior STEMI; identifies spontaneous vs drug-induced Type 1 | Integrates IT V1-V2 ST elevation + MR coved morphology classification; coved Type 1 = diagnostic Brugada (not ischemia); saddle-back Type 2 = probable BrS (needs provocation); integrates RRC arrhythmia history for risk stratification |

### Primary Agent
**MR** — Brugada syndrome is defined entirely by its repolarization morphology in V1-V2: the distinction between Type 1 (coved), Type 2 (saddle-back), and Type 3 patterns is a morphology determination within MR's domain.

### Cross-Domain Hints
- IT → CDS: `cross_domain_hint: "ST elevation detected in V1-V2 without clear anterior STEMI territory pattern — CDS should integrate MR morphology assessment; coved morphology indicates Brugada Type 1, not ischemia"`
- MR → IT: `cross_domain_hint: "Coved/saddle-back ST morphology in V1-V2 identified — IT must not classify as anterior STEMI; this is Brugada pattern requiring CDS arbitration"`
- MR → CDS: `cross_domain_hint: "Type 1 coved morphology confirmed in V1-V2 — CDS should integrate RRC arrhythmia history (syncope, VF, VT) for Brugada risk stratification and differentiate spontaneous vs drug-induced Type 1"`
- RRC → CDS: `cross_domain_hint: "Arrhythmia history (VF/VT/syncope) context available — if MR confirms Type 1 coved morphology, CDS should apply Brugada risk stratification (spontaneous Type 1 = higher risk than drug-induced Type 1)"`

### CDS Specific Role
CDS performs critical arbitration for Brugada syndrome: when IT flags ST elevation in V1-V2 and MR identifies coved morphology, CDS overrides territorial ischemia interpretation and generates the Brugada Type 1 diagnosis. For Type 2 saddle-back patterns, CDS notes that provocation testing (ajmaline/flecainide challenge) may be required to unmask Type 1. CDS integrates RRC's arrhythmia history findings to apply risk stratification: spontaneous Type 1 + prior VF/VT or syncope = high risk; asymptomatic incidental Type 1 = intermediate risk requiring electrophysiology referral. This reference file documents all three Brugada types and their agent handling for cross-referencing by individual type files.

## 9. Edge Cases and Pitfalls

1. **Concealed Brugada**: Patient with known BrS may have completely normal ECG at presentation. Single normal ECG does not exclude BrS. History or genetic testing is required.
2. **Fever-masked presentation**: A febrile patient with new ST elevation in V1–V2 that physicians attribute to "STEMI equivalent" when it is Brugada unmasked by fever. Aggressive antipyretics and repeat ECG after defervescence are critical.
3. **Electrode misplacement**: V1 and V2 placed in 3rd ICS (too low) misses RVOT signal. Standard placement may show only Type 2 when Type 1 exists at high placement. All suspected Brugada cases should have high-placement V1–V2 performed.
4. **RBBB misclassification**: True RBBB with saddle-back ST changes in V1 is commonly mislabeled as Brugada. The lateral leads (I, V5–V6) showing broad S waves and the absence of coved morphology confirm RBBB without Brugada.
5. **Spontaneous fluctuation**: A Type 1 pattern may be present on morning ECG but absent on afternoon ECG. Serial ECGs at different times improve sensitivity.
6. **VF in apparently normal ECG patient with fever**: In ER, VF arrest during febrile illness in a structurally normal heart should trigger Brugada workup after resuscitation, even if ECG on admission appeared normal.
7. **Sodium channel blocker iatrogenic**: Flecainide or procainamide prescribed for AF in an undiagnosed BrS patient can precipitate VF. Prior to prescribing sodium channel blockers, Brugada pattern should be excluded.
8. **Type 2 over-diagnosis**: Saddle-back ST elevation is common and non-specific. Type 2 alone should never be reported as "Brugada" without provocation test or clinical context. It is "suspicious for Brugada" at most.
