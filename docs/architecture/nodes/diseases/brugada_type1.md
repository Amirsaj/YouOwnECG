## 1. Pathophysiology Root Cause

### 1.1 What Goes Wrong (Anatomy/Physiology)
- Brugada syndrome is a genetic cardiac channelopathy most commonly caused by loss-of-function mutations in SCN5A, which encodes the alpha-subunit of the cardiac voltage-gated sodium channel (Nav1.5). Over 500 SCN5A variants have been identified, accounting for ~20-25% of clinically confirmed cases; the remaining 75-80% have no identifiable mutation (genotype-negative Brugada)
- The functional defect is a reduction in the inward sodium current (INa) during phase 0 and phase 1 of the cardiac action potential. This reduces the depolarization reserve — the myocardium has fewer available sodium channels to generate the rapid upstroke
- The pathology is concentrated in the right ventricular outflow tract (RVOT) epicardium. The RVOT epicardium has a physiologically higher density of transient outward potassium current (Ito) channels compared to the endocardium and compared to the left ventricle. In normal hearts, this creates a small phase 1 notch in the epicardial action potential. In Brugada syndrome, the reduced INa can no longer counterbalance the prominent Ito, leading to an exaggerated phase 1 repolarization notch or even loss of the epicardial action potential dome entirely
- The loss of the epicardial action potential dome in the RVOT creates a transmural voltage gradient between the fully depolarized endocardium and the prematurely repolarizing epicardium. This gradient manifests as ST elevation on the surface ECG in the right precordial leads (V1-V3) that overlie the RVOT
- Additional genes implicated include CACNA1C, CACNB2 (L-type calcium channel subunits), GPD1L, SCN1B, SCN3B, KCNE3, and HCN4, all converging on the same final pathway: reduced inward current or enhanced outward current during the early phases of the RVOT epicardial action potential
- Inheritance is autosomal dominant with incomplete penetrance; male predominance 8-10:1 due to higher Ito density in male RVOT epicardium (testosterone upregulates Ito expression)

### 1.2 Electrical Consequence
- **Transmural voltage gradient (repolarization hypothesis):** Loss of the epicardial action potential dome in the RVOT creates a voltage difference between endocardium (still depolarized at plateau phase) and epicardium (already repolarizing). This generates an injury-like current that projects as ST elevation in leads overlying the RVOT
- **Phase 2 re-entry (arrhythmogenesis):** The action potential dome may be lost in some epicardial regions but preserved in adjacent regions. This creates marked dispersion of repolarization within the RVOT epicardium. The preserved dome regions can re-excite the already-repolarized dome-lost regions, triggering phase 2 re-entry → closely coupled premature ventricular complexes (PVCs) → polymorphic VT → VF
- **Depolarization hypothesis (complementary):** Delayed conduction through the RVOT due to reduced INa may also contribute to the ECG pattern. Late activation of the RVOT produces a terminal positive deflection in V1 (pseudo-RBBB pattern) and contributes to the ST elevation through delayed depolarization rather than pure repolarization abnormality. Recent evidence suggests both mechanisms coexist
- **Autonomic modulation:** Vagal tone increases Ito and decreases INa → worsens the ionic imbalance → explains why arrhythmic events cluster during sleep, rest, and post-prandial states (high vagal tone). Sympathetic stimulation increases ICa-L which partially restores the dome → explains why exercise and isoproterenol can normalize the ECG
- **Temperature sensitivity:** Fever accelerates Ito kinetics and shifts Na+ channel inactivation to more negative voltages, further reducing INa availability. This is why fever is the single most important modifiable trigger for Brugada — febrile illness can unmask a Type 1 pattern in previously normal-appearing ECGs and precipitate VF

### 1.3 Why It Appears on ECG
- The transmural voltage gradient in the RVOT projects a positive injury-like current toward the anterior chest wall leads V1-V3. The RVOT is located anteriorly and superiorly in the mediastinum, directly beneath the V1-V2 electrode positions
- The coved ST elevation represents the epicardial repolarization gradient: the convex (dome-shaped) ST segment corresponds to the period during which the endocardium is still at plateau phase while the epicardium is already repolarizing
- The subsequent T-wave inversion reflects the reversal of the normal transmural repolarization gradient — normally the epicardium repolarizes first (producing an upright T-wave), but in Brugada the epicardium repolarizes prematurely (or loses its dome), so the endocardium repolarizes last, creating a repolarization vector directed AWAY from the anterior leads → inverted T-wave
- The changes are confined to V1-V3 because only the RVOT epicardium has the unique combination of high Ito density and reduced INa. The remainder of the RV and the entire LV have sufficient depolarization reserve to maintain normal action potential morphology
- Leads V1-V3 positioned in higher intercostal spaces (2nd or 3rd instead of standard 4th) may increase sensitivity because the higher placement is more directly over the RVOT

## 2. ECG Presentation — Lead by Lead

### 2.1 Diagnostic Criteria (2025 AHA/ESC Guidelines)

| Criterion | Threshold | Source |
|-----------|-----------|--------|

| Coved morphology definition | High take-off ST segment → gradual downsloping → negative T-wave; the ST segment and T-wave form a continuous curve without a distinct J-point–to–T-wave transition | 2025 ESC; Bayés de Luna 2012 |

### 2.3 Key Leads (Most Diagnostic)
- **V1-V2**: The two most sensitive and specific leads. The coved Type 1 pattern is most reliably identified here. V1 and V2 directly overlie the RVOT and receive the maximal projection of the transmural repolarization gradient
- **V3**: Variably involved — its inclusion depends on RVOT size, cardiac rotation, and electrode placement. When V3 shows the coved pattern, it adds confidence but is not required for diagnosis
- **High intercostal leads (V1-V3 in 2nd or 3rd ICS)**: In cases where standard V1-V3 show Type 2 (saddle-back) or borderline findings, repositioning electrodes to higher intercostal spaces may reveal Type 1 coved morphology. This is because the higher placement positions the electrode more directly over the RVOT, which is located superiorly in the mediastinum
- **All other leads (I, II, III, aVR, aVL, aVF, V4-V6) should be NORMAL** — this is itself a diagnostic feature. Brugada Type 1 is a localized RVOT phenomenon. Widespread ST changes suggest an alternative diagnosis

### 2.4 Beat-by-Beat Considerations
- The Brugada Type 1 pattern can be **intermittent** — it may appear and disappear spontaneously over hours to days. The pattern is modulated by autonomic tone, body temperature, heart rate, and drug exposure
- At faster heart rates (sympathetic activation), the pattern may NORMALIZE because increased ICa-L restores the epicardial dome
- At slower heart rates (vagal tone dominance), the pattern becomes more prominent because reduced ICa-L and enhanced Ito worsen the epicardial dome loss
- **Post-PVC beats**: After a premature beat and compensatory pause, the longer diastolic interval allows more Ito recovery → the first post-pause beat may show a more exaggerated coved pattern. This is diagnostically useful in borderline cases
- **Fever**: May convert a normal ECG to overt Type 1 pattern within minutes. Serial ECGs during fever are critical in patients with known or suspected Brugada
- **All beats should show the same pattern when the Type 1 pattern is manifest** — beat-to-beat morphology alternation is NOT a feature of Brugada (unlike TdP where T-wave alternans may occur). However, the transition FROM normal to Type 1 may be gradual over sequential beats as autonomic conditions change
- **Nocturnal/sleep**: Continuous ECG monitoring shows the Type 1 pattern is most prominent between midnight and 6 AM, correlating with peak vagal tone and the highest risk period for VF

## 3. Morphology Details (What the Agent Must See)

### 3.1 P-wave Changes
- **Morphology**: Normal — Brugada syndrome does not affect atrial depolarization. P-waves remain sinus in origin with normal morphology
- **Duration**: Normal (<120 ms)
- **Axis**: Normal (0° to +75°)
- **Exception**: If VF occurs, P-waves are obviously lost; if atrial arrhythmias coexist (AFib occurs in ~10-20% of Brugada patients due to RVOT abnormalities extending to atrial tissue), P-waves may be absent or replaced by fibrillatory activity
- **Key point**: Normal P-waves preceding every QRS helps distinguish Brugada from other causes of ST elevation

### 3.2 PR Interval Changes
- **Duration**: Usually normal (120-200 ms). Some Brugada patients show mildly prolonged PR interval (>200 ms) due to reduced INa in the AV node/His-Purkinje system — this is seen in approximately 10-15% of cases and may represent a more severe sodium channel dysfunction phenotype
- **Pattern**: Constant; no Wenckebach or dropped beats
- **Diagnostic implication**: PR prolongation in a patient with coved ST in V1-V3 supports the diagnosis (consistent with generalized sodium channel dysfunction) but is not required

### 3.3 QRS Complex Changes
- **Duration**: Often mildly prolonged (100-120 ms) in V1-V2 due to delayed RVOT depolarization. Frankly wide QRS (>120 ms) is less common but does occur, particularly in V1-V2 (localized conduction delay, not a true bundle branch block)
- **Morphology**: rSR' or rsR' pattern in V1 — resembles incomplete or complete RBBB. However, unlike true RBBB, the delayed terminal forces are localized to V1-V3 and do NOT produce reciprocal wide S-waves in I and V6. The S-wave duration in V1 is prolonged (>80 ms from the nadir of the S to the peak of the R')
- **Amplitude**: Normal
- **Axis**: Usually normal. QRS axis shifts are not characteristic of Brugada
- **Key distinction from true RBBB**: In Brugada, the terminal R' in V1 blends seamlessly into the coved ST elevation — there is no clear J-point separating the QRS from the ST segment. In RBBB, the terminal R' is followed by a clearly demarcated ST segment

### 3.4 ST Segment Changes
- **Direction**: ELEVATION in V1-V3; isoelectric everywhere else
- **Morphology**: **Coved (convex upward)** — this is the defining feature. The ST segment originates from an elevated J-point (≥2 mm above baseline), forms a smooth convex dome (arching upward), then descends gradually into a negative T-wave. The overall shape resembles an arch or a dome (hence "coved"). There is NO isoelectric segment between the J-point and the T-wave onset — the ST and T form one continuous curve
- **Measurement point**: J-point elevation is measured from the isoelectric baseline (TP or TQ segment). ≥2 mm (0.2 mV) required for diagnosis
- **CRITICAL morphological distinction — Type 1 (coved) vs Type 2 (saddle-back)**:
  - **Type 1 (coved)**: J-point elevated ≥2 mm → convex ST → descending → inverted T. Looks like an arch/dome followed by a valley. Only pattern that is spontaneously diagnostic
  - **Type 2 (saddle-back)**: J-point elevated ≥2 mm → concave ST → positive T-wave. Looks like a saddle with two humps (r' and T-wave). NOT diagnostic spontaneously — requires drug provocation to convert to Type 1

### 3.5 T-wave Changes
- **Direction**: **Inverted** in V1-V3 in Type 1 pattern. The T-wave inversion is a direct continuation of the descending limb of the coved ST segment — the ST-T complex forms one continuous curve from the J-point through the inverted T-wave to the return to baseline
- **Amplitude**: T-wave inversion is typically 1-3 mm deep in V1-V2
- **Symmetry**: Asymmetric — the descending limb (from ST) is more gradual than the ascending return to baseline
- **Key point**: In Type 2 (saddle-back), the T-wave remains UPRIGHT — this is the critical distinction. Coved ST → inverted T = Type 1 (diagnostic). Saddle-back ST → upright T = Type 2 (not diagnostic alone)
- **Normal leads**: T-waves are upright and normal in all leads outside V1-V3

### 3.6 QT/QTc Changes
- **QTc**: Usually normal. Brugada syndrome is not characterized by QT prolongation
- **Subtle finding**: Some studies report mildly prolonged QTc in V1-V2 (but not in other leads) due to the delayed RVOT repolarization — this is a localized effect, not global QT prolongation
- **Torsades de Pointes risk**: The arrhythmia in Brugada is polymorphic VT/VF, NOT TdP. The mechanism is phase 2 re-entry, not early afterdepolarizations. However, the clinical presentation (syncope → polymorphic wide complex tachycardia) can look similar on monitoring

### 3.7 Other Features
- **Epsilon waves**: NOT present in Brugada (epsilon waves are characteristic of ARVC — a key differential)
- **Delta waves**: Absent (no pre-excitation)
- **U-waves**: Not characteristic
- **Fragmented QRS (fQRS)**: May be present in V1-V3, reflecting localized conduction abnormalities in the RVOT. fQRS in the context of Brugada has been associated with higher arrhythmic risk
- **Pseudo-RBBB**: The rSR' pattern in V1 is often called "pseudo-RBBB" because it mimics RBBB morphologically but has a different mechanism (delayed RVOT depolarization vs right bundle conduction delay)
- **S-wave duration in V1**: The interval from the nadir of the S-wave to the peak of the R' (or to the beginning of ST elevation) is prolonged (>80 ms) — a useful quantitative marker distinguishing Brugada from benign RBBB

## 4. Differential Diagnosis

### 4.1 Mimics (What Looks Like This But Isn't)

| Mimic Condition | Shared Features | Distinguishing Features |
|----------------|-----------------|----------------------|
| Right Bundle Branch Block (RBBB) | rSR' in V1, ST changes in V1-V2 | RBBB: QRS ≥120 ms with wide S-wave in I and V6; ST depression (not coved elevation) in V1-V2; T-wave inversion in V1-V3 follows a clearly demarcated ST segment, not a continuous coved curve. The R' in RBBB is taller and narrower than in Brugada |
| Incomplete RBBB | rSR' in V1 with mildly prolonged QRS | QRS 100-120 ms; ST segment is isoelectric after the R', not elevated or coved; T-wave may be inverted but there is a flat ST segment between QRS end and T-wave onset |
| Early Repolarization (anterior variant) | ST elevation in V1-V3 | ER: ST elevation is CONCAVE upward ("smiley face"), not convex/coved; T-waves are tall and upright, not inverted; J-point elevation is typically <2 mm; responds to exercise (normalizes); no rSR' pattern |
| Pectus excavatum | ST elevation and T-wave inversion in V1-V3 | Chest wall deformity compresses the heart; changes resolve with high lead placement (opposite of Brugada, where high leads UNMASK the pattern); physical exam reveals the deformity; no rSR' morphology |
| Hyperkalemia (pseudo-Brugada pattern) | ST elevation with wide QRS in V1-V3 | HyperK: changes are DIFFUSE (peaked T-waves in ALL leads, not just V1-V3); QRS widening is diffuse, not localized to V1-V3; PR prolongation and P-wave loss occur; clinical context (renal failure, K+ level) differentiates; resolves with K+ correction |
| Athlete heart (RV remodeling) | Mild ST elevation in V1-V2, possible T-wave inversion | Athletic heart: T-wave inversion in V1-V3 is common in athletes (especially Afro-Caribbean); ST elevation is concave, not coved; no rSR'; resolves with detraining; echocardiogram shows physiological (symmetric) RV enlargement |
| ARVC (Arrhythmogenic RV Cardiomyopathy) | T-wave inversion V1-V3, possible ST elevation, RV arrhythmias | ARVC: epsilon waves after QRS in V1-V3 (small high-frequency potentials); T-wave inversion WITHOUT coved ST elevation; structural RV abnormalities on imaging (fat/fibrosis); family history of sudden death or ARVC; task force criteria for diagnosis |
| Acute RV STEMI (proximal RCA) | ST elevation in V1-V3 | RV STEMI: Acute onset with chest pain; ST elevation in V1-V3 PLUS V3R-V4R (right-sided leads); reciprocal ST depression in lateral leads; evolves over time (hyperacute T → ST elevation → Q waves); troponin positive |
| Posterior STEMI (mirror pattern) | Tall R-waves in V1-V2 | Posterior STEMI: Tall R-wave (not rSR') in V1-V2 representing reciprocal Q-waves; ST depression (not elevation) in V1-V2 in most cases; tall upright T-waves in V1-V2 (reciprocal T inversion); ST elevation in V7-V9 |

### 4.2 Coexisting Conditions
- **Brugada + Fever**: Fever unmasks or worsens the Type 1 pattern. Any febrile patient with known Brugada must have continuous ECG monitoring and aggressive antipyretic therapy. Fever can convert a genotype-positive/phenotype-negative patient to overt Type 1 → VF
- **Brugada + Atrial Fibrillation**: ~10-20% of Brugada patients develop AFib (shared substrate: reduced INa in atrial tissue). AFib with Brugada complicates ECG interpretation — the irregularly irregular rhythm may make it harder to identify the coved ST pattern. AFib itself does not change the diagnostic criteria for the coved pattern
- **Brugada + Na+ channel blocking drugs**: Class IC antiarrhythmics (flecainide, propafenone), Class IA (procainamide, ajmaline), tricyclic antidepressants, cocaine, and high-dose propranolol can unmask or worsen the Brugada pattern. This is both a diagnostic tool (provocative testing) and a clinical danger (accidental unmasking)
- **Brugada + Electrolyte abnormalities**: Hyponatremia, hyperkalemia, and hypercalcemia can all modulate the Brugada pattern by altering the INa/Ito balance. Electrolytes must be checked in any new Brugada presentation

## 5. STAT Classification

| Priority | Criteria |
|----------|----------|
| **STAT** | Yes — Brugada Type 1 carries risk of sudden cardiac death from polymorphic VT/VF. The arrhythmia can occur without warning in previously asymptomatic individuals. Spontaneous Type 1 pattern on ECG requires immediate cardiology consultation |
| **Time-sensitive** | Identification must be immediate when the ECG is obtained. While the pattern itself does not require emergency intervention (unlike STEMI), the risk stratification and disposition are time-sensitive. If identified during fever, the urgency is HIGHER because fever is a known trigger for VF in Brugada — aggressive temperature control is needed immediately |
| **Clinical action** | (1) Immediate cardiology consultation; (2) Continuous telemetry monitoring; (3) If febrile: aggressive antipyretics (acetaminophen/ibuprofen), cooling measures, target normothermia; (4) Avoid all Na+ channel blockers, Class IA/IC drugs, and vagotonic maneuvers; (5) Risk stratify using Shanghai Score: personal history of syncope, documented VT/VF, family history of sudden death <45, positive genetic testing; (6) High-risk patients (prior cardiac arrest, spontaneous Type 1 + syncope): ICD implantation; (7) Intermediate risk: electrophysiology study for VF inducibility; (8) Medication card: patient must carry a list of drugs to avoid (www.brugadadrugs.org) |

### 6.1 Reasoning Domains Required to Detect This Condition
- **Morphology analysis (primary)**: The core of Brugada Type 1 detection is recognizing the coved ST-T morphology in V1-V3. This requires distinguishing convex (coved) from concave (saddle-back/early repol) ST elevation, and confirming the inverted T-wave follows without an isoelectric segment
- **Lead-group correlation (primary)**: The agent must verify that the coved pattern is LIMITED to V1-V3 and that all other leads are normal. Widespread ST changes should trigger doubt about a Brugada diagnosis
- **Interval measurement (supporting)**: QRS duration in V1-V2 (may be mildly prolonged), PR interval (may be mildly prolonged), QTc (should be normal globally). These are supporting rather than diagnostic
- **Amplitude measurement (primary)**: J-point elevation must be ≥2 mm in at least one of V1-V3. Precise measurement of the J-point relative to the TP baseline is required
- **Pattern differentiation (primary)**: Cross-domain reasoning is essential — the agent must differentiate Brugada Type 1 from RBBB, ARVC, early repolarization, hyperkalemia pseudo-Brugada, and other right precordial ST elevation causes. This requires morphological pattern comparison AND clinical context
- **Does NOT require**: Rate analysis (Brugada occurs at any rate), rhythm regularity analysis (rhythm is normal sinus), axis calculation (axis is normal), temporal pattern analysis (no evolution over time like STEMI)

### 6.2 Feature Dependencies
- **ESSENTIAL features from SDA-1**:
  - ST segment morphology classification in V1, V2, V3 (convex/coved vs concave vs horizontal)
  - J-point amplitude in V1, V2, V3 (absolute measurement in mm)
  - T-wave polarity in V1, V2, V3 (inverted vs upright)
  - Continuous ST-T curve detection (is there a smooth transition from elevated ST to inverted T without an isoelectric segment?)
  - QRS terminal morphology in V1 (rSR' detection)
- **SUPPORTING features from SDA-1**:
  - QRS duration in V1-V2 (mild prolongation supports diagnosis)
  - PR interval (mild prolongation supports sodium channelopathy)
  - ST segment morphology in V4-V6, limb leads (should be NORMAL — absence of changes is a supporting feature)
  - T-wave polarity in all 12 leads (T-wave inversion should be confined to V1-V3)
  - Heart rate (lower rate may make pattern more prominent)
- **EXCLUSION features**:
  - Diffuse ST changes across multiple lead groups → NOT Brugada (consider STEMI, pericarditis, hyperkalemia)
  - Peaked T-waves in leads outside V1-V3 → suggests hyperkalemia, not Brugada
  - Epsilon waves in V1-V3 → suggests ARVC
  - Wide S-waves in I and V6 with rSR' in V1 → true RBBB rather than Brugada pseudo-RBBB
- **Per-beat vs aggregate**: Aggregate analysis is sufficient for Brugada detection. Per-beat analysis is not typically needed unless assessing intermittency or post-PVC augmentation

### 6.3 Cross-Condition Interactions
- **Brugada masks or is masked by**:
  - RBBB and Brugada can coexist — when true RBBB is present, the additional coved ST morphology may be harder to identify because RBBB itself produces T-wave inversion in V1-V3. The distinguishing feature is the coved (convex) morphology of the ST segment, which is NOT a feature of isolated RBBB
  - Hyperkalemia can produce a "pseudo-Brugada" pattern that is indistinguishable from true Brugada on a single ECG. Requires K+ level and clinical context. K+ correction resolves pseudo-Brugada
- **Detecting Brugada requires ruling out**:
  - RBBB (wide S in I/V6 present in RBBB, absent in Brugada)
  - ARVC (epsilon waves, structural RV changes on imaging)
  - Early repolarization (concave ST, upright T-waves)
  - RV STEMI (acute clinical context, troponin, evolving ECG)
- **Brugada affects other condition interpretation**: If Brugada is identified, the baseline ST elevation in V1-V3 makes it difficult to diagnose concurrent anterior STEMI or RV STEMI in those leads. Comparison with prior ECGs becomes essential

### 6.4 Reasoning Chain Sketch
- **Minimum reasoning chain (fewest steps to high confidence)**:
  1. Identify ST elevation ≥2 mm in V1, V2, or V3
  2. Classify ST morphology as COVED (convex, dome-shaped)
  3. Confirm T-wave is INVERTED following the coved ST
  4. Confirm continuous curve from elevated J-point → coved ST → inverted T (no isoelectric segment)
  5. Verify V4-V6 and limb leads show NO ST elevation → localized to right precordial
  → High confidence Brugada Type 1

- **Full reasoning chain (complete evidence assembly)**:
  1. Detect ST elevation ≥2 mm in ≥1 of V1-V3
  2. Classify ST morphology: coved (convex) → proceed; saddle-back (concave + upright T) → Type 2, not diagnostic
  3. Confirm inverted T-wave in same leads as coved ST
  4. Assess J-point to T-wave nadir: continuous curve without plateau → supports coved morphology
  5. Check QRS in V1: rSR' pattern? If yes, assess S-wave duration (>80 ms supports Brugada over RBBB)
  6. Check leads I and V6 for wide S-waves: present → consider true RBBB; absent → supports Brugada
  7. Verify V4-V6, I, II, III, aVR, aVL, aVF all normal → localized pattern → supports Brugada
  8. Check for epsilon waves in V1-V3: present → consider ARVC instead
  9. Check for diffuse peaked T-waves: present → consider hyperkalemia pseudo-Brugada
  10. Assess heart rate: bradycardia may augment pattern; tachycardia may suppress
  11. Flag STAT condition with risk stratification recommendation

### 6.5 Confidence Anchors
- **HIGH confidence features**:
  - Coved (convex) ST elevation ≥2 mm in V1 or V2 with directly following inverted T-wave
  - Pattern LIMITED to V1-V3, all other leads normal
  - rSR' in V1 without wide S-wave in I/V6 (pseudo-RBBB, not true RBBB)
- **LOWER confidence if absent**:
  - If ST elevation is <2 mm but coved morphology is present → borderline, may need high intercostal leads or provocation
  - If T-wave is upright after ST elevation → Type 2 saddle-back, NOT Type 1
  - If QRS is frankly wide (>140 ms) with broad S in I/V6 → more likely RBBB
- **PATHOGNOMONIC combination**: Coved ST ≥2 mm in V1-V3 + inverted T-wave in same leads + normal ST-T in all remaining leads + absence of clinical ischemia = virtually diagnostic of Brugada Type 1 (near-100% specificity)
- **Classification thresholds**:
  - **Possible**: ST elevation in V1-V3 with equivocal morphology (not clearly coved, not clearly saddle-back) — recommend high intercostal leads and cardiology review
  - **Probable**: Coved ST ≥2 mm in 1 lead of V1-V3, inverted T in same lead, other leads normal
  - **Definite**: Coved ST ≥2 mm in ≥1 of V1-V3, inverted T, localized to right precordial leads, no alternative explanation

### 6.6 Difficulty Score
| Dimension | Score (1-5) | Notes |
|-----------|-------------|-------|
| Signal clarity needed | 4 | The coved vs saddle-back distinction requires clean ST segment morphology; baseline wander, muscle artifact in V1-V2 can obscure the convex shape; T-wave polarity must be reliably determined |
| Number of leads required | 2 | Can be diagnosed from V1-V3 alone (3 leads), but confirmation requires verifying ALL other leads are normal (full 12-lead needed). The diagnostic information is in V1-V3 but differential diagnosis needs all 12 |
| Cross-domain reasoning | 3 | Primarily morphology-based, but requires lead-group correlation (localized vs diffuse), amplitude measurement, and differential reasoning (RBBB, ARVC, early repol). Less cross-domain than conditions like Wellens (morphology + ischemia context) |
| Temporal pattern complexity | 2 | Pattern is constant when present (not evolving like STEMI). However, intermittency is a known feature — the pattern may not be present on every ECG. No beat-to-beat analysis needed for single ECG interpretation |
| Differential complexity | 5 | Many mimics: RBBB, incomplete RBBB, early repolarization, pectus excavatum, athlete heart, ARVC, hyperkalemia pseudo-Brugada, RV STEMI, posterior STEMI mirror. Distinguishing Type 1 from Type 2 is critical and subtle. This is one of the highest differential complexity conditions |
| Rarity in PTB-XL | 5 | Brugada Type 1 is rare in the general population (~0.05-0.1% prevalence) and very rare in PTB-XL. Few if any validated examples may exist in the dataset. May require external datasets (e.g., Brugada registries) for validation |
| **Overall difficulty** | **3.5** | **The core morphology is distinctive (coved ST + inverted T in V1-V3), but the differential is extensive and the rarity makes validation challenging. High signal clarity requirements and subtle morphological distinctions (coved vs saddle-back) elevate difficulty.** |

## 6A. Agent Assignment

**Architecture:** Option E — 3 Parallel Phase 1 Specialists + 1 Sequential CDS (Node 2.1 Gate Passed 2026-03-27)

| Agent | Role for Brugada Type 1 | Key Features Used |
|-------|--------------------------|-------------------|
| **RRC** (Rhythm/Rate/Conduction) | Supporting | Rhythm assessment (PVC, VT, VF initiation in Brugada context), heart rate, PR interval (first-degree AV block common in Brugada), QRS duration and right conduction delay morphology |
| **IT** (Ischemia/Territory) | Supporting | ST elevation in V1–V2 must be flagged but must NOT be classified as anterior STEMI territory; IT must suppress territorial STEMI call and emit cross_domain_hint for CDS; assesses whether ST morphology is coved (Brugada) vs convex (STEMI) |
| **MR** (Morphology/Repolarization) | Primary | Coved ST morphology in V1–V2 (≥2 mm J-point elevation with downsloping ST segment and T-wave inversion), rSr' or RSR' pattern with J-wave, Type 1 vs Type 2 (saddle-back) morphology distinction, high intercostal lead placement patterns (V1–V2 at 3rd/2nd ICS), RBBB-like terminal QRS distortion |
| **CDS** (Cross-Domain Synthesis) | Required — CRITICAL: prevents IT from classifying Brugada ST as STEMI | Receives IT's ST elevation flag + MR's coved morphology classification; overrides IT's potential anterior STEMI call; confirms Brugada Type 1 diagnosis based on coved morphology (MR) + absence of territorial reciprocal changes (IT) + RRC's conduction/arrhythmia context |

### Primary Agent
**MR** — Brugada Type 1 is a morphology diagnosis defined by the coved ST pattern (≥2 mm downsloping ST with T-wave inversion in V1–V2), which is the MR agent's primary domain of QRS morphology and repolarization patterns.

### Cross-Domain Hints
- IT emits `cross_domain_hint: "ST elevation detected in V1–V2 — morphology may represent Brugada coved pattern rather than anterior STEMI; MR coved morphology classification required before territory assignment"` when ST elevation is identified in V1–V2 without V3–V5 involvement.
- RRC emits `cross_domain_hint: "Arrhythmia (PVC/VT) detected with right conduction delay morphology — Brugada syndrome context; MR coved ST analysis should be prioritized"` when arrhythmia accompanies RBBB-like morphology.

### CDS Specific Role
CDS performs the critical cross-domain resolution that prevents false STEMI activation: it receives IT's ST elevation flag in V1–V2 and MR's coved morphology classification, then applies the override logic — if MR confirms coved pattern (downsloping ST ≥2 mm + T inversion in V1–V2) and IT finds no reciprocal changes in inferior/lateral leads, CDS suppresses the anterior STEMI call and labels the diagnosis as Brugada Type 1. CDS also integrates RRC's arrhythmia findings to assess acute decompensation risk (VF initiation).

## 9. Edge Cases and Pitfalls

- **Intermittent pattern**: The Brugada Type 1 pattern can be present on one ECG and absent on the next. A single normal ECG does NOT exclude Brugada. Serial ECGs, high intercostal leads, and provocative testing may be needed
- **Fever-induced Brugada**: Febrile patients (especially children with SCN5A mutations) may present with de novo Type 1 pattern. The ECG may normalize after defervescence. These patients still carry the genetic substrate and require follow-up. Fever-induced Type 1 carries the same arrhythmic risk as spontaneous Type 1 during the febrile period
- **Drug-induced Brugada phenocopies**: Na+ channel blockers (flecainide, propafenone, procainamide), cocaine, tricyclic antidepressants, and lithium can induce a Type 1 pattern in susceptible individuals. Some of these are true Brugada unmasked by drugs; others are "Brugada phenocopies" in patients without the genetic substrate. Distinction requires genetic testing and follow-up after drug clearance
- **Lead placement artifact**: V1 and V2 placed too high (2nd intercostal space instead of 4th) may unmask a subtle Brugada pattern — this is intentionally used as a diagnostic technique but can also occur accidentally, creating a false impression of a new finding
- **Age variation**: Brugada is rare in children, with peak presentation in the 3rd-4th decade. However, febrile convulsions in children with SCN5A mutations may be the first presentation. Elderly patients may have age-related conduction changes that mimic or mask the Brugada pattern
- **Sex variation**: 8-10:1 male predominance. The same SCN5A mutation may produce Type 1 in males but be phenotypically silent in females due to lower Ito density (estrogen effect). Female carriers may only manifest during provocation testing or extreme triggers
- **Ethnic variation**: Higher prevalence in Southeast Asian populations (Thailand, Philippines, Japan) — known as "Lai Tai" (Thailand) or "Bangungut" (Philippines), historically attributed to unexplained nocturnal sudden death in young males
- **Post-cardioversion**: After electrical cardioversion for AFib or VF, the Brugada pattern may transiently appear or become more prominent due to autonomic shifts. Do not make a new diagnosis based solely on immediate post-cardioversion ECG
- **Hyperkalemia pseudo-Brugada**: Severe hyperkalemia (K+ >7.0) can produce a coved-appearing ST elevation in V1-V3 that is virtually indistinguishable from Brugada Type 1 on ECG alone. Clinical context (renal failure, missed dialysis) and K+ level are essential. The pseudo-Brugada pattern resolves completely with K+ correction
- **Pectus excavatum**: Chest wall deformity can compress the heart against the sternum, producing V1-V3 ST-T changes. Unlike Brugada, moving the electrodes to higher intercostal spaces typically NORMALIZES the pattern (in Brugada, higher leads UNMASK or AUGMENT the pattern)
- **Concealed Brugada**: Genotype-positive patients with persistently normal ECGs (no spontaneous or fever-induced Type 1) may only be unmasked by pharmacological provocation. The agent cannot detect these — they require clinical suspicion and referral for provocative testing
