## 1. Pathophysiology Root Cause

### 1.1 Normal T-Wave Genesis
The T wave represents ventricular repolarization. Normally, repolarization proceeds in the opposite direction to depolarization (epicardium repolarizes before endocardium) despite depolarization spreading from endocardium to epicardium. This reversal is because epicardial cells have shorter action potentials. The net result is that the T-wave vector is concordant with (same direction as) the QRS vector. A T wave is therefore "normally" upright in leads with upright QRS deflections.

### 1.2 Mechanisms of T-Wave Inversion
T-wave inversion (TWI) occurs when the normal repolarization sequence is disturbed:
- **Primary TWI**: Direct abnormality of myocardial repolarization due to ischemia, inflammation, or channelopathy. The action potential duration is altered non-uniformly across myocardial layers.
- **Secondary TWI**: Consequence of abnormal depolarization (LBBB, RBBB, LVH, RVH). The repolarization sequence is secondarily disturbed because the depolarization sequence was abnormal. The T wave inverts in the same leads where the QRS is predominantly positive — the opposite of the rule for primary TWI.
- **Persistent juvenile pattern**: In children and adolescents, T-wave inversion in V1–V3 (and sometimes V4) is a normal variant reflecting the dominance of the thin RV wall in the developing heart. This pattern normally resolves by adulthood but may persist as a normal variant.

### 1.3 Clinical Significance Range
TWI spans the entire clinical spectrum from normal variant to immediately life-threatening. The critical variables are: which leads are affected (territorial = ischemic zone; diffuse = non-ischemic); depth (>5 mm significant; >10 mm = giant TWI); symmetry (symmetric = ischemia/ARVC/HCM; asymmetric = strain); acuity (new vs chronic); and associated symptoms.

## 2. ECG Presentation — Lead by Lead

### 2.1 Diagnostic Criteria (2025 AHA/ESC Guidelines)

| Pattern | Diagnostic Threshold | Leads | Clinical Association |
|---|---|---|---|
| Wellens Type A | Biphasic T (positive-then-negative) in V2–V3 | V2–V3 ± V1 | Critical proximal LAD stenosis |
| Wellens Type B | Deep symmetric T inversion ≥2 mm in V2–V3 | V2–V3 ± V4 | Critical proximal LAD stenosis |
| RV strain (chronic) | T inversion V1–V4, right precordial | V1–V4 | RVH, pulmonary hypertension, ARVC |
| RV strain (acute) | New T inversion V1–V4 + S1Q3T3 | V1–V4 | Massive pulmonary embolism |
| LV strain | Asymmetric T inversion lateral leads | I, aVL, V4–V6 | LVH, aortic stenosis, hypertensive heart |
| Juvenile pattern | T inversion V1–V3 | V1–V3 | Normal in children; may persist in adults |
| Post-STEMI | Territorial symmetric T inversion | Infarct zone | Days after STEMI onset; may persist |
| Apical HCM | Giant symmetric T inversion ≥10 mm | V2–V5 | Hypertrophic cardiomyopathy, apical variant |
| Myocarditis | Diffuse, non-territorial | Multiple | Inflammation; ST changes may precede |
| Takotsubo | Deep global T inversions, resolving | V1–V6 + II/III/aVF | After initial ST elevation; days 2–5 |
| NSTEMI | Territorial T inversion | Ischemic territory | No ST elevation; troponin positive |
| Digitalis | Downsloping lateral T, scooped | I, aVL, V4–V6 | Medication effect; not true inversion |

### 2.2 Lead-by-Lead Manifestation

| Lead | Common Causes of T-Wave Inversion |
|---|---|
| I | LV strain, LVH, LBBB (secondary), lateral ischemia, Takotsubo, myocarditis |
| II | Inferior ischemia (NSTEMI/post-STEMI), myocarditis, Takotsubo, RV strain (rare) |
| III | Inferior territory (NSTEMI/post-STEMI), normal if QRS inverted in III, PE (S1Q3T3) |
| aVR | Normally inverted; abnormally upright T in aVR = global ischemia (LMCA/LAD) |
| aVL | High lateral ischemia, LV strain, Takotsubo, LVH |
| aVF | Inferior territory ischemia, post-inferior STEMI |
| V1 | RV strain (PE, ARVC, RVH), RBBB (secondary), Brugada Type 1 |
| V2 | Wellens Type A or B (LAD stenosis), RV strain, ARVC, Brugada, posterior STEMI (V1–V3 reciprocal) |
| V3 | Wellens Type A or B, RV strain, ARVC, LV strain (transition) |
| V4 | Wellens extension, apical HCM, Takotsubo, global ischemia |
| V5 | Lateral ischemia, LV strain (LVH/AS), LBBB (secondary), apical HCM |
| V6 | Lateral ischemia, LV strain, LBBB (secondary), Takotsubo |

### 2.3 Key Leads
- **V2–V3 isolated**: Wellens pattern (LAD stenosis) — the most time-critical TWI pattern in emergency medicine
- **V1–V4 right precordial**: RV strain (chronic = cor pulmonale/ARVC; acute = PE)
- **I/aVL/V4–V6 lateral**: LV strain (LVH), lateral ischemia/NSTEMI, Takotsubo
- **II/III/aVF inferior**: Inferior NSTEMI, post-inferior STEMI, Takotsubo
- **V2–V5 diffuse precordial**: Apical HCM (giant, symmetric), Takotsubo, global ischemia

### 2.4 Beat-by-Beat Considerations
- TWI is a static finding per ECG; dynamic changes (appearance or deepening) are clinically significant
- Post-STEMI T inversion: begins as ST resolves (typically 24–72 hours post-reperfusion); deepens over days; may persist for months — a normal healing pattern unless new
- Wellens syndrome: TWI may be present BETWEEN anginal episodes when the patient is pain-free. Stress testing is contraindicated (can precipitate VF/STEMI).
- Rate: TWI does not change significantly with rate (unlike ST elevation which can be rate-dependent in Brugada/ER)

## 3. Morphology Details

### 3.1 Wellens Type A (Biphasic)
Biphasic T wave in V2–V3: positive initial deflection followed by negative terminal deflection. The peak of the positive portion is small; the negative portion descends steeply. This represents heterogeneous repolarization across the anterior wall during LAD ischemia recovery. Seen when the patient is chest-pain free (between anginal episodes). The LAD stenosis is typically proximal (Type A and B both indicate proximal LAD lesion).

### 3.2 Wellens Type B (Deep Symmetric)
Deep, symmetric T-wave inversion in V2–V3 (may extend to V4). Both limbs of the T wave are symmetric. Depth commonly 5–10 mm. Represents a more advanced ischemic pattern than Type A. Clinically identical significance — both indicate critical proximal LAD stenosis requiring urgent catheterization.

### 3.3 Giant T-Wave Inversion (Apical HCM)
Symmetric T-wave inversions ≥10 mm in the precordial leads, typically V2–V5. The inversion is deep and symmetric with a characteristic morphology. Seen in apical variant of HCM (Yamaguchi syndrome, more common in Japanese patients). The QRS may show prominent voltage (precordial) but unlike concentric HCM does not always meet LVH criteria by limb leads.

### 3.4 LV Strain Asymmetric T Inversion
The T wave in LV strain is asymmetric: slow downsloping initial limb, rapid upstroke terminal limb (opposite of ischemic T inversion where both limbs are steep). Often accompanied by ST depression with a downsloping (concave-down) shape. The asymmetry distinguishes strain from primary ischemic T inversion.

### 3.5 Juvenile Pattern
Flat or inverted T waves in V1–V3 (occasionally V4) that are a normal developmental finding in pediatric patients. In adults, V1 T inversion is always normal. V2–V3 inversion in a young adult female may be normal juvenile variant. Context: asymptomatic, no other abnormalities.

### 3.6 Post-Ischemic T Inversion
Symmetric, territorial. After STEMI reperfusion, the ischemic zone shows symmetric T inversions (Pardee T waves) that follow the territory of the occluded artery. This is a normal healing sign — "ischemic memory." If new ST elevation appears in the same territory months later, consider aneurysm or re-infarction.

### 3.7 Digitalis Scooped Pattern
Not true T-wave inversion in most cases. The ST-T complex develops a characteristic "scooped" or "reverse-check-mark" appearance: downsloping ST followed by flat or slightly inverted T with a prolonged ST-T duration. Seen in lateral leads. Digitalis effect is a normal finding in patients on therapeutic digoxin; toxicity presents with arrhythmias, not just ST-T changes.

## 4. Differential Diagnosis

### 4.1 Mimics

| Pattern | Key Differentiator |
|---|---|
| Wellens vs NSTEMI anterior | Wellens: T inversion while pain-free, no troponin elevation at ECG time (may be normal), critical significance; NSTEMI: troponin positive |
| RV strain (PE) vs anterior NSTEMI | PE: S1Q3T3, sinus tachycardia, right axis shift, V1–V4 TWI without V5–V6 involvement; NSTEMI: LAD territory V1–V5 |
| LV strain vs lateral NSTEMI | LV strain: asymmetric T inversion with LVH voltage; NSTEMI: symmetric, troponin positive |
| Juvenile pattern vs RV strain | Juvenile: asymptomatic, young age, V1–V3 only, no right axis deviation; RV strain: V1–V4, may have right axis, clinical context |
| Takotsubo vs anterior STEMI | Takotsubo: deep global T inversion appears AFTER initial ST elevation resolves; diffuse not territorial; LV apical ballooning on echo; stress trigger |
| Apical HCM vs Wellens | HCM: ≥10 mm symmetric V2–V5 without troponin elevation; Wellens: typically V2–V3, prior anginal symptoms |

### 4.2 Coexisting Conditions
- LBBB + ischemia: TWI in LBBB always secondary (expected in lateral leads); detecting primary ischemic TWI in LBBB requires Sgarbossa criteria
- LVH + ischemia: strain pattern makes ischemic TWI difficult to detect; new deepening of T inversion beyond baseline strain is suspicious
- PE + RV infarction: rare; right coronary artery supplies RV; RV infarction (inferior STEMI equivalent) may coexist with massive PE

## 5. STAT Classification

| Pattern | STAT Level | Action |
|---|---|---|
| Wellens Type A or B (new, confirmed) | STAT — Critical | Cardiology consult, avoid stress test, urgent cath consideration |
| New deep TWI V1–V4 + dyspnea + tachycardia | STAT — Urgent | Rule out PE: D-dimer, CTA if indicated |
| New territorial TWI + troponin rise (NSTEMI) | STAT — Urgent | ACS pathway, anticoagulation, cardiology |
| Giant TWI ≥10 mm V2–V5 (new finding) | Urgent | Cardiology consult; echo for HCM vs Takotsubo |
| Deep global T inversions post-ST elevation (Takotsubo) | Urgent | Monitoring, echo, exclude ACS |
| Lateral TWI with LVH voltage (chronic) | Non-urgent | Document, outpatient follow-up |
| V1–V3 TWI, young female, asymptomatic | Normal variant | No action; document juvenile pattern |
| New TWI any territory + acute symptoms | STAT — Urgent | ACS/PE workup until excluded |

## 6. Reasoning Complexity Analysis

### 6.1 Reasoning Domains Required
- Lead-by-lead TWI pattern recognition and distribution mapping
- Morphology classification: symmetric vs asymmetric; depth; biphasic (Wellens) recognition
- Temporal context: new vs chronic (requires prior ECG comparison)
- Clinical integration: symptoms, troponin, echocardiography findings
- Multi-pattern detection: multiple causes of TWI may coexist (e.g., PE + tachycardia + RV strain)
- Secondary vs primary TWI distinction (LBBB/RVH secondary changes)

### 6.2 Feature Dependencies
- Wellens pattern requires that patient was recently pain-free; T inversion during active chest pain may represent a different ischemic phase
- LV strain asymmetry requires accurate waveform measurement (automatic algorithms often misclassify asymmetric strain as normal)
- Giant TWI (HCM) depth measurement requires accurate baseline identification; interference from baseline wander can over/underestimate
- Prior ECG comparison is essential for all TWI interpretations — "new" TWI carries dramatically different significance from chronic

### 6.3 Cross-Condition Interactions
- Takotsubo + Wellens: Both produce deep precordial T inversions. Takotsubo follows ST elevation; Wellens follows chest pain without STE. Echo differentiates: Takotsubo has apical ballooning; LAD stenosis-induced Wellens has normal wall motion during pain-free period.
- PE + RBBB: New RBBB in PE may produce secondary T inversions V1–V3 that mask primary RV strain pattern; both signify severe RV compromise
- Apical HCM + AF: HCM frequently develops AF; AF rate-controls T-wave appearance; assess ST-T in regular rhythm segments

### 6.4 Reasoning Chain Sketch
1. Detect T-wave inversion in each lead; record depth and morphology
2. Map distribution: isolated (V2–V3) / right precordial (V1–V4) / lateral (I/aVL/V4–V6) / inferior (II/III/aVF) / global
3. Classify morphology: symmetric vs asymmetric; biphasic; depth ≥5 mm, ≥10 mm
4. Check for secondary TWI markers: wide QRS (LBBB/RBBB), LVH voltage, RVH axis
5. Compare to prior ECG if available: new vs chronic
6. Apply distribution-based differential: Wellens (V2–V3) / RV strain (V1–V4) / LV strain (lateral) / ischemia (territorial) / global
7. Integrate clinical data: symptoms, troponin, echo, drugs
8. Assign diagnosis confidence and STAT level

### 6.5 Confidence Anchors
- Biphasic V2–V3 in pain-free patient with prior angina: high confidence Wellens
- Symmetric V2–V5 ≥10 mm: high confidence giant TWI; HCM vs Takotsubo requires echo
- Asymmetric lateral TWI with LVH voltage: high confidence LV strain
- New V1–V4 TWI + sinus tachycardia + dyspnea: high confidence RV strain; PE probable
- Diffuse TWI following ST elevation recovery: high confidence post-Takotsubo or post-reperfusion

### 6.6 Difficulty Score

| Dimension | Score (1–5) | Rationale |
|---|---|---|
| Morphology Recognition | 4 | Symmetric vs asymmetric; biphasic Wellens; giant TWI; all distinct |
| Interval Measurement | 2 | Depth measurement; no complex intervals |
| Differential Diagnosis | 5 | Longest DDx of any ECG finding; >10 causes with different urgency levels |
| Clinical Integration | 4 | Troponin, echo, symptoms, drugs all required for final diagnosis |

## 9. Edge Cases and Pitfalls

1. **Wellens in pain-free patient**: The clinical danger is that the patient presents to ER pain-free, ECG shows non-specific TWI in V2–V3, and patient is cleared. These patients have critical LAD stenosis and can infarct massively within hours to days of the Wellens ECG. Any V2–V3 biphasic or symmetric deep TWI in a patient with recent chest pain = Wellens until proven otherwise.
2. **Stress testing contraindicated in Wellens**: The critical pitfall — ordering an exercise stress test in a patient with Wellens pattern can trigger massive anterior STEMI. The ECG pattern indicates viable-but-ischemic myocardium poised for infarction.
3. **LV strain masking ischemia**: In hypertensive patients with LVH and chronic LV strain, new lateral TWI deepening may be missed because the baseline already shows strain. Comparison to prior ECG for new changes is essential.
4. **Juvenile pattern in adults**: T inversion in V1–V3 in a young asymptomatic adult female is a common normal variant (persistent juvenile pattern). This should not be labeled as ischemia or RV strain. Age, sex, and symptom context are essential.
5. **Takotsubo misdiagnosis window**: On day 1, Takotsubo may look like anterior STEMI (ST elevation). On days 2–5, the deep global T inversions appear. This post-STE TWI phase is often incorrectly attributed to post-STEMI reperfusion pattern when the actual diagnosis is Takotsubo. Echo apical ballooning is the differentiator.
6. **LBBB masking primary TWI**: In LBBB, T waves are expected to invert in lateral leads (secondary change). New ischemic TWI cannot be assessed by standard morphology in LBBB — use modified Sgarbossa criteria for ischemia detection.
7. **Depth underestimation with baseline wander**: Automated algorithms are susceptible to baseline wander artifactually lifting or depressing the baseline, causing under- or overestimation of TWI depth. Filtering and baseline correction required before automated measurement.
