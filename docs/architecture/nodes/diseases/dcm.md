## 1. Pathophysiology Root Cause

### 1.1 What Goes Wrong (Anatomy/Physiology)
Dilated cardiomyopathy (DCM) is characterized by LV (and often biventricular) dilation with impaired systolic function in the absence of abnormal loading conditions (i.e., not from hypertension or valvular disease alone). Causes include:
- **Idiopathic / genetic**: ~30–35% of cases; mutations in TTN (titin, most common), LMNA, SCN5A, MYH7, TPM1, and others
- **Ischemic cardiomyopathy**: coronary artery disease → myocardial infarction → remodeling and dilation; the most common cause overall (~65%)
- **Alcohol-related cardiomyopathy**: direct myocardial toxicity
- **Viral myocarditis**: post-infectious (coxsackievirus, parvovirus B19, COVID-19, HIV)
- **Peripartum cardiomyopathy**: occurs in final month of pregnancy or first 5 months post-partum
- **Tachycardia-mediated cardiomyopathy**: prolonged uncontrolled tachycardia (AF with rapid ventricular response)
- **Chemotherapy-induced**: anthracyclines, cyclophosphamide, trastuzumab
- **Infiltrative diseases** (amyloid, sarcoid): may present with DCM-like pattern

The LV becomes dilated (LVEDV markedly increased), wall motion becomes globally reduced, ejection fraction falls below 50% (often <35% in symptomatic patients). The dilated chamber has thin walls relative to its size, decreased contractility, and increased wall stress.

### 1.2 Electrical Consequence
The dilated, poorly contractile LV creates several electrical changes:
- **Chamber dilation**: increased electrode-to-myocardium distance → attenuates QRS voltage (paradoxically, despite large mass, voltage may be normal or reduced)
- **Fibrosis and scar**: patchy fibrosis throughout the LV myocardium → creates electrically silent regions → Q waves in non-coronary territory distributions
- **Conduction disease**: LMNA mutations and other genetic forms frequently cause progressive conduction system disease; the dilated chamber stretches and disrupts the His-Purkinje system → LBBB
- **LBBB** (most characteristic finding): the left bundle branch fails first as the LV dilates; this creates abnormal, slow LV activation entirely through the right bundle → abnormal QRS morphology
- **Abnormal depolarization from dilation**: the enlarged, rounded LV chamber changes the geometry of depolarization → poor R-wave progression in precordial leads
- **Sympathetic activation**: compensatory tachycardia → sinus tachycardia; may also trigger PVCs and NSVT

### 1.3 Why It Appears on ECG
LBBB from DCM reflects failure of the left bundle branch to rapidly activate the dilated LV. The RV depolarizes first (from the intact right bundle), then the LV depolarizes slowly, cell-by-cell through gap junctions, from right to left. This creates:
- Broad, notched QRS in lateral leads (I, aVL, V5-V6): slow LV activation
- Absence of septal Q waves in V5-V6: the normal left-to-right septal depolarization is lost; both ventricles now depolarize right-to-left
- Poor R-wave progression V1-V4: the LV activation is delayed and abnormal; early precordial leads see slow, rightward depolarization before the LV forces arrive

## 2. ECG Presentation — Lead by Lead

### 2.1 Diagnostic Criteria (2025 AHA/ESC Guidelines)

| Criterion | Threshold | Source |
|---|---|---|
| LBBB | QRS ≥120 ms; broad monophasic R in I/V5-V6; absent septal Q in V5-V6; QS or rS in V1 | AHA/ESC 2013 LBBB criteria |
| Poor R-wave progression | R wave in V3 ≤3 mm; or R wave fails to increase from V1 to V4 | AHA 2009 ECG Standardization |
| Pathological Q waves | Q wave ≥40 ms in 2 contiguous leads; OR Q ≥25% of R-wave amplitude; in non-coronary distribution | Goldberger's; Chou's |
| Sinus tachycardia | HR >100 bpm; compensatory in low-output state | Clinical context |
| LVH criteria | Sokolow-Lyon ≥35 mm; Cornell ≥28/20 mm — may or may not be present | AHA 2009 |
| Low QRS voltage | QRS amplitude <5 mm in all limb leads AND <10 mm in all precordial leads | Goldberger's |
| Atrial fibrillation | Irregularly irregular rhythm; absent P waves | AHA/ESC AF Guidelines 2023 |

### 2.2 Lead-by-Lead Manifestation

| Lead | Expected Finding | Why | Sensitivity |
|---|---|---|---|
| I | Broad notched R (LBBB); or normal; or Q wave | LBBB: slow LV lateral activation | High (if LBBB) |
| II | Normal or T inversion; non-specific ST-T changes | Non-specific in DCM | Low-Moderate |
| III | Q wave possible; non-specific changes | Fibrosis in inferior LV wall (in ischemic DCM) | Moderate |
| aVR | Broad QS or rS (in LBBB); or QR | Reciprocal to LBBB lateral forces | Moderate |
| aVL | Broad R (LBBB); or Q; T inversion | LBBB lateral forces; or LV strain | Moderate |
| aVF | Non-specific; T inversion possible | Inferior LV changes from ischemic or fibrotic DCM | Low-Moderate |
| V1 | QS or rS (LBBB); or poor R-wave progression | In LBBB: V1 has no septal R; in non-LBBB DCM: anterior forces reduced from dilation | High (if LBBB) |
| V2 | QS or small r; poor R progression | Anterior force loss | High |
| V3 | Small r or QS; poor R progression | Transition zone — anterior force loss | High |
| V4 | Transition; R may begin to emerge here; Q possible | Anterior forces partially restored as LV lateral depolarization arrives | Moderate |
| V5 | Broad R (LBBB); or tall R (LVH); absent septal Q | LBBB: monophasic broad R with no septal Q | High (if LBBB) |
| V6 | Broad monophasic R (LBBB); absent septal Q | Same LBBB pattern; septal Q absent | High (if LBBB) |

### 2.3 Key Leads (Most Diagnostic)
- **V1, V2, V3**: Poor R-wave progression or QS (in LBBB or from anterior force loss)
- **I, V5-V6**: LBBB pattern (broad R, absent septal Q) — the most specific DCM-associated conduction finding
- **II, III, aVF**: Pathological Q waves in ischemic DCM (inferior MI-related)

### 2.4 Beat-by-Beat Considerations
- **PVCs**: common in DCM, especially with EF <35%; frequent PVCs worsen outcome
- **NSVT**: runs of ≥3 VT beats at ≥120 bpm; marker of sudden death risk; triggers ICD evaluation in EF <35%
- **AF**: occurs in ~25% of DCM patients; irregularly irregular rhythm; may be the cause (tachycardia-mediated) or consequence of DCM
- **Complete heart block**: particularly in LMNA-related genetic DCM; can cause syncope or sudden death

## 3. Morphology Details

### 3.1 P-wave Changes
- **LAE**: broad, notched P ≥120 ms; deep negative terminal V1 component — from elevated LA filling pressure
- In AF: P waves absent; irregularly irregular RR intervals
- Sinus tachycardia: P waves present, rate >100 bpm; short PR
- P-pulmonale: if pulmonary hypertension develops in late DCM

### 3.2 PR Interval Changes
- First-degree AV block (PR >200 ms): common in DCM, especially genetic forms (LMNA)
- Complete AV block: LMNA-related DCM has high rate of progressive AV block → sudden death; pacemaker indicated
- Short PR: not typical; if present, consider infiltrative disease or storage disease

### 3.3 QRS Complex Changes
- **LBBB** (QRS ≥120 ms): the single most characteristic finding in DCM
  - QS or rS in V1; broad monophasic R in I, V5-V6; no septal Q in V5-V6
  - LV activation delay: left lateral activation occurs entirely through slow myocardial conduction
- **LBBB as a progression marker**: development of new LBBB in a patient with known DCM indicates worsening conduction disease
- **Poor R-wave progression** (without frank LBBB): R in V3 ≤3 mm; suggests anterior LV wall fibrosis or loss of anterior forces from dilation
- **Pathological Q waves**: non-ischemic distribution (not following a single coronary territory); from patchy fibrosis
- **Low voltage**: QRS amplitude <5 mm limb leads; from pericardial effusion (which may accompany DCM) or severe myocardial replacement by fat/fibrosis
- **Notched QRS (fragmented QRS)**: multiple notches within the QRS complex; suggests myocardial fibrosis/scar

### 3.4 ST Segment Changes
- **LBBB secondary ST-T changes**: ST depression in V5-V6 (leads with broad R); ST elevation in V1-V3 (leads with QS) — these are NORMAL secondary changes of LBBB, not ischemia
- **Concordant ST changes in LBBB**: ST elevation ≥1 mm in leads with positive QRS (I, V5-V6) = Sgarbossa criterion — strongly suggests superimposed acute ischemia
- Non-specific ST depression: common in DCM from LV strain or ischemia
- **Pericardial effusion** (if present): diffuse ST elevation (pericarditis pattern) + low voltage

### 3.5 T-wave Changes
- Non-specific T-wave flattening or inversion: diffuse, non-territorial distribution
- Deep T inversions in lateral leads if LV strain pattern
- In LBBB: secondary T-wave inversions in V5-V6 (leads with broad R) are expected — not independently ischemic

### 3.6 QT/QTc Changes
- QTc frequently prolonged (>440 ms) in DCM
- LBBB itself causes apparent QT prolongation from wide QRS — use corrected measures
- QTc >500 ms: high risk of torsades de pointes, especially if antiarrhythmic drugs co-administered
- QTc prolongation predicts worse outcomes in DCM

### 3.7 Other Features
- **Fragmented QRS**: RSR' or notched QRS in any lead without frank BBB — marker of myocardial fibrosis; associated with higher SCD risk in DCM
- **Epsilon-wave-like changes**: rarely, fibrotic DCM can mimic ARVC with late potentials
- **Ventricular tachycardia**: LBBB morphology (RV origin) or RBBB morphology (LV origin); treat as SCD risk

## 4. Differential Diagnosis

### 4.1 Mimics

| Mimic Condition | Shared Features | Distinguishing Features |
|---|---|---|
| Ischemic cardiomyopathy | LBBB; Q waves; poor R progression; LV dysfunction | Ischemic: Q waves follow coronary territory; history of MI/CAD; coronary angiography differentiates |
| Myocarditis | Dilated LV, LBBB, ST changes, T inversions, AF | Myocarditis: acute onset; elevated troponin; viral prodrome; cardiac MRI (LGE in mid-wall or epicardial pattern) |
| HCM | High voltage; Q waves; T inversions | HCM: thick walls (not dilated-thin); septal Q narrow; apical T inversions; septal/PW ratio >1.3 on echo |
| Cardiac amyloidosis | Low voltage; Q waves; heart failure | Amyloid: low voltage + thick walls on echo (pseudohypertrophy) — opposite of DCM (low voltage + thin walls); granular sparkling on echo; serum protein electrophoresis |
| Sarcoidosis | AV block; LBBB; Q waves; VT | Sarcoid: patchy fibrosis; mosaic pattern on cardiac MRI; systemic sarcoid features |
| ARVC | LBBB-morphology VT; RV dilation | ARVC: epsilon wave; T inversions V1-V3; RV origin VT; RV dilation dominates; fibrofatty replacement on MRI |
| Cardiac tamponade | Low voltage; sinus tachycardia | Tamponade: electrical alternans; Beck's triad; pericardial effusion on echo |

### 4.2 Coexisting Conditions
- **Ventricular arrhythmias**: PVCs, NSVT, VT/VF — directly related to fibrotic substrate
- **AF**: ~25%; either cause or consequence of DCM
- **Progressive AV block**: especially LMNA mutations → complete heart block
- **Heart failure**: both acute decompensated and chronic heart failure; volume overload → bilateral pleural effusions, pulmonary edema

## 5. STAT Classification

| Priority | Criteria |
|---|---|
| STAT (Immediate) | DCM + hemodynamic instability (cardiogenic shock); LBBB + hypotension + acute heart failure — must exclude acute STEMI with LBBB (Sgarbossa criteria) |
| STAT (Immediate) | DCM + sustained VT or complete heart block — LMNA-related DCM is high risk |
| Urgent | New LBBB in patient with dyspnea and no prior cardiac history — needs urgent echo to exclude DCM |
| Urgent | DCM + new AF with rapid ventricular response — rate control and anticoagulation |
| Routine | Known DCM on stable follow-up; no new symptoms or rhythm changes |

## 6. Reasoning Complexity Analysis (Feeds Into Node 2.1 — Agent Architecture Research)
> NOTE: Does NOT pre-assign agents. Documents reasoning complexity for Node 2.1.

### 6.1 Reasoning Domains Required
- LBBB detection and characterization (QRS ≥120 ms, morphology criteria)
- R-wave progression analysis across precordial leads (V1-V6 amplitude trend)
- Q-wave distribution mapping: ischemic territory vs. non-territorial (fibrotic DCM)
- Voltage analysis: LVH vs. low voltage
- Rhythm analysis: sinus tachycardia, AF detection, PVC/NSVT detection
- Ischemic mimicry detection: Sgarbossa criteria in LBBB context
- Context-based differential: ischemic vs. non-ischemic DCM — ECG cannot reliably distinguish

### 6.2 Feature Dependencies
- LBBB presence triggers separate interpretation logic (secondary ST-T changes expected; voltage criteria partially invalidated)
- Sgarbossa criteria require LBBB to be confirmed first, then concordant ST analysis
- Q-wave distribution analysis requires coronary territory mapping (inferior, lateral, anterior) to distinguish ischemic vs. non-ischemic
- Fragmented QRS requires high-resolution signal analysis at the notch level

### 6.3 Cross-Condition Interactions
- **STEMI with LBBB**: the most dangerous overlap — DCM with LBBB can mask STEMI; Sgarbossa must be applied
- **HCM vs. DCM**: both cause high-voltage ECGs with Q waves but opposite structural phenotypes (thick vs. thin walls)
- **ARVC vs. DCM**: ARVC can cause LBBB-morphology VT; ARVC involves RV; DCM involves LV
- **Myocarditis**: acute cause of DCM-like ECG; cardiac MRI differentiates

### 6.4 Reasoning Chain Sketch
1. Detect rhythm — sinus tachycardia? AF? PVCs? NSVT?
2. Measure QRS duration — if ≥120 ms, characterize morphology → confirm LBBB criteria
3. If LBBB confirmed: apply Sgarbossa criteria (concordant ST elevation ≥1 mm in any lead = STEMI equivalent)
4. Assess R-wave progression V1-V6 — flag if poor (R in V3 ≤3 mm)
5. Analyze Q waves — measure width (≥40 ms?), distribution (coronary territory? non-territorial?)
6. Measure QRS voltage — LVH or low voltage?
7. Assess P waves — LAE? P-pulmonale?
8. Measure QTc
9. Synthesize: LBBB + poor R progression + non-territorial Q waves + LAE = DCM pattern
10. Flag: cannot distinguish ischemic from non-ischemic DCM from ECG alone
11. Assign confidence, STAT flag

### 6.5 Confidence Anchors
- High confidence for DCM pattern: LBBB + poor R-wave progression + LAE + non-territorial Q waves
- Moderate confidence: LBBB alone in new presentation — needs echo to confirm LV dilation
- Cannot confirm: ischemic vs. non-ischemic DCM — state explicitly as limitation
- Uncertainty triggers: concurrent LBBB (invalidates many voltage and Q criteria); acute STEMI mimicry must be excluded

## 6A. Agent Assignment

**Architecture:** Option E — 3 Parallel Phase 1 Specialists + 1 Sequential CDS (Node 2.1 Gate Passed 2026-03-27)

| Agent | Role for Dilated Cardiomyopathy | Key Features Used |
|-------|--------------------------|-------------------|
| **RRC** (Rhythm/Rate/Conduction) | Supporting | Sinus tachycardia (common in DCM due to sympathetic activation); LBBB or IVCD pattern (QRS ≥120ms — present in ~25% of DCM patients); arrhythmias including VT, AFib; left axis deviation |
| **IT** (Ischemia/Territory) | Supporting — must NOT independently classify DCM fibrosis Q-waves as acute MI | Detects Q-waves (from myocardial fibrosis mimicking old MI) and ST-T changes; emits cross_domain_hint to CDS; does not classify Q-waves as ischemic without CDS adjudication |
| **MR** (Morphology/Repolarization) | Primary | Poor R-wave progression (loss of normal R-wave amplitude increase from V1-V6); low voltage overall (QRS <5mm in limb leads, <10mm in precordial leads — may indicate severe DCM); or tall voltage with LBBB pattern (QRS broad R in I/V5-V6, QS in V1-V3); non-specific ST-T abnormalities diffusely; LBBB morphology when present |
| **CDS** (Cross-Domain Synthesis) | Required — integrates LBBB + ischemia (Sgarbossa); Q-wave fibrosis pattern determination | If LBBB detected by RRC + MR: applies full Sgarbossa criteria for ischemia assessment; integrates IT Q-wave detections: fibrosis Q-waves in DCM are typically non-territorial, diffuse, and associated with poor R-wave progression (not focal coronary territory) — CDS differentiates from ischemic Q-waves |

### Primary Agent
**MR** — DCM's primary ECG manifestations are morphology features: poor R-wave progression (gradual loss of precordial R-wave amplitude), LBBB pattern (when present), and diffuse ST-T abnormalities; these are all MR domain features.

### Cross-Domain Hints
- RRC emits (if QRS ≥120ms): `cross_domain_hint: "QRS ≥120ms in DCM context — MR must confirm LBBB or IVCD morphology; if LBBB confirmed, IT must suspend standard ischemia criteria and CDS must apply Sgarbossa"`
- IT emits: `cross_domain_hint: "Q-waves detected in DCM context — CDS must determine if Q-wave pattern is consistent with fibrosis (non-territorial, diffuse, with poor R-wave progression) vs focal ischemic MI (territorial, with ST-T changes in corresponding leads)"`

### CDS Specific Role
CDS serves two functions in DCM. First, if RRC detects QRS ≥120ms and MR confirms LBBB morphology, CDS activates Sgarbossa ischemia assessment (concordant STE ≥1mm, concordant STD ≥1mm in V1-V3, Smith-modified discordant STE ≥25% of S-wave depth) — identical to the standalone LBBB protocol. Second, CDS adjudicates Q-waves: IT may detect Q-waves across multiple territories; CDS cross-references the Q-wave distribution pattern with MR poor R-wave progression: if Q-waves are diffuse (not confined to a single coronary territory) and co-occur with poor R-wave progression and LBBB/IVCD morphology, CDS classifies these as fibrosis Q-waves consistent with DCM, not acute or old MI. Focal, territorial Q-waves in a specific coronary distribution retain ischemic classification.

### 8.3 Clinician Explanation (Plain Language)

**ER Nurse version:**
"The left side of the heart is enlarged and weak, which is causing the electrical signal to travel slowly through the left ventricle — you can see this as a wide complex on the tracing (LBBB). This is called dilated cardiomyopathy and means the heart muscle is not pumping well. The patient may need an urgent echocardiogram and heart failure treatment."

**Cardiologist version:**
"DCM ECG pattern: LBBB (QRS ≥120 ms) with poor R-wave progression V1-V3 and LAE. Non-territorial Q waves suggest patchy fibrosis rather than coronary distribution infarct. If acute presentation: apply Sgarbossa criteria — concordant ST elevation ≥1 mm or ST/S ratio ≤–0.25 must be excluded to rule out STEMI-equivalent. ECG cannot distinguish ischemic from non-ischemic DCM — echocardiography and coronary evaluation required."

## 9. Edge Cases and Pitfalls

- **LBBB masks STEMI**: New LBBB in acute chest pain = presumptive STEMI until proven otherwise; always apply Sgarbossa criteria; do not delay catheterization lab activation based on ECG alone
- **Secondary ST-T changes of LBBB are expected**: ST depression V5-V6 and ST elevation V1-V2 are normal in LBBB — do not diagnose ischemia from these alone
- **Tachycardia-mediated cardiomyopathy**: if the cause is AF with rapid ventricular response, controlling rate may fully reverse the cardiomyopathy — reversible DCM; rate control is the treatment
- **LMNA-related DCM**: high risk of complete AV block and SCD; always check for progressive PR prolongation, fascicular blocks, complete AV block in DCM
- **Low voltage in DCM**: may indicate pericardial effusion — clinical exam and echo urgently needed; electrical alternans suggests tamponade
- **Fragmented QRS**: multiple notches in QRS without classic BBB; marker of fibrosis; often overlooked; associated with increased SCD risk
- **DCM cannot be diagnosed from ECG alone**: ECG provides pattern recognition (LBBB, poor R progression); confirmatory echo is mandatory
- **Peripartum DCM**: may present with new LBBB + symptoms in pregnant/post-partum woman — high-risk window; do not delay evaluation
