## 1. Pathophysiology Root Cause

### 1.1 What Goes Wrong
- The myocardium (muscular wall of the heart) becomes inflamed — distinct from pericarditis, which inflames only the surrounding sac. In myocarditis, the cardiomyocytes themselves are damaged by direct viral infection, immune-mediated injury, or both
- Most common causes: viral (Coxsackie B3 and B4, parvovirus B19, adenovirus, Epstein-Barr virus, influenza, COVID-19 SARS-CoV-2), autoimmune (giant cell myocarditis, sarcoidosis, systemic lupus erythematosus), bacterial (Borrelia burgdorferi/Lyme, Chagas disease — Trypanosoma cruzi), drug-mediated (clozapine, anthracyclines, immune checkpoint inhibitors), and giant cell myocarditis (most aggressive, often fatal without transplant)
- COVID-19 myocarditis deserves special mention: can occur after acute infection AND after mRNA vaccination (very rare, predominantly young males, typically mild and self-limiting). ECG in COVID-19 myocarditis often shows dramatic, global T-wave inversions unlike classical viral myocarditis
- Pathologically: cardiomyocyte necrosis, interstitial edema, lymphocytic infiltrate. Necrosis differentiates myocarditis from pericarditis (pericarditis has NO necrosis — hence no troponin elevation in pure pericarditis)
- The distribution of injury in myocarditis is patchy and unpredictable — it can affect any region of the LV, RV, septum, or conduction system in a non-territorial pattern. This is the key reason the ECG is non-specific and highly variable

### 1.2 Electrical Consequence
- Cardiomyocyte necrosis and edema disrupt the normal action potential and intercellular coupling. Injured cells depolarize abnormally (prolonged action potential, altered resting membrane potential), producing regional injury currents analogous to — but not identical to — MI
- When inflammation involves the conduction system (AV node, His bundle, bundle branches), conduction block can result: first-, second-, or third-degree AV block depending on severity and location
- Diffuse myocardial inflammation creates a repolarization abnormality across large areas of ventricle — manifesting as widespread ST-T changes
- Severe acute myocarditis can trigger ventricular arrhythmias (PVCs, VT, VF) by creating heterogeneous repolarization, triggered activity from injured cells, and re-entry substrates
- When concurrent pericarditis is present (myopericarditis), the atrial epicardial injury current also causes PR depression — adding the pericarditis ECG signature on top of the myocarditis changes

### 1.3 Why It Appears on ECG
- Patchy myocardial necrosis creates regional injury currents visible as ST elevation or depression (depending on which layer — epicardial vs endocardial — is more severely affected)
- Unlike STEMI (territorial, one vascular supply) or pericarditis (diffuse, uniformly epicardial), myocarditis creates a PATCHY, NON-TERRITORIAL pattern that does not follow vascular anatomy
- Conduction system involvement produces PR prolongation, AV block patterns, and bundle branch blocks
- The ECG can be normal in mild myocarditis (focal injury too small to generate a visible surface potential change), or it can mimic virtually any cardiac condition — STEMI, pericarditis, DCM, WPW, or electrical instability
- Sinus tachycardia is the most common and often the only ECG finding — a reflection of systemic inflammation, fever, and sympathetic activation

## 2. ECG Presentation — Lead by Lead

### 2.1 Diagnostic Criteria (2025 AHA/ESC Guidelines)

| Criterion | Threshold | Notes |
|-----------|-----------|-------|
| **Sinus tachycardia** | HR >100 bpm, most common finding | Reflects inflammation, fever, reduced cardiac output; present in most cases |
| **Diffuse ST elevation** | ≥1 mm in ≥2 contiguous leads (when present) | Resembles pericarditis; may be patchy rather than truly diffuse |
| **T-wave inversions** | Inverted T-waves, may be focal or widespread | Global T-wave inversions suggest severe/fulminant myocarditis; giant T-wave inversions in COVID-19 myocarditis |
| **PR depression** | Present when concurrent pericarditis (myopericarditis) | Same mechanism as pericarditis; atrial epicardial inflammation |
| **New AV block** | First-, second-, or third-degree AV block | New onset, especially in young patient with fever — myocarditis until proven otherwise |
| **Bundle branch block** | New LBBB, RBBB, or fascicular block | Conduction system inflammation |
| **PVCs / VT** | Frequent PVCs, non-sustained or sustained VT | Irritable myocardium; ominous sign in fulminant myocarditis |
| **Low voltage** | QRS <5 mm limb leads / <10 mm precordial leads | Suggests associated pericardial effusion |

**ESC 2013 / AHA 2020 Position Statement**: There is NO specific ECG criterion for myocarditis. ECG is required as part of the work-up but cardiac MRI (Lake Louise Criteria 2018) is the diagnostic gold standard.

### 2.2 Lead-by-Lead Manifestation

| Lead | Expected Finding | Why | Sensitivity |
|------|-----------------|-----|-------------|
| I | Variable: ST elevation, flat or inverted T-wave, or normal | Lateral myocardium involvement — patchy | Low |
| II | ST elevation and/or T-wave inversion; PR depression if myopericarditis | Inferior-lateral projection; best for PR assessment | Moderate |
| III | ST elevation and/or T-wave inversion; may be patchy | Inferior myocardium | Low |
| aVR | ST depression if concurrent pericarditis; otherwise variable | Reciprocal to diffuse inferior-lateral changes | Moderate for myopericarditis |
| aVL | Variable: ST changes, T-wave inversion, or normal | High lateral wall; patchy involvement | Low |
| aVF | ST changes or T-wave inversions | Inferior myocardium | Low |
| V1 | May show new RBBB, PR prolongation, or be normal | Conduction system involvement and RV pathology visible here | Moderate for conduction changes |
| V2 | ST elevation or T-wave inversion | Anterior septal wall; prominent in fulminant myocarditis | Moderate |
| V3 | ST elevation or deep T-wave inversions | Anterior wall; common region of involvement | Moderate |
| V4 | ST elevation (mimicking anterior STEMI) or T-wave inversions | Anterior LV; may be prominent in focal myocarditis | Moderate |
| V5 | T-wave inversions; ST changes variable | Anterolateral wall | Low-Moderate |
| V6 | T-wave inversions; ST changes variable | Lateral wall | Low |

### 2.3 Key Leads
- **No single lead is diagnostic** — the most important concept in myocarditis ECG interpretation
- Lead II: most useful for PR depression (myopericarditis), inferior ST changes, and T-wave inversions
- V1-V2: useful for detecting new RBBB, conduction changes, and anterior ST elevation/T-wave inversion
- V2-V4: anterior T-wave inversions, particularly giant T-wave inversions in COVID-19 myocarditis
- The PATTERN to recognize: non-territorial ST/T changes + new conduction abnormality + tachycardia in a young patient with chest pain/fever/elevated troponin = myocarditis until cardiac MRI excludes
- aVR reciprocal changes if concurrent pericardial inflammation is present

### 2.4 Beat-by-Beat Considerations
- Frequent PVCs may interrupt the baseline rhythm; morphology of PVCs may originate from multiple foci (multifocal PVCs suggest diffuse myocardial irritability)
- AV block: if 2° Mobitz II or 3° AV block, expect the RR interval to be irregular (with dropped beats) or to show complete AV dissociation with independent P and QRS rhythms
- In 3° AV block from myocarditis: the escape rhythm is typically junctional (narrow) if the block is at the AV node (narrow QRS <120 ms), or ventricular (wide) if below the His bundle
- New 3° AV block in a young patient (age <40) without prior cardiac history = myocarditis until proven otherwise — this is one of the most clinically important pattern-recognition rules in ECG
- VT in myocarditis: often focal (due to inflammation) rather than scar-based re-entry; may be right ventricular outflow tract (LBBB morphology, inferior axis) or non-specific morphology depending on location

## 3. Morphology Details

### 3.1 P-wave Changes
- Usually normal sinus P-waves; sinus tachycardia produces shortened PP interval
- Atrial myocarditis can cause P-wave morphology changes, atrial premature beats, atrial fibrillation, or atrial flutter — particularly with extensive inflammatory involvement
- In myopericarditis: P-wave morphology itself is normal but the PR SEGMENT after the P-wave is depressed (atrial epicardial injury current)
- Giant cell myocarditis and sarcoidosis may produce atrial arrhythmias disproportionate to the degree of ventricular involvement

### 3.2 PR Interval Changes
- **PR prolongation (first-degree AV block)**: Inflammation near the AV node slows conduction — PR >200 ms. This is an early and common sign of conduction system involvement
- **PR depression**: ONLY when concurrent pericarditis is present (myopericarditis). The PR depression in myopericarditis is identical to that in isolated pericarditis — diffuse, best seen in lead II, reciprocal elevation in aVR
- **Wenckebach (Mobitz I)**: Progressive PR prolongation with a dropped beat — indicates reversible AV nodal dysfunction from inflammation. Common in Lyme carditis specifically
- **Mobitz II**: Fixed PR with sudden dropped beats — indicates infranodal block; more ominous
- **Complete AV block**: Complete AV dissociation — most feared arrhythmic manifestation of myocarditis. Requires temporary pacing. In a young person, the prior probability for myocarditis is extremely high

### 3.3 QRS Complex Changes
- **Duration**: May be prolonged if bundle branch block develops (new LBBB or RBBB from conduction system inflammation)
- **Voltage**: Low voltage if associated pericardial effusion. New low voltage in a previously normal ECG should prompt evaluation for effusion
- **Q-waves**: Myocarditis can cause pathological Q-waves if myocardial necrosis is extensive enough — this is uncommon but when present, it mimics MI exactly (transmural myocarditis). Importantly, cardiac MRI will show a non-territorial distribution of late gadolinium enhancement, unlike the coronary territory pattern in MI
- **Fragmented QRS (fQRS)**: Notching in QRS suggesting focal conduction delay through scarred/inflamed myocardium — may be seen in chronic/subacute myocarditis
- **Axis**: Variable; right axis deviation may appear with RV involvement; left axis deviation may occur with fascicular block

### 3.4 ST Segment Changes
- **Direction and pattern**: Highly variable. May mimic pericarditis (diffuse ST elevation), anterior STEMI (ST elevation V1-V5), inferior STEMI (ST elevation II, III, aVF), or show ST depression (subendocardial injury pattern)
- **Morphology**: ST elevation in myocarditis may be concave (like pericarditis) or convex (like STEMI), reflecting the patchy nature — focal transmural inflammation produces STEMI-like morphology
- **Critical distinguishing feature**: ST elevation in myocarditis is NON-TERRITORIAL — it does not precisely map to one coronary artery territory. ST elevation in V2-V5 that does not conform to LAD anatomy (e.g., also involves inferior or lateral leads without a wrap-around LAD) should raise suspicion for myocarditis, especially in a young patient
- **Resolution**: ST elevation in myocarditis may resolve over hours to days as inflammation subsides, without developing Q-waves (unlike STEMI where Q-waves typically develop)

### 3.5 T-wave Changes
- **Inversions**: Can be focal (e.g., V1-V3 only) or global (all precordial + limb leads). Global T-wave inversions suggest fulminant myocarditis or extensive inflammation
- **Giant T-wave inversions**: Deeply inverted (>5 mm), widespread T-wave inversions, particularly V1-V6 with QTc prolongation — most characteristic of COVID-19 myocarditis. Also seen in giant cell myocarditis
- **Flat T-waves**: Less dramatic than inversions but represent repolarization abnormality; may be diffuse or patchy
- **Temporal evolution**: Unlike pericarditis, myocarditis does NOT follow a predictable 4-stage pattern. T-wave changes may develop rapidly, persist for weeks, or resolve and recur if inflammation is ongoing

### 3.6 QT/QTc Changes
- **QTc prolongation**: Common in myocarditis due to diffuse repolarization abnormality. QTc >500 ms indicates significant risk
- **TdP risk**: Markedly prolonged QTc from myocarditis creates genuine Torsades de Pointes risk — this is a direct mechanism of sudden cardiac death in myocarditis
- **COVID-19 myocarditis**: QTc is often dramatically prolonged (>550 ms) alongside global T-wave inversions — a pattern that should immediately raise myocarditis concern in a COVID-19 context
- **Monitoring requirement**: Serial QTc monitoring mandatory in acute myocarditis, especially if arrhythmias are present or immunosuppressive therapy is being considered

### 3.7 Other Features
- **Sinus tachycardia**: Most common and often only ECG abnormality. Caused by fever, inflammation, sympathetic activation, and reduced cardiac output. Persistent resting tachycardia in a young patient with chest pain and elevated troponin warrants myocarditis work-up
- **Ventricular arrhythmias**: PVCs (may be frequent and multifocal), non-sustained VT, and sustained VT. Fulminant myocarditis with refractory VT/VF is a leading cause of sudden cardiac death in young athletes
- **Bundle branch blocks**: New LBBB or RBBB suggests conduction system inflammation — always investigate when new in a young patient without structural heart disease
- **Fragmented QRS**: Subtle notching of QRS in multiple leads — may represent heterogeneous conduction delay through inflamed myocardium

## 4. Differential Diagnosis

### 4.1 Mimics

| Mimic Condition | Shared Features | Distinguishing Features |
|----------------|-----------------|------------------------|
| **Acute pericarditis** | Diffuse ST elevation, PR depression, chest pain | Pericarditis: NO troponin elevation (or only mildly elevated if myopericarditis); ECG evolves through 4 stages; friction rub on exam; responds to NSAIDs. Myocarditis: significant troponin elevation; more likely to have AV block or ventricular arrhythmias; wall motion abnormalities on echo; cardiac MRI distinguishes definitively |
| **STEMI (anterior)** | ST elevation V2-V5, chest pain, troponin elevation | STEMI: precise LAD territory distribution; reciprocal changes in inferior leads; evolves with Q-wave development; culprit lesion on angiography. Myocarditis: non-territorial; no culprit on angiography; cardiac MRI shows non-ischemic late gadolinium enhancement pattern (typically epicardial, not subendocardial) |
| **STEMI (inferior)** | ST elevation II, III, aVF | STEMI inferior: reciprocal depression in I and aVL; RCA territory; culprit on angiography. Myocarditis: inferior ST elevation without precise RCA territory; may have concurrent anterior changes; non-territorial |
| **Stress cardiomyopathy (Takotsubo)** | Diffuse ST elevation then deep T-wave inversions; no culprit lesion | Takotsubo: triggered by stress event; apical ballooning on echo; predominantly female; QTc often ≥500 ms; resolves over weeks. Myocarditis: any patient; fever common; troponin may be higher; MRI pattern differs |
| **Brugada syndrome** | Right precordial ST changes | Brugada: Type 1 coved ST in V1-V2 without troponin elevation; fever can unmask Brugada pattern — CRITICAL overlap: fever in known Brugada patient may produce Type 1 ECG changes without myocarditis |
| **Benign early repolarization** | ST elevation, young patient | BER: stable, no symptoms, no troponin, no PR changes, no arrhythmias |
| **Hypertrophic cardiomyopathy** | LVH voltage, T-wave changes | HCM: long-standing, chronic changes; no acute illness; echo shows hypertrophy |

### 4.2 Coexisting Conditions
- **Myopericarditis**: Myocarditis + concurrent pericardial inflammation — the ECG shows BOTH pericarditis features (diffuse ST elevation, PR depression, Spodick's sign) AND myocarditis features (troponin elevation, arrhythmias). This is the most common presentation of viral myocarditis
- **Pericardial effusion**: Can develop secondary to myocarditis — adds low voltage and possible electrical alternans to the ECG. If tamponade develops, this becomes a cardiac emergency
- **Dilated cardiomyopathy**: Chronic myocarditis (unrecognized acute phase) can progress to DCM — ECG shows LVH, LBBB, non-specific ST-T changes, AF
- **Channelopathies unmasked**: Fever from acute myocarditis can unmask Brugada syndrome (SCN5A mutation) — producing Type 1 Brugada pattern on ECG. This is an important and dangerous overlap

## 5. STAT Classification

| Priority | Criteria |
|----------|----------|
| **STAT** | YES — myocarditis is STAT when: (1) new complete (3°) AV block is present (requires temporary pacing preparation); (2) sustained VT or VF; (3) hemodynamic compromise (cardiogenic shock); (4) fulminant myocarditis presentation with rapidly declining LV function |
| **Urgent** | YES — new AV block (any degree) in young patient; frequent PVCs or non-sustained VT; significantly elevated troponin with new ECG changes; QTc >500 ms |
| **Time-sensitive** | YES — the primary urgency driver is differentiating myocarditis from STEMI (angiography may be required if STEMI cannot be excluded); delay in diagnosis of fulminant myocarditis may lead to death or need for mechanical circulatory support |
| **Clinical action** | Continuous cardiac monitoring (telemetry); serial ECGs; serial troponin; echocardiogram (wall motion, effusion, function); cardiac MRI (Lake Louise criteria) when patient is stable; avoid NSAIDs if significant LV dysfunction; consider immunosuppression only for specific subtypes (giant cell, sarcoid, autoimmune); mechanical support (IABP, Impella, ECMO) for fulminant myocarditis with cardiogenic shock |

### 6.1 Reasoning Domains Required
- **Rhythm analysis**: Detection of sinus tachycardia, AV block (all degrees), bundle branch blocks, PVCs, VT — requires full rhythm analysis capability
- **ST segment analysis**: Assess ST changes across all 12 leads; determine if pattern is territorial (STEMI) or non-territorial (myocarditis/pericarditis)
- **T-wave analysis**: Identify global vs focal T-wave inversions; detect giant T-wave inversions; assess for pattern consistency with specific myocarditis subtypes (COVID-19)
- **QRS morphology**: New BBB, fragmented QRS, low voltage, Q-wave formation
- **QTc measurement**: Precise QTc calculation; escalation threshold at >500 ms; TdP risk assessment
- **PR segment analysis**: PR depression if myopericarditis; PR prolongation for AV block
- **Contextual reasoning**: Myocarditis ECG interpretation is HEAVILY context-dependent — age of patient, fever/viral illness, troponin level, COVID-19 context, prior ECG are all required for meaningful interpretation
- **Differential exclusion**: Must actively exclude STEMI (territoriality check), Brugada (fever + right precordial changes), and pericarditis

### 6.2 Feature Dependencies
- **ESSENTIAL features**: Heart rate (tachycardia is most common finding); ST segment direction and morphology in all leads; T-wave polarity across all leads; PR interval and segment; QRS duration; QTc
- **SUPPORTING features**: AV block pattern (if present); PVC morphology and frequency; QRS voltage; fragmented QRS; lead distribution of ST/T changes
- **EXCLUSION features**: Territorial ST elevation with reciprocal changes → STEMI first; Type 1 Brugada pattern → consider Brugada; pure diffuse ST elevation with PR depression but NO troponin → pericarditis more likely
- **Per-beat vs aggregate**: AV block requires per-beat PR interval assessment; PVC morphology requires per-beat QRS analysis; ST/T changes are aggregate (stable across sinus beats)

### 6.3 Cross-Condition Interactions
- **Myocarditis vs STEMI**: Highest-stakes differential when ST elevation is present. Cannot be reliably distinguished on ECG alone — requires angiography and cardiac MRI
- **Myocarditis + Brugada unmasking**: Fever raises the risk of Type 1 Brugada pattern emerging in susceptible patients. The system should flag: "Fever present + right precordial ST elevation — consider Brugada unmasking vs myocarditis vs both"
- **Myocarditis + Long QT**: Myocarditis-induced QTc prolongation may interact with any underlying genetic long QT syndrome to dramatically increase TdP risk
- **Myopericarditis vs isolated myocarditis**: The presence of PR depression shifts interpretation toward myopericarditis; clinical significance is mainly in treatment decisions (NSAIDs + colchicine for pericarditis component)

### 6.4 Reasoning Chain Sketch
**Minimum reasoning chain:**
1. Detect sinus tachycardia → consider systemic illness/myocarditis in young patient
2. Assess ST-T changes across all 12 leads → if present, assess for territorial vs non-territorial pattern
3. Check for new AV block or BBB → if present in young patient, strong trigger for myocarditis work-up

**Full reasoning chain:**
1. Determine heart rate and rhythm → sinus tachycardia? AV block present?
2. Assess PR interval and PR segment → prolongation (1° AV block)? PR depression (pericardial involvement)?
3. Measure ST segment in all 12 leads → elevated? Direction of elevation (territorial vs diffuse)?
4. If ST elevation → map to vascular territories → does it fit LAD, RCA, or LCx territory precisely? → if yes, escalate to STEMI pathway → if no, consider myocarditis
5. Assess T-wave polarity and amplitude → global inversions? Giant inversions (COVID-19 pattern)? Deep precordial inversions?
6. Measure QTc → >500 ms? Assess TdP risk
7. Check for PVCs/VT → multifocal PVCs suggest diffuse myocardial irritability
8. Assess QRS voltage → low voltage → flag for effusion evaluation
9. Assess for new BBB → new LBBB/RBBB in young patient without structural heart disease → myocarditis
10. Synthesize → escalate appropriately: STEMI-equivalent (needs cath), fulminant myocarditis (needs CCU), stable myocarditis (needs MRI), or cannot exclude myocarditis (needs troponin + echo + MRI)

### 6.5 Confidence Anchors
- **HIGH confidence**: New complete AV block + elevated troponin + young patient with viral prodrome + non-territorial ST changes = myocarditis until proven otherwise
- **MODERATE confidence**: Non-territorial ST elevation + tachycardia + mild troponin elevation without AV block — probable myocarditis; STEMI not excluded without angiography
- **LOW confidence**: Sinus tachycardia alone without ST/T changes — cannot diagnose myocarditis from ECG; clinical and lab correlation essential
- **Cannot diagnose from ECG alone**: Myocarditis has NO pathognomonic ECG pattern. The ECG establishes urgency, guides differential, and identifies complications (AV block, VT, tamponade) — it does NOT confirm myocarditis. Cardiac MRI is required for definitive diagnosis
- **Absent features that LOWER confidence**: If precise coronary territory ST elevation with reciprocal changes → strongly favors STEMI over myocarditis; if PR depression without troponin elevation → favors isolated pericarditis

| Dimension | Score (1-5) | Notes |

| Number of leads required | 5 | Full 12-lead required; no single lead or lead group is diagnostic; pattern is defined by what is NOT present (territorial) as much as what IS |

### Primary Agent
**IT** — The dominant ECG abnormality in myocarditis is diffuse or focal ST elevation, which drives the highest-stakes differential (myocarditis vs STEMI); IT's territory analysis is the critical first-pass classifier.

### Cross-Domain Hints
- IT → CDS: `cross_domain_hint: "diffuse_ST_elevation_without_dominant_territory — evaluate for myocarditis vs pericarditis vs Takotsubo"`
- RRC → CDS: `cross_domain_hint: "new_AV_block_or_PVCs_in_young_patient — flag myocarditis as etiology"`
- MR → CDS: `cross_domain_hint: "QTc_prolongation_with_diffuse_ST_changes — assess arrhythmia risk; distinguish from long QT syndromes"`

### CDS Specific Role
CDS integrates IT's ST pattern distribution, RRC's arrhythmia findings (AV block, PVCs, VT), and MR's QTc and voltage data to distinguish myocarditis from its two key differentials. Against STEMI: myocarditis lacks dominant reciprocal changes and its ST pattern does not match a single coronary territory. Against pericarditis: myocarditis may show focal (territorial) ST elevation and has higher arrhythmia burden; pericarditis always shows diffuse concave ST elevation with PR depression and no focal ischemia pattern. CDS also computes an arrhythmia risk score using RRC's AV block grade and PVC/VT frequency combined with MR's QTc.

## 9. Edge Cases and Pitfalls

- **STEMI mimic — most dangerous pitfall**: Focal myocarditis can produce ST elevation precisely mimicking anterior STEMI (V2-V5) or inferior STEMI (II, III, aVF). The ECG alone cannot reliably distinguish these. Young patient with normal coronaries at catheterization and ST elevation = myocarditis as the leading diagnosis. The system MUST NOT label non-territorial ST elevation as definitively STEMI without territorial confirmation.
- **Normal ECG in myocarditis**: Up to 30% of biopsy-proven myocarditis cases have a normal or near-normal ECG. Sinus tachycardia alone may be the only finding. The system should not reassure users that a normal ECG excludes myocarditis.
- **Brugada unmasking by fever**: Fever in a patient with underlying SCN5A mutation can produce Type 1 Brugada pattern (coved ST elevation in V1-V2). This can co-occur with myocarditis (both triggered by the same viral infection) or be mistaken for myocarditis. The system should flag: "Fever + right precordial ST elevation — consider Brugada syndrome unmasking; genetic testing may be warranted."
- **COVID-19 myocarditis pattern**: The global T-wave inversions of COVID-19 myocarditis are dramatically different from classical viral myocarditis ECG (which often shows diffuse ST elevation). The system should recognize this as a distinct subtype and not require ST elevation for myocarditis consideration in a COVID-19 context.
- **Lyme carditis AV block**: Lyme disease (Borrelia burgdorferi) characteristically causes high-degree AV block (often progressing rapidly from 1° to complete block) in young patients who spent time in endemic areas. AV block from Lyme is often transient and responds to antibiotics. The system should include geographic/exposure context when flagging new AV block.
- **Giant cell myocarditis**: The most aggressive form — rapid progression to cardiogenic shock, VT storm, and complete AV block. ECG may show rapidly changing patterns. High-grade AV block + VT in a young patient with no prior history = urgent consideration of giant cell myocarditis; requires endomyocardial biopsy.
- **Sarcoid myocarditis**: Presents with bundle branch blocks (RBBB most common), ventricular arrhythmias, and high-degree AV block — often in middle-aged adults. May coexist with pulmonary sarcoidosis. ECG pattern overlaps with arrhythmogenic cardiomyopathy.
- **Post-COVID vaccine myocarditis**: Typically young male, 2-4 days after second mRNA vaccine dose. ECG often shows ST elevation or T-wave inversions. Clinical course is usually mild, but STAT evaluation is required at presentation to exclude STEMI and assess LV function.
