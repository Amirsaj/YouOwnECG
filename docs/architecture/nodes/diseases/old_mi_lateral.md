## 1. Pathophysiology Root Cause

### 1.1 Lateral Wall Anatomy and Arterial Supply
The lateral wall of the left ventricle extends from the lateral margin adjacent to the mitral valve annulus to the lateral free wall, from base to apex. This territory is supplied by two arterial systems depending on the sub-territory:

**High lateral wall** (basal lateral, near the base):
- Supplied primarily by the **first diagonal branch (D1)** of the LAD
- Alternatively supplied by the **first obtuse marginal branch (OM1)** of the left circumflex artery (LCx)
- Recorded by leads **I and aVL**

**Low lateral wall** (mid and apical lateral, lateral free wall):
- Supplied predominantly by the **left circumflex artery (LCx)** via obtuse marginal branches (OM1, OM2)
- In left-dominant individuals, LCx also supplies the inferior wall
- Recorded by leads **V5 and V6**

Because of this dual supply and the relatively small muscle mass compared to the anterior wall, lateral MI is often less dramatic in presentation than anterior MI. Pure lateral MI from an isolated LCx or diagonal occlusion is one of the more commonly "missed" MIs — LCx territory is less well-represented by the standard 12-lead ECG.

### 1.2 Scar Formation
As with all completed transmural MIs, the lateral wall scar is electrically silent. Recording electrodes overlying the lateral territory (I, aVL, V5, V6) detect only forces directed away from the lateral wall, producing pathological Q waves. The small muscle mass of the lateral wall means that lateral Q waves are often smaller in absolute amplitude compared to anterior or inferior Q waves — a practical diagnostic challenge.

### 1.3 LCx Occlusion — The "Culprit Artery" Problem
The left circumflex is the most frequently overlooked infarct-related artery on the standard 12-lead ECG. Multiple studies have shown that 30–50% of LCx occlusions produce no ST-elevation on the standard 12-lead, and may produce only minimal or no ECG abnormality acutely. This is because:
1. The LCx wraps posterolaterally and its territory is sub-optimally represented by the precordial leads (V1-V6 are oriented primarily anteriorly)
2. In many patients, V5-V6 are partially oriented toward LCx territory but not perfectly aligned
3. Posterior leads (V7-V9) are required to capture LCx territory fully

## 2. ECG Presentation — Lead by Lead

### 2.1 Diagnostic Criteria (2025 AHA/ESC Guidelines)

Per the Fourth Universal Definition of MI:

**Pathological Q-wave criteria for lateral MI** (≥2 contiguous lateral leads):
- **Duration ≥40ms** in leads I, aVL, V5, or V6
- **Depth ≥25% of the amplitude of the following R wave**
- **QS complex** in any of the above lateral leads (no R wave at all)
- Small q waves in V5-V6 are normal (septal q waves of normal activation); these must NOT be labeled pathological unless they meet width or depth criteria

**Special note on lead I**: A Q wave in lead I can be a normal finding in some individuals (depending on the heart's electrical axis). It must meet the ≥40ms OR ≥25% R-wave depth criterion to be considered pathological.

**Special note on aVL**: aVL is the most specific lead for high lateral MI but its normal Q-wave range varies. A Q wave in aVL meeting ≥40ms criteria is strongly pathological. A small Q in aVL (<40ms, <25%) is a common normal variant.

### 2.2 Lead-by-Lead Manifestation

| Lead | Finding in Chronic Lateral MI | Notes |
|------|-------------------------------|-------|
| I | Pathological Q (≥40ms or ≥25% R) or QS | High lateral MI marker — high specificity |
| II | Usually normal | Not primary lateral territory |
| III | Usually normal | Not primary lateral territory |
| aVR | Usually normal | Opposite polarity from lateral leads |
| aVL | Pathological Q or QS | High lateral MI marker — most specific lead for high lateral |
| aVF | May show reciprocal changes | Acute high lateral MI: ST depression aVF reciprocally; chronic: may normalize |
| V1 | Normal or R-wave changes | Not primary lateral territory |
| V2 | Normal | Not primary lateral territory |
| V3 | Normal | Not primary lateral territory |
| V4 | Normal or transitional | Not primary territory unless apical lateral involvement |
| V5 | Pathological Q or reduced R-wave | Low lateral territory — LCx territory |
| V6 | Pathological Q or reduced R-wave | Low lateral territory — LCx territory |

**Sub-territory distinction**:
- Q waves in I + aVL (without V5/V6 involvement) → **high lateral MI** (D1 or OM1 occlusion)
- Q waves in V5 + V6 (without I/aVL involvement) → **low lateral MI** (LCx/OM branches)
- Q waves in I, aVL, V5, V6 → **extensive lateral MI** (LCx or LAD + diagonal)

### 2.3 Key Leads
- **I and aVL**: Primary diagnostic leads for high lateral MI; aVL is the most specific
- **V5 and V6**: Primary diagnostic leads for low lateral MI (LCx territory)
- **Reciprocal leads**: In high lateral MI, inferior leads (especially aVF, III) show reciprocal ST depression acutely; in chronic phase, this relationship normalizes
- **V7-V9**: Posterior leads that capture LCx territory most accurately; not part of standard 12-lead but should be obtained when LCx occlusion is suspected

### 2.4 Beat-by-Beat Considerations
- Small lateral Q waves may be intermittently visible or partially obscured by baseline wander in the limb leads
- aVL Q-wave morphology is particularly susceptible to respiratory artifact — vertical heart axis makes aVL findings variable
- V5-V6 septal q waves are normal and narrow (<30ms); do not over-diagnose lateral MI from physiological septal q waves
- In AF, assess Q-wave pattern across multiple beats for consistency

## 3. Morphology Details

### 3.1 Normal Septal Q Waves vs. Pathological Lateral Q Waves
This is the most critical morphological distinction:

**Normal septal q waves in V5-V6**:
- Present in most healthy individuals
- Width: typically 10–20ms (less than one small square)
- Depth: small (<25% of R wave)
- Represents normal left-to-right septal activation producing a small initial leftward-then-rightward deflection
- These are NORMAL and must not be labeled as MI

**Pathological Q waves in V5-V6 from lateral MI**:
- Width ≥40ms (at least one full small square)
- Depth often ≥25% of R wave
- May evolve to QS complex in severe lateral MI
- Accompanied by loss of lateral R-wave voltage (R-wave amplitude reduction from prior ECG or compared to V4)

### 3.2 High Lateral Isolation (I + aVL) Without V5-V6
When Q waves are found only in I and aVL without involvement of V5-V6, this represents a high lateral (basal lateral) MI pattern. This is the territory of the first diagonal or first marginal branch. The relatively small muscle mass of the high lateral basal wall means these Q waves are often subtle — small amplitude Q that could be dismissed as a normal variant.

Clinical implication: High lateral isolated Q waves (I + aVL) are easily under-reported. The system must actively flag these even when they are small.

### 3.3 Loss of Lateral R-Wave Voltage
Even in the absence of frank pathological Q waves, loss of lateral R-wave amplitude compared to a prior ECG may indicate lateral MI. R-wave amplitude in V5-V6 should normally be 5–15mm; significant reduction from baseline is a soft sign of lateral MI. This is particularly relevant for chronic LCx territory infarction where Q waves may not fully meet criteria.

### 3.4 T-Wave Changes in Chronic Phase
- T-wave inversions in I, aVL, V5, V6 may persist chronically
- Persistent T-wave inversion in aVL is a common residual finding after high lateral MI
- Isolated T-wave inversion in aVL is not diagnostic of MI alone but raises suspicion in the right clinical context

### 3.5 ST Segment in Chronic Phase
- ST segments normalize in the chronic phase in most cases
- Persistent ST elevation with Q waves in lateral leads: consider lateral LV aneurysm (rare but reported)
- New lateral ST elevation or depression must not be attributed to old changes

### 3.6 QRS Axis
- High lateral MI (I + aVL territory) can produce right axis deviation (loss of lateral leftward forces) — less commonly seen than left axis deviation from anterior or inferior involvement
- Typical axis in isolated lateral MI: normal or mildly rightward

### 3.7 Amplitude Changes
- LCx-territory MI often shows lower amplitude ECG changes than LAD territory MI because the standard 12-lead provides suboptimal vector coverage of the posterolateral wall
- Low QRS voltage in lateral leads combined with Q waves strengthens the MI probability

## 4. Differential Diagnosis

### 4.1 Mimics
| Mimic | Distinguishing Feature |
|-------|------------------------|
| Normal septal q in V5-V6 | Narrow (<30ms), small amplitude; within normal variant range; no clinical history |
| Normal q in aVL | Small, narrow (<40ms); aVL very sensitive to axis; check in context of heart position |
| LBBB | Eliminates normal septal q waves in V5-V6; cannot diagnose lateral MI Q in LBBB |
| WPW with lateral pathway | Short PR; delta waves alter Q-wave morphology; pseudo-Q possible |
| LVH with lateral strain | Fixed ST depression/T-wave inversion in I, aVL, V5-V6 from LVH; voltage criteria present; Q waves not prominent |
| LAFB | Small q in aVL is part of LAFB pattern — do not over-interpret as MI |
| Dilated cardiomyopathy | Poor R-wave progression and pseudo-Q waves; diffuse; no coronary territory |

### 4.2 Coexisting Conditions
- **Lateral MI + Anterior MI**: LAD occlusion proximal to D1 causes both anterior (V1-V4) and high lateral (I, aVL) infarction — a common pattern. Combined Q-wave findings are expected.
- **Lateral MI + Inferior MI**: LCx occlusion in left-dominant individuals or large LCx can cause both inferior and lateral MI simultaneously. Q waves in inferior leads (II, III, aVF) + lateral leads (I, aVL, V5-V6) can coexist.
- **LAFB + high lateral MI**: LAFB produces a small q in aVL and deep S in inferior leads as part of its normal pattern. High lateral MI adds a pathological (wide) Q in aVL. The distinction requires Q-wave duration measurement: LAFB q in aVL is typically narrow; MI Q in aVL is wide (≥40ms).
- **LVH + lateral MI**: LVH increases lateral R-wave voltage, which may obscure lateral Q waves by increasing the R-wave amplitude (making the Q less than 25% of R). Lateral MI in the context of significant LVH may be under-detected by standard Q-wave criteria.

## 6. Reasoning Complexity Analysis

### 6.1 Septal Q vs. Lateral MI Q Distinction
The most common interpretive challenge: normal septal q waves in V5-V6 are present in the majority of healthy individuals. The system must apply strict width (≥40ms) and depth (≥25% R-wave) criteria and not label normal septal q waves as pathological. This requires precise automated Q-wave measurement, not just Q-wave detection.

### 6.2 aVL Sensitivity and Specificity Balance
aVL has high specificity for high lateral MI but its Q-wave findings are highly sensitive to the cardiac axis. In vertical hearts (tall, thin individuals) or with respiratory variation, small Q waves in aVL are normal. The system must apply Q-wave duration criteria rigorously and avoid over-diagnosing from aVL alone.

### 6.3 LCx Under-Representation
The LCx territory is poorly represented by the standard 12-lead ECG. The system should note this limitation explicitly: "Posterolateral LCx territory has suboptimal standard 12-lead coverage; posterior leads (V7-V9) recommended for complete assessment in clinical context."

### 6.4 Combined Territory Recognition
Lateral MI rarely occurs in isolation from either anterior or inferior MI. The system should look for combined patterns and correctly identify the probable culprit vessel territory.

### 6.5 LAFB Q in aVL — Masking and False Positive
LAFB produces a small q in aVL as part of its activation pattern. This can be misinterpreted as lateral MI. The system must detect LAFB (left axis deviation, rS in inferior leads, qR in aVL with narrow q) and distinguish LAFB-associated aVL q from pathological lateral MI Q.

### 6.6 Difficulty Score

| Dimension | Score (1–5) | Rationale |
|-----------|-------------|-----------|
| Septal Q vs. Lateral MI Q | 3 | Requires precise width/depth measurement; not just Q detection |
| aVL Interpretation | 3 | Axis-dependent; easy to over- or under-diagnose |
| LCx Coverage Limitation | 2 | System acknowledges limitation; posterior leads flag |
| LAFB Interference | 3 | Must detect LAFB first; narrow vs. wide Q discrimination |
| Combined Territory Analysis | 2 | Straightforward once Q-wave territories mapped |
| Acuity Escalation Logic | 2 | Clear trigger: new ST elevation in lateral leads |
| **Overall Difficulty** | **2.0** | Moderate; main challenge is Q-wave precision and LAFB interference |

## 6A. Agent Assignment

**Architecture:** Option E — 3 Parallel Phase 1 Specialists + 1 Sequential CDS (Node 2.1 Gate Passed 2026-03-27)

| Agent | Role for Old Lateral MI | Key Features Used |
|-------|--------------------------|-------------------|
| **RRC** (Rhythm/Rate/Conduction) | Not involved | No rhythm or conduction features specific to old lateral MI |
| **IT** (Ischemia/Territory) | Primary | Pathological Q-waves in I, aVL, V5–V6 (≥40 ms duration or ≥25% R-wave amplitude); LCx territory assignment (high lateral = LCx; low lateral = OM branch); no concurrent ST elevation |
| **MR** (Morphology/Repolarization) | Supporting | Lateral voltage changes (diminished R in I, aVL, V5–V6); LAFB detection (produces small q in aVL that must be distinguished from lateral MI Q) |
| **CDS** (Cross-Domain Synthesis) | Standard integration only | Confirms Q-wave territory matches lateral coronary distribution; differentiates pathological lateral Q from LAFB-associated q in aVL; standard integration |

### Primary Agent
**IT** — Old lateral MI is defined by pathological Q-waves in I/aVL/V5–V6 mapped to the LCx territory; Q-wave measurement and coronary territory assignment are IT-domain functions.

### Cross-Domain Hints
- IT → MR: `cross_domain_hint: "Q_wave_in_aVL — confirm LAFB absent; LAFB produces small q in aVL that mimics lateral MI"`

### CDS Specific Role
CDS performs standard integration, confirming that IT's lateral Q-waves are pathological (meeting duration/depth criteria) and located in a consistent LCx territory distribution. The key CDS task is applying the LAFB interference check: if MR identifies LAFB (left axis deviation, rS inferior leads, qR in aVL), CDS requires that the Q in aVL exceeds the LAFB-expected q width before confirming lateral MI. CDS also notes that isolated lateral MI is less common and often accompanies anterior or inferior MI findings.

## 9. Edge Cases and Pitfalls

**1. Normal septal q waves in V5-V6 are the single most common false positive for lateral MI.** The normal depolarization sequence begins in the left side of the septum, creating a small initial leftward then rightward force that produces a small narrow q wave in V5-V6 and a small r wave in V1. These septal q waves are normal, narrow (typically 10-20ms), and present in the majority of healthy adults. A system that flags all Q waves in V5-V6 as pathological will have an unacceptable false-positive rate. Strict ≥40ms width criterion is the guard.

**2. LAFB q in aVL — the most common source of false-positive high lateral MI.** Left anterior fascicular block changes septal activation direction and produces a characteristic pattern: q in I and aVL (narrow), r in aVL, deep S in inferior leads, left axis deviation. The q wave in aVL from LAFB is a normal expected finding and is NOT a sign of lateral MI. Distinguishing it from MI requires: (a) measuring Q width (LAFB q is narrow, MI Q is ≥40ms), (b) identifying left axis deviation as a supporting LAFB feature, and (c) correlating with clinical history.

**3. LCx "silent territory" in standard 12-lead.** Studies have documented that 30-50% of isolated LCx occlusions produce no ST elevation on the standard 12-lead ECG. This means a patient with acute lateral MI from LCx may present with ST depression in V1-V3 (reciprocal from posterolateral ST elevation not captured by standard leads), minimal lateral changes, or a completely normal ECG. In the chronic phase, lateral Q waves may be small and easily overlooked. Clinical history of prior LCx territory MI should lower the threshold for interpreting borderline lateral Q findings.

**4. High lateral MI (I + aVL) is commonly under-reported.** The relatively small muscle mass of the high basal lateral wall produces small Q waves in I and aVL that may be near the threshold of normal. Combined with the axis-dependence of aVL and the frequent dismissal of small limb-lead Q waves, high lateral MI is one of the most under-diagnosed patterns. The system must apply consistent criteria regardless of Q-wave amplitude.

**5. Combined anterior + high lateral from proximal LAD occlusion.** Occlusion of the LAD proximal to D1 causes a combined anterior (V1-V4) + high lateral (I, aVL) infarction. If the system detects both anterior and high lateral Q waves, it should report this as consistent with proximal LAD territory rather than treating them as two separate infarctions.

**6. LVH masking lateral Q waves.** Significant LVH increases lateral R-wave amplitude substantially. This can reduce the Q/R ratio below the 25% threshold even if Q-wave duration meets criteria. The combination of high lateral voltage (from LVH) and lateral MI (reducing the R wave absolute amplitude while keeping it higher than without LVH) creates a confounded picture. Q-wave width criterion (≥40ms) is more reliable in the context of LVH.

**7. Posterior MI appearing as "lateral changes."** True posterior MI produces tall, broad R waves in V1-V2 (the mirror of Q waves in posterior leads). These R waves in V1-V2 combined with upright T-waves in V1 can be confusing when a lateral MI pattern is also present. The system should evaluate V1-V2 for posterior MI markers when LCx territory findings are identified.
