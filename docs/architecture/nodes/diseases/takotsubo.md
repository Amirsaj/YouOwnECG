## 1. Pathophysiology Root Cause

### 1.1 Underlying Mechanism
Catecholamine surge (epinephrine, norepinephrine) triggered by intense emotional or physical stress causes diffuse microvascular spasm and direct myocardial toxicity. The apical and mid-ventricular myocardium — which has the highest density of sympathetic nerve terminals and β-adrenergic receptors — is disproportionately affected. The basal segments are relatively spared, producing the characteristic apical ballooning ("takotsubo" = octopus trap in Japanese).

### 1.2 Electrophysiological Consequence
Catecholamine excess disrupts calcium handling in cardiomyocytes. The acute phase produces regional transmural ischemia (ST elevation) across the stunned apical territory. As the myocardium recovers but remains electrically abnormal, prolonged repolarization anomalies emerge: deep, widespread T-wave inversions and marked QTc prolongation. The repolarization abnormality outlasts mechanical dysfunction by days to weeks.

### 1.3 Why the ECG Pattern Mimics Anterior STEMI
The apical LV territory — stunned by catecholamine toxicity — overlaps spatially with the LAD distribution. Transmural injury current from apical myocardium projects onto anterior precordial leads (V1-V5) identically to a proximal LAD occlusion. This is why the initial ECG cannot reliably distinguish Takotsubo from anterior STEMI. Echocardiography (wall motion abnormality extending beyond single coronary territory) and coronary angiography (absence of obstructive CAD) are required for definitive distinction.

## 2. ECG Presentation — Lead by Lead

### 2.1 Diagnostic Criteria

| Phase | Timing | Key ECG Features |
|---|---|---|
| Acute (hyperacute) | 0–6 hours | Diffuse ST elevation V1–V5, may see hyperacute T waves |
| Early evolution | 6–24 hours | ST elevation resolves, QTc begins to prolong |
| Established | 24–72 hours | Deep widespread T-wave inversions V2–V6; QTc ≥500 ms common |
| Recovery | Days to weeks | Gradual T-wave normalization; QTc normalizes last |
| Full recovery | Weeks to months | ECG may normalize completely |

**Formal criterion (Mayo Clinic criteria — ECG component):** New ST elevation and/or T-wave inversion in the setting of transient apical wall motion abnormality, no obstructive coronary artery disease.

### 2.2 Lead-by-Lead Manifestation

| Lead | Acute Phase | Established Phase (24–72 h) | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| I | Mild ST elevation or isoelectric | Shallow T inversion | Lateral involvement variable |
| II | Mild ST elevation | T inversion, mild | Part of diffuse pattern |
| III | Variable; may show elevation | T inversion or isoelectric | |
| aVR | ST depression (reciprocal) | ST depression resolves | Reciprocal to diffuse anterior pattern |
| aVL | Mild ST elevation or flat | T inversion | |
| aVF | Variable | T inversion, mild | Inferior involvement varies |
| V1 | ST elevation 1–3 mm | Deep T inversion (biphasic→negative) | Earliest lead affected |
| V2 | ST elevation 2–4 mm | Deepest T inversions (up to 10 mm) | Most prominent in established phase |
| V3 | ST elevation 2–4 mm | Deep T inversion 3–10 mm | Hallmark lead |
| V4 | ST elevation 1–3 mm | Deep T inversion | |
| V5 | ST elevation 0.5–2 mm | T inversion, moderate | |
| V6 | Minimal elevation or flat | Shallow T inversion | Lateral boundary |

### 2.3 Key Leads
- **Most diagnostic:** V2–V4 in established phase (deep symmetric T inversions, QTc prolongation)
- **Acute phase mimicry:** V1–V5 ST elevation indistinguishable from anterior STEMI
- **aVR:** Reciprocal ST depression in acute phase (vs. ST elevation in LMCA/proximal LAD occlusion)

### 2.4 Beat-by-Beat Considerations
QTc must be measured on every beat because Takotsubo causes the longest QTc prolongation of any non-congenital condition. QTc ≥500 ms creates substrate for Torsades de Pointes (TdP). Rate correction: use Bazett (QTc = QT/√RR) as standard; Fridericia formula preferred at extremes of rate. Monitor for QTc trends over the 24–72 hour window — peak QTc prolongation typically occurs at 48 hours.

## 3. Morphology Details

### 3.1 ST Segment (Acute Phase)
Concave-upward (coved or saddle-back) ST elevation V1–V5. J-point elevation prominent. The morphology can be identical to anterior STEMI — convex "tombstone" pattern has been reported. No reliable morphological differentiator from STEMI on acute ECG.

### 3.2 T Waves (Established Phase)
Deep, symmetric T-wave inversions. Depth commonly 3–10 mm in V2–V4. Symmetry (equal downslope and upslope) suggests repolarization abnormality rather than ischemic asymmetric inversion. The terminal portion of the T wave is sharply negative. "Giant T-wave inversions" (>10 mm) can occur and raise concern for CNS injury pattern — clinical correlation essential.

### 3.3 QTc Prolongation
Most striking ECG feature in established phase. QTc commonly 500–600 ms. This is the major arrhythmia risk. Distinguish from: drug-induced QT prolongation (look for polypharmacy), hypokalemia/hypomagnesemia (electrolytes often abnormal in stress states), congenital LQTS (prior ECGs).

### 3.4 QRS Complex
Generally narrow. Q waves can develop in anterior leads (V1–V4) due to myocardial stunning — these are typically transient and do not represent true necrosis (unlike STEMI). Poor R-wave progression is common in established phase.

### 3.5 P Wave and Rhythm
Sinus tachycardia is common acutely (catecholamine surge). Sinus rhythm typically maintained. Ventricular arrhythmias (PVCs, VT, TdP) can occur — driven by QTc prolongation and electrolyte disturbances. TdP risk peaks at 48–72 hours when QTc is longest.

### 3.6 Axis
Leftward axis shift common in established phase. Not diagnostically specific.

### 3.7 Comparison to CNS (Cerebrovascular) T-wave Pattern
Both Takotsubo and CNS injuries (subarachnoid hemorrhage, ischemic stroke) produce deeply inverted, widely spread T waves with QTc prolongation. CNS pattern: even more dramatic inversions, may see U waves, bradycardia. The distinction is clinical — not ECG-based. SAH can also trigger catecholamine surge and true Takotsubo.

## 4. Differential Diagnosis

### 4.1 Mimics

| Condition | ECG Overlap | Key Differentiator |
|---|---|---|
| Anterior STEMI (LAD occlusion) | Identical acute ST elevation V1–V5 | Cath: no obstructive CAD; echo: wall motion beyond LAD territory |
| Myocarditis | Diffuse ST elevation, T inversions | Global vs. regional wall motion; viral prodrome; CMR |
| CNS injury (SAH, stroke) | Deep T inversions, QTc prolongation | Neurological symptoms; head CT; more dramatic T waves |
| LQTS (congenital or acquired) | QTc prolongation, T-wave morphology changes | Baseline ECG; genetics; no wall motion abnormality |
| Prinzmetal angina | ST elevation that resolves | Episodic; normal between episodes; cath shows spasm |
| Type 2 MI (demand ischemia) | ST/T changes | Troponin pattern; identifiable precipitant; echo |

### 4.2 Coexisting Conditions
- **True STEMI can coexist:** Catecholamine surge can cause plaque rupture. Do not assume Takotsubo and dismiss ischemia without cath.
- **Electrolyte disturbances:** Hypokalemia and hypomagnesemia compound QTc prolongation — must check and correct urgently.
- **Pheochromocytoma:** Can trigger recurrent Takotsubo-like episodes. Screen in atypical or recurrent cases.

## 5. STAT Classification

| Phase | Classification | Rationale |
|---|---|---|
| Acute (0–6 h) | **STAT — STEMI-equivalent** | Indistinguishable from anterior STEMI on initial ECG; cath required |
| Established (24–72 h, QTc ≥500 ms) | **STAT — TdP risk** | QTc ≥500 ms requires urgent electrolyte correction and monitoring |
| Established (24–72 h, QTc <500 ms) | **Urgent** | Ongoing monitoring required; hemodynamic instability possible |
| Recovery phase | **Routine follow-up** | Serial ECG to confirm QTc normalization |

## 6. Reasoning Complexity Analysis

### 6.1 Pattern Recognition Challenge
The acute ECG is a near-perfect mimic of anterior STEMI. No single ECG feature reliably distinguishes the two. The diagnosis of Takotsubo requires integration of ECG + echo + cath. The system must flag both possibilities in acute phase.

### 6.2 Temporal Evolution Requirement
The ECG pattern evolves over 1–3 days. A single snapshot is insufficient. The system must track serial ECGs and recognize the characteristic ST-elevation → normalization → deep T-inversion sequence.

### 6.3 QTc Monitoring Obligation
Once Takotsubo is suspected or confirmed, QTc must be serially monitored and the system must flag QTc ≥500 ms with a TdP warning.

### 6.4 Demographic Context
Post-menopausal female with emotional/physical stressor is classic. Young males and males of any age are less common but do occur. Demographic data improves pre-test probability but cannot exclude Takotsubo.

### 6.5 Integration with Non-ECG Data
Echo and cath data are required for final diagnosis. The ECG agent must communicate uncertainty clearly and recommend these tests in the acute phase.

### 6.6 Difficulty Score

| Dimension | Score (1–5) | Rationale |
|---|---|---|
| Pattern recognition | 4 | Acute phase identical to anterior STEMI |
| Temporal reasoning | 4 | Multi-day ECG evolution required |
| Differential complexity | 4 | Multiple serious conditions in differential |
| Data integration | 5 | ECG alone insufficient; echo/cath required |
| QTc risk stratification | 3 | Well-defined threshold (≥500 ms) |
| Demographic weighting | 2 | Classic demographics helpful but not definitive |
| Arrhythmia vigilance | 3 | TdP risk real but defined window |
| **Overall** | **3.5** | **Moderate-high complexity** |

## 6A. Agent Assignment

**Architecture:** Option E — 3 Parallel Phase 1 Specialists + 1 Sequential CDS (Node 2.1 Gate Passed 2026-03-27)

| Agent | Role for Takotsubo Cardiomyopathy | Key Features Used |
|-------|--------------------------|-------------------|
| **RRC** (Rhythm/Rate/Conduction) | Supporting | Sinus tachycardia (rate >100 bpm in acute phase); QTc measured for TdP risk stratification |
| **IT** (Ischemia/Territory) | Co-Primary | ST elevation in anterior/apical leads (V1–V5); pattern spans territory beyond single coronary distribution; absence of focal reciprocal changes in non-inferior leads; aVR elevation |
| **MR** (Morphology/Repolarization) | Co-Primary | Diffuse deep T-wave inversions across precordial leads V1–V6 evolving within 24–48 h; QTc prolongation (often >500 ms); symmetric T-wave inversion pattern; R-wave amplitude change across serial ECGs |
| **CDS** (Cross-Domain Synthesis) | Required — Takotsubo vs anterior STEMI resolution | Integrates IT territory analysis (multi-vessel distribution), MR T-wave inversion pattern (diffuse + deep), and QTc prolongation to distinguish Takotsubo from anterior STEMI; flags TdP risk when QTc >500 ms |

### Primary Agent
**IT and MR are co-primary** — Takotsubo requires both territory analysis (IT: ST elevation not matching one coronary artery) and morphology tracking (MR: deep diffuse T-wave inversions with QTc prolongation); neither domain alone is sufficient.

### Cross-Domain Hints
- IT → CDS: `cross_domain_hint: "anterior_ST_elevation_spanning_multiple_coronary_territories — evaluate for Takotsubo vs anterior STEMI"`
- MR → CDS: `cross_domain_hint: "diffuse_deep_T_inversion_with_QTc_prolongation — correlate with IT territory pattern for Takotsubo classification"`
- MR → RRC: `cross_domain_hint: "QTc_>500ms_in_Takotsubo — flag TdP risk; assess heart rate and rhythm"`

### CDS Specific Role
CDS resolves the critical Takotsubo vs anterior STEMI differential by synthesizing IT's territory analysis with MR's T-wave and QTc findings. The distinguishing logic: Takotsubo ST elevation spans beyond the LAD territory (extends into RCA or LCx territory), diffuse deep T-inversions appear early and are disproportionate to enzyme elevation, and QTc prolongs substantially. CDS also computes TdP risk score from MR's QTc measurement and RRC's heart rate, triggering a high-risk alert if QTc >500 ms.

## 9. Edge Cases and Pitfalls

- **Do not dismiss STEMI based on demographics alone.** Post-menopausal female presentation does not rule out real STEMI. Cath is required.
- **Reverse Takotsubo (basal ballooning):** Rare variant where basal segments balloon and apex is spared. ECG shows inferior/lateral ST elevation rather than anterior. May be triggered by pheochromocytoma.
- **Right ventricular Takotsubo:** RV involvement produces RBBB pattern or ST elevation in V1. Rare.
- **QTc normalization lag:** T waves can normalize while QTc remains prolonged. Monitor QTc independently of T-wave appearance.
- **Drug interactions:** Many ER medications (ondansetron, haloperidol, azithromycin) further prolong QTc. The system must flag polypharmacy risk if Takotsubo is flagged.
- **LBBB at baseline:** Sgarbossa criteria apply — Takotsubo in the setting of LBBB will show concordant ST changes. Increases complexity.
- **Recurrence:** 5–10% of Takotsubo patients have recurrent episodes. Prior ECGs showing the same evolution pattern are diagnostically valuable.
