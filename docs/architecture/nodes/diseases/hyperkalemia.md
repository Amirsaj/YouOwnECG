## 1. Pathophysiology Root Cause

### 1.1 What Goes Wrong (Anatomy/Physiology)
- Extracellular potassium concentration rises above normal (>5.0 mEq/L), typically from renal failure, rhabdomyolysis, acidosis, tissue necrosis, iatrogenic K+ supplementation, or ACEi/ARB/K+-sparing diuretic use
- Elevated extracellular K+ reduces the transmembrane potassium gradient (normally ~140 mEq/L intracellular vs ~4 mEq/L extracellular)
- This depolarizes the resting membrane potential from the normal -90 mV toward threshold — at K+ ~7.0 mEq/L, RMP approaches -70 mV; at K+ ~9.0 mEq/L, RMP approaches -60 mV
- Depolarized RMP causes progressive inactivation of voltage-gated Na+ channels (fast sodium channels), reducing Na+ channel availability
- The entire myocardium is affected — atria, conduction system, and ventricles — but atrial tissue is MORE sensitive than ventricular tissue (atrial myocytes have fewer Na+ channels and a less negative resting potential at baseline)

### 1.2 Electrical Consequence
- **Phase 3 acceleration** (early hyperK): Increased extracellular K+ accelerates phase 3 repolarization by increasing the potassium equilibrium potential and enhancing outward K+ current (IKr/IK1) — this produces tall, narrow, peaked T-waves
- **Phase 0 slowing** (moderate hyperK): Reduced Na+ channel availability decreases the upstroke velocity of phase 0 depolarization → slowed conduction velocity → QRS widening, PR prolongation
- **Atrial standstill** (severe hyperK): Atrial myocytes become unexcitable before ventricular myocytes due to their less negative resting potential → P-waves disappear, but the sinus node (which uses Ca2+ for depolarization, not Na+) may still fire and conduct directly to the ventricles via specialized atrial pathways (sinoventricular rhythm)
- **Global inexcitability** (critical hyperK): At extreme K+ levels, ventricular myocytes also lose sufficient Na+ channel availability → QRS merges with T into a smooth sinusoidal waveform (sine wave), then VF or asystole
- Progressive sequence: peaked T → PR prolongation → P-wave loss → QRS widening → sine wave → VF/asystole

### 1.3 Why It Appears on ECG
- Peaked T-waves: Accelerated phase 3 repolarization creates a steeper, more rapid return to baseline, producing a tall, narrow, symmetric T-wave. Most prominent in leads where the T-wave vector is largest (precordial leads V2-V4 facing the LV free wall, and lead II aligned with the mean electrical axis)
- QRS widening: Slowed depolarization spread through ventricular myocardium projects wider QRS complexes onto ALL leads because the entire depolarization sequence is prolonged — not a focal conduction block but a diffuse myocardial effect
- P-wave loss: Atrial tissue becomes electrically silent → no P-wave vector is generated → P-wave disappears simultaneously from all leads
- Sine wave: QRS and T merge because depolarization is so slow that repolarization of early-activated tissue begins before depolarization of late-activated tissue completes → a smooth, continuous undulation with no distinct QRS or T boundary in any lead
- aVR changes: Terminal R-wave in aVR develops from delayed depolarization of basal myocardium and has been shown to predict arrhythmic risk

## 2. ECG Presentation — Lead by Lead

### 2.1 Diagnostic Criteria (2025 AHA/ESC Guidelines)

| Criterion | Threshold | Source |
|-----------|-----------|--------|
| Peaked T-waves | Tall (>6mm limb, >10mm precordial), narrow base, symmetric, "tented" | AHA 2025 Electrolyte Emergency Statement |
| PR prolongation | >200 ms with progressive K+ elevation | ESC 2025 Arrhythmia Guidelines |
| P-wave loss | Complete absence of P-waves (sinoventricular rhythm) | AHA 2025 |
| QRS widening | >120 ms diffusely (non-bundle-branch pattern) | AHA 2025 |
| Sine wave | Smooth undulating pattern, no distinct QRS-T boundary | AHA 2025 |
| Terminal R-wave in aVR | R' or dominant R in aVR >3mm | Durfey et al., Am J Emerg Med 2017 |
| ECG changes correlate with K+ level | See progression below — though correlation is imperfect | AHA/ACEP consensus |
| K+ 5.5-6.0 mEq/L | Peaked T-waves (earliest sign) | AHA 2025 |
| K+ 6.0-7.0 mEq/L | PR prolongation, P-wave flattening, early QRS widening | AHA 2025 |
| K+ 7.0-8.0 mEq/L | P-wave loss, marked QRS widening, bizarre QRS | AHA 2025 |
| K+ >8.0 mEq/L | Sine wave, VF, asystole | AHA 2025 |

### 2.2 Lead-by-Lead Manifestation

| Lead | Expected Finding | Why (Vector Explanation) | Sensitivity |
|------|-----------------|------------------------|-------------|
| I | Peaked T-wave (moderate amplitude); QRS widening at higher K+ | Lead I is perpendicular to the mean T-wave axis in many patients, so T peaking may be less dramatic than in V2-V4; QRS widening is diffuse and always visible | Moderate for peaked T; High for QRS widening |
| II | Peaked T-wave (prominent); PR prolongation → P-wave loss; QRS widening | Lead II is aligned with the mean electrical axis (~+60°); T-wave vector projects strongly here; PR prolongation is easily measured in this long-axis lead | High for peaked T; High for PR/P-wave changes |
| III | Peaked T-wave (variable amplitude); P-wave loss; QRS widening | Lead III has variable T-wave amplitude at baseline; peaked T may be less obvious; P-wave changes and QRS widening still visible | Moderate for peaked T; High for QRS widening |
| aVR | Widened terminal R-wave; progressive QRS widening; peaked T inversion (positive T becomes tall upright in aVR when K+ very high) | aVR sees the heart from the right shoulder; normally QRS is mostly negative and T is negative; delayed basal depolarization creates terminal R; at extreme K+, the T-wave may become positive | High (terminal R in aVR is an independent predictor of arrhythmic events) |
| aVL | Peaked T-wave (variable); QRS widening | aVL projects leftward and superior; T-wave peaking depends on axis orientation; may show less dramatic peaking | Moderate |
| aVF | Peaked T-wave (moderate-to-prominent); PR prolongation visible; P-wave loss; QRS widening | aVF is aligned inferiorly; T-wave peaking is usually visible; this lead mirrors lead II for P-wave and PR changes | Moderate-High |
| V1 | Peaked T-wave (may be less prominent or even inverted at baseline); QRS widening with loss of normal rSR' or rS pattern → bizarre wide QRS; P-wave loss | V1 is close to the RV; T-wave is often inverted at baseline so "peaking" manifests as increased T-wave amplitude (tall negative or biphasic → tall positive); QRS widening is dramatic here | Moderate for peaked T; High for QRS widening |
| V2 | Peaked T-wave (VERY prominent — one of the key leads); QRS widening; P-wave loss | V2 overlies the interventricular septum/LV transition; normally has tall T-waves at baseline, which become exaggerated; the large T-wave vector projects maximally onto V2-V4 | Very High for peaked T |
| V3 | Peaked T-wave (VERY prominent — key lead); QRS widening; progressive morphology changes | V3 overlies the anterior LV; similar reasoning to V2 — large T-wave vector; T-wave becomes tall, narrow, tented, symmetric | Very High for peaked T |
| V4 | Peaked T-wave (prominent); QRS widening | V4 overlies the LV apex; T-wave is normally upright and moderate here; peaking is clearly visible; QRS widening affects all precordial leads | High for peaked T |
| V5 | Peaked T-wave (moderate); QRS widening; loss of normal septal Q if QRS morphology distorts | V5 is lateral; T-wave peaking is present but less dramatic than V2-V4; QRS widening is diffuse | Moderate for peaked T; High for QRS widening |
| V6 | Peaked T-wave (moderate); QRS widening; eventual sine wave | V6 is far lateral; T-wave peaking is typically less prominent than mid-precordial leads; sine wave pattern visible in all leads at extreme K+ | Moderate for peaked T; High for sine wave |

### 2.3 Key Leads (Most Diagnostic)
- **V2-V4**: Most sensitive for peaked T-waves — these leads have the largest normal T-wave amplitude and show the earliest hyperkalemic changes
- **Lead II**: Best for monitoring PR prolongation and P-wave loss (long-axis view of atrial depolarization)
- **aVR**: Terminal R-wave amplitude in aVR is an independent predictor of arrhythmia risk in hyperkalemia — often underappreciated
- **V1**: Useful for detecting bizarre QRS morphology (pseudo-RBBB or pseudo-Brugada patterns)
- **All leads (sine wave)**: At critical K+ levels, the sine wave pattern is visible in every lead — no single lead is superior

### 2.4 Beat-by-Beat Considerations
- Hyperkalemia produces CONSTANT changes across all beats — it is a metabolic (not structural or intermittent) process
- No beat-to-beat variability is expected unless hyperkalemia coexists with another arrhythmia
- Exception: If hyperkalemia triggers PVCs, there will be ectopic beats interspersed with the hyperkalemic baseline morphology
- In the transition to VF, there may be brief runs of polymorphic ventricular tachycardia before degeneration into VF
- Rate may be misleadingly normal or slow — bradycardia in the setting of peaked T-waves is ominous (K+ likely >7.0)
- During treatment with IV calcium, ECG changes may rapidly reverse beat-by-beat — this can serve as a real-time monitor of treatment efficacy

## 3. Morphology Details (What the Agent Must See)

### 3.1 P-wave Changes
- **Mild hyperK** (5.5-6.0): P-wave morphology is usually normal
- **Moderate hyperK** (6.0-7.0): P-wave amplitude decreases (flattening), P-wave duration may increase slightly
- **Severe hyperK** (7.0-8.0): P-waves disappear completely — atrial standstill. The sinus node still fires (it uses Ca2+ channels), but the atrial myocardium cannot propagate the impulse → sinoventricular conduction (impulse reaches His bundle via internodal pathways)
- **Axis**: P-wave axis is normal until P-waves are lost; no axis deviation pattern
- **Key distinction**: P-wave loss in hyperK is GLOBAL (all leads simultaneously) unlike focal atrial pathology

### 3.2 PR Interval Changes
- **Mild hyperK**: PR interval is normal
- **Moderate hyperK**: PR interval progressively prolongs beyond 200 ms — this resembles first-degree AV block but is caused by slowed atrial conduction, not AV nodal delay
- **Severe hyperK**: PR interval becomes unmeasurable because P-waves are absent
- **Pattern**: Progressive prolongation with rising K+ (not Wenckebach-type, not sudden)
- The PR prolongation in hyperK is due to diffuse atrial myocardial slowing, NOT AV nodal block

### 3.3 QRS Complex Changes
- **Duration**: Normal (<120 ms) in mild hyperK; progressively widens at K+ >6.0 mEq/L; can reach 200+ ms in severe hyperK
- **Morphology**: Widening is DIFFUSE and SYMMETRIC (not the typical RBBB or LBBB pattern) — affects all parts of the QRS simultaneously. May produce:
  - Pseudo-LBBB pattern (wide QRS with loss of septal Q, broad R in lateral leads)
  - Pseudo-RBBB pattern (wide QRS with RSR' in V1)
  - Pseudo-Brugada pattern (ST elevation in V1-V3 with wide QRS mimicking Type 1 Brugada)
  - Bizarre, unclassifiable morphology at extreme K+ levels
- **Amplitude**: QRS amplitude may initially increase slightly due to enhanced repolarization gradients, then decreases at extreme K+ as myocardial excitability fails
- **Axis**: Extreme axis deviation (right or left) can occur with severe QRS widening — the axis becomes unpredictable and should not be used for structural diagnosis in the setting of hyperK

### 3.4 ST Segment Changes
- **Direction**: ST segment changes are variable and secondary to the repolarization abnormality
- **In mild-moderate hyperK**: ST segment is typically depressed or isoelectric; the peaked T-wave may appear to arise directly from the end of the QRS with minimal ST segment
- **In severe hyperK (pseudo-STEMI)**: ST elevation can occur in V1-V3 (pseudo-Brugada) or diffusely, mimicking STEMI — this is one of the most dangerous mimics in emergency medicine
- **Morphology**: When present, ST elevation in hyperK tends to be coved (convex upward), similar to Brugada Type 1
- **Key**: ST segment changes in hyperK are always accompanied by QRS widening and T-wave peaking — isolated ST changes without these features argue against hyperK

### 3.5 T-wave Changes
- **THE hallmark of hyperkalemia**: Peaked (tented) T-waves
- **Direction**: Upright in leads where T-wave is normally upright; the T-wave becomes more prominent in its normal direction
- **Amplitude**: Tall — exceeds normal limits (>6 mm in limb leads, >10 mm in precordial leads), though absolute amplitude is less specific than morphology
- **MORPHOLOGY IS KEY**: The hyperkalemic T-wave has a NARROW BASE and SYMMETRIC ascending/descending limbs ("tented" or "tent-like"). This is in contrast to:
  - Normal tall T-waves: broad base, asymmetric (gradual rise, steeper descent)
  - Hyperacute STEMI T-waves: broad base, often asymmetric, associated with ST elevation
  - LVH tall T-waves: usually discordant to QRS, with strain pattern
- **T-wave to QRS amplitude ratio**: In hyperK, the T-wave amplitude may approach or exceed the QRS amplitude — this ratio is a useful quantitative marker
- **Progression**: As K+ rises further, the peaked T merges with the widened QRS into the sine wave pattern, and the T-wave loses its distinct peaked appearance

### 3.6 QT/QTc Changes
- QT interval is typically SHORTENED in mild-moderate hyperkalemia (K+ 5.5-7.0) due to accelerated phase 3 repolarization
- QTc shortening may be an early clue, especially when paired with peaked T-waves
- At severe hyperK (>7.0), QT measurement becomes unreliable because QRS widening distorts the boundaries of the QT interval
- At sine wave stage, QT is unmeasurable
- Torsades de Pointes is NOT a typical consequence of hyperkalemia (unlike hypokalemia) — the arrhythmic risk is VF/asystole via direct myocardial inexcitability

### 3.7 Other Features
- **U-waves**: Typically ABSENT in hyperkalemia (U-waves are a feature of HYPOkalemia); absence of U-waves in the setting of tall T-waves supports hyperK over hypoK
- **J-waves (Osborn waves)**: Not a feature of hyperK; their presence suggests hypothermia as a confounder
- **Terminal R-wave in aVR**: A distinct feature — progressive widening of the terminal portion of the QRS in aVR creates a prominent R-wave; amplitude >3 mm is associated with increased arrhythmic risk
- **Heart rate**: Often normal or bradycardic; inappropriate bradycardia for clinical context should raise suspicion for hyperK
- **Rhythm**: Sinus rhythm (with progressive changes) → sinoventricular rhythm (no P-waves but regular) → slow idioventricular rhythm → VF or asystole

## 4. Differential Diagnosis

### 4.1 Mimics (What Looks Like This But Isn't)

| Mimic Condition | Shared Features | Distinguishing Features |
|----------------|-----------------|----------------------|
| Hyperacute STEMI T-waves | Tall, prominent T-waves in precordial/inferior leads | STEMI: BROAD-based T, asymmetric, associated with ST elevation, territorial distribution (LAD/RCA/LCx territory); HyperK: NARROW-based T, symmetric ("tented"), diffuse (all territories), often with shortened QT |
| Benign early repolarization (BER) | Tall T-waves, especially in young patients; ST elevation | BER: concave ST elevation, T-waves are broad-based and asymmetric, normal QRS duration, normal P-waves; HyperK: peaked narrow T, progressive QRS widening, P-wave changes |
| Normal variant tall T-waves | Tall T-waves, especially in thin young males, V2-V4 | Normal variant: stable over time, broad-based, QRS normal width, no P-wave changes, not progressive; HyperK: narrow-based tented, progressive |
| LVH with repolarization | Tall T-waves in V5-V6 (if no strain pattern) | LVH: voltage criteria met, T-waves are secondary to hypertrophy (discordant to QRS), tall R in V5-V6; HyperK: T peaking is concordant with QRS, diffuse not just lateral |
| Posterior MI (reciprocal) | Tall T-waves in V1-V2 | Posterior MI: tall T in V1-V2 specifically (reciprocal of posterior T-wave inversion), often with ST depression in V1-V3, tall R in V1; HyperK: peaked T in V2-V4 AND limb leads, diffuse |
| Brugada Type 1 (pseudo-Brugada from hyperK) | Coved ST elevation in V1-V3, wide QRS | True Brugada: normal K+, no peaked T in other leads, RBBB morphology, changes limited to V1-V3; hyperK pseudo-Brugada: elevated K+, peaked T diffusely, QRS widening in ALL leads, resolves with calcium |
| Hypothermia | Wide QRS, bradycardia | Hypothermia: Osborn/J waves present, history of cold exposure, QT prolongation (not shortening); HyperK: no Osborn waves, peaked T, QT shortening |
| TCA overdose | Wide QRS, terminal R in aVR | TCA overdose: sinus tachycardia (not bradycardia), rightward QRS axis, R in aVR due to Na+ channel blockade; HyperK: normal or slow rate, peaked T-waves |

### 4.2 Coexisting Conditions
- **Hyperkalemia + Acute MI**: Both cause T-wave changes; peaked T of hyperK may mask hyperacute T of STEMI, or hyperacute T may be misattributed to hyperK. Key: check K+ urgently; if peaked T is territorial (not diffuse), treat as STEMI until proven otherwise
- **Hyperkalemia + Hypothermia**: Both cause QRS widening and bradycardia. Osborn waves suggest hypothermia; peaked T suggests hyperK. Can coexist (e.g., environmental exposure with rhabdomyolysis)
- **Hyperkalemia + Digoxin toxicity**: Digoxin inhibits Na+/K+-ATPase → raises extracellular K+. Bidirectional VT in the setting of peaked T-waves suggests combined toxicity
- **Hyperkalemia + Renal failure + Acidosis**: This triad is extremely common. Acidosis worsens hyperK by driving K+ out of cells. QRS widening from hyperK may be compounded by acidosis-related conduction slowing
- **Hyperkalemia + Preexisting LBBB/RBBB**: Baseline wide QRS makes it harder to detect hyperK-related QRS widening — must compare to prior ECG. Peaked T-waves remain the key clue

## 5. STAT Classification

| Priority | Criteria |
|----------|----------|
| **STAT** | YES — K+ >7.0 mEq/L with ECG changes (QRS widening, P-wave loss, sine wave) = imminent cardiac arrest. Even K+ 6.0-7.0 with peaked T and QRS widening warrants emergent treatment. |
| **Time-sensitive** | MINUTES. Sine wave pattern can degenerate to VF/asystole within minutes. Even moderate hyperK with ECG changes requires treatment within 30-60 minutes to prevent deterioration. |
| **Clinical action** | 1. **IV Calcium gluconate** (10 mL of 10% over 2-3 minutes) — membrane stabilization, works within 1-3 minutes, does NOT lower K+ but protects the heart. Repeat if ECG changes persist. 2. **Insulin 10 units IV + dextrose 25g IV** — drives K+ intracellularly, onset 15-30 min. 3. **Sodium bicarbonate** if acidotic. 4. **Albuterol** nebulized (shifts K+ intracellularly). 5. **Kayexalate/patiromer** for K+ elimination. 6. **Emergent hemodialysis** if refractory or K+ >7.0 with ECG changes. 7. Continuous cardiac monitoring. 8. Avoid any K+-containing IV fluids. |

## 6. Reasoning Complexity Analysis (Feeds Into Node 2.1 — Agent Architecture Research)

### 6.1 Reasoning Domains Required to Detect This Condition
- **Morphology comparison**: T-wave shape analysis (narrow base, symmetric, tented vs broad-based) — the CORE reasoning task
- **Interval measurement**: QT interval (shortened in early hyperK), QRS duration (widened in moderate-severe), PR interval (prolonged before P-wave loss)
- **Lead-group correlation**: Peaked T-waves should be DIFFUSE (present in multiple lead groups simultaneously) — territorial peaking argues against hyperK and for STEMI
- **Temporal/progressive pattern**: The progressive nature is diagnostic — peaked T alone is nonspecific, but peaked T + widened QRS + P-wave loss is near-pathognomonic
- **Metabolic context integration**: Clinical context (renal failure, dialysis, medications) dramatically increases pretest probability — the agent must be able to incorporate clinical metadata if available
- **Rate analysis**: Bradycardia in the context of peaked T is ominous and supports severe hyperK
- Cross-domain reasoning is ESSENTIAL: morphology (T-wave shape) + interval measurement (QRS, QT) + lead-group correlation (diffuse vs territorial) + rate analysis (bradycardia) must be synthesized together
- Sequential reasoning is required: the finding pattern is hierarchical — "IF peaked T-waves THEN check QRS width AND check P-waves AND check QT AND assess diffuse vs territorial distribution"

### 6.2 Feature Dependencies
**ESSENTIAL features (from SDA-1):**
- T-wave amplitude (per lead, absolute and relative to QRS)
- T-wave morphology: base width, symmetry index, peak sharpness
- T-wave to QRS amplitude ratio
- QRS duration (per lead and maximum)
- P-wave presence/absence (binary per lead)
- PR interval (when P-waves are present)
- QT/QTc interval
- Heart rate
- QRS morphology classification (normal vs bundle-branch pattern vs nonspecific widening)

**SUPPORTING features (increase confidence):**
- Terminal R-wave amplitude in aVR
- QRS axis (extreme axis deviation supports severe hyperK)
- T-wave amplitude change across serial ECGs (if available)
- ST segment morphology (coved elevation in V1-V3 may indicate pseudo-Brugada)
- U-wave presence (absence supports hyperK; presence supports hypoK)

**EXCLUSIONARY features (if abnormal, argue against hyperK as sole diagnosis):**
- Territorial T-wave peaking limited to one coronary territory → consider STEMI
- T-wave inversion in leads where peaking would be expected → argues against hyperK
- Normal or prolonged QT with wide QRS → consider other causes of wide QRS
- Delta waves → WPW, not hyperK

**Per-beat vs aggregate:**
- Aggregate analysis is sufficient — hyperK produces constant changes across all beats
- Per-beat analysis may be needed only to detect coexisting PVCs or transition to VF

### 6.3 Cross-Condition Interactions
- **HyperK masks STEMI**: Diffuse peaked T-waves can obscure the territorial hyperacute T-waves of early STEMI; QRS widening can mask Q waves of established MI
- **HyperK mimics Brugada**: Severe hyperK produces pseudo-Brugada pattern (coved ST elevation in V1-V3 with wide QRS) — must check K+ before diagnosing Brugada
- **HyperK mimics complete heart block**: Sinoventricular rhythm (absent P-waves with regular wide QRS) can look like third-degree AV block with ventricular escape — but in hyperK, the rate is usually faster and the rhythm responds to calcium treatment
- **HyperK + hypokalemia differential**: Opposite conditions — hypoK produces ST depression, T-wave flattening, prominent U-waves; hyperK produces peaked T, shortened QT, absent U-waves. These are mutually exclusive on ECG
- **HyperK + digoxin**: Digoxin can cause hyperK; digoxin ECG effects (scooped ST, short QT) may combine with hyperK changes in confusing ways

### 6.4 Reasoning Chain Sketch

**Minimum reasoning chain (high confidence):**
1. Detect peaked T-waves (narrow base, symmetric, tall) in V2-V4 AND limb leads (DIFFUSE) → suspect hyperK
2. Measure QRS duration — if widened (>120 ms) diffusely → strong support for hyperK
3. Check for P-waves — if absent with regular QRS → sinoventricular rhythm → very high confidence hyperK
4. → STAT alert: severe hyperkalemia

**Full reasoning chain (complete evidence assembly):**
1. T-wave morphology analysis: Calculate base width, symmetry index, peak sharpness for each lead
2. Spatial distribution: Confirm peaked T is DIFFUSE (present in ≥2 lead groups: limb + precordial)
3. QRS duration: Measure in all 12 leads; classify widening pattern (diffuse vs bundle-branch)
4. P-wave assessment: Present/absent in all leads; if present, measure PR interval
5. QT/QTc measurement: Short QTc supports early hyperK
6. Rate assessment: Bradycardia in context increases severity estimate
7. aVR analysis: Terminal R-wave amplitude — if >3 mm, flag arrhythmic risk
8. Differential exclusion:
   - If peaked T is territorial (V1-V4 only) → consider anterior STEMI
   - If peaked T has broad base → consider hyperacute STEMI T-waves
   - If V1-V3 coved ST elevation with wide QRS but no diffuse peaked T → consider true Brugada
9. Severity staging: Classify as mild (peaked T only) / moderate (+ PR prolongation or QRS widening) / severe (P-wave loss, marked QRS widening) / critical (sine wave)
10. → Generate finding with confidence score and severity stage

### 6.5 Confidence Anchors

**HIGH confidence features:**
- Peaked (narrow-based, symmetric) T-waves DIFFUSELY (multiple lead groups) — this alone gives moderate confidence
- Peaked T + QRS widening (non-bundle-branch pattern) — high confidence
- Peaked T + QRS widening + P-wave loss — very high confidence (near-pathognomonic)
- Sine wave pattern — pathognomonic

**Features that LOWER confidence:**
- Peaked T limited to a single coronary territory (V1-V4 or II/III/aVF) — favors STEMI
- Normal QRS duration with isolated peaked T — could be normal variant, BER, or early hyperK (lower severity confidence)
- Broad-based T-wave "peaking" without narrow tented morphology — may be hyperacute STEMI or normal variant
- Presence of prominent U-waves — argues against hyperK, favors hypoK

**Pathognomonic combination:**
- Sine wave pattern (smooth undulation, no distinct QRS-T boundary) = hyperkalemia until proven otherwise
- Peaked T + wide QRS + absent P-waves + bradycardia = severe hyperkalemia (near 100% specific in the right clinical context)

**Classification thresholds:**
- *Possible*: Peaked T-waves (narrow, symmetric) in ≥2 leads, diffuse distribution, normal QRS
- *Probable*: Peaked T-waves + QRS widening (>120 ms, non-bundle-branch pattern) OR peaked T + shortened QTc
- *Definite*: Peaked T + QRS widening + P-wave loss OR sine wave pattern

## 6A. Agent Assignment

**Architecture:** Option E — 3 Parallel Phase 1 Specialists + 1 Sequential CDS (Node 2.1 Gate Passed 2026-03-27)

| Agent | Role for Hyperkalemia | Key Features Used |
|-------|--------------------------|-------------------|
| **RRC** (Rhythm/Rate/Conduction) | Primary (co-primary) | PR interval prolongation (early hyperkalemia), P-wave amplitude decrease and eventual disappearance (K+ >6.5 mEq/L), QRS widening (K+ >7 mEq/L — progressive from 120 to >200 ms), sine wave morphology (severe, K+ >8–9 mEq/L), junctional or ventricular escape rate, bradycardia |
| **IT** (Ischemia/Territory) | Supporting | Peaked T-waves must NOT be classified as hyperacute STEMI; IT must suppress territorial ischemia calls when peaked T-waves are diffuse; flags for CDS if peaked T-waves are limited to a single territory (raises STEMI suspicion) |
| **MR** (Morphology/Repolarization) | Primary (co-primary) | Peaked (tented) T-wave morphology — narrow-based, symmetric, tall (>6 mm precordial, >5 mm limb), T/R ratio assessment, QRS morphology widening pattern (diffuse, not LBBB/RBBB specific), sine wave recognition, T-wave amplitude and base width measurement across all leads |
| **CDS** (Cross-Domain Synthesis) | Required — CRITICAL: resolves peaked T-waves as hyperkalemia vs hyperacute STEMI | Integrates MR's T-wave morphology (narrow base = hyperkalemia; broad base = hyperacute) with IT's lead distribution analysis (diffuse = hyperkalemia; territorial V1–V4 or II/III/aVF = ischemia) and RRC's PR/QRS widening; applies progressive severity staging |

### Primary Agent
Both **MR** and **RRC** are co-primary: MR owns peaked T-wave morphology and QRS widening pattern, while RRC owns PR prolongation, P-wave disappearance, and the progressive conduction sequence.

### Cross-Domain Hints
- MR emits `cross_domain_hint: "Peaked narrow-based T-waves detected across multiple lead groups — IT must assess territorial distribution to distinguish hyperkalemia from hyperacute STEMI"` when tall symmetric T-waves are identified.
- IT emits `cross_domain_hint: "Peaked T-waves are territorially limited to anterior or inferior leads — hyperacute STEMI must be considered; CDS required for distribution-based differentiation"` when peaked T-waves are not diffuse.

### CDS Specific Role
CDS resolves the critical dual diagnosis of peaked T-waves as either hyperkalemia or hyperacute STEMI. The decision algorithm: if peaked T-waves are DIFFUSE (all leads) AND P-wave amplitude is reduced AND PR is prolonged AND QRS is widening → hyperkalemia. If peaked T-waves are TERRITORIAL (V1–V4 or II/III/aVF) AND P-waves and PR intervals are normal AND no QRS widening → hyperacute STEMI. CDS applies this distribution-plus-conduction integration and generates a severity-staged hyperkalemia diagnosis (mild/moderate/severe/critical) based on the combination of findings from RRC and MR.

## 9. Edge Cases and Pitfalls

- **Hyperkalemia mimicking STEMI**: Severe hyperK can produce ST elevation (especially in V1-V3) that triggers a false STEMI alert. Key differentiators: diffuse QRS widening (not territorial), peaked T in non-STEMI leads, absence of reciprocal changes in a STEMI pattern, clinical context (renal failure). A stat K+ level is mandatory before cath lab activation if the pattern is atypical.
- **Hyperkalemia mimicking Brugada (pseudo-Brugada)**: Coved ST elevation in V1-V3 with wide QRS from hyperK is well-documented. The entire pattern resolves with calcium administration and K+ correction. Always check K+ before diagnosing new Brugada.
- **Chronic hyperkalemia in dialysis patients**: Patients with ESRD on dialysis may have chronically elevated K+ (5.5-6.5) with adapted ECG. Their "baseline" ECG may show mild peaked T-waves. New changes (QRS widening, P-wave loss) on top of chronic peaked T are the actionable findings.
- **Hyperkalemia + hypothermia**: Both cause QRS widening and bradycardia. Look for Osborn/J waves (hypothermia) vs peaked T (hyperK). They can coexist in environmental exposure victims with rhabdomyolysis.
- **Normal variant tall T-waves in young males**: Precordial T-waves in healthy young men (especially thin body habitus) can be tall — but they are BROAD-based and ASYMMETRIC, not narrow and tented. QRS duration is normal, P-waves are normal, and QT is normal.
- **Rate-K+ dissociation**: ECG changes do not always correlate perfectly with serum K+ levels. Patients with rapid K+ rise show ECG changes at lower absolute levels than patients with chronic elevation. Acidosis, hypocalcemia, and hyponatremia potentiate the ECG effects of hyperK at any given K+ level.
- **Pseudohyperkalemia**: Hemolyzed blood sample gives falsely elevated K+ — the ECG will be NORMAL. Always correlate lab values with ECG findings.
- **Hyperkalemia in the presence of pacemaker**: Paced rhythms obscure native QRS and T-wave morphology. Pacemaker output may fail in severe hyperK as the stimulation threshold rises. Failure to capture in a pacemaker-dependent patient with hyperK is a distinct emergency.
- **Drug-induced hyperkalemia superimposed on Na+ channel blockade**: Patients on drugs that block Na+ channels (flecainide, procainamide) who develop hyperK will have amplified QRS widening — the effects are synergistic and the arrhythmic risk is compounded.
- **Pediatric considerations**: Children have higher normal T-wave amplitudes, especially in V2-V4. The T-wave morphology (narrow base, symmetric) is more important than absolute amplitude for hyperK diagnosis in pediatric patients.
