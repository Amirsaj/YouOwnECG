## 1. Pathophysiology Root Cause

### 1.1 What Goes Wrong (Anatomy/Physiology)
Arrhythmogenic right ventricular cardiomyopathy (ARVC) is a heritable myocardial disease caused by mutations in desmosomal proteins that maintain cell-to-cell adhesion in cardiomyocytes. The key genes involved are:
- **PKP2** (plakophilin-2): most common (~40% of mutation-positive cases); anchors desmosomal complex
- **DSP** (desmoplakin): second most common; linker between desmosome and intermediate filaments
- **DSG2** (desmoglein-2): desmosomal cadherin; structural role
- **DSC2** (desmocollin-2): desmosomal cadherin
- **JUP** (junction plakoglobin): Naxos disease (ARVC + palmoplantar keratoderma + woolly hair)
- **TMEM43**, **RYR2**, **LMNA**: non-desmosomal causes

When desmosomal proteins are dysfunctional:
- Cardiomyocytes detach under mechanical stress (exercise) → cell death
- Dead cardiomyocytes are replaced by **fibrofatty tissue** (fibrous tissue + adipocytes)
- This replacement preferentially affects the RV free wall, RV inflow tract, RV apex, and RV outflow tract (the "triangle of dysplasia")
- The LV may also be involved, especially in DSP and FLNC mutations (predominantly left-dominant ARVC variant)
- Fibrofatty replacement creates:
  - Electrically silent zones (scar) → Q waves or delayed activation
  - Interfaces between viable myocardium and scar → arrhythmia substrate (re-entry circuits)
  - RV dilation and dysfunction (progressive)

### 1.2 Electrical Consequence
Fibrofatty replacement of the RV myocardium creates several electrical phenomena:
- **Slow conduction through fibrofatty tissue**: islands of surviving RV myocardium conduct slowly through fibrous matrix → **delayed RV terminal activation**
- **Epsilon wave**: the last, tiny electrical signal from these slow-conducting RV muscle islands emerges after the main QRS has ended — a small positive notch visible in V1-V3 after the S wave
- **T-wave inversions V1-V3**: abnormal RV repolarization from diseased myocardium; when the QRS is not widened, this is a major diagnostic criterion
- **Terminal activation duration prolongation**: time from S-wave nadir to end of QRS in V1-V3 ≥55 ms — reflects delayed terminal RV activation
- **RBBB pattern**: as more RV myocardium is replaced, the RV bundle branch and free wall conduction fails → incomplete or complete RBBB
- **VT with LBBB morphology**: the arrhythmia originates in the RV (diseased RV substrate); an impulse originating in the RV activates the LV last → LBBB morphology on surface ECG

### 1.3 Why It Appears on ECG
The epsilon wave represents survival of a small mass of RV myocardium surrounded by fibrofatty tissue, conducting very slowly and activating after the main QRS — a micro-potential visible at the end of QRS in V1-V2. It is small, often only 1–2 mm in amplitude, appearing as a notch or blip after the QRS ends in the right precordial leads. Signal-averaged ECG (SAECG) amplifies these late potentials and is more sensitive than the surface ECG.

## 2. ECG Presentation — Lead by Lead

### 2.1 Diagnostic Criteria (2025 AHA/ESC Guidelines — Based on 2010 Task Force Criteria, Updated 2019)

| Criterion | Threshold | Source |
|---|---|---|
| Epsilon wave | Small positive deflection after QRS end in V1-V3 (major criterion) | 2010 ARVC Task Force Criteria; Marcus 2010 |
| T-wave inversions V1-V3 (with QRS <120 ms) | T-wave inversion beyond V3 in absence of RBBB — major criterion | 2010 Task Force |
| T-wave inversions V1-V2 | Minor criterion (patients >14 years, without RBBB) | 2010 Task Force |
| Terminal activation duration (TAD) V1-V3 | ≥55 ms from S-wave nadir to end of QRS — minor criterion | 2010 Task Force |
| Signal-averaged ECG late potentials | Filtered QRS ≥114 ms; OR terminal RMS voltage <20 μV; OR low-amplitude signal duration ≥38 ms | 2010 Task Force |
| VT with LBBB morphology | Right-axis deviation or left-axis deviation LBBB-morphology VT — minor or major criterion depending on imaging correlation | 2010 Task Force |
| Incomplete RBBB | QRS 100–119 ms with rSR' pattern in V1 | Minor criterion |

### 2.2 Lead-by-Lead Manifestation

| Lead | Expected Finding | Why | Sensitivity |
|---|---|---|---|
| I | Normal or broad R (RBBB pattern in late disease) | LV activation normal unless RBBB; I is not an RV-facing lead | Low |
| II | Non-specific; possible T inversion in inferior extension | Inferior RV involvement | Low-Moderate |
| III | Q wave or T inversion if inferior RV involvement | Inferior RV wall — triangle of dysplasia can extend here | Low |
| aVR | QR or QS (RBBB pattern) | Reciprocal to terminal RV activation forces | Low |
| aVL | Normal or S wave (RBBB) | Lateral lead; less RV-facing | Low |
| aVF | T inversion if inferior RV involvement | Inferior RV wall | Low-Moderate |
| V1 | **Epsilon wave** (after QRS, before T wave); rSR' or QR; T inversion; prolonged TAD | RV free wall slow activation projects anteriorly and rightward → V1 and V2 are closest to RV | High (most specific) |
| V2 | **Epsilon wave** most visible here; T inversion; prolonged TAD | Same reason; V2 also faces anterior RV | High |
| V3 | T inversion (major criterion if QRS <120 ms); possible epsilon | T inversions extending to V3 = major criterion; epsilon less visible | High |
| V4 | T inversion (minor criterion); may extend here in severe disease | Extension of RV repolarization abnormality | Moderate |
| V5 | T inversion if LV involved (DSP, FLNC variant) | LV involvement in left-dominant ARVC | Low-Moderate |
| V6 | T inversion if LV involvement | LV involvement | Low |

### 2.3 Key Leads (Most Diagnostic)
- **V1, V2**: Epsilon wave — the pathognomonic finding (present in only ~30% but highly specific)
- **V1-V3**: T-wave inversions — most sensitive finding; present in most symptomatic ARVC patients
- **V1-V3**: Terminal activation duration ≥55 ms — minor criterion but easily measurable
- **Rhythm strip**: LBBB-morphology VT — origin in diseased RV

### 2.4 Beat-by-Beat Considerations
- **Exercise-induced VT**: classic trigger; mechanical stress during exercise causes further desmosome disruption and arrhythmia; the hallmark clinical presentation
- **PVCs with LBBB morphology**: common; can be thousands per day; frequent LBBB-morphology PVCs on Holter are a diagnostic clue
- **VF**: the mechanism of SCD in ARVC — often exercise-triggered in young patients
- **Epsilon wave may only be visible in some beats**: if there is beat-to-beat variation in RV activation, epsilon waves may be intermittent

## 3. Morphology Details

### 3.1 P-wave Changes
- Typically normal
- May show RA enlargement if significant RV dilation raises RA pressure
- Biatrial enlargement in biventricular (left-dominant) ARVC

### 3.2 PR Interval Changes
- Usually normal
- Not typically a primary ARVC feature
- AV conduction disease is not characteristic (distinguishes from sarcoidosis, which also causes VT + Q waves)

### 3.3 QRS Complex Changes
- **Incomplete RBBB**: rSR' in V1 with QRS 100–119 ms; reflects partial failure of RV conduction
- **Complete RBBB**: QRS ≥120 ms with rSR' in V1; in advanced disease
- **Epsilon wave**: small positive deflection AFTER the S wave ends in V1-V2, before the T wave; the most specific ARVC ECG feature; looks like a "blip" or "notch" at the QRS-ST junction; only ~30% sensitivity
- **Prolonged terminal activation duration (TAD)**: measured from S-wave nadir to end of ALL depolarization activity in V1, V2, or V3; ≥55 ms = minor criterion
- **Fragmented QRS**: multiple notches within QRS in V1-V3; reflects RV conduction heterogeneity

### 3.4 ST Segment Changes
- ST changes are generally not a primary feature
- Slight ST elevation in V1-V3 may appear in the setting of RBBB (expected secondary change)
- The area between QRS end and T-wave onset in V1-V2 is where the epsilon wave sits — subtle ST "elevation" at the J-point may represent misidentified epsilon wave

### 3.5 T-wave Changes
- **T-wave inversions V1-V3** (with QRS <120 ms): the most sensitive ARVC ECG finding; present in ~60–70% of affected patients
- **Major criterion**: T inversions in V1-V3 (or beyond, to V4-V6 in some morphologies) when QRS <120 ms
- **Minor criterion**: T inversions in V1-V2 only
- T inversions reflect abnormal repolarization in diseased RV myocardium — the fibrofatty replacement prolongs and distorts RV repolarization
- In normal individuals: T-wave inversions in V1-V2 can be a normal variant (especially in women and young patients) — requires careful context interpretation

### 3.6 QT/QTc Changes
- Not a primary ARVC criterion
- QTc may be normal or mildly prolonged
- Extreme QTc prolongation is not typical — distinguishes from Long QT syndrome-related SCD risk

### 3.7 Other Features
- **Signal-averaged ECG (SAECG) late potentials**: amplified recording of micro-potentials at QRS end; positive SAECG is a major diagnostic criterion; not performed from standard ECG
- **LBBB-morphology VT**: on rhythm strip or during Holter; inferior axis VT = RVOT origin; superior axis VT = RV inferior wall origin; this axis variation helps localize the arrhythmia substrate
- **Frequent PVCs**: LBBB morphology on rhythm strip; may be thousands per day; isolated LBBB-morphology PVCs with inferior axis = RVOT origin; raise ARVC suspicion in young patient

## 4. Differential Diagnosis

### 4.1 Mimics

| Mimic Condition | Shared Features | Distinguishing Features |
|---|---|---|
| Normal variant T inversions V1-V2 | T inversions in right precordial leads | Normal: T inversions only V1-V2; no epsilon wave; no TAD prolongation; no arrhythmia; common in young women and children |
| RBBB (incomplete or complete) | rSR' V1; T inversions V1-V3 | RBBB: expected secondary T inversions in V1-V3; QRS ≥120 ms (complete); no epsilon wave; no RV structural disease |
| RVH | T inversions V1-V3; RAD; right-axis changes | RVH: from pressure overload (pulmonary hypertension); no epsilon wave; no LBBB-morphology VT; echo shows RV hypertrophy not dilation |
| Cardiac sarcoidosis | VT; AV block; Q waves; T inversions | Sarcoid: AV block common (distinguishes from ARVC); VT often LBBB or RBBB morphology; cardiac MRI shows patchy LGE in mid-wall; systemic features |
| Brugada syndrome | T inversions + J-point elevation V1-V3; VF risk | Brugada: ST elevation (coved or saddleback pattern) not T inversion; sodium channel mutations (SCN5A); fever-induced; no RV structural disease |
| Idiopathic RVOT VT | LBBB-morphology VT | Benign; no structural RV disease; normal ECG at rest; no epsilon wave; no T inversions; catecholamine-sensitive; responds to beta-blockers/verapamil |
| Myocarditis | T inversions; RV/LV dysfunction; VT | Myocarditis: acute; viral prodrome; elevated troponin; cardiac MRI shows LGE; reversible in many cases |

### 4.2 Coexisting Conditions
- **Desmosomal mutations with LV involvement**: DSP and FLNC mutations → left-dominant ARVC; LV fibrosis; LV T inversions; mimics DCM
- **Family members**: ARVC is autosomal dominant with variable penetrance; family screening ECG and echo is mandatory
- **AF/flutter**: may develop in late ARVC with significant RV dilation

## 5. STAT Classification

| Priority | Criteria |
|---|---|
| STAT (Immediate) | ARVC + sustained VT or VF → electrical storm; cardiac arrest in young patient with LBBB-morphology VT |
| STAT (Immediate) | Syncope during exercise in young patient with T inversions V1-V3 and LBBB-morphology PVCs — ARVC SCD risk |
| Urgent | New epsilon wave or T inversions V1-V3 in young patient with frequent LBBB PVCs — requires cardiology evaluation and cardiac MRI within 24-48 hours |
| Urgent | Known ARVC with new frequent PVCs or NSVT on rhythm strip |
| Routine | Known ARVC on stable monitoring without arrhythmia progression |

## 6. Reasoning Complexity Analysis (Feeds Into Node 2.1 — Agent Architecture Research)
> NOTE: Does NOT pre-assign agents. Documents reasoning complexity for Node 2.1.

### 6.1 Reasoning Domains Required
- Epsilon wave detection: sub-millimeter amplitude signal at QRS-ST junction in V1-V2; requires high-resolution signal analysis; heavily affected by noise and baseline artifact
- T-wave inversion characterization: V1-V3 distribution; confirm QRS <120 ms (to exclude RBBB-secondary changes)
- Terminal activation duration (TAD) measurement: from S-wave nadir to last QRS deflection in V1-V3; requires precise endpoint detection
- QRS morphology: RBBB pattern classification (incomplete vs. complete)
- VT morphology analysis: LBBB vs. RBBB morphology; axis classification (inferior vs. superior)
- Differential exclusion: normal V1-V2 T inversions (women, athletes) vs. ARVC; Brugada vs. ARVC; RBBB secondary changes vs. primary T inversions

### 6.2 Feature Dependencies
- Epsilon wave detection is the hardest signal quality challenge in ECG analysis — requires low-noise, high-sampling-rate acquisition; standard 12-lead ECGs may not capture it reliably
- T-wave inversions in V1-V3 are only a major criterion when QRS <120 ms; requires prior RBBB status determination
- TAD measurement requires identifying S-wave nadir (V1) and the absolute end of all QRS activity — susceptible to noise artifacts being misidentified as late potentials
- VT morphology requires arrhythmia to be present during recording

### 6.3 Cross-Condition Interactions
- **RBBB + T inversions**: RBBB makes T inversions V1-V3 expected (secondary) — removes their diagnostic value; ARVC must be identified from other criteria
- **RVOT VT vs. ARVC VT**: cannot be distinguished from VT morphology alone — requires structural imaging; ARVC has structural RV abnormalities
- **Left-dominant ARVC (DSP)**: ECG may show lateral T inversions and LV features; may be misidentified as DCM or myocarditis
- **Brugada overlap**: some ARVC mutations (especially DSP) can have overlapping J-point elevation patterns

### 6.4 Reasoning Chain Sketch
1. Detect rhythm — PVCs present? LBBB morphology? VT on strip?
2. Measure QRS duration — <120 ms? ≥120 ms?
3. Characterize QRS morphology — RBBB? (rSR' V1, terminal R in V1)
4. If QRS <120 ms: assess T waves in V1-V3 — inverted? depth?
5. If T inversions V1-V3 + QRS <120 ms → major criterion for ARVC
6. Scan V1-V2 for epsilon wave: small positive deflection after QRS ends, before T wave onset
7. Measure TAD in V1-V3: S-wave nadir to last deflection — flag if ≥55 ms
8. If VT present: characterize LBBB morphology + axis (inferior axis = RVOT; superior = inferior RV wall)
9. Apply 2010 Task Force scoring
10. Flag differential: RBBT, normal variant, Brugada, sarcoidosis
11. State clearly: ARVC is a multi-modality diagnosis — ECG is one criterion; cardiac MRI + echo required

### 6.5 Confidence Anchors
- High confidence: epsilon wave + T inversions V1-V3 (QRS <120 ms) + LBBB-morphology VT + family history
- Moderate confidence: T inversions V1-V3 alone + frequent LBBB PVCs — needs further evaluation
- Low confidence: isolated TAD prolongation or incomplete RBBB — minor criteria only; insufficient alone
- Uncertainty triggers: epsilon wave is extremely noise-sensitive; absent epsilon does NOT exclude ARVC (only 30% sensitive); RBBB present (negates T-inversion major criterion)

## 6A. Agent Assignment

**Architecture:** Option E — 3 Parallel Phase 1 Specialists + 1 Sequential CDS (Node 2.1 Gate Passed 2026-03-27)

| Agent | Role for Arrhythmogenic Right Ventricular Cardiomyopathy | Key Features Used |
|-------|--------------------------|-------------------|
| **RRC** (Rhythm/Rate/Conduction) | Supporting | Right-sided arrhythmias: PVCs with LBBB morphology (right ventricular origin — QRS in V1 is predominantly negative, like LBBB); non-sustained or sustained VT with LBBB morphology; right precordial QRS duration prolongation ≥110ms in V1 (without meeting complete RBBB criteria) |
| **IT** (Ischemia/Territory) | Supporting — must NOT independently classify ARVC right precordial T-wave inversions as anterior ischemia | T-wave inversions in V1-V4 detected as a finding; emits cross_domain_hint to CDS to prevent anterior ischemia misclassification; does not classify independently |
| **MR** (Morphology/Repolarization) | Primary | Epsilon waves: low-amplitude notch or deflection at the terminal QRS-ST junction in V1-V3 (pathognomonic for ARVC — represents delayed RV depolarization from fibrofatty replacement); T-wave inversions in V1-V4 (right precordial leads, beyond V1 in patients >14 years without RBBB); QRS duration ≥110ms in V1-V3 localized (not meeting complete RBBB); S-wave upstroke duration ≥55ms in V1-V3 (SAECG-equivalent indicator); Fontaine lead sensitivity for epsilon waves |
| **CDS** (Cross-Domain Synthesis) | Required — CRITICAL: integrates epsilon waves + right precordial T inversions + right-sided arrhythmias for ARVC suspicion flag | Receives MR epsilon wave + T-wave inversion findings + RRC right-sided PVC/VT arrhythmia origin; if epsilon waves in V1-V3 AND T-wave inversions V1-V4 AND right-sided PVCs (LBBB morphology) are all present, CDS generates ARVC high-suspicion flag; IT T-wave inversion suppression required (these are not anterior ischemia) |

### Primary Agent
**MR** — ARVC's most specific ECG findings are morphology features: epsilon waves in V1-V3 (pathognomonic), T-wave inversions in right precordial leads, and localized QRS prolongation in V1-V3; all are in the MR domain.

### Cross-Domain Hints
- MR emits: `cross_domain_hint: "Epsilon waves detected in V1-V3 and/or T-wave inversions in V1-V4 — IT must not classify right precordial T-wave inversions as anterior ischemia; RRC must evaluate for right-sided PVCs (LBBB morphology); CDS must integrate all three findings for ARVC suspicion flag"`
- RRC emits (if LBBB-morphology PVCs): `cross_domain_hint: "PVCs with LBBB morphology detected (right ventricular origin) — CDS must integrate with MR epsilon waves and right precordial T inversions for ARVC diagnostic criteria"`
- IT emits: `cross_domain_hint: "T-wave inversions V1-V4 detected — CDS must determine if this represents ARVC right precordial repolarization abnormality (MR epsilon waves + RRC right-sided arrhythmia) vs anterior ischemia"`

### CDS Specific Role
CDS is the critical integrator for ARVC, which requires multi-domain findings to reach diagnostic suspicion. CDS receives: (1) MR epsilon waves in V1-V3 (low-amplitude terminal QRS notch — pathognomonic), (2) MR T-wave inversions in V1-V4, (3) RRC right-sided PVCs or VT with LBBB morphology, and (4) IT T-wave inversion detection (which must be suppressed as ischemia). When epsilon waves + right precordial T inversions beyond V1 + right-sided arrhythmias are all present, CDS generates a high-suspicion ARVC flag and recommends cardiac MRI and electrophysiology evaluation. CDS suppresses the IT anterior ischemia interpretation. If only T-wave inversions are present without epsilon waves or right-sided arrhythmias, CDS generates a lower-suspicion ARVC note requiring further investigation.

## 9. Edge Cases and Pitfalls

- **Epsilon wave is the hardest finding in ECG analysis**: only ~30% sensitivity; its absence does NOT exclude ARVC; signal quality must be excellent; noise can create false epsilon-like artifacts
- **T inversions in V1-V2 as normal variant**: common in young women, children, athletes — context matters; if isolated V1-V2 inversions without other criteria, low suspicion; V3 inversion with QRS <120 ms significantly raises likelihood
- **RBBB invalidates T-inversion major criterion**: if RBBB present, T inversions in V1-V3 are expected secondary changes — cannot be used as major ARVC criterion; the diagnosis must rely on other criteria
- **Epsilon wave vs. artifact**: right precordial leads are prone to respiratory and muscle artifact; a true epsilon wave is a consistent, reproducible deflection present in multiple beats; artifact is inconsistent
- **Exercise test is diagnostic and provocative**: exercise-induced LBBB-morphology VT is a major feature; resting ECG may be normal in early ARVC
- **Left-dominant ARVC (DSP mutations)**: T inversions may be in lateral leads (V4-V6, I, aVL) rather than right precordial — mimics DCM or myocarditis; check for concurrent RV features
- **Naxos disease**: JUP mutation; autosomal recessive; presents with ARVC + woolly hair + palmoplantar keratoderma — skin findings are a clinical clue
- **Athletic training and ARVC**: endurance athletes have higher rates of epsilon waves and T inversions due to RV remodeling from exercise; ARVC must be distinguished from athlete's heart — family history, genetic testing, and MRI are key
