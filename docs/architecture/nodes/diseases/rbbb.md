## 1. Pathophysiology Root Cause

### 1.1 What Goes Wrong (Anatomy/Physiology)
- The right bundle branch is a long, thin fascicle that runs along the right side of the interventricular septum before fanning out into the right ventricular (RV) myocardium via Purkinje fibers
- In RBBB, conduction through this fascicle is delayed (incomplete RBBB, 100-119ms) or completely blocked (complete RBBB, ≥120ms)
- The block can occur at any level: proximal (main bundle), distal, or diffuse within the RV Purkinje network
- Because the right bundle is a single, long structure (unlike the left bundle which fans into two fascicles), it is vulnerable to stretch, ischemia, or surgical trauma
- Common causes: normal variant (especially young, athletic individuals), pulmonary embolism, atrial septal defect, right heart strain, anterior MI (LAD supplies proximal right bundle), cardiomyopathy, post-cardiac surgery (right ventriculotomy), myocarditis, degenerative fibrosis (Lenegre/Lev disease)

### 1.2 Electrical Consequence
- Septal depolarization proceeds normally (left-to-right via left bundle) — this preserves the initial septal vector
- The left ventricle (LV) depolarizes normally via the intact left bundle branch and its fascicles
- The RV depolarizes LATE — after LV depolarization is complete — via slow myocyte-to-myocyte spread from the septum rightward
- This delayed RV depolarization creates a TERMINAL vector directed rightward and anteriorly (toward V1, away from I and V6)
- Net result: QRS is widened (≥120ms) with a terminal rightward/anterior force that is unopposed by simultaneous LV activity
- Repolarization is secondarily altered in RV leads (V1-V3): ST depression and T-wave inversion are expected (discordant to the terminal R')

### 1.3 Why It Appears on ECG
- **Initial vector (normal):** Left-to-right septal depolarization → small r in V1, small q in V5-V6 (preserved because left bundle is intact)
- **Middle vector (normal):** LV free wall depolarization dominates → S wave in V1 (vector moving away from V1), R wave in I and V6 (vector moving toward lateral leads)
- **Terminal vector (abnormal):** Delayed RV depolarization → large, broad R' in V1-V2 (vector moving toward these anterior/rightward leads), broad S wave in I and V6 (vector moving away from leftward leads)
- The "M-shaped" rSR' in V1 is the hallmark: r = septal, S = LV, R' = delayed RV — three distinct sequential events now visible because they no longer overlap temporally

## 2. ECG Presentation — Lead by Lead

### 2.1 Diagnostic Criteria (2025 AHA/ESC Guidelines)

| Criterion | Threshold | Source |
|-----------|-----------|--------|
| QRS duration | ≥120 ms (complete); 100-119 ms (incomplete) | AHA/ACC/HRS 2018, ESC 2022 |
| V1-V2 morphology | rSR', rsR', or broad notched R wave | AHA/ACC/HRS 2018 |
| Lateral S wave | Wide, slurred S wave in leads I and V6 | AHA/ACC/HRS 2018 |
| ST-T changes | Secondary repolarization abnormality (ST depression, T inversion) in V1-V3 | Expected finding, not required for diagnosis |

All three primary criteria (QRS ≥120ms, rSR' in V1-V2, wide S in I/V6) must be present for complete RBBB.

### 2.2 Lead-by-Lead Manifestation

| Lead | Expected Finding | Why (Vector Explanation) | Sensitivity |
|------|-----------------|------------------------|-------------|
| I | Normal initial R, followed by wide slurred S wave | Terminal RV vector directed rightward (away from lead I at 0°); S wave represents the late, unopposed RV depolarization | High — wide S in I is a required criterion |
| II | Small terminal S wave possible; overall morphology relatively preserved | Lead II (+60°) is partially perpendicular to the rightward terminal vector; mild terminal negativity from the anterior/rightward RV force | Moderate |
| III | Small terminal R or r' possible; variable | Lead III (+120°) has a rightward component; may pick up some terminal positivity from the rightward RV vector | Low — variable presentation |
| aVR | Wide QR or qR pattern; terminal R wave | aVR (-150°) faces the cavity; terminal rightward vector projects partially toward aVR, producing a late R wave | Moderate |
| aVL | Normal initial R, terminal S wave | aVL (-30°) is leftward/superior; terminal rightward vector moves away from aVL, producing terminal S | Moderate |
| aVF | Small terminal S wave possible; relatively normal morphology | aVF (+90°) is inferior; the anteriorly-directed terminal vector is mostly perpendicular to aVF | Low |
| V1 | **rSR' or rsR' ("M-shaped", "rabbit ears")** — classic pattern; sometimes broad monophasic R | r = normal left-to-right septal depolarization (toward V1); S = LV depolarization (away from V1); R' = delayed RV depolarization (directly toward V1). This is the MOST diagnostic lead. | Very High — most specific finding |
| V2 | rSR' similar to V1, R' may be taller; sometimes RSR' | Same mechanism as V1; V2 is slightly more leftward so may pick up more LV voltage in the middle portion | High |
| V3 | Transition zone; may show rSR' (attenuated) or normal RS with mild terminal slurring | V3 is transitional; the rightward terminal vector is less directly toward V3 than V1-V2 | Moderate |
| V4 | Relatively normal RS; possible mild terminal S slurring | V4 is near the transition zone; LV forces dominate, terminal RV vector contribution diminishes | Low |
| V5 | qRS pattern with wide, slurred terminal S wave | Normal septal q preserved (left bundle intact); R from LV depolarization; terminal S from delayed RV vector moving away from V5 | High |
| V6 | **qRS with wide, slurred S wave** | Same as V5 — the wide terminal S is a required diagnostic criterion; represents the late RV vector moving away from the lateral leads | Very High — required criterion |

### 2.3 Key Leads (Most Diagnostic)
- **V1** — the single most diagnostic lead; rSR' pattern is the hallmark of RBBB
- **V2** — confirmatory; should show similar rSR' morphology
- **I and V6** — required for the wide S wave criterion; confirms the terminal rightward vector
- **Earliest change detection:** QRS widening is visible across all leads simultaneously, but the rSR' in V1 and wide S in I/V6 are the most immediately recognizable patterns
- **No reciprocal changes** — RBBB is a conduction abnormality, not an ischemic event; "reciprocal" does not apply in the traditional sense

### 2.4 Beat-by-Beat Considerations
- Complete RBBB is typically constant across all beats (fixed conduction block)
- **Rate-dependent (intermittent) RBBB:** RBBB that appears only at higher heart rates when the right bundle's refractory period is exceeded; disappears at slower rates. Important to recognize because it is not structural.
- **Alternating bundle branch block (RBBB alternating with LBBB):** Ominous — indicates bilateral conduction disease with high risk of complete heart block
- **Post-extrasystolic beats:** A premature beat may be followed by an RBBB-pattern beat (Ashman phenomenon — more common for RBBB morphology in atrial fibrillation due to longer refractory period of the right bundle)
- Most diagnostic: consistent beats at baseline rate; rate-dependent RBBB should be noted when present

## 3. Morphology Details (What the Agent Must See)

### 3.1 P-wave Changes
- Morphology: Normal (RBBB does not affect atrial conduction)
- Duration: Normal (unless concurrent atrial enlargement from the underlying cause, e.g., ASD → right atrial enlargement)
- Axis: Normal
- Note: If P-waves are absent or abnormal, this is from a coexisting condition (e.g., atrial fibrillation, atrial flutter), not from RBBB itself

### 3.2 PR Interval Changes
- Duration: Normal in isolated RBBB
- If PR is prolonged: suggests additional first-degree AV block (conduction disease may involve AV node or left bundle too)
- Pattern: Constant (1:1 P-QRS relationship preserved)
- Prolonged PR + RBBB + left fascicular block = trifascicular disease (high risk of complete heart block)

### 3.3 QRS Complex Changes
- Duration: ≥120 ms (complete RBBB); 100-119 ms (incomplete RBBB)
- Morphology: rSR' or rsR' in V1-V2; wide slurred S in I, V5-V6; the terminal portion of QRS is prolonged (initial activation is normal)
- Amplitude: R' in V1 is typically prominent (often taller than initial r); voltage criteria for LVH remain generally applicable (unlike LBBB) because initial and middle QRS vectors are preserved
- Axis: May cause mild right axis deviation due to terminal rightward forces, but RBBB alone usually does not shift axis significantly. If marked RAD (+120° or beyond): consider RVH coexisting. If marked LAD: consider coexisting left anterior fascicular block (bifascicular block).

### 3.4 ST Segment Changes
- Direction: ST depression in V1-V3 (secondary to altered repolarization)
- Morphology: Downsloping or horizontal ST depression in V1-V3
- These are **expected secondary repolarization changes** — they do NOT indicate ischemia
- **CRITICAL:** ST changes in RBBB are confined to V1-V3 (rightward/anterior leads). ST elevation or depression in leads I, II, aVL, aVF, V4-V6 should be evaluated normally for ischemia. RBBB does NOT mask STEMI in the lateral, inferior, or anterolateral distributions.
- Measurement: J-point assessment in V1-V3 must account for the secondary changes; in other leads, standard J-point criteria apply

### 3.5 T-wave Changes
- Direction: T-wave inversion in V1-V3 (secondary, expected in RBBB)
- Amplitude: Moderate inversion, asymmetric
- Symmetry: Asymmetric T-wave inversion in V1-V3 is typical (secondary); if symmetric deep T inversions appear, consider ischemia or PE superimposed
- T-waves in lateral leads (I, V5-V6) should be upright and normal; if inverted, suggests a separate pathology
- Specific patterns: None unique beyond the secondary repolarization abnormality

### 3.6 QT/QTc Changes
- QT interval: May appear prolonged due to QRS widening, but QTc should be measured using the JT interval (QT minus QRS) or a formula corrected for QRS width
- Clinical significance: The widened QRS artificially prolongs the QT; true repolarization duration is usually normal in isolated RBBB
- Use JTc (JT corrected) to assess true repolarization time when QRS is wide
- Torsades de Pointes risk: Not directly increased by RBBB itself

### 3.7 Other Features
- No U-waves, Osborn waves, epsilon waves, or delta waves specific to RBBB
- Look for secondary clues to etiology: right atrial enlargement (peaked P in II > 2.5mm → PE or ASD), RV strain pattern (S1Q3T3 → PE), right axis deviation (RVH)
- Condition-specific marker: The "terminal conduction delay" — the last 40ms of the QRS is directed rightward/anteriorly, distinguishing RBBB from other causes of QRS widening

## 4. Differential Diagnosis

### 4.1 Mimics (What Looks Like This But Isn't)

| Mimic Condition | Shared Features | Distinguishing Features |
|----------------|-----------------|----------------------|
| Brugada syndrome (Type 1) | rSR'-like pattern in V1-V2, ST changes in V1-V3 | Brugada has COVED ST elevation (≥2mm) descending into inverted T-wave in V1-V3; no wide S in I/V6; QRS may not be ≥120ms; characteristic "shark fin" morphology |
| Brugada syndrome (Type 2) | rSR' in V1-V2, saddleback ST | Saddleback ST elevation ≥0.5mm in V1-V2; T-wave is upright or biphasic (not inverted as in typical RBBB secondary changes); high takeoff of ST segment |
| Right ventricular hypertrophy (RVH) | Tall R in V1, right axis deviation | RVH shows tall monophasic R or qR in V1 (not rSR'); prominent R:S ratio >1 in V1; RAD is usually more marked; QRS is typically <120ms unless concurrent RBBB |
| WPW (Type A) | Broad QRS, tall R in V1 | Delta wave present (slurred upstroke at QRS onset); short PR interval (<120ms); pre-excitation in multiple leads; initial QRS is abnormal (unlike RBBB where initial r is normal) |
| Posterior STEMI | Tall R in V1-V2 | Tall R in V1-V2 but WITHOUT the rSR' pattern; ST depression in V1-V3 (reciprocal to posterior STE); upright T-waves in V1-V2; clinical context of acute chest pain |
| Normal variant rSR' in V1 | rSR' in V1 | QRS <100ms; R' is small and narrow; no wide S in I/V6; common in young, thin individuals; variant of normal |
| Ventricular tachycardia (RBBB morphology) | Wide QRS with RBBB-like pattern | VT is fast (>100bpm regular), AV dissociation present, fusion/capture beats, concordance in precordial leads, bizarre axis; no preceding P-waves for each QRS |

### 4.2 Coexisting Conditions
- **RBBB + Left Anterior Fascicular Block (LAFB):** Bifascicular block — RBBB pattern PLUS left axis deviation (beyond -45°). Common. Indicates disease in two of the three fascicles. Risk of progression to complete heart block.
- **RBBB + Left Posterior Fascicular Block (LPFB):** Bifascicular block — RBBB pattern PLUS right axis deviation (>+120°). Less common but more concerning. Must exclude RVH as cause of RAD.
- **RBBB + First-degree AV block:** Trifascicular disease if RBBB + fascicular block + prolonged PR. High risk of progression.
- **RBBB + Acute MI:** RBBB does NOT mask STEMI in most territories. New RBBB in anterior MI indicates proximal LAD occlusion (supplies the right bundle) — poor prognosis. Standard ST criteria apply in all leads except V1-V3 where secondary changes exist.
- **RBBB + PE:** Acute RBBB (new) with sinus tachycardia, S1Q3T3, right axis deviation, and right heart strain suggests acute PE. The combination of findings, not RBBB alone, is diagnostic.
- **RBBB + Atrial Fibrillation:** Ashman phenomenon — aberrantly conducted beats with RBBB morphology after long-short cycle sequences. Must distinguish from PVCs.

## 5. STAT Classification

| Priority | Criteria |
|----------|----------|
| **STAT** | Not inherently STAT. Isolated, chronic RBBB is benign. However, NEW RBBB with clinical context (chest pain, dyspnea, hemodynamic instability) IS STAT — suggests acute PE, anterior MI, or acute right heart strain. |
| **Time-sensitive** | New RBBB: must be identified immediately. The clinical question shifts to "Why is this new?" — PE workup, troponin, echocardiography. Chronic RBBB: no time urgency for the RBBB itself, but must still be flagged to avoid confusing secondary ST-T changes with ischemia. |
| **Clinical action** | New RBBB + chest pain/dyspnea: CT pulmonary angiography to rule out PE, serial troponins, echocardiography for RV strain. New RBBB + anterior STEMI: emergent catheterization (proximal LAD). Chronic RBBB: document, note secondary ST-T changes to prevent false STEMI activation, cardiology follow-up if bifascicular or trifascicular. |

### 6.1 Reasoning Domains Required to Detect This Condition
- **Interval measurement:** QRS duration must be ≥120ms (or 100-119ms for incomplete)
- **Morphology comparison:** rSR' pattern recognition in V1-V2; wide S wave recognition in I and V6
- **Lead-group correlation:** Must confirm findings across right precordial (V1-V2) AND lateral (I, V6) leads simultaneously — neither finding alone is sufficient
- **Axis calculation:** Axis assessment helps identify coexisting fascicular blocks (bifascicular/trifascicular)
- Cross-domain reasoning is moderate: detection itself is straightforward pattern recognition, but clinical significance assessment requires integrating temporal context (new vs old) and cross-condition interactions (RBBB + STEMI, RBBB + PE)
- Sequential reasoning: "IF QRS ≥120ms AND rSR' in V1-V2 AND wide S in I/V6 THEN RBBB" → simple AND logic, no conditional branching for detection

### 6.2 Feature Dependencies
- **ESSENTIAL:** QRS duration (ms), QRS morphology in V1 and V2 (rSR' detection), S-wave width/slurring in leads I and V6
- **SUPPORTING:** QRS axis (to detect coexisting fascicular blocks), ST-segment levels in V1-V3 (to confirm secondary changes), T-wave direction in V1-V3, heart rate (to identify rate-dependent RBBB)
- **EXCLUDING:** Delta wave presence (→ WPW, not RBBB), coved ST elevation in V1-V2 (→ Brugada), QRS <100ms with rSR' (→ normal variant)
- **Per-beat analysis:** Needed for intermittent/rate-dependent RBBB detection; aggregate analysis sufficient for fixed RBBB

### 6.3 Cross-Condition Interactions
- **RBBB effect on STEMI detection:** Minimal. RBBB secondary changes are confined to V1-V3. ST criteria in I, II, III, aVL, aVF, V4-V6 remain fully interpretable. Even in V1-V3, concordant ST elevation (ST elevated despite expected depression) may indicate anterior STEMI.
- **RBBB + fascicular blocks:** RBBB must be detected first, then axis deviation evaluated to classify bifascicular or trifascicular block
- **RBBB + RVH:** Both show prominent R waves in V1; must distinguish rSR' (RBBB) from monophasic tall R (RVH). Can coexist.
- **RBBB + atrial fibrillation:** Ashman phenomenon produces RBBB-morphology aberrant beats that may be confused with PVCs
- **RBBB + PE:** New RBBB is a supporting finding for PE but not diagnostic alone; must be integrated with sinus tachycardia, S1Q3T3, clinical context

### 6.4 Reasoning Chain Sketch
**Minimum reasoning chain (high confidence):**
1. Measure QRS duration → ≥120ms
2. Examine V1 morphology → rSR' or rsR' (M-shaped)
3. Examine lead I and V6 → wide, slurred S wave
4. All three present → RBBB confirmed

**Full reasoning chain (complete evidence assembly):**
1. Measure QRS duration → ≥120ms (or 100-119ms for incomplete)
2. Examine V1 morphology → rSR', rsR', or broad notched R
3. Examine V2 morphology → confirm similar pattern
4. Examine leads I and V6 → wide slurred terminal S wave
5. Check ST-T in V1-V3 → confirm secondary changes (ST depression, T inversion) — expected
6. Calculate QRS axis → normal = isolated RBBB; LAD = RBBB + LAFB; RAD = RBBB + LPFB or RVH
7. Check PR interval → prolonged = possible trifascicular disease
8. Assess temporal context → new vs chronic (comparison with prior ECG if available)
9. If new: flag for clinical correlation (PE, MI, right heart strain)
10. Check for coexisting ST changes outside V1-V3 → if present, evaluate for concurrent ischemia

### 6.5 Confidence Anchors
- **HIGH confidence:** rSR' in V1 + QRS ≥120ms + wide S in I and V6 (all three present = near-certain diagnosis)
- **LOWER confidence if absent:** Missing wide S in I/V6 (consider RVH or Brugada instead); QRS <120ms with rSR' (incomplete RBBB or normal variant); atypical V1 morphology (broad R without clear rSR')
- **Pathognomonic combination:** rSR' in V1 with QRS ≥120ms and wide terminal S in I and V6 — this is essentially diagnostic; specificity >95%
- **Classification thresholds:**
  - "Possible": QRS 100-119ms with rSR' in V1 (may be incomplete RBBB or normal variant)
  - "Probable": QRS ≥120ms with rSR' in V1 but S wave in I/V6 is borderline
  - "Definite": All three criteria met with QRS ≥120ms

**Architecture:** Option E — 3 Parallel Phase 1 Specialists + 1 Sequential CDS (Node 2.1 Gate Passed 2026-03-27)

| Agent | Role for Right Bundle Branch Block | Key Features Used |
|-------|--------------------------|-------------------|
| **RRC** (Rhythm/Rate/Conduction) | Co-Primary | QRS duration ≥120ms; terminal conduction delay ≥40ms rightward/anteriorly; PR interval assessment for coexisting AV disease; rate context for rate-dependent RBBB |
| **IT** (Ischemia/Territory) | Supporting | ST changes in leads outside V1-V3 assessed by standard criteria; concordant ST elevation in V1-V3 (ST elevated despite expected secondary depression) flagged as possible anterior STEMI superimposed on RBBB |
| **MR** (Morphology/Repolarization) | Co-Primary | rSR' or rsR' morphology in V1-V2; wide slurred terminal S wave in leads I and V6 (≥40ms S-wave duration); secondary ST depression and T-wave inversion in V1-V3 (expected, not ischemic) |
| **CDS** (Cross-Domain Synthesis) | Required — resolves RBBB + ischemia coexistence | Integrates RRC QRS duration + MR rSR' morphology to confirm RBBB; determines whether IT ST findings outside V1-V3 represent true ischemia; RBBB does not mask STEMI in lateral/inferior territories — standard IT criteria apply there; flags new RBBB for clinical escalation |

### Primary Agent
**RRC and MR co-primary** — RBBB diagnosis requires both QRS duration measurement (RRC domain) and specific morphology recognition: rSR' in V1-V2 and wide S in I/V6 (MR domain); neither alone is sufficient.

### Cross-Domain Hints
- RRC emits: `cross_domain_hint: "QRS ≥120ms with terminal rightward delay detected — MR must confirm rSR' in V1-V2 and wide S in I/V6 for RBBB diagnosis"`
- MR emits: `cross_domain_hint: "rSR' confirmed in V1-V2 with wide S in I/V6 — IT must evaluate ST segments outside V1-V3 by standard ischemia criteria; secondary ST depression in V1-V3 is expected and non-ischemic"`
- MR emits (if concordant ST elevation in V1-V3): `cross_domain_hint: "ST elevation in V1-V3 exceeds expected secondary depression — IT must evaluate for superimposed anterior STEMI (concordant ST elevation pattern)"`

### CDS Specific Role
CDS confirms RBBB by integrating RRC QRS duration (≥120ms) with MR morphology (rSR' in V1-V2, wide S in I/V6). It then resolves the ischemia assessment boundary: secondary ST-T changes in V1-V3 are suppressed as ischemic signals, while IT findings in leads I, II, III, aVL, aVF, and V4-V6 are treated as fully interpretable by standard criteria. If IT detects concordant ST elevation in V1-V3 (elevation despite expected secondary depression), CDS escalates this as possible anterior STEMI superimposed on RBBB. CDS also checks RRC axis output: if LAD is present, it documents RBBB + LAFB (bifascicular block); if RAD is present, it documents RBBB + LPFB or coexisting RVH. New RBBB (absent on prior ECG context) triggers a CDS escalation flag for PE, proximal LAD ischemia, or acute right heart strain evaluation.

## 9. Edge Cases and Pitfalls

- **Incomplete RBBB (QRS 100-119ms):** Same morphological pattern but narrower QRS. Very common in young, healthy individuals — often a normal variant. Must not be over-called as pathological. The agent should flag it but with lower clinical significance.
- **Rate-dependent RBBB:** Appears only at higher heart rates. If a recording captures the transition (normal → RBBB as rate increases), the agent must not interpret intermittent wide QRS beats as PVCs. Key clue: preceding P-waves with consistent PR intervals.
- **Ashman phenomenon (in atrial fibrillation):** A beat following a long-short RR cycle may conduct with RBBB aberrancy. Looks like a PVC but is actually an aberrantly conducted supraventricular beat. The RBBB morphology (rSR' in V1) favors aberrancy; a monophasic or bizarre QRS in V1 favors VT/PVC.
- **RBBB + acute anterior STEMI:** New RBBB with concordant ST elevation in V1-V4 indicates proximal LAD occlusion — very high mortality. The agent must recognize that ST elevation EXCEEDING the expected secondary depression in V1-V3 is abnormal and suggests superimposed STEMI.
- **Post-cardiac surgery RBBB:** Extremely common after right ventriculotomy (e.g., Tetralogy of Fallot repair, ventricular septal defect closure). Usually permanent. Must be documented as chronic/expected.
- **RBBB masquerading as Brugada:** In leads V1-V2 placed one interspace too high (common technical variation), RBBB can mimic Brugada pattern. If Brugada is suspected, repeat ECG with standard and high V1-V2 placement.
- **Elderly patients with RBBB + LAFB:** Common combination in aging conduction system. Low threshold for monitoring (Holter) if symptomatic (syncope, presyncope) — risk of progression to complete heart block.
- **Pediatric RBBB:** rSR' in V1 is a normal finding in children (juvenile pattern). QRS must be ≥120ms adjusted for age; normal childhood rSR' has QRS well within normal limits for age.
- **Technical artifacts:** Limb lead reversal (RA/LA swap) can produce pseudo-RBBB patterns. Verify lead placement if RBBB is unexpected. High-frequency noise can obscure the rSR' notch.
