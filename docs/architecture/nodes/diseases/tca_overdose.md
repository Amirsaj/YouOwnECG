## 1. Pathophysiology Root Cause

### 1.1 Primary Mechanisms of TCA Toxicity
Tricyclic antidepressants (TCAs — amitriptyline, imipramine, nortriptyline, desipramine, clomipramine) produce toxicity through three simultaneous mechanisms:

**1. Fast sodium (Na+) channel blockade (Class IA-like):**
The dominant cardiac mechanism. TCAs bind fast Na+ channels (similar to Class IA antiarrhythmics) → slow phase 0 depolarization velocity → slowed cardiac conduction → QRS widening. The effect is use-dependent (more pronounced at faster rates) and state-dependent (ischemic, acidotic tissue more affected).

**2. Potassium (K+) channel blockade (IKr):**
Similar to Class IA drugs — TCAs also block cardiac K+ channels → prolonged phase 3 repolarization → QT prolongation. This contributes to TdP risk in TCA overdose.

**3. Anticholinergic toxidrome:**
Muscarinic receptor blockade → sinus tachycardia (dominates acutely), urinary retention, dry mouth, hyperthermia, delirium. The early ECG finding of sinus tachycardia in TCA overdose is anticholinergic, not cardiac toxicity — but it is an early warning sign.

### 1.2 The Terminal R Wave in aVR — Mechanism
The most diagnostically specific single ECG finding in TCA toxicity is a terminal R wave in lead aVR ≥3 mm. The mechanism:

The His-Purkinje system and right bundle branch conduct the terminal forces of the QRS. In TCA toxicity, Na+ channel block is more pronounced in the right bundle branch (preferential right bundle block effect):
1. Right bundle conduction slows disproportionately
2. Terminal depolarization vector is directed superiorly and rightward (from left-to-right delayed right ventricular activation)
3. aVR faces rightward and superiorly → sees this rightward terminal vector as a positive (R') deflection
4. Result: terminal R wave (or S-wave slurring becoming positive) in aVR ≥3 mm

This terminal R in aVR represents the same rightward-superior terminal QRS force seen as: S wave in lead I (deep S), R' in aVR, and the clinical equivalent of right-axis deviation of the terminal QRS forces.

### 1.3 The Sodium Bicarbonate Treatment Mechanism
Sodium bicarbonate is the primary treatment for TCA-induced QRS widening and arrhythmias:
1. **Sodium load:** Hypertonic sodium solution increases extracellular Na+ → enhances Na+ channel availability → partially overcomes TCA Na+ channel blockade → narrows QRS
2. **Alkalinization:** TCA binding to Na+ channel is pH-dependent — acidosis worsens Na+ channel blockade (increases TCA ionization and binding affinity). Alkalinization (pH 7.45–7.55) reduces TCA-channel binding → reverses conduction slowing
3. **Result:** IV NaHCO3 boluses narrow QRS, reduce terminal R in aVR, reduce arrhythmia risk

This is one of the most elegant treatment mechanisms in clinical toxicology — the treatment directly addresses both components of the mechanism.

## 2. ECG Presentation — Lead by Lead

### 2.1 Diagnostic Criteria

| Criterion | Finding | Risk Threshold |
|---|---|---|
| QRS widening | QRS >100 ms | Significant risk of seizures and cardiovascular collapse |
| QRS widening (severe) | QRS >160 ms | Very high risk of cardiovascular collapse and VT/VF |
| Terminal R in aVR | R wave ≥3 mm in aVR (terminal positive deflection) | Highly specific for TCA toxicity — STAT finding |
| R/S ratio in aVR | R/S ratio >0.7 | Additional specificity for terminal rightward forces |
| QTc prolongation | QTc >500 ms | TdP risk |
| Sinus tachycardia | Heart rate 100–150 bpm | Early anticholinergic sign |
| Right axis deviation | Terminal 40 ms rightward | Same rightward terminal force as R in aVR |
| TdP | Polymorphic VT | Combined Na+ and K+ channel block |

### 2.3 Key Leads
**aVR** is the single most important lead in TCA overdose. It faces rightward and superiorly — the direction of the terminal QRS force produced by preferential right bundle Na+ block. R wave ≥3 mm in aVR or terminal positive deflection ≥3 mm = specific sign of significant TCA toxicity.

**Lead I** shows deep S wave (reciprocal to the rightward terminal forces that produce R in aVR).

**V1** may show RBBB-like morphology (R') from the same right bundle preferential block.

### 2.4 Beat-by-Beat Considerations
- Sinus tachycardia is the predominant rhythm early; rate 100–160 bpm from anticholinergic effect
- As toxicity progresses: QRS widens → PVCs → wide-complex tachycardia
- Sinus tachycardia + wide QRS = TCA toxicity until proven otherwise in the overdose context
- Terminal R in aVR can be seen on individual beats — does not vary significantly beat-to-beat
- Monomorphic VT (from Na+ block reentry) or TdP (from K+ block)

## 3. Morphology Details

### 3.1 Terminal R Wave in aVR — Measurement
- Measurement: height of the terminal positive deflection in aVR (the R wave, or terminal portion of an R')
- Threshold: ≥3 mm (≥0.3 mV)
- Normal aVR: primarily negative QRS (rS or QS pattern) — the heart is directed away from aVR normally
- TCA toxicity: abnormal positive terminal deflection (R wave) ≥3 mm = pathological rightward terminal forces

### 3.2 R/S Ratio in aVR
- Normal: <0.7 (predominantly negative QRS in aVR)
- TCA toxicity: R/S >0.7 in aVR
- High specificity when combined with R ≥3 mm

### 3.3 QRS Duration and Morphology
- Normal: <100 ms
- TCA toxicity concern: 100–120 ms
- Significant risk: >120 ms
- Severe risk: >160 ms
- The widening is diffuse — not classic LBBB or RBBB morphology — though V1 may show RBBB-like pattern from preferential right bundle block
- QRS widening is use-dependent: faster rates → more widening

### 3.4 QT/QTc Prolongation
- Combined Na+ block (QRS widening artificially lengthens QT) + K+ block (true QT prolongation)
- The JT interval (measured from J-point to end of T wave, excluding QRS) may be more clinically useful to assess true repolarization prolongation independent of QRS width
- TdP risk if JT interval is prolonged

### 3.5 ST and T Wave Changes
- Non-specific ST-T changes may occur from ischemia secondary to tachycardia and hemodynamic compromise
- Not primary diagnostic features

### 3.6 Rhythm Progression
1. Sinus tachycardia (early)
2. QRS widening with maintained sinus tachycardia
3. Aberrantly-conducted sinus tachycardia → resembles VT
4. Monomorphic VT (reentry facilitated by Na+ block)
5. TdP (from K+ block)
6. VF → cardiac arrest

## 4. Differential Diagnosis

### 4.1 Mimics

| Condition | Overlapping Feature | Distinguishing Feature |
|---|---|---|
| LBBB | QRS widening | LBBB: fixed morphology, specific criteria; no tachycardia; no terminal R in aVR |
| Class IC drug toxicity | QRS widening | History of flecainide/propafenone; no terminal R in aVR; no anticholinergic features |
| Hyperkalemia | QRS widening | Hyperkalemia: absent P waves, peaked T waves, bradycardia (not tachycardia); no R in aVR |
| Brugada syndrome | RBBB + ST changes | Brugada: right precordial ST elevation; no QRS widening mechanism; different history |
| VT | Wide-complex tachycardia | VT: no preceding sinus tachycardia with progressive widening; different QRS morphology |
| SVT with aberrancy | Wide-complex tachycardia | History and medication context differentiate |

### 4.2 Coexisting Toxins
- **Benzodiazepines:** Commonly co-ingested; reduce seizure risk (beneficial) but cause sedation/respiratory depression
- **Alcohol:** Common co-ingestion; acidosis from alcohol worsens TCA Na+ channel block
- **Other QT-prolonging drugs:** Additive QTc effect
- **Anticholinergic agents:** Additive anticholinergic toxidrome
- **Metabolic acidosis (any cause):** Worsens TCA Na+ channel blockade → worsens QRS widening

## 6. Reasoning Complexity Analysis

### 6.1 Pattern Recognition Complexity
Moderate-high. The combination of sinus tachycardia + wide QRS + terminal R in aVR is recognizable but requires deliberate attention to aVR (a lead often overlooked in standard ECG interpretation).

### 6.2 aVR — The Most Important Lead
aVR is systematically underutilized in clinical practice. TCA toxicity diagnosis depends on a specific measurement in aVR. The reasoning system must prioritize aVR morphology analysis in any overdose context or wide-QRS + tachycardia scenario.

### 6.3 Distinguishing TCA-Wide QRS from Other Causes
Wide QRS differential in the ER is broad. The key discriminating factors for TCA:
- Clinical context: overdose/suicide attempt
- Sinus tachycardia (not bradycardia — eliminates hyperkalemia, Class IA toxicity)
- Terminal R in aVR (not present in LBBB, RBBB, hyperkalemia)
- Anticholinergic features: dry, flushed, tachycardic, confused

### 6.4 Treatment Window
The clinical course can be rapid — minutes from wide QRS to VF in severe overdose. The reasoning system must generate an urgent STAT alert for QRS >100 ms with terminal R in aVR, triggering immediate sodium bicarbonate administration.

### 6.5 NaHCO3 as Monitoring Target
Response to sodium bicarbonate is an ECG-monitored treatment. Repeat ECGs assess QRS narrowing as evidence of response. Failure to narrow QRS despite multiple bicarbonate boluses → consider lipid emulsion therapy or ECMO for refractory toxicity.

### Primary Agent
**RRC** — TCA toxicity's life-threatening manifestations are conduction and rate findings: sinus tachycardia, QRS widening (sodium channel blockade), terminal R in aVR, and rightward terminal QRS axis all fall within RRC's domain.

### Cross-Domain Hints
- RRC → MR: `cross_domain_hint: "QRS widening ≥100ms detected with possible rightward terminal deflection — MR should measure terminal R wave amplitude in aVR; ≥3mm indicates TCA sodium channel blockade"`
- RRC → CDS: `cross_domain_hint: "Sinus tachycardia + QRS widening ≥100ms detected — CDS should integrate MR terminal R in aVR measurement; combined findings may indicate TCA overdose, requiring treatment-level alert"`
- MR → CDS: `cross_domain_hint: "Terminal R in aVR measured at [X]mm — if ≥3mm with QRS widening and tachycardia (RRC), CDS should generate TCA sodium channel blockade diagnosis with treatment-altering flag"`

### CDS Specific Role
CDS plays a critical integration role for TCA overdose: the combination of RRC-detected sinus tachycardia, QRS widening ≥100ms, and MR-measured terminal R wave in aVR ≥3mm forms the diagnostic triad for TCA sodium channel blockade. CDS applies the treatment-altering rule: QRS >100ms correlates with seizure risk; QRS >160ms and terminal R in aVR ≥3mm correlate with ventricular arrhythmia risk. CDS generates a high-priority output flagging TCA toxicity when the triad is present and specifies the sodium channel blockade mechanism, which guides sodium bicarbonate therapy decisions. CDS also notes QTc prolongation (potassium channel block component) as an additional arrhythmia risk factor.

## 9. Edge Cases and Pitfalls

1. **Aberrant conduction vs. VT:** Wide-complex tachycardia in TCA overdose may represent either sinus tachycardia with aberrant conduction (TCA-widened QRS) or true VT. Given the toxicity context, both require immediate treatment. Sodium bicarbonate narrows QRS if it is TCA-aberrant conduction; VT requires additional intervention (cardioversion, further NaHCO3).

2. **Missed terminal R in aVR — the "overlooked lead":** aVR is routinely neglected in clinical ECG interpretation. Many clinicians skip aVR when reviewing ECGs, focusing on leads with more frequent pathology. The terminal R in aVR in TCA toxicity is the most specific single ECG finding and will be missed if aVR is not assessed deliberately.

3. **Hyperkalemia vs. TCA — wide QRS discrimination:** Both cause wide QRS. Key differentiating features:
   - Hyperkalemia: sinus bradycardia or absent P waves; peaked T waves; no terminal R in aVR
   - TCA: sinus tachycardia; terminal R in aVR; anticholinergic features
   Both can cause cardiac arrest. If uncertain → treat both (NaHCO3 helps both conditions).

4. **Sinus tachycardia rate reducing the terminal R visibility:** At very fast rates (>150 bpm), individual QRS complexes may be narrow enough in aVR that the terminal R is harder to see. Slowing recording to 25 mm/s standard and measuring amplitude carefully with calipers is essential.

5. **Recovery phase:** After sodium bicarbonate treatment, QRS narrows and terminal R in aVR diminishes. Serial ECGs are essential to confirm resolution. Premature discharge with residual QRS widening = relapse risk as bicarbonate effect wanes.

6. **Lipid emulsion therapy:** For refractory TCA toxicity not responding to NaHCO3 (QRS remains wide, ongoing hemodynamic compromise), IV lipid emulsion (20% intralipid) can be given. TCAs are lipophilic and partition into the lipid phase → "lipid sink" removes drug from cardiac tissue. This is a toxicology-specific intervention.

7. **ECMO for TCA arrest:** Refractory VF in TCA overdose is a specific indication for extracorporeal membrane oxygenation (ECMO). The cardiac muscle is pharmacologically stunned, not structurally damaged. With enough time for drug metabolism, cardiac function recovers. ECMO bridges the patient through the toxic period. ECMO candidacy should be considered early in severe cases.
