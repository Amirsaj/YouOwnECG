## 1. Pathophysiology Root Cause

### 1.1 What Goes Wrong (Anatomy/Physiology)

The SA node itself is structurally normal — it is being driven faster than its intrinsic rate by external stimuli. Sinus tachycardia is not a primary arrhythmia; it is always a physiological or pathophysiological response. The principal mechanisms are:

**Sympathetic activation**: Catecholamines (endogenous epinephrine/norepinephrine or exogenous) bind beta-1 receptors on SA node cells, accelerating phase 4 depolarization slope and increasing the I_f "funny current." This is the common final pathway for: pain, anxiety, fever, exercise, hypovolemia, anemia, hypoxia, pulmonary embolism, sepsis, and stimulant drugs (cocaine, amphetamines, caffeine, bronchodilators).

**Vagal withdrawal**: Reduced parasympathetic tone removes the normal braking effect on the SA node. This occurs in dehydration, early shock, and some pharmacologic states (anticholinergics, atropine).

**Direct metabolic drivers**: Hyperthyroidism (thyroid hormone upregulates beta-receptors and directly accelerates SA nodal automaticity); pheochromocytoma (catecholamine excess).

### 1.2 Electrical Consequence

The SA node fires at >100 bpm. All other electrical properties are preserved: activation still originates at the SA node, spreads via the normal atrial pathways, traverses the AV node, and activates the ventricles normally. The electrical sequence is identical to NSR — only the rate is increased.

At very high rates (>150 bpm), the PR interval may shorten slightly (AV node conduction accelerates with sympathetic stimulation). The QT interval shortens with increasing rate (rate-dependent repolarization). At rates >150–160 bpm, the P-wave may partially merge with the preceding T-wave, reducing P-wave visibility.

### 1.3 Why It Appears on ECG

Because SA node origin and normal conduction are preserved, all P-wave morphology criteria for sinus rhythm are met: P-wave axis 0–75°, upright in II, inverted in aVR. The only ECG change is a shorter RR interval (higher rate). The clinically critical implication is that sinus tachycardia is a symptom, not a diagnosis — the ECG identifies the rhythm, but the ER clinician must identify the cause.

## 2. ECG Presentation — Lead by Lead

### 2.1 Diagnostic Criteria (2025 AHA/ESC Guidelines)

1. Heart rate >100 bpm (RR interval <600 ms)
2. P-wave precedes every QRS with constant morphology (axis 0–75°)
3. Upright P in lead II; inverted P in aVR
4. Constant PR interval (may be at lower end of normal: 120–180 ms)
5. QRS <120 ms (unless pre-existing BBB or accessory pathway)
6. Gradual onset and termination (not abrupt — key differentiator from AVNRT/SVT)
7. Rate varies with activity, respiration, or pharmacologic challenge (e.g., carotid sinus massage slows but does not terminate)

### 2.2 Lead-by-Lead Manifestation

| Lead | Expected Finding | Why | Sensitivity |
|------|-----------------|-----|-------------|
| I | Upright P (may be small), positive QRS, upright T | Preserved sinus P-vector; leftward QRS | High |
| II | Upright P (may overlap preceding T at high rates), positive QRS, upright T | Most diagnostic lead; P-axis alignment | Highest — P-wave in II is primary identifier |
| III | Upright or biphasic P, variable QRS | P may be small at high rates | Moderate |
| aVR | Inverted P (mandatory), negative QRS | Required for sinus confirmation | High |
| aVL | Upright or small P, biphasic QRS | Less aligned with P-axis | Moderate |
| aVF | Upright P, positive QRS | Inferior lead, confirms inferior P-axis | High |
| V1 | Biphasic P (+ then −), rS QRS | P may become more positive as rate rises | Moderate — may lose biphasic morphology at very high rates |
| V2 | Upright or biphasic P, rS | Variable | Low |
| V3 | Upright P, RS transition | Variable | Low |
| V4 | Upright P, RS or qRS | Transitional | Moderate |
| V5 | Upright P, qRs | Lateral | High — confirms preserved QRS morphology |
| V6 | Upright P, qRs | Most lateral | High |

**Critical high-rate phenomenon**: At rates approaching 150 bpm, the P-wave of the current beat may be obscured within the T-wave of the preceding beat. In V1, the biphasic P may appear as a notch on the T-wave descending limb. Carefully inspect ALL leads — P-waves are often visible in at least one lead even when buried in others.

### 2.3 Key Leads (Most Diagnostic)

- **Lead II**: Standard diagnostic lead. Upright P with visible PR interval.
- **aVR**: Mandatory P inversion confirms sinus origin at any rate.
- **V1**: Biphasic P morphology helps distinguish sinus P from ectopic P at high rates.
- **Long rhythm strip (any lead)**: Essential to confirm gradual rate changes and exclude paroxysmal SVT.

### 2.4 Beat-by-Beat Considerations

- Rate should be fairly constant across the strip; slow variation is acceptable (physiologic)
- Abrupt onset or termination is NOT sinus tachycardia — classify as paroxysmal SVT pending further analysis
- Carotid sinus massage/Valsalva: sinus tachycardia slows gradually then resumes; AVNRT/SVT terminates abruptly or not at all
- At 150 bpm: MANDATORY exclusion of atrial flutter with 2:1 block (atrial rate 300 bpm with every-other flutter wave conducted)

## 3. Morphology Details (What the Agent Must See)

### 3.1 P-wave Changes

- **Morphology**: Identical to NSR — preserved sinus P-wave shape. P-waves should look the same as they would at slower rates.
- **Amplitude**: May increase slightly with sympathetic drive (catecholamines increase atrial contractility, increasing P amplitude)
- **Duration**: Normal, 80–120 ms
- **Axis**: 0–75° — identical to NSR. If axis shifts, suspect ectopic atrial tachycardia.
- **P-in-T phenomenon**: At rates >150 bpm, the P-wave may be buried in the T-wave of the preceding beat. This creates a "peaked" or "notched" T-wave appearance. This is not T-wave abnormality — it is obscured P.
- **Key rule**: P-wave morphology must be CONSTANT across all beats. Variable P morphology = NOT sinus tachycardia.

### 3.2 PR Interval Changes

- **Normal to short**: 120–180 ms. Shortening at higher rates is physiological (sympathetic augmentation of AV nodal conduction velocity).
- **PR <120 ms at high rates**: Can occur physiologically. If PR is consistently short at all rates, consider pre-excitation (delta wave).
- **PR constancy**: Must remain fixed beat-to-beat. Variable PR suggests AV block or junctional escape.

### 3.3 QRS Complex Changes

- **Duration**: Identical to patient's baseline (<120 ms unless pre-existing BBB)
- **Morphology**: Preserved. Rate-related aberrant conduction (Ashman phenomenon) can cause occasional wide QRS beats at very high rates — do not misclassify as PVC or VT.
- **Rate-related BBB**: At extreme rates (>170 bpm), functional right or left bundle branch block can develop transiently. This is rate-related aberrancy, not fixed BBB.

### 3.4 ST Segment Changes

- **Expected**: Isoelectric. Sinus tachycardia does not directly cause ST changes.
- **Rate-related ST depression**: Mild downsloping ST depression may appear at high rates due to rate-related subendocardial demand ischemia — especially in patients with coronary artery disease. This is a secondary finding, not a primary feature of sinus tachycardia.
- **If ST elevation present**: The sinus tachycardia may be the response to an underlying STEMI or pericarditis — these findings are independent and must be flagged.

### 3.5 T-wave Changes

- **Expected**: Concordant with QRS, normal morphology.
- **T-wave "peaking"**: At high rates, the P-wave of the next beat may create a notch or peak on the T-wave descent. This mimics hyperkalemic T-waves but is rate-dependent.
- **Flattened T-waves**: May accompany sympathetic activation (catecholamine effect) or tachycardia-mediated cardiomyopathy if prolonged.

### 3.6 QT/QTc Changes

- **QT**: Shortens with increasing rate (absolute QT may be 280–320 ms at 150 bpm)
- **QTc**: Should remain normal (350–440 ms). If QTc is prolonged despite tachycardia, suspect Long QT syndrome exacerbated by sympathetic trigger.
- **Measurement challenge**: At high rates, T-wave offset may merge with the next P-wave, making QT measurement unreliable. Use the lead with clearest T-wave offset (often V5 or V6).

### 3.7 Other Features

- **RR interval regularity**: Slightly variable — sinus tachycardia can have mild beat-to-beat variation (5–10%). Perfectly regular narrow-complex tachycardia at exactly 150 bpm is more consistent with atrial flutter 2:1 than sinus tachycardia.
- **Response to vagal maneuvers**: Gradual slowing, then resumption. This is diagnostically useful but is a clinical feature, not an ECG feature per se.

## 4. Differential Diagnosis

### 4.1 Mimics

| Condition | How it mimics sinus tachycardia | Key differentiating feature |
|-----------|--------------------------------|----------------------------|
| Atrial flutter with 2:1 block | Regular narrow tachycardia at ~150 bpm | Sawtooth flutter waves in II, III, aVF; perfectly regular rate; visible flutter waves in V1 |
| AVNRT (typical) | Regular narrow tachycardia 150–250 bpm | Abrupt onset/termination; retrograde P-waves buried in QRS (pseudo-R' in V1, pseudo-S in II); terminates with vagal maneuver |
| Ectopic atrial tachycardia | Regular tachycardia with P before QRS | P-wave morphology differs from sinus; non-standard axis; may have 2:1 AV conduction |
| Atrial tachycardia with 1:1 conduction | P-wave before every QRS | P-wave axis outside 0–75°; abrupt onset; rate often >150 bpm |
| Sinus node reentrant tachycardia | Normal sinus P-wave morphology | Abrupt onset and termination (reentrant); rate typically 100–160 bpm |
| Junctional tachycardia | Regular narrow tachycardia | Retrograde P-waves (negative II, aVF) or absent P-waves; PR <120 ms or RP>PR |

**The 150 bpm Rule**: Any regular narrow tachycardia at approximately 150 bpm must be presumed atrial flutter with 2:1 conduction until proven otherwise. Apply vagal maneuver (clinically) or examine V1 meticulously for flutter waves before accepting "sinus tachycardia at 150 bpm."

### 4.2 Coexisting Conditions

The ER agent must actively search for the CAUSE of sinus tachycardia — the rhythm itself is the sign, not the disease:

| Cause | Additional ECG findings to search for |
|-------|--------------------------------------|
| Pulmonary embolism | S1Q3T3 pattern, right heart strain, new RBBB, sinus tachycardia |
| Inferior STEMI | ST elevation II, III, aVF — sinus tach from pain/vagal response |
| Anterior STEMI | ST elevation V1–V4 — sinus tach from pain or pump failure |
| Hypovolemia/hemorrhage | Sinus tachycardia alone; no specific ECG finding |
| Hyperthyroidism | Sinus tachycardia, AF, atrial flutter; short QT |
| Sepsis | Sinus tachycardia; may see ST changes with septic cardiomyopathy |
| Drug toxicity (stimulants) | Sinus tachycardia; may see ST changes, QRS widening |
| Anemia | Sinus tachycardia; no specific ECG finding |

## 5. STAT Classification

| Priority | Criteria |
|----------|----------|
| STAT — Critical | Sinus tachycardia >150 bpm with co-existing STEMI, or hemodynamic instability (hypotension + tachycardia = presumed shock) |
| STAT — Urgent | Sinus tachycardia with signs of PE (S1Q3T3, new RBBB), or rate >150 bpm requiring atrial flutter exclusion |
| Urgent | Sinus tachycardia 120–150 bpm without obvious cause — mandates clinical workup (sepsis, PE, hemorrhage protocol) |
| Non-urgent | Sinus tachycardia 100–120 bpm with known benign cause (pain, anxiety, fever post-treatment) |
| NOT STAT (rhythm alone) | Sinus tachycardia is never STAT based on rate alone — underlying cause drives priority |

**Key ER principle**: The agent must NEVER close the diagnostic loop at "sinus tachycardia." It must flag the need for underlying cause identification and scan for co-existing morphologic abnormalities that suggest the cause.

## 6. Reasoning Complexity Analysis

### 6.1 Reasoning Domains Required

- Rate calculation and threshold determination (>100 bpm)
- P-wave morphology verification (sinus origin confirmation)
- High-rate P-wave identification (P-in-T detection algorithm)
- Atrial flutter exclusion (critical at rates 140–160 bpm)
- Co-existing morphology analysis (ST elevation, right heart strain, etc.)
- Clinical context integration (cause identification)

### 6.2 Feature Dependencies

- Reliable P-wave detection at high rates is technically challenging — fiducial detection algorithms degrade with P-in-T overlap
- QT measurement requires visible T-wave offset, which may be obscured at high rates
- Flutter wave detection requires analysis of the isoelectric baseline between QRS complexes in II, III, aVF, V1

### 6.3 Cross-Condition Interactions

- If rate is 140–160 bpm: cross-reference atrial flutter node (2.7.X) for 2:1 flutter exclusion
- If ST elevation present: cross-reference relevant STEMI node for co-analysis
- If S1Q3T3 or new RBBB: cross-reference pulmonary embolism node
- If QRS is wide: consider rate-related aberrancy vs. VT with retrograde P-waves (rare)

### 6.4 Reasoning Chain Sketch

```
1. Measure heart rate → >100 bpm confirmed
2. Identify P-waves → present before every QRS?
   (If P buried in T-wave, use V1 biphasic P as anchor)
3. Measure P-wave axis → 0–75°? (upright II, inverted aVR)
4. Confirm P morphology is constant beat-to-beat
5. Measure PR interval → 120–200 ms, constant?
6. Measure QRS duration → <120 ms?
7. Assess onset: gradual or abrupt?
   → Abrupt: reclassify as SVT/atrial flutter pending further analysis
8. If rate 140–160 bpm → mandatory atrial flutter exclusion:
   a. Inspect II, III, aVF for sawtooth baseline
   b. Inspect V1 for rapid regular atrial deflections
9. Classify as sinus tachycardia only if all criteria met
10. Scan for co-existing morphologic abnormalities → report separately
11. Flag: "Underlying cause must be identified"
```

### 6.5 Confidence Anchors

- Visible upright P in II with clear PR interval + rate >100 bpm: high confidence sinus tachycardia
- Rate exactly 150 bpm + regular: low confidence sinus tach, high suspicion flutter 2:1
- Gradual rate changes visible on strip: increases confidence for sinus (not reentrant)
- Abrupt onset on strip: immediately reclassify to SVT evaluation pathway

### 6.6 Difficulty Score

| Dimension | Score (1–5) | Notes |
|-----------|-------------|-------|
| Pattern Recognition | 1 | Rate + sinus P-wave morphology is straightforward |
| Differential Complexity | 3 | Flutter 2:1 exclusion at 150 bpm is genuinely difficult; ectopic AT vs. sinus requires careful P-axis analysis |
| Measurement Precision | 2 | P-in-T detection at high rates requires algorithm sophistication |
| Clinical Integration | 3 | Identifying the underlying cause requires multi-system clinical reasoning beyond ECG alone |
| Inter-lead Reasoning | 2 | Flutter exclusion requires simultaneous analysis of multiple leads |
| Rare Variant Handling | 2 | Rate-related aberrancy, sinus node reentry, and high-rate P-wave burial are non-trivial edge cases |
| **Overall** | **1.8** | **Rate calculation is easy; cause identification and high-rate differential are the complexity drivers** |

**Architecture:** Option E — 3 Parallel Phase 1 Specialists + 1 Sequential CDS (Node 2.1 Gate Passed 2026-03-27)

| Agent | Role for Sinus Tachycardia | Key Features Used |
|-------|--------------------------|-------------------|
| **RRC** (Rhythm/Rate/Conduction) | Primary | Rate >100 bpm, regular RR intervals, upright P-waves in I/II/aVF preceding every QRS, PR interval 120–200 ms, 1:1 AV relationship |
| **IT** (Ischemia/Territory) | Not involved | ST/T assessment not part of tachycardia classification |
| **MR** (Morphology/Repolarization) | Not involved | Morphology not relevant to this rhythm diagnosis |
| **CDS** (Cross-Domain Synthesis) | Standard integration only | Confirms sinus tachycardia; flags if rate >150 bpm for ectopic atrial tachycardia differential; integrates clinical context if provided |

### Primary Agent
**RRC** — The diagnosis rests entirely on rate (>100 bpm) and confirming normal sinus P-wave morphology and 1:1 AV conduction.

### Cross-Domain Hints
No cross-domain hints required — single-domain condition.

### CDS Specific Role
CDS performs standard integration of the single Phase 1 finding. When rate exceeds 150 bpm, CDS raises the differential for ectopic atrial tachycardia or AVNRT, since true sinus tachycardia rarely exceeds 150 bpm at rest. If IT concurrently detects ST changes, CDS flags demand ischemia consideration but does not alter the rhythm classification.

## 9. Edge Cases and Pitfalls

- **The 150 bpm trap**: The single most dangerous error in ECG interpretation. Atrial flutter with 2:1 conduction produces a perfectly regular narrow tachycardia at ~150 bpm that is frequently miscalled "sinus tachycardia." The treatment for flutter 2:1 includes rate control and anticoagulation; this is not needed for sinus tachycardia. Always inspect the baseline between QRS complexes in II, III, aVF, and V1 before accepting sinus tachycardia at this rate.
- **P-wave buried in T-wave**: At rates >150 bpm, the P-wave may be completely hidden within the T-wave of the preceding beat. Systematically inspect all 12 leads — there is almost always one lead where the P is visible. V1 is often most reliable due to the biphasic P morphology.
- **Inappropriate sinus tachycardia (IST)**: A clinical syndrome, not an ECG diagnosis. IST produces persistent sinus tachycardia at rest without identifiable cause. ECG shows normal sinus tachycardia. The agent cannot diagnose IST — it requires clinical exclusion of secondary causes.
- **Rate-related BBB**: At very high rates, functional bundle branch block may develop (Ashman phenomenon in rapid sinus tachycardia is rare but reported). Wide QRS during tachycardia with clear sinus P-waves should prompt BBB diagnosis, not VT.
- **Sinus tachycardia masking inferior STEMI**: Tachycardia-driven heart rate can reduce ST elevation amplitude (rate-dependent). A patient in sinus tachycardia from inferior STEMI pain may have attenuated ST elevation. Always measure ST in context; do not rely on absolute thresholds alone.
- **Drug-induced sinus tachycardia**: Salbutamol/albuterol (asthma), atropine (bradycardia treatment), cocaine/amphetamines, levothyroxine excess. These should be flagged in clinical context integration.
- **Hyperthyroid state**: Sinus tachycardia can be the presenting ECG finding. If sinus tachycardia is persistent without obvious cause in a middle-aged woman, flag thyroid function testing.
