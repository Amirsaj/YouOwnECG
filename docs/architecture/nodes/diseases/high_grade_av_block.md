## 1. Pathophysiology Root Cause

### 1.1 What Goes Wrong

High-grade AV block is a spectrum of severely impaired AV conduction in which multiple consecutive P-waves fail to conduct, yet some P-waves still successfully traverse the AV junction — distinguishing it from complete heart block (where NO P-waves conduct). The term encompasses:
- **2:1 AV block:** Every other P-wave is blocked (1 conducts, 1 fails, 1 conducts, 1 fails...)
- **3:1 AV block:** Two consecutive P-waves fail for every one that conducts
- **4:1 AV block and beyond:** Three or more consecutive failures per conducted beat
- **Advanced AV block:** General term for ≥ 2 consecutive non-conducted P-waves with some preserved conduction

High-grade AV block represents either severe AV nodal dysfunction (usually reversible — vagal, ischemic, drug-induced) or severe infranodal disease (usually progressive — fibrosis, bilateral bundle branch disease, infiltrative disease). The anatomical site is ambiguous without further analysis and requires differentiation using QRS width and clinical context.

**Why 2:1 is the most clinically treacherous ratio:** At 2:1, there is only ONE conducted beat visible per blocked beat — making it mathematically impossible to observe beat-to-beat PR variability. This means the cardinal distinction between Mobitz Type I and Type II (progressive vs. constant PR) cannot be made from the 2:1 pattern alone. The 2:1 block therefore exists in a diagnostic limbo that demands additional evidence: longer strips, variable ratio segments, QRS width, and clinical context.

Etiologies span the full spectrum:
- **AV nodal level:** Vagal excess, inferior MI (RCA AV nodal ischemia), drug toxicity (digoxin, beta-blockers, CCBs), myocarditis, Lyme carditis
- **Infranodal level:** Anterior MI (LAD septal perforators — His bundle, bundle branches), bilateral bundle branch fibrosis (Lev/Lenègre disease), sarcoidosis, amyloidosis, post-cardiac surgery
- **Mixed level:** Severe infiltrative disease affecting both AV node and His-Purkinje system

### 1.2 Electrical Consequence

At 2:1 block, the ventricular rate is exactly half the atrial rate:
- Atrial rate 60bpm → ventricular rate 30bpm (symptomatic bradycardia)
- Atrial rate 80bpm → ventricular rate 40bpm (borderline)
- Atrial rate 150bpm → ventricular rate 75bpm (DECEPTIVELY NORMAL — this is the critical trap)

At 3:1, 4:1 block, the ventricular rate falls even further. A patient in 4:1 AV block with a sinus rate of 80bpm has a ventricular rate of 20bpm — peri-arrest.

The conducted beats in high-grade AV block have a PR interval that reflects the level of block:
- **Constant PR:** Favors infranodal (Mobitz Type II behavior)
- **Variable PR:** Favors AV nodal (Wenckebach behavior, observed in variable-ratio segments)
- **Very long PR (>300ms):** May represent severe AV nodal delay with only every-other-beat surviving

Between conducted beats, non-conducted P-waves march at the sinus rate — visible on the ECG as P-waves occurring without corresponding QRS complexes, often landing on or near T-waves.

### 1.3 Why It Appears on ECG

The regular, on-time P-waves (constant PP interval) with a reduced number of QRS complexes is the surface ECG signature. At 2:1, P-waves appear at regular intervals but only every other one is followed by a QRS — creating a distinctive pattern of paired P-QRS and isolated P. At 3:1 and beyond, the P-waves "march through" the QRS complexes and T-waves with some falling in vulnerable positions (T-wave peak).

The ventricular rate may appear innocuously normal (at high atrial rates with 2:1 block), making this one of the most dangerous "normal-looking" rhythm diagnoses on the ECG. Systematic PP interval analysis — independent of the QRS — is mandatory.

## 2. ECG Presentation — Lead by Lead

### 2.1 Diagnostic Criteria (2025 AHA/ESC Guidelines)

**2:1 AV Block:**
- Regular P-waves at rate R (PP interval constant)
- QRS rate = R/2 (every other P conducts)
- Conducted PR: constant (cannot determine if Type I or Type II — document as "2:1 AV block, type indeterminate")
- QRS morphology: narrow suggests AV nodal; wide (≥ 120ms) suggests infranodal/Type II
- Must verify: the blocked P-wave is a true on-time sinus P (not a PAC, not a flutter wave)

**Advanced (≥ 3:1) AV Block:**
- ≥ 2 consecutive non-conducted P-waves
- At least some P-waves DO conduct (distinguishes from complete AV block)
- Conduction ratio variable or fixed (3:1, 4:1, etc.)
- Conducted beats may show PR progression (Wenckebach at core) or constant PR (Type II at core)

**Distinction from Complete Heart Block:**
- High-grade: SOME P-waves conduct (PR relationship preserved for conducted beats)
- Complete heart block: NO P-waves conduct; P and QRS rates are independent; PR varies randomly

### 2.2 Lead-by-Lead Manifestation

| Lead | Expected Finding | Why | Sensitivity |
|------|-----------------|-----|-------------|
| I | Every other P (in 2:1) followed by QRS; QRS morphology varies by site | Lateral lead — QRS width assessment | Moderate |
| II | PRIMARY: clear P-wave identification; every other P without QRS visible | Upright P-waves best seen in II; baseline non-conducted P appears as isolated hump | High |
| III | P-waves visible; inferior perspective important in inferior MI | Inferior lead | Moderate |
| aVR | Inverted P; alternating conducted/non-conducted in 2:1 | Confirms sinus P morphology | Moderate |
| aVL | Variable P visibility; lateral QRS | Lateral | Low-Moderate |
| aVF | Clear P-waves; inferior perspective | Inferior — confirms AV block in inferior MI context | Moderate |
| V1 | CRITICAL: biphasic P clearly distinct from T-wave; best lead for counting ALL P-waves | Non-conducted P in V1 falls on T-wave but often visible as notch or deformity | High |
| V2 | P counting; anterior | Anterior | Moderate |
| V3 | P counting; T-wave overlap may hide P | Transitional | Low-Moderate |
| V4 | QRS morphology; T-wave overlap | Transitional | Low |
| V5 | QRS morphology assessment | Lateral | Low-Moderate |
| V6 | Lateral QRS; P may be visible | Lateral | Low |

**Best leads for P-wave counting:** V1 and II together — use both to account for P-waves landing at different phases of the cardiac cycle. P-wave visibility varies by where in the cycle the non-conducted P falls.

### 2.3 Key Leads

- **Lead II:** The primary diagnostic lead for P-wave identification. At 2:1 block with a normal sinus rate, the non-conducted P-wave falls on the T-wave of the preceding conducted beat and may appear as a peaked or asymmetric T-wave.
- **V1:** Biphasic P-waves in V1 are often distinguishable even when falling on a T-wave — the negative deflection of the biphasic P creates a characteristic morphology change in the T-wave.
- **All leads simultaneously (12-lead):** The non-conducted P-wave will deform the T-wave differently in each lead depending on the P-wave polarity in that lead. Surveying all 12 leads identifies the P-wave position.

### 2.4 Beat-by-Beat Considerations

The critical analytical task in high-grade AV block is P-wave counting, independent of QRS analysis:

1. Measure the PP interval from two clearly visible, unambiguous P-waves (those immediately before a QRS, where they are most visible)
2. Use this PP interval to MARCH OUT all expected P-wave positions across the rhythm strip
3. Confirm that the marched P-wave positions correspond to visible deflections (even subtle ones) on the T-waves of non-conducted beats
4. Count the P:QRS ratio at each point
5. Measure PR for every conducted beat
6. Assess PR behavior: is it constant (favors Type II) or variable (favors Type I)?

At 2:1, the non-conducted P-wave always falls at a fixed position relative to the subsequent T-wave. If the PP interval is 400ms (sinus rate 150bpm) and the PR of the conducted beat is 180ms, the non-conducted P follows the conducted QRS by 200ms — landing on the T-wave upslope. This creates a T-wave that appears bifid or peaked, which is the most common way high-rate 2:1 block hides in plain sight.

## 3. Morphology Details

### 3.1 P-Wave Morphology
Sinus P-waves throughout — regular, on-time, consistent morphology. PP interval is constant regardless of QRS occurrence. This constancy distinguishes high-grade AV block from complete AV dissociation (where P-waves may be sinus but QRS complexes arise from a competing junctional or ventricular escape focus, with no fixed PP-QRS relationship).

### 3.2 PR Interval (Conducted Beats)
In 2:1 block: only one PR measurement per cycle. Document it, note whether it is normal or prolonged, but do not use it to type the block. In variable-ratio blocks (e.g., alternating 2:1 and 3:2), the additional conducted beats allow PR variability assessment — if the 3:2 segment shows progressive PR, the underlying pattern is Wenckebach.

### 3.3 QRS Complex
QRS morphology is the most important secondary clue for block site:
- **Narrow QRS (< 120ms):** AV nodal block likely — more benign, may respond to atropine, less likely to progress abruptly
- **Wide QRS (≥ 120ms, RBBB or LBBB pattern):** Infranodal block likely — does not respond well to atropine, high progression risk, pacing required
- **Wide QRS + 2:1 block:** Treat as Mobitz Type II until proven otherwise
- **Narrow QRS + 2:1 block + inferior STEMI context:** Likely Wenckebach (Type I) as the underlying mechanism

### 3.4 The "Normal Rate" Deception at High Atrial Rates
This is the defining clinical trap of 2:1 AV block. At a sinus rate of 150bpm (PP = 400ms), 2:1 AV block produces a ventricular rate of 75bpm — indistinguishable from normal sinus rhythm by ventricular rate alone. The only ECG evidence is the non-conducted P-wave deforming the T-wave.

Example: PP = 400ms (sinus 150bpm). PR = 180ms for conducted beats. Non-conducted P falls at 200ms after QRS onset — directly on the T-wave upstroke. The T-wave appears peaked and asymmetric. Without actively measuring PP and marching out P-waves, this diagnosis is missed.

### 3.5 Pause Duration
At 2:1, the pause (RR interval between successive conducted beats) = exactly 2x PP interval. At 3:1, pause = 3x PP. Confirming this relationship validates the conduction ratio. If the pause does not fit an integer multiple of PP, reconsider the diagnosis (sinus arrhythmia with coincidental PR changes? PACs? AFib with irregular conduction?).

### 3.6 Escape Beats
In high-grade AV block with very slow ventricular rates, the His-Purkinje system or ventricular myocardium may generate escape beats — narrow junctional (rate 40-60bpm) or wide ventricular (rate 20-40bpm). Escape beats interrupt the regular 2:1 or 3:1 pattern and may be mistaken for conducted beats. Escape beats have no fixed PR relationship to the preceding P-wave — their timing is governed by the escape focus rate, not the sinus rate.

### 3.7 Distinction from Complete Heart Block
The critical boundary: in high-grade AV block, some P-waves conduct with a FIXED PR. In complete heart block, ZERO P-waves conduct — the PR varies randomly as P-waves and QRS complexes march through each other completely independently. The test: does any P-QRS pair maintain a consistent, physiologically appropriate PR? If yes: high-grade block. If no fixed PR relationship in any beat: complete heart block.

## 4. Differential Diagnosis

### 4.1 Mimics

| Condition | How it Mimics High-Grade AV Block | Distinguishing Feature |
|-----------|-----------------------------------|----------------------|
| Complete (third degree) AV block | No conducted beats; P-waves march independently | CHB: NO consistent PR across any beats; QRS escape rate independent of PP |
| Atrial flutter with 2:1 conduction | Flutter waves at ~300bpm with 2:1 conduction looks like 2:1 AV block | Flutter: atrial rate ~300bpm sawtooth; rate 150bpm ventricular; no isoelectric baseline between flutter waves |
| Sinus rhythm with blocked PAC bigeminy | Every other "P-wave" is a PAC — looks like 2:1 block | PAC: different P-wave morphology; premature coupling interval; PP irregular (PAC resets sinus) |
| Sinus arrhythmia with long PR | Variable PP with long PR may simulate occasional dropped beats | Sinus arrhythmia: no true dropped beat; every P conducts (just with variable PP) |
| Isorhythmic AV dissociation | P-waves "walking through" QRS; some appearing to conduct | Dissociation: atrial and ventricular rates nearly identical; PR varies; not a conduction block |
| Nonconducted PAC bigeminy | Alternating sinus + blocked PAC | PAC morphology different; short coupling interval; sinus PP remains regular |
| Normal sinus rhythm at 75bpm with 2:1 block at 150bpm atrial rate | Ventricular rate 75bpm — looks completely normal | Only detected by finding 2 P-waves per QRS; T-wave deformation; PP = 400ms, RR = 800ms |

### 4.2 Coexisting Conditions

- **Inferior STEMI:** 2:1 or 3:1 AV block in inferior STEMI = severe AV nodal ischemia (RCA). Usually Wenckebach at core. More concerning than simple Wenckebach — may predict progression to complete heart block.
- **Anterior STEMI:** 2:1 or 3:1 with wide QRS in anterior STEMI = severe infranodal ischemia (LAD septal perforators). Catastrophic combination — emergency temporary pacing + revascularization.
- **Digoxin toxicity:** High-degree AV block + regularized AFib + PAT with block = severe digoxin toxicity. Digoxin-specific Fab antibodies.
- **Hyperkalemia:** Can produce high-grade AV block as part of progressive conduction failure. Treat K+ urgently.
- **Lyme carditis:** Can produce advanced and fluctuating AV block — may resolve rapidly with antibiotics, but temporary pacing required during the acute phase if hemodynamically significant.

## 5. STAT Classification

| Parameter | Assessment |
|-----------|------------|
| 2:1 AV block, asymptomatic, narrow QRS | URGENT (ORANGE) — cannot type; may be benign or dangerous; requires monitoring and longer rhythm strip |
| 2:1 AV block, wide QRS | STAT (RED) — likely Type II; treat as such; cardiology consult, pacing evaluation |
| 2:1 AV block with ventricular rate causing hemodynamic compromise | CRITICAL (RED) — transcutaneous pacing immediately |
| 2:1 at sinus rate 150bpm causing ventricular rate 75bpm (deceptively normal) | URGENT (ORANGE) — after detection; hemodynamic assessment; monitor for compromise |
| 3:1 or higher ratio AV block | STAT (RED) — ventricular rate likely inadequate; pacing evaluation |
| High-grade AV block + inferior STEMI | STAT (RED) — RCA ischemia; monitor for CHB |
| High-grade AV block + anterior STEMI | CRITICAL (RED) — dual emergency; pacing + revascularization |
| High-grade AV block + syncope/presyncope | STAT (RED) — Stokes-Adams; immediate pacing evaluation |
| High-grade AV block + digoxin toxicity | STAT (RED/ORANGE) — digoxin Fab, telemetry, do NOT give atropine if digoxin toxic |

**Default STAT level: ORANGE (urgent) for any confirmed high-grade AV block. RED for wide QRS, hemodynamic compromise, or coexisting STEMI.**

## 6. Reasoning Complexity Analysis

### 6.1 Signal Clarity Needed
Highest signal clarity in the AV block group. The non-conducted P-wave — which is the diagnostic cornerstone — is often partially or fully buried in a T-wave. ECGdeli must detect P-waves even when their amplitude is reduced by T-wave overlap. Reliable PP interval extraction from clearly visible P-waves (before a QRS) and extrapolation to buried P positions is a core technical challenge. Baseline wander particularly degrades this analysis.

### 6.2 Number of Leads Required
Full 12-lead mandatory. The non-conducted P-wave's visibility varies by lead depending on P-wave polarity and T-wave amplitude. Multiple leads must be surveyed simultaneously to confirm the P-wave position. V1 and II are the primary leads, but V4-V6 may show the P-wave more clearly when it falls on the T-wave downslope. QRS morphology requires full 12-lead for BBB characterization.

### 6.3 Cross-Domain Reasoning
High cross-domain reasoning. The algorithm must:
- Count P-waves independently of QRS (temporal analysis)
- Verify PP interval constancy (rate analysis)
- Characterize QRS width and BBB morphology (morphology analysis)
- Estimate probable block site from QRS width (anatomical reasoning)
- Assess concurrent STEMI (ST analysis in multiple leads)
- Determine STAT level based on ventricular rate AND clinical context
- Recognize the "normal ventricular rate" deception pattern at high atrial rates
- Attempt Type I vs. Type II classification where data allows

### 6.4 Temporal Pattern Complexity
High. The core task is marching out P-waves across the entire rhythm strip at the measured PP interval, confirming P-wave presence at each expected position even when hidden in T-waves. This requires:
- Accurate PP interval measurement from at least 2 clearly visible P-waves
- Propagation of that interval across the full strip
- Verification at each marched position
- Rate calculation: ventricular rate = atrial rate / conduction ratio
- Assessment of ratio stability or variability across the strip

The "normal rate" deception adds a meta-level complexity: the algorithm must avoid anchoring on the ventricular rate and must independently analyze the atrial rate.

### 6.5 Differential Complexity
High. Multiple critical differentials: atrial flutter (most dangerous misnomer — treatment is completely different), complete heart block (requires explicit confirmation that some P-waves conduct), blocked PAC bigeminy (mistreating would miss the PAC trigger), sinus rhythm at normal ventricular rate when atrial rate is elevated with 2:1 block. The 2:1 block cannot be typed without additional data — this ambiguity must be explicitly communicated.

### 6.6 Difficulty Score

| Dimension | Score | Rationale |
|-----------|-------|-----------|
| Signal clarity needed | 5/5 | Non-conducted P-waves buried in T-waves; highest detection challenge in AV block spectrum |
| Number of leads required | 4/5 | Full 12-lead mandatory; multi-lead P-wave survey required |
| Cross-domain reasoning | 4/5 | P-counting + BBB morphology + STEMI + rate deception + STAT stratification |
| Temporal pattern complexity | 4/5 | P-wave marching across full strip; rate relationship analysis; constancy/variability of ratio |
| Differential complexity | 4/5 | AFL vs. 2:1 block; CHB vs. high-grade; blocked PAC bigeminy; rate deception |
| Rarity in PTB-XL | 3/5 | Advanced AV blocks less common; 2:1 somewhat represented; higher ratios rare |
| **Overall difficulty** | **3.2/5** | **P-wave detection challenge + rate deception + typing ambiguity = highest in AV block group** |

## 6A. Agent Assignment

**Architecture:** Option E — 3 Parallel Phase 1 Specialists + 1 Sequential CDS (Node 2.1 Gate Passed 2026-03-27)

| Agent | Role for High-Grade AV Block | Key Features Used |
|-------|--------------------------|-------------------|
| **RRC** (Rhythm/Rate/Conduction) | Primary | ≥2 consecutive non-conducted P-waves (3:1, 4:1 block ratio), or 2:1 block with wide QRS; constant PP interval, slow ventricular rate, escape beats if present, AV dissociation patterns |
| **IT** (Ischemia/Territory) | Supporting | ST elevation in anterior (LAD/septal) or inferior (RCA) territory as ischemic etiology; high-grade AVB in anterior STEMI indicates extensive His-Purkinje damage |
| **MR** (Morphology/Repolarization) | Supporting | QRS morphology of conducted beats and escape beats: wide QRS in conducted beats confirms infra-Hisian level; escape beat morphology (narrow = His, wide = ventricular) determines escape pacemaker site |
| **CDS** (Cross-Domain Synthesis) | Required — urgency escalation and progression risk | Integrates block ratio (RRC) with QRS morphology (MR) to estimate block level; incorporates ischemic context (IT) to flag high-grade AVB as a STEMI complication; assesses risk of progression to complete heart block |

### Primary Agent
**RRC** — High-grade AVB is defined by the ratio of non-conducted to conducted P-waves (≥2:1 with ≥2 consecutive drops); counting P-waves against QRS complexes and measuring the conduction ratio is a core RRC task.

### Cross-Domain Hints
- RRC → MR: `cross_domain_hint: "high_grade_AV_block — measure conducted QRS width and escape beat morphology to classify block level and escape pacemaker"`
- RRC → IT: `cross_domain_hint: "high_grade_AV_block — evaluate for concurrent STEMI as ischemic cause"`

### CDS Specific Role
CDS integrates the block ratio from RRC, the QRS morphology from MR, and the ischemic context from IT to produce a complete assessment: block level (AV nodal vs His-Purkinje), ischemic vs non-ischemic etiology, escape rhythm reliability (narrow vs wide QRS escape), and urgency classification. High-grade AVB with anterior STEMI receives maximum urgency. High-grade AVB with wide QRS escape rhythm (ventricular) is flagged as more unstable than narrow QRS (His) escape.

- QRS width as surrogate for block site in 2:1 AV block

- High-grade AV block in inferior STEMI vs. anterior STEMI: different etiologies, different urgency
- Lyme carditis: fluctuating high-grade AV block; reversible with antibiotics; temporary pacing in acute phase

### 7.2 Key Clinical Thresholds for RAG Retrieval
- 2:1 P:QRS ratio → high-grade AV block classification trigger; cannot type without longer strip
- 3:1 or higher → advanced AV block; ventricular rate assessment mandatory
- 2:1 + wide QRS → treat as Mobitz Type II; STAT escalation
- 2:1 + atrial rate ≥ 100bpm → deceptive ventricular rate warning; verify actual rate
- 2:1 + ST elevation V1-V4 → anterior STEMI + infranodal block emergency
- 2:1 + ST elevation II/III/aVF → inferior STEMI + AV nodal ischemia protocol
- Ventricular rate < 50bpm with high-grade block → hemodynamic compromise assessment; pacing threshold
- 2:1 + digoxin history → do NOT give atropine; digoxin Fab evaluation

## 9. Edge Cases and Pitfalls

**Pitfall 1 — The rate deception (most dangerous pitfall in entire AV block group):** A patient with a sinus rate of 150bpm and 2:1 AV block has a ventricular rate of 75bpm. Without P-wave counting, the ECG reads as "normal sinus rhythm at 75bpm." The non-conducted P-wave falls on the T-wave and may appear as T-wave peaking or asymmetry. Any T-wave that looks peaked or bifid in the context of a slightly fast sinus rate should trigger P-wave counting. Always measure PP interval independently of QRS.

**Pitfall 2 — Atrial flutter with 2:1 conduction:** At 300bpm flutter rate, 2:1 conduction gives a ventricular rate of 150bpm — this is a classic presentation that may look like sinus tachycardia at first glance, or like 2:1 AV block with "P-waves" at 300bpm. Flutter waves are NOT isoelectric P-waves — they show continuous sawtooth morphology with no true isoelectric baseline. Ventricular rate of exactly 150bpm in an adult should always trigger flutter search. Treatment (cardioversion vs. AV nodal conduction block) is completely different from treating sinus 2:1 AV block.

**Pitfall 3 — Confusing high-grade with complete AV block:** In 3:1 or 4:1 block with a slow ventricular rate, the QRS complexes may appear "escaped" and seem independent of P-waves. The distinction is critical: in high-grade block, at least some P-waves conduct with a FIXED PR. In complete heart block, NO P-wave conducts — the PR varies randomly. Finding even one clear P-QRS pair with a consistent PR confirms high-grade (not complete) block. This distinction affects management: high-grade may still respond to atropine (if AV nodal); complete block essentially never does.

**Pitfall 4 — Blocked PAC bigeminy mimicking 2:1 AV block:** Alternating sinus beat followed by nonconducted PAC followed by sinus beat looks identical to 2:1 AV block at first glance. The PAC has a DIFFERENT P-wave morphology and a SHORT coupling interval (P-wave appears early, not on-time). The sinus PP interval is INTERRUPTED by the PAC (PAC resets or partially resets the sinus node). In 2:1 AV block, PP intervals are REGULAR — the blocked P-wave appears on time, at exactly the measured PP interval from the preceding P-wave.

**Pitfall 5 — Digoxin toxicity and atropine contraindication:** In digoxin-toxic patients with high-grade AV block, atropine is CONTRAINDICATED — it can precipitate refractory ventricular fibrillation by enhancing the direct ventricular pro-arrhythmic effects of digoxin while transiently increasing AV conduction. The algorithm must flag digoxin context and suppress atropine recommendations, replacing with digoxin-specific Fab antibodies and pacing.

**Pitfall 6 — Functional vs. pathologic 2:1 block during atrial tachycardia:** If the atrial rate is very fast (e.g., 200bpm in atrial tachycardia), 2:1 block may be physiologic AV nodal protection — the AV node simply cannot conduct at 200bpm. This is not pathologic AV block; it is rate-dependent block. The clinical question is the atrial arrhythmia, not the AV conduction. Functional 2:1 block resolves when the atrial rate decreases.

**Pitfall 7 — Very long first-degree block plus Wenckebach appearing as 2:1:** A patient with severe first-degree block (PR = 350ms) and a Wenckebach cycle length of 2:1 will show every other P conducting with PR = 350ms. The non-conducted P may be hidden 350ms after the prior P — on the T-wave upslope. This looks like functional 2:1 but is actually a Wenckebach with a very long first-beat PR. The key is finding a segment with 3:2 or 4:3 conduction where the PR increment and cycle reset can be observed.
