## 1. Pathophysiology Root Cause

### 1.1 Total Electrical Alternans — Pericardial Tamponade
In pericardial tamponade, fluid accumulation within the rigid pericardial sac compresses the heart. The intrapericardial pressure equals or exceeds the filling pressure of the right ventricle (and, in severe cases, both ventricles), impairing diastolic filling. The heart compensates by developing a pendular swinging motion within the fluid — oscillating at approximately 1 Hz (one complete swing per two cardiac cycles). This swinging motion produces a beat-to-beat change in the electrical axis of the heart (~20° oscillation per beat), which manifests as alternating QRS amplitude and axis on the surface ECG.

The classic tamponade electrical alternans pattern is perfectly regular alternation: one beat with higher QRS amplitude, next beat with lower QRS amplitude, in consistent repeating fashion. All leads are affected simultaneously. Total electrical alternans (QRS + T wave alternans) is more specific for tamponade than QRS alternans alone.

### 1.2 T-Wave Alternans — Arrhythmic Substrate
Microvolt T-wave alternans (MTWA) is a completely different phenomenon from tamponade. At the cellular level, beat-to-beat alternation in the action potential duration (APD) — driven by calcium handling instability — creates alternating repolarization patterns. In significant APD alternans, adjacent myocardial cells develop discordant APD alternans (neighboring cells alternate out of phase with each other), creating localized refractory period heterogeneity that provides substrate for re-entrant VT/VF. Clinical T-wave alternans (visible on standard ECG, ≥1 mm) indicates severe electrical instability — seen in TdP, severe LQTS, acute ischemia, and advanced cardiomyopathy.

### 1.3 QRS-Only Alternans
QRS amplitude alternation without T-wave alternans occurs in: SVT (bundle branch aberrancy alternates), cardiomyopathy (severe LV dysfunction with alternating contractility), and large pericardial effusions without tamponade physiology. The mechanism in cardiomyopathy involves mechanical alternans (pulsus alternans) directly producing electrical alternans.

### 1.4 P-Wave Alternans
Beat-to-beat alternation in P-wave morphology or amplitude. Seen in tamponade (swinging atria), atrial disease, or certain supraventricular arrhythmias. Tamponade produces all three simultaneously (P + QRS + T alternans = total electrical alternans).

## 2. ECG Presentation — Lead by Lead

### 2.1 Diagnostic Criteria (2025 AHA/ESC Guidelines)

| Type | ECG Criteria | Clinical Association | Mechanism |
|---|---|---|---|
| Total electrical alternans | Beat-to-beat alternation in QRS + T amplitude/axis, all leads | Pericardial tamponade (highly specific) | Pendular cardiac motion in effusion |
| QRS alternans only | Beat-to-beat QRS amplitude alternation, consistent in multiple leads | Tamponade, large effusion, SVT, cardiomyopathy | Pendular motion or aberrancy |
| T-wave alternans (visible) | Beat-to-beat T amplitude/polarity alternation ≥1 mm | TdP risk, LQTS, severe ischemia, advanced cardiomyopathy | Discordant cellular APD alternans |
| Microvolt T-wave alternans | Sub-millimeter T alternation (requires signal averaging) | Risk stratification for VT/VF in cardiomyopathy | Same as above; subtler |
| P-wave alternans | Beat-to-beat P amplitude alternation | Tamponade, atrial disease | Atrial swinging in effusion |

### 2.3 Key Leads
- **All precordial leads** in tamponade: alternation is global, simultaneous, regular — best demonstrated in V5 for amplitude measurement
- **V1–V3** in T-wave alternans: precordial leads show repolarization alternans most prominently
- **Any single lead on long rhythm strip** for confirming alternation regularity and pattern (tamponade: perfectly alternating; arrhythmic: may be less perfect)

### 2.4 Beat-by-Beat Considerations
The defining criterion for electrical alternans is: every-other-beat variation that is regular and consistent across multiple beats. Random or irregular QRS amplitude variation is not electrical alternans — it represents artifact, ectopy, or arrhythmia. The period of electrical alternans in tamponade corresponds to the swinging frequency (typically 0.8–1.2 Hz).

In tamponade, rate is typically sinus tachycardia (compensatory to maintain cardiac output despite reduced stroke volume). The combination sinus tachycardia + electrical alternans is highly suspicious. Adding low voltage QRS completes the "tamponade triad."

## 3. Morphology Details

### 3.1 QRS Alternation in Tamponade
The QRS complex alternates between two morphologies on consecutive beats. Beat A: QRS with higher amplitude and/or slightly different axis. Beat B: QRS with lower amplitude and/or complementary axis shift. The axis shift is approximately 20° between alternating beats, visible as the QRS in any given lead being taller on one beat and shorter on the next. The QRS morphology (width, shape) itself does not change — only amplitude changes.

### 3.2 T-Wave Alternation in Tamponade
T waves alternate in height alongside QRS alternation. Both positive T waves simply differ in magnitude between beats. The T inversion does not alternate direction — both beats have upright T waves, merely different amplitude. This distinguishes tamponade T alternans from arrhythmic T-wave alternans where the T wave may actually invert on alternating beats.

### 3.3 Visible T-Wave Alternans (Arrhythmic)
In malignant arrhythmic T-wave alternans, the T wave may alternate between:
- Large upright and small upright (amplitude alternans)
- Upright and flat or inverted (polarity alternans — more ominous)
When T-wave polarity alternates (positive on beat A, negative or flat on beat B), this represents severe discordant APD alternans across the myocardial wall — the highest-risk variant.

### 3.4 P-Wave Alternation
In tamponade, P-wave amplitude alternates along with QRS. This is because the atria also swing within the pericardial fluid. P-wave alternation is best seen in lead II. The PR interval typically remains constant (AV conduction unaffected).

### 3.5 Low Voltage Accompanying Tamponade
Pericardial fluid attenuates the electrical signal (fluid is a poor conductor of biological electrical signals compared to cardiac muscle). QRS amplitude in ALL leads is reduced. The combination of low QRS voltage across all leads PLUS electrical alternans PLUS sinus tachycardia = tamponade triad. Each individual feature is non-specific; the triad is highly specific.

### 3.6 Rate Relationship in SVT Alternans
In SVT with QRS alternans, the alternating QRS morphology often represents alternating bundle branch aberrancy — one beat conducts normally, the next beats aberrantly through a bundle branch still in relative refractoriness. Rate-dependent LBBB or RBBB can produce this pattern. This is functionally benign (no structural implication) but can be confused with ventricular bigeminy.

### 3.7 Pulsus Alternans Relationship
Pulsus alternans (alternating strong and weak pulses) is the hemodynamic correlate of electrical alternans in cardiomyopathy. The ECG electrical alternans in this context arises because mechanical stroke volume alternation generates alternating preloads and chamber pressures, which in turn alter the spread of depolarization and axis.

## 4. Differential Diagnosis

### 4.1 Mimics

| Condition | Differentiating Feature |
|---|---|
| Bigeminy (PVC alternating) | Every other beat has a morphologically different QRS (ectopic morphology); compensatory pause; not regular alternation of identical QRS |
| Respiratory variation | Gradual cyclic variation (3–5 beat cycle, not every-other-beat); disappears with breath-hold |
| Electrode movement artifact | Seen in one lead only; other leads stable; patient motion |
| Second-degree AV block (2:1) | P wave visible before each QRS; PR relationship alternates; QRS itself does not change amplitude |
| SVT with alternating aberrancy | Narrow-to-wide alternation (different QRS morphology each beat); context of tachycardia |
| Atrial fibrillation | Irregular RR intervals; no consistent alternating pattern |

### 4.2 Coexisting Conditions
- **Tamponade + pericarditis**: Pericarditis causing effusion that develops tamponade physiology; ECG may show BOTH diffuse concave STE (pericarditis Stage 1) AND electrical alternans (tamponade)
- **Tamponade + malignancy**: Malignant pericardial effusion (lymphoma, lung cancer, breast cancer) — most common cause of large effusion with tamponade; electrical alternans is a late sign indicating tamponade physiology
- **Tamponade + low voltage**: Almost always co-occur in true tamponade; the combination is essentially diagnostic

## 5. STAT Classification

| Finding | STAT Level | Action |
|---|---|---|
| Total electrical alternans (QRS + T) + hypotension + tachycardia | STAT — Critical | Emergent echo, pericardiocentesis preparation |
| Total electrical alternans + normotension | STAT — Urgent | Emergent echo; tamponade physiology likely; cardiology immediately |
| QRS alternans alone + large effusion on clinical exam | STAT — Urgent | Bedside echo, hemodynamic assessment |
| T-wave alternans (visible, ≥1 mm) + no tachycardia | Urgent | Rule out electrolyte abnormality, ischemia, LQTS; cardiology |
| T-wave alternans + TdP on same monitoring strip | STAT — Critical | ACLS protocol, electrolyte correction, suppress TdP trigger |
| P + QRS + T alternans simultaneously | STAT — Critical | Tamponade diagnosis; emergent echo |
| SVT with QRS alternans | Urgent | Rate control/conversion; echo to assess for effusion |

## 6. Reasoning Complexity Analysis

### 6.1 Reasoning Domains Required
- Pattern recognition: every-other-beat amplitude alternation vs random variation vs artifact
- Multi-lead analysis: confirming simultaneous alternation in multiple leads (tamponade) vs single-lead phenomenon
- Type classification: total (tamponade) vs T-only (arrhythmic) vs QRS-only (effusion/SVT/cardiomyopathy) vs P-only
- Clinical integration: blood pressure, JVP, pulsus paradoxus, dyspnea, history of malignancy/pericarditis
- Hemodynamic context: hypotension + tachycardia + electrical alternans = tamponade until proven otherwise
- Rhythm interpretation: ruling out bigeminy (ectopy) vs true electrical alternans

### 6.2 Feature Dependencies
- Alternans detection requires a long rhythm strip (≥10 consecutive beats); single complexes insufficient
- QRS amplitude measurement must reference same lead across consecutive beats
- The "tamponade triad" requires separate detection of: (1) low voltage, (2) sinus tachycardia, (3) electrical alternans — all three components must be assessed
- Hemodynamic data (BP, HR, pulsus paradoxus) is essential clinical integration

### 6.3 Cross-Condition Interactions
- Electrical alternans + LQTS: T-wave alternans (especially polarity alternans) in a patient with prolonged QTc indicates imminent TdP; highest risk combination
- Electrical alternans + hypokalemia: hypokalemia can trigger T-wave alternans; electrolyte correction mandatory
- Tamponade + cardiac tamponade after MI (Dressler syndrome): late post-MI pericarditis can produce effusion and tamponade weeks after MI

### 6.4 Reasoning Chain Sketch
1. On rhythm strip or 12-lead, detect beat-to-beat QRS amplitude variation
2. Confirm: is it every-other-beat? (alternans) vs random (artifact, ectopy) vs cyclic/gradual (respiratory)
3. Check: does alternation involve QRS only, T wave only, or both (total)?
4. Check: does alternation involve P waves?
5. Verify: is QRS morphology the same on alternating beats (axis shift only) vs different morphology (ectopy)?
6. Assess QRS width: narrow = SVT aberrancy possible; wide = consider VT bigeminy
7. Cross-reference: low voltage present? Sinus tachycardia present? → Tamponade triad
8. Integrate clinical: BP, JVP, pulsus paradoxus, history
9. If total electrical alternans or tamponade triad → STAT critical; bedside echo

### 6.5 Confidence Anchors
- Total electrical alternans (P + QRS + T) + hypotension + tachycardia: extremely high confidence tamponade
- QRS amplitude alternation on rhythm strip + clinical large effusion: high confidence tamponade physiology
- T-wave polarity alternans + long QT + TdP on strip: high confidence malignant T-wave alternans
- Beat-to-beat QRS variation in only one lead: low confidence electrical alternans; likely artifact or respiratory

### 6.6 Difficulty Score

| Dimension | Score (1–5) | Rationale |
|---|---|---|
| Morphology Recognition | 2 | The alternating pattern is visually recognizable once beat-to-beat comparison is done |
| Interval Measurement | 2 | Amplitude measurement per beat; no complex intervals |
| Differential Diagnosis | 3 | Bigeminy and artifact exclusion; type classification (tamponade vs arrhythmic) |
| Clinical Integration | 3 | Hemodynamic context essential; tamponade triad recognition |
| Rarity / Prior Probability | 3 | Tamponade is uncommon; T-wave alternans is uncommon outside critical care |
| Arrhythmia Risk Stratification | 3 | Tamponade = mechanical crisis; T-wave alternans = arrhythmic crisis; both require urgent action |
| **Overall** | **2.8** | Recognition is straightforward; clinical urgency is the driver of difficulty |

| Agent | Role for Electrical Alternans | Key Features Used |
|-------|--------------------------|-------------------|
| **RRC** (Rhythm/Rate/Conduction) | Supporting | Heart rate and rhythm regularity context: electrical alternans occurs in regular rhythms (sinus tachycardia, SVT); rapid rate (often >100 bpm) is the typical setting for alternans related to pericardial effusion or tamponade; per-beat QRS amplitude tracking |
| **IT** (Ischemia/Territory) | Not involved | No ischemia-specific features in electrical alternans |
| **MR** (Morphology/Repolarization) | Primary | Beat-to-beat alternation in QRS amplitude (measuring peak-to-peak QRS height across consecutive beats; alternation ≥1mm in at least one lead); P-wave alternans (less common, confirms atrial involvement); T-wave alternans (may co-occur); low QRS voltage overall (QRS <5mm in limb leads — if present, raises pericardial effusion suspicion) |
| **CDS** (Cross-Domain Synthesis) | Standard integration only — integrates alternans with low voltage for tamponade flag | Integrates MR alternans finding (beat-to-beat QRS amplitude alternation) with RRC rate context (regular rhythm with tachycardia) and MR low voltage (if present); if alternans + low voltage + sinus tachycardia: CDS generates pericardial effusion/tamponade flag |

### Primary Agent
**MR** — electrical alternans is a morphology phenomenon defined by beat-to-beat alternation in QRS amplitude (≥1mm); measurement of sequential QRS amplitudes is in the MR domain.

### Cross-Domain Hints
- MR emits: `cross_domain_hint: "Beat-to-beat QRS amplitude alternation ≥1mm detected — RRC must confirm regular rhythm (alternans requires regular rate context); if low QRS voltage also present, CDS must flag pericardial effusion/tamponade"`

### CDS Specific Role
CDS integrates MR alternans detection (beat-to-beat QRS amplitude variation ≥1mm) with RRC rhythm context (regular rhythm — required for alternans pattern recognition) and MR low-voltage status. The critical CDS output is the tamponade flag: if electrical alternans co-occurs with low QRS voltage (QRS <5mm in limb leads) AND sinus tachycardia from RRC, CDS generates a high-priority pericardial effusion/tamponade alert. Electrical alternans without low voltage in the context of SVT at fast rates may represent a functional alternans without structural pericardial disease; CDS notes this distinction. Standard integration otherwise.

## 9. Edge Cases and Pitfalls

1. **Bigeminy mistaken for electrical alternans**: PVC bigeminy (every other beat is a PVC) creates an alternating QRS amplitude pattern. The differentiator: in bigeminy, the two QRS morphologies are different (normal QRS alternating with ectopic wide QRS); in tamponade alternans, both QRS complexes have the same morphology with only amplitude (axis) differing.
2. **Single-lead pseudo-alternans**: An electrode near the axilla or over a bony prominence may pick up motion artifact that mimics alternation in that lead only. Electrical alternans in tamponade affects ALL leads simultaneously. Any single-lead finding requires multi-lead confirmation.
3. **Tamponade without electrical alternans**: Only ~20% of cardiac tamponade cases produce classical electrical alternans. The absence of alternans does NOT rule out tamponade. Clinical and echo assessment cannot be replaced by ECG alone.
4. **Effusion without tamponade**: Large pericardial effusions may produce low voltage without electrical alternans. Tamponade physiology (elevated pericardial pressure) is what causes the pendular motion and alternans. An effusion that does not compress cardiac chambers does not produce alternans.
5. **Rate-dependent appearance**: Electrical alternans in tamponade may only be present at certain heart rates. At very fast rates, the pendular oscillation may not complete a full cycle between beats. The alternans may appear and disappear as rate varies.
6. **Microvolt T-wave alternans vs visible T-wave alternans**: MTWA (measured by signal averaging during stress testing) is a risk stratification tool for sudden death in cardiomyopathy — not detectable on standard ECG. Visible T-wave alternans (≥1 mm) on standard ECG is a much later, more severe finding indicating acute electrical instability.
