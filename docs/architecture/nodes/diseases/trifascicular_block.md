## 1. Pathophysiology Root Cause

### 1.1 Anatomic Substrate

The His-Purkinje conduction system fans into three fascicles after the bundle of His: the right bundle branch (RBB), the left anterior fascicle (LAF), and the left posterior fascicle (LPF). Trifascicular block implies disease of all three. In practice, "trifascicular block" is a clinical-ECG construct rather than a histologically confirmed entity — what the ECG shows is bifascicular block plus first-degree AV block, inferring that the one surviving conducting limb is also diseased.

### 1.2 Cellular Mechanism

Disease most commonly results from fibrocalcific degeneration (Lenegre–Lev disease), ischemic damage to the proximal conduction system, sarcoidosis, Chagas disease, or infiltrative cardiomyopathy. The bifascicular disease slows or blocks two fascicles completely. The prolonged PR interval indicates that the AV node, His bundle, or the single remaining fascicle is conducting slowly — either due to intrinsic AV nodal disease (not true trifascicular) or diseased remaining fascicular tissue (true trifascicular). The distinction matters: AV nodal PR prolongation suggests a stable, vagally-modulated delay; infra-Hisian prolongation (His-ventricular interval >55ms on electrophysiology study) indicates imminent complete AV block.

### 1.3 Hemodynamic Consequence

Trifascicular block by itself does not impair cardiac output. The danger is progression to complete (third-degree) AV block with ventricular standstill or a slow, unreliable junctional/ventricular escape rhythm. Syncope in the setting of trifascicular pattern should be presumed to represent paroxysmal complete AV block until electrophysiology study proves otherwise. Sudden cardiac death risk is real, driven by bradyasystole rather than VF in most cases.

## 2. ECG Presentation — Lead by Lead

### 2.1 Diagnostic Criteria (2025 AHA/ESC Guidelines)

Trifascicular block is diagnosed by the simultaneous presence of:

1. Bifascicular block: either RBBB + LAFB (most common combination) or RBBB + LPFB
2. First-degree AV block: PR interval ≥200ms in adults
3. Sinus rhythm (required to measure PR)

No QRS duration threshold beyond what is required for the bifascicular component. QRS morphology must fully satisfy RBBB criteria (rsR' or rSR' in V1, wide S in I/V6, QRS ≥120ms) PLUS either LAFB (left-axis deviation beyond −45°, qR in I/aVL, rS in II/III/aVF) or LPFB (right-axis deviation beyond +90° with no other cause, rS in I/aVL, qR in III/aVF).

### 2.2 Lead-by-Lead Manifestation

| Lead | Expected Finding | Why | Sensitivity |
|------|-----------------|-----|-------------|
| I | Wide terminal S-wave; qR pattern if LAFB present | RBBB: left-to-right terminal conduction; LAFB: superior initial vector | High |
| II | rS pattern (LAFB) or qR pattern (LPFB); wide QRS | Inferior lead reflects fascicular axis deviation | High |
| III | rS (LAFB, strongly negative QRS axis) or qR (LPFB) | Most sensitive inferior lead for fascicular axis | High |
| aVR | Wide terminal R wave (RBBB) | Terminal rightward force from RBBB reaches aVR positively | Moderate |
| aVL | qR pattern (LAFB); narrow q from septal activation | Lateral augmented lead aligned with LAF superior vector | High for LAFB |
| aVF | rS (LAFB) | Inferior augmented lead opposite to leftward superior axis | High for LAFB |
| V1 | rsR' or rSR'; wide, notched R' (right rabbit ear) | Right ventricular activation delayed by RBBB | Very high |
| V2 | rsR' or rSR'; may show secondary R' | Same as V1; confirms right ventricular conduction delay | High |
| V3 | Transitional; wide QRS | Transition zone not strongly diagnostic | Low |
| V4 | Transitional to lateral pattern | Mid-precordial transition | Low |
| V5 | Wide terminal S-wave | RBBB: delayed right-to-left terminal forces reach V5/V6 late | High |
| V6 | Wide terminal S-wave; qRS pattern | RBBB hallmark in lateral precordial leads | Very high |
| All leads | PR interval ≥200ms measured from first deflection of P to first deflection of QRS | Slow conduction through AV node, His bundle, or diseased remaining fascicle | Moderate (PR measures interval, not mechanism) |

### 2.3 Key Leads

- **V1**: RBBB morphology confirmation — rSR' or rsR' pattern is required
- **I and aVL**: Confirm fascicular deviation (qR for LAFB; S-wave for LPFB)
- **II, III, aVF**: Axis confirmation — LAFB requires QRS axis ≤−45°; LPFB requires ≥+90°
- **Any lead with clear P-wave onset**: PR measurement; lead II or V1 typically clearest

### 2.4 Beat-by-Beat Considerations

The PR interval must be measured consistently across multiple beats to confirm first-degree AV block is stable and not a transient vagal phenomenon. In patients with paroxysmal complete AV block, some beats may show the full trifascicular pattern; occasionally Holter telemetry captures the transition to 2:1 or complete block. Look for PR stability over at least 3–5 consecutive beats. If PR interval varies beat-to-beat (Wenckebach), this changes the diagnosis.

## 3. Morphology Details

### 3.1 QRS Width

QRS ≥120ms driven by RBBB component. In RBBB + LAFB, QRS width is typically 120–150ms. Widths >160ms raise concern for additional IVCD or drug effect (sodium channel blockade).

### 3.2 QRS Axis

RBBB + LAFB: axis −45° to −90° (left axis deviation). RBBB + LPFB: axis +90° to +180° (right axis deviation). The axis deviation is the ECG signature of which left fascicle is blocked.

### 3.3 P-Wave Morphology

Normal sinus P-wave expected. P-wave morphology should be evaluated: broad bifid P (P mitrale) or tall peaked P (P pulmonale) may indicate structural heart disease underlying the conduction system disease.

### 3.4 PR Interval

≥200ms, measured in the lead showing the earliest P-wave onset (usually II or V1). Must be constant to call first-degree AV block; if PR progressively lengthens, this is Mobitz I superimposed on bifascicular block — an even more unstable pattern.

### 3.5 ST-T Wave Changes

ST-T changes in RBBB are expected in the right precordial leads (V1–V3): ST depression and T-wave inversion in leads with dominant R' are a normal consequence of abnormal repolarization (secondary ST-T changes). ST elevation in V1–V3 in the setting of RBBB must raise concern for concomitant right ventricular pathology or Brugada pattern. ST changes in lateral leads (I, aVL, V5–V6) are not expected from RBBB alone and suggest ischemia.

### 3.6 Delta Waves

Absent. Delta waves would indicate WPW, not a conduction system block.

### 3.7 QT Interval

QTc should be measured. Trifascicular block from same disease processes that cause QT prolongation (sarcoidosis, drug toxicity) may coexist. Corrected QT measurement in wide QRS requires JT interval method or careful manual correction.

## 4. Differential Diagnosis

### 4.1 Mimics

| Mimic | How to Distinguish |
|-------|--------------------|
| RBBB + LAFB + Wenckebach (Mobitz I) | Progressive PR lengthening before dropped beat — more ominous, not first-degree alone |
| RBBB + LAFB + 2:1 AV block | Every other P-wave blocked — PR constant on conducted beats, but 2:1 ratio present |
| LBBB (may mimic axis shift) | LBBB does NOT show rSR' in V1; monophasic QS or rS in V1; different axis behavior |
| Drug-induced QRS widening (TCA, Ia/Ic antiarrhythmics) | History, terminal R in aVR for TCA; no PR prolongation unless bradycardic; not fascicular pattern |
| Hyperkalemia causing wide QRS | Peaked T-waves, P-wave flattening, sine wave progression; no classic BBB morphology |
| Left ventricular hypertrophy (axis leftward) | Does not produce wide QRS or RBBB morphology; voltage criteria different |

### 4.2 Coexisting Conditions

- STEMI can cause new trifascicular block (especially anterior STEMI involving the LAD which supplies RBB and LAF)
- New trifascicular block in the context of anterior STEMI is a class I indication for temporary pacing
- Hyperkalemia can exaggerate PR prolongation in a patient with baseline bifascicular disease
- Sarcoidosis and Chagas: multifocal conduction disease can produce changing bifascicular patterns

## 5. STAT Classification

| Priority | Criteria |
|----------|---------|
| STAT — Immediate Cardiology Consult | New trifascicular block + syncope or presyncope |
| STAT — Immediate Cardiology Consult | New trifascicular block + anterior STEMI |
| STAT — Immediate Cardiology Consult | Trifascicular block + hemodynamic instability |
| HIGH — Urgent Admission | New trifascicular block (asymptomatic) — EPS study or pacemaker evaluation urgently required |
| HIGH — Urgent Review | Known trifascicular block + new symptoms (dyspnea, presyncope) |
| ROUTINE | Known, chronic, stable trifascicular block on serial ECGs — rate unchanged, no new symptoms |

**Key clinical rule:** Syncope + trifascicular pattern = presumed complete heart block until proven otherwise. Do not discharge without monitoring.

## 6. Reasoning Complexity Analysis

### 6.1 Reasoning Domains Required

- Pattern recognition: RBBB morphology (rSR' V1, terminal S in I/V6)
- Axis calculation: quantitative left or right axis deviation measurement
- Interval measurement: PR interval with millisecond precision
- Integration: combining three separate ECG findings into a single diagnosis
- Clinical contextualization: distinguishing new vs. chronic, symptomatic vs. not

### 6.2 Feature Dependencies

```
RBBB confirmed? → Yes
    ↓
QRS axis: <−45°? → LAFB confirmed → Bifascicular block present
    OR
QRS axis: >+90° (with no other cause)? → LPFB confirmed → Bifascicular block present
    ↓
PR interval ≥200ms in sinus rhythm? → First-degree AV block
    ↓
All three criteria met → TRIFASCICULAR BLOCK
```

RBBB must be confirmed FIRST. Axis must be calculated after RBBB is confirmed (RBBB itself can cause mild rightward axis shift — do not diagnose LPFB in RBBB unless axis is convincingly >+90°).

### 6.3 Cross-Condition Interactions

- STEMI (anterior): New RBBB in anterior STEMI implies proximal LAD occlusion — trifascicular block in this context is a pacing emergency
- Hyperkalemia: May superficially widen QRS and prolong PR — must exclude metabolic cause
- WPW: Short PR is opposite finding — if PR is short, reconsider
- Mobitz I vs. first-degree: Progressive PR lengthening changes management significantly

### 6.4 Reasoning Chain Sketch

```
1. Confirm sinus rhythm (P-waves present, constant morphology)
2. Measure QRS duration → ≥120ms required
3. Evaluate V1 morphology → rSR' or rsR' confirms RBBB
4. Measure QRS axis → determine which left fascicle is blocked
5. Confirm morphologic criteria for LAFB or LPFB in limb leads
6. Measure PR interval across multiple beats → confirm constant ≥200ms
7. Exclude other causes of PR prolongation (drugs, vagal, AV nodal disease alone)
8. Assign STAT level based on clinical context (new vs. chronic, symptoms)
```

### 6.5 Confidence Anchors

- High confidence: rSR' V1 + terminal S in I/V6 = RBBB confirmed
- High confidence: Axis ≤−45° with qR in aVL + rS in inferior leads = LAFB confirmed
- High confidence: PR ≥200ms in multiple consecutive sinus beats = first-degree AV block confirmed
- Moderate confidence: PR prolongation mechanism (AV nodal vs. His-Purkinje) cannot be determined from surface ECG alone — requires EP study

### 6.6 Difficulty Score

| Dimension | Score (1–5) | Notes |
|-----------|-------------|-------|
| Signal clarity needed | 3 | Need clear P-waves for PR; clear V1 for RBBB |
| Number of leads required | 3 | V1, I, aVL, II/III/aVF, one PR-measuring lead — 6+ leads needed |
| Cross-domain reasoning | 3 | Three separate criteria must be integrated simultaneously |
| Temporal pattern complexity | 2 | Single beat sufficient once criteria confirmed; Holter needed if paroxysmal |
| Differential complexity | 3 | LBBB, drug effect, 2:1 block all must be excluded |
| Rarity in PTB-XL | 3 | Uncommon label; bifascicular more common; true trifascicular less |
| Overall difficulty | 3 | Must identify bifascicular component and measure PR together |

## 6A. Agent Assignment

**Architecture:** Option E — 3 Parallel Phase 1 Specialists + 1 Sequential CDS (Node 2.1 Gate Passed 2026-03-27)

| Agent | Role for Trifascicular Block | Key Features Used |
|-------|--------------------------|-------------------|
| **RRC** (Rhythm/Rate/Conduction) | Primary | QRS duration ≥120ms (RBBB component); axis deviation −45° to −90° or >+90° (fascicular block component); PR interval >200ms (first-degree AV block component); alternating RBBB/LBBB pattern (complete bilateral BBB variant) |
| **IT** (Ischemia/Territory) | Not involved | No ischemia-specific features in trifascicular block |
| **MR** (Morphology/Repolarization) | Supporting | RBBB morphology confirmation: rSR' in V1-V2 with wide slurred S in I/V6; alternating BBB morphology if present (RBBB pattern alternating with LBBB pattern across beats) |
| **CDS** (Cross-Domain Synthesis) | Required — high-risk complete heart block flag | Synthesizes RRC triad (QRS ≥120ms + axis deviation + PR >200ms) with MR RBBB morphology to confirm trifascicular disease; generates high-priority flag for pacemaker evaluation; distinguishes from bifascicular block (missing PR prolongation) |

### Primary Agent
**RRC** — trifascicular block is defined by three concurrent conduction abnormalities all in the RRC domain: BBB (QRS ≥120ms), fascicular block (axis deviation), and first-degree AV block (PR >200ms); MR provides morphology support.

### Cross-Domain Hints
- RRC emits: `cross_domain_hint: "Bifascicular block with PR >200ms detected — CDS must confirm trifascicular disease pattern and generate high-risk complete heart block flag; MR must confirm RBBB morphology"`

### CDS Specific Role
CDS confirms trifascicular block by requiring all three RRC criteria simultaneously: QRS ≥120ms (RBBB), axis deviation (fascicular block component — LAD for LAFB or RAD for LPFB), and PR >200ms (first-degree AV block). MR RBBB morphology (rSR' in V1-V2) is integrated as a confirmatory finding. CDS generates a high-priority escalation flag indicating high risk of progression to complete heart block, with recommendation for cardiology consultation and pacemaker evaluation. If alternating RBBB/LBBB morphology is detected by MR across beats, CDS treats this as an alternative trifascicular presentation (bilateral bundle branch disease) requiring immediate escalation.

## 9. Edge Cases and Pitfalls

**RBBB + left axis deviation does not always mean LAFB**: Left axis deviation in RBBB can result from inferior MI (pathologic Q-waves in inferior leads with RBBB morphology). Check for q-waves in inferior leads — pathologic Q-waves (>40ms wide) suggest prior MI, not LAFB.

**LPFB is rare**: True LPFB requires exclusion of all other causes of right axis deviation — right ventricular hypertrophy, PE, lateral MI, normal variant in young thin patients. Do not diagnose LPFB casually.

**PR prolongation is not always infra-Hisian**: The surface ECG cannot localize PR prolongation. Only EP study (His bundle recording) can determine if delay is AV nodal (benign) vs. His-Purkinje (ominous). Document uncertainty in confidence statement.

**Rate-related changes**: At fast rates, RBBB may become incomplete or the axis may shift slightly due to rate-dependent aberrancy. Measure criteria at resting rate when possible.

**Drug effects mimicking trifascicular pattern**: Flecainide, propafenone, procainamide, and amiodarone can cause PR prolongation and QRS widening with RBBB-like morphology. History of antiarrhythmic use must trigger alternative explanation.

**New vs. old requires comparison**: A patient with chronic trifascicular block who presents with syncope requires immediate evaluation even if ECG is unchanged from baseline, because paroxysmal complete block may not be captured in the resting ECG. The trifascicular pattern on resting ECG is a risk marker, not a real-time indicator of block.
