## 1. Pathophysiology Root Cause

### 1.1 What Goes Wrong (Anatomy/Physiology)
- Complete failure of impulse conduction from the atria to the ventricles at any level of the AV conduction system (AV node, His bundle, or bilateral bundle branches)
- The SA node continues to depolarize the atria at its intrinsic rate (60-100 bpm), but no impulses reach the ventricles
- The ventricles are rescued by a subsidiary pacemaker: junctional (AV node/His bundle, 40-60 bpm) or ventricular (Purkinje fibers/myocardium, 20-40 bpm)
- Block at the AV node (supra-nodal) is most commonly caused by inferior MI (RCA supplies AV node in 85% of patients), enhanced vagal tone, or AV nodal-blocking drugs (beta-blockers, non-dihydropyridine calcium channel blockers, digoxin)
- Block at or below the His bundle (infra-nodal) is caused by anterior MI (LAD supplies the His-Purkinje system), degenerative fibrosis (Lenegre disease, Lev disease), infiltrative disease (sarcoidosis, amyloidosis), or post-cardiac surgery
- Congenital complete AV block occurs with structural heart disease (L-transposition, AV septal defects) or maternal anti-Ro/La antibodies (neonatal lupus)

### 1.2 Electrical Consequence
- Two independent pacemakers drive the heart simultaneously: the SA node drives the atria, and an escape pacemaker drives the ventricles
- Atrial depolarization (P-waves) occurs at the SA node rate — normal morphology, normal P-P intervals
- Ventricular depolarization (QRS complexes) occurs at the escape rate — QRS morphology depends on the escape site
- Junctional escape (above bifurcation of His): depolarization follows normal His-Purkinje pathways, producing a narrow QRS complex (≤120ms), rate 40-60 bpm
- Ventricular escape (below His bifurcation): depolarization spreads via slow myocyte-to-myocyte conduction, producing a wide QRS complex (>120ms, often >140ms), rate 20-40 bpm
- The AV dissociation is obligatory — there is no temporal relationship between atrial and ventricular activation

### 1.3 Why It Appears on ECG
- P-waves appear at regular intervals (constant P-P) reflecting SA node automaticity
- QRS complexes appear at regular intervals (constant R-R) reflecting escape pacemaker automaticity
- The P-wave rate is faster than the QRS rate (atrial rate > ventricular rate)
- P-R intervals vary continuously because P-waves and QRS complexes are independent — P-waves "march through" the QRS complexes
- The P-waves superimpose on different parts of the cardiac cycle: some fall on the ST segment, some on the T-wave, some are buried within the QRS — this creates subtle morphology distortions in the ST-T complex that vary beat to beat
- QRS morphology in all 12 leads reflects the escape site: junctional escape produces normal QRS axis and morphology; ventricular escape produces QRS morphology dependent on the focus location (often LBBB or RBBB-like pattern)

## 2. ECG Presentation — Lead by Lead

### 2.1 Diagnostic Criteria (2025 AHA/ESC Guidelines)

| Criterion | Threshold | Source |
|-----------|-----------|--------|
| P-wave and QRS independence | No fixed relationship between P-waves and QRS complexes | AHA/ACC/HRS 2018 Bradycardia Guidelines; ESC 2021 Pacing Guidelines |
| Constant P-P intervals | P-P intervals regular (±10% variability acceptable for sinus arrhythmia) | Standard ECG interpretation |
| Constant R-R intervals | R-R intervals regular (±10% variability acceptable) | Standard ECG interpretation |
| Atrial rate > ventricular rate | SA node rate (60-100) exceeds escape rate (20-60) | Diagnostic requirement |
| No conducted beats | Zero P-waves are followed by a QRS at a fixed PR interval | Distinguishes from high-grade 2nd degree AVB |
| Escape QRS duration | Narrow (≤120ms) = junctional; Wide (>120ms) = ventricular | Localizes block level |

### 2.3 Key Leads (Most Diagnostic)
- **Lead II**: Best lead for identifying P-waves marching through — P-wave amplitude is maximal in lead II because the normal P-wave axis (~60°) aligns with lead II's axis
- **V1**: Most important lead for characterizing escape QRS morphology (junctional vs ventricular, RBBB vs LBBB pattern)
- **aVF**: Supplementary to lead II for P-wave identification and inferior axis assessment of escape rhythm
- **Rhythm strip (any lead)**: Long rhythm strip essential to demonstrate constant P-P, constant R-R, and variable PR — at least 10 seconds needed

### 2.4 Beat-by-Beat Considerations
- AV dissociation is present on EVERY beat — it is a constant (not intermittent) pattern in complete AVB
- However, by pure chance, a P-wave may occasionally fall just before a QRS at a "normal" PR interval — this is coincidental, not conduction. To prove it is coincidental, demonstrate that other P-waves at the same coupling interval do NOT produce a QRS
- P-waves superimposed on QRS complexes create "pseudo" notching — this changes beat-to-beat QRS morphology subtly
- P-waves falling on T-waves create beat-to-beat T-wave morphology variation — a clue to AV dissociation
- Escape rhythm regularity: junctional escape is typically very regular; ventricular escape may show slight irregularity
- Watch for "warm-up" phenomenon: escape rate may gradually increase after onset of complete block

## 3. Morphology Details (What the Agent Must See)

### 3.1 P-wave Changes
- Morphology: Normal sinus P-wave morphology (upright in I, II, aVF; biphasic in V1) — the atria are depolarized normally by the SA node
- Duration: Normal (80-120ms) — atrial conduction is unaffected
- Rate: Sinus rate (60-100 bpm at rest), regular P-P intervals
- Axis: Normal P-wave axis (0° to +75°)
- Key observation: P-waves are frequently hidden within QRS complexes, ST segments, or T-waves — producing subtle distortions that change from beat to beat. The agent must identify these "buried" P-waves by looking for slight morphology variations in ST-T segments across consecutive beats

### 3.2 PR Interval Changes
- Duration: VARIABLE — there is no fixed PR interval because P-waves and QRS are independent
- Pattern: PR interval changes from beat to beat in a non-repeating pattern; this is the hallmark finding
- Critical distinction: In 2nd degree AVB Type I (Wenckebach), PR progressively prolongs then drops; in complete AVB, PR varies randomly with no pattern
- Measurement: PR interval measurement is meaningless in complete AVB — report as "dissociated" or "variable with no fixed relationship"

### 3.3 QRS Complex Changes
- **Junctional escape (supra-nodal block)**:
  - Duration: Narrow (≤120ms)
  - Morphology: Normal QRS morphology because depolarization uses the intact His-Purkinje system
  - Amplitude: Normal
  - Axis: Normal (-30° to +90°)
  - Rate: 40-60 bpm
- **Ventricular escape (infra-nodal block)**:
  - Duration: Wide (>120ms, often 140-200ms)
  - Morphology: Bizarre, often resembling LBBB or RBBB depending on escape focus location; notched, slurred
  - Amplitude: May be increased (wide QRS generates larger voltage)
  - Axis: Depends on escape focus — left ventricular focus produces rightward axis; right ventricular focus produces leftward axis; apical focus produces superior axis
  - Rate: 20-40 bpm (UNRELIABLE — may fail at any time)

### 3.4 ST Segment Changes
- Direction: No primary ST changes from complete AVB itself
- However: P-waves superimposed on ST segments create pseudo-ST elevation or depression that varies beat-to-beat — the agent must not misinterpret this as ischemia
- If underlying cause is MI: true ST changes will be present (inferior STEMI with complete AVB shows ST elevation in II, III, aVF independent of P-wave position)
- Measurement point: Standard J-point measurement, but must account for P-wave distortion

### 3.5 T-wave Changes
- Direction: Junctional escape: T-waves normal morphology; ventricular escape: discordant T-waves (opposite direction to QRS, similar to BBB)
- Beat-to-beat variation in T-wave morphology is a key clue — P-waves falling on T-waves change their shape from beat to beat
- Symmetry: Ventricular escape T-waves may be symmetric (repolarization abnormality secondary to wide QRS)
- The agent should flag beat-to-beat T-wave morphology variation as a clue to hidden P-waves

### 3.6 QT/QTc Changes
- No primary QT prolongation from complete AVB
- QTc correction is unreliable at very slow rates (Bazett formula overcorrects at low HR)
- Use Fridericia (QTcF) or Framingham formulas at rates <50 bpm
- If QT is prolonged: consider medication effect, electrolyte abnormality, or congenital long QT as contributing cause

### 3.7 Other Features
- **Cannon A-waves**: Not visible on ECG but clinically important — occur when atria contract against closed AV valves (P-wave coincides with QRS or follows just after QRS)
- **Escape rhythm variability**: Occasional longer R-R intervals (dropped escape beats) signal escape failure — a pre-arrest warning
- **Retrograde P-waves**: In junctional escape, retrograde atrial activation may occasionally capture the atria, producing inverted P-waves in II, III, aVF immediately after the QRS — but in true complete AVB, this is rare because the sinus node rate dominates
- **QRS alternans**: Very rare; if present, consider additional hemodynamic compromise

## 4. Differential Diagnosis

### 4.1 Mimics (What Looks Like This But Isn't)

| Mimic Condition | Shared Features | Distinguishing Features |
|----------------|-----------------|----------------------|
| High-grade 2nd degree AV block | Very slow ventricular rate, many non-conducted P-waves, P-waves marching independently of most QRS complexes | In high-grade AVB, SOME P-waves conduct — look for occasional QRS at a fixed PR interval after a P-wave. If even ONE P-wave conducts, it is NOT complete AVB |
| AV dissociation (non-pathological) | Independent P-waves and QRS, variable PR intervals | In AV dissociation due to enhanced subsidiary pacemaker (e.g., junctional tachycardia), the ventricular rate is EQUAL TO or FASTER than the atrial rate. In complete AVB, the atrial rate is always faster than the ventricular rate |
| Isorhythmic AV dissociation | P-waves and QRS at nearly identical rates, variable PR | Atrial and ventricular rates are nearly equal (within 1-2 bpm); P-waves and QRS drift in and out of phase. In complete AVB, atrial rate clearly exceeds ventricular rate |
| Atrial fibrillation with slow ventricular response | Slow, regular ventricular rate | No discrete P-waves — only fibrillatory baseline. Regular R-R intervals in the setting of AFib = complete AVB (the normal irregularity of AFib is replaced by a regular escape rhythm) |
| Mobitz Type II with high-ratio block (3:1, 4:1) | Slow ventricular rate, many non-conducted P-waves | Fixed conduction ratio (e.g., every 3rd or 4th P-wave conducts at a constant PR interval); in complete AVB, zero P-waves conduct |
| Sinus bradycardia with junctional escape | Slow rate, narrow QRS | In sinus bradycardia, P-waves precede every QRS at a fixed PR interval; there is no dissociation |

### 4.2 Coexisting Conditions
- **Complete AVB + Acute MI**: Most common urgent presentation. Inferior MI → AV nodal block (typically transient, narrow escape, relatively stable). Anterior MI → His-Purkinje block (typically permanent, wide escape, high mortality). The agent must assess for concurrent ST changes in the ischemia territory
- **Complete AVB + Atrial Fibrillation**: No P-waves visible, but the diagnostic clue is a regular ventricular rhythm in AFib — normally AFib produces an irregularly irregular rhythm. A regular slow ventricular rate in the context of fibrillatory baseline = complete AVB with junctional or ventricular escape
- **Complete AVB + Hyperkalemia**: Hyperkalemia can progressively impair conduction, widening the QRS further and potentially causing the escape rhythm to fail. Look for peaked T-waves, widened QRS beyond what the escape focus alone would produce
- **Complete AVB + Pacemaker**: In paced patients, complete AVB may be the underlying rhythm with the pacemaker providing ventricular pacing. Turn off pacemaker to reveal underlying complete AVB (done only in monitored settings)

## 5. STAT Classification

| Priority | Criteria |
|----------|----------|
| **STAT** | YES — Complete AV block is an immediate life-threatening emergency. Ventricular escape rhythms are inherently unstable and may fail at any moment, resulting in asystole and cardiac arrest. Even stable-appearing junctional escape rhythms warrant urgent evaluation. |
| **Time-sensitive** | Minutes. Infra-nodal (wide QRS) escape: immediate risk of asystole — transcutaneous pacing must be available within minutes. Supra-nodal (narrow QRS) escape: more stable but still requires urgent cardiology consultation and continuous monitoring. Any symptomatic complete AVB requires immediate intervention. |
| **Clinical action** | 1) Transcutaneous pacing pads applied immediately. 2) Atropine 0.5mg IV for supra-nodal block (may improve AV nodal conduction); ineffective and potentially harmful in infra-nodal block. 3) Isoproterenol or dopamine drip if atropine fails. 4) Transvenous pacing catheter if sustained. 5) Permanent pacemaker implantation (Class I indication for symptomatic complete AVB). 6) Identify and treat reversible causes (stop offending drugs, treat MI, correct electrolytes). |

### 6.1 Reasoning Domains Required to Detect This Condition
- **Rate analysis**: Calculate atrial rate (P-P intervals) and ventricular rate (R-R intervals) independently
- **Rhythm regularity**: Verify P-P regularity and R-R regularity independently
- **AV relationship analysis**: Determine whether there is a fixed PR relationship — this is the CRITICAL reasoning step. Must measure PR intervals across multiple beats and demonstrate they are variable with no repeating pattern
- **Beat-by-beat P-wave detection**: Must identify ALL P-waves, including those buried in QRS complexes, ST segments, and T-waves. This requires morphology analysis of subtle beat-to-beat waveform variations
- **QRS morphology analysis**: Narrow vs wide QRS determines block level (supra-nodal vs infra-nodal) and risk stratification
- **Cross-domain reasoning**: Required — must correlate rate analysis + rhythm regularity + AV relationship + QRS morphology to reach diagnosis. Also requires ischemia context if MI-related
- **Sequential reasoning**: YES — "IF P-P regular AND R-R regular AND PR variable AND atrial rate > ventricular rate AND no conducted beats THEN complete AVB. THEN IF QRS narrow → junctional escape (supra-nodal block). IF QRS wide → ventricular escape (infra-nodal block, HIGHER DANGER)."

### 6.2 Feature Dependencies
- **ESSENTIAL features**:
  - P-wave detection and timing (all beats) — from fiducial detection (FPT columns 0-2)
  - R-peak detection and timing (all beats) — from fiducial detection (FPT column 5)
  - P-P interval calculation — derived from P-wave timing
  - R-R interval calculation — derived from R-peak timing
  - PR interval measurement per beat — must be computed for every P-QRS pair
  - QRS duration — from fiducial detection (FPT columns 3, 7)
  - Heart rate (atrial and ventricular, computed separately)
- **SUPPORTING features**:
  - QRS morphology classification (LBBB, RBBB, or normal pattern) — helps localize escape focus
  - P-wave axis — confirms sinus origin of atrial rhythm
  - QRS axis — helps identify escape focus location
  - Beat-to-beat ST-T morphology variability — clue to hidden P-waves
  - T-wave morphology stability — beat-to-beat T-wave shape changes suggest superimposed P-waves
- **EXCLUSION features**:
  - If ANY P-wave has a fixed temporal relationship to a subsequent QRS (constant PR for that beat) → NOT complete AVB (may be high-grade 2nd degree)
  - If ventricular rate ≥ atrial rate → NOT complete AVB (may be AV dissociation from enhanced automaticity)
- **Per-beat vs aggregate**:
  - PR interval measurement: PER-BEAT (must show variability)
  - P-P regularity: AGGREGATE (statistical measure across all beats)
  - R-R regularity: AGGREGATE (statistical measure across all beats)
  - P-wave detection: PER-BEAT (must find every P-wave, including hidden ones)

### 6.3 Cross-Condition Interactions
- **Affects other conditions**: Complete AVB obscures PR interval assessment — conditions like WPW, 1st degree AVB, or dual AV node pathways cannot be evaluated during complete AVB
- **Affected by other conditions**: Atrial fibrillation eliminates P-waves, so classic complete AVB criteria (P-P regularity, visible P-waves) do not apply. Instead, complete AVB + AFib is diagnosed by regular R-R intervals in the setting of a fibrillatory baseline
- **Must rule out first**: High-grade 2nd degree AVB (look for any conducted beats), AV dissociation from accelerated junctional/ventricular rhythm (compare rates — in AV dissociation the subsidiary rate equals or exceeds sinus rate), isorhythmic dissociation (rates nearly equal)
- **Condition combinations changing interpretation**: Complete AVB + inferior STEMI → likely transient (AV nodal ischemia); complete AVB + anterior STEMI → likely permanent (His-Purkinje necrosis), much higher mortality, more urgent pacing

### 6.4 Reasoning Chain Sketch
- **Minimum reasoning chain** (fewest steps to high confidence):
  1. Detect slow ventricular rate (<60 bpm)
  2. Identify regular R-R intervals
  3. Identify P-waves occurring at a faster, regular rate
  4. Confirm PR intervals are variable with no fixed relationship
  5. Confirm no conducted beats → Complete AVB
  6. Assess QRS width → narrow (junctional) or wide (ventricular) escape
- **Full reasoning chain** (complete evidence assembly):
  1. Measure all P-P intervals → confirm regular (σ < 10% of mean)
  2. Measure all R-R intervals → confirm regular (σ < 10% of mean)
  3. Calculate atrial rate from P-P and ventricular rate from R-R
  4. Confirm atrial rate > ventricular rate
  5. Measure PR interval for every P-QRS pair → demonstrate no fixed value
  6. Search for any conducted beats (consistent PR for any P-wave) → must find zero
  7. Evaluate for hidden P-waves in QRS/ST/T by analyzing beat-to-beat morphology variation
  8. Classify QRS duration: narrow (≤120ms) vs wide (>120ms)
  9. If wide: classify QRS morphology (LBBB pattern, RBBB pattern, other)
  10. Assess QRS axis of escape rhythm → localize escape focus
  11. Screen for concurrent ischemia (ST changes independent of P-wave distortion)
  12. Screen for concurrent hyperkalemia (peaked T, widened QRS beyond escape pattern)
  13. Assess escape rhythm stability (any pauses? dropped escape beats?)
  14. Risk-stratify: junctional escape (moderate risk) vs ventricular escape (high risk, STAT)

### 6.5 Confidence Anchors
- **HIGH confidence features**:
  - Regular P-P + regular R-R + PR continuously variable + atrial rate > ventricular rate + no conducted beats = definite complete AVB
  - P-waves clearly marching through QRS/ST/T segments
  - Ventricular rate 20-40 with wide QRS = definite infra-nodal complete AVB
- **Features that LOWER confidence**:
  - Only a few beats available (short strip) — may not be able to prove dissociation
  - P-waves not clearly identifiable (low amplitude, superimposed on large QRS)
  - Atrial fibrillation present (no P-waves to assess)
  - Heart rate >60 bpm (less likely complete AVB unless enhanced escape automaticity)
- **Pathognomonic combination**:
  - Completely independent atrial and ventricular rhythms + atrial rate > ventricular rate + zero conducted beats = 100% specific for complete AVB
- **Classification thresholds**:
  - "Possible": Slow ventricular rate + apparent AV dissociation but P-waves not clearly identified in all beats
  - "Probable": Regular P-P + regular R-R + variable PR demonstrated over 5+ beats + atrial rate > ventricular rate
  - "Definite": All criteria met over ≥10 seconds of recording + zero conducted beats confirmed + P-waves identified in all beats including those hidden in QRS/ST/T

### Primary Agent
**RRC** — complete AV block is a rhythm/conduction diagnosis defined by AV dissociation with independent atrial and ventricular rates, which is entirely in the RRC agent's domain.

### Cross-Domain Hints
- IT emits `cross_domain_hint: "Inferior or anterior STEMI detected — complete AV block in this context likely ischemic in etiology; CDS must escalate urgency tier"` when ischemic ST changes are found alongside the complete AVB pattern.
- MR emits `cross_domain_hint: "Wide escape QRS (≥120 ms) detected — block is infra-His; higher pacing urgency; forward morphology findings to CDS for level-of-block determination"` when escape beats are wide.

### CDS Specific Role
CDS performs the critical ischemic-etiology determination: it cross-references RRC's complete AVB diagnosis with IT's territorial ST analysis. If IT confirms inferior STEMI (RCA occlusion causing AV nodal artery ischemia) or anterior STEMI (septal ischemia causing His-Purkinje damage), CDS generates an ischemic complete AVB diagnosis with highest urgency tier (simultaneous reperfusion + pacing decision). CDS also integrates MR's escape QRS morphology to classify block level: narrow escape = AV nodal block (often reversible with atropine); wide escape = infra-His block (requires transcutaneous then transvenous pacing).

## 9. Edge Cases and Pitfalls

- **Complete AVB with atrial fibrillation**: No P-waves visible, so classic criteria cannot be applied. The clue is a REGULAR ventricular rhythm in the setting of AFib (which is normally irregularly irregular). Regular R-R + fibrillatory baseline + slow rate = complete AVB + AFib. The agent must not dismiss this as "just slow AFib"
- **Intermittent complete AVB**: Complete block may come and go (paroxysmal). A recording may show normal conduction transitioning to complete AVB and back. The agent must scan the entire recording for episodes of AV dissociation
- **Complete AVB in paced patients**: Underlying rhythm is complete AVB but pacemaker provides ventricular beats. Pacing spikes precede QRS complexes. If pacemaker is malfunctioning, underlying slow escape rhythm may be seen between paced beats
- **Very slow sinus rate mimicking near-equal rates**: If sinus rate is 50 and junctional escape is 45, rates are similar, and the distinction between complete AVB and isorhythmic dissociation becomes difficult. Long recording and careful analysis of P-QRS relationships over many beats is required
- **Hidden P-waves**: In patients with high-amplitude QRS complexes (ventricular escape), low-amplitude P-waves may be completely buried. The agent should look for beat-to-beat subtle morphology changes in QRS/ST/T as indirect evidence. May need to filter or increase gain
- **Congenital complete AVB**: Young patients (even infants) with complete AVB from birth. Junctional escape rate is often higher (50-70 bpm) and more reliable than acquired complete AVB. QRS is typically narrow. May be well-tolerated for years but still requires monitoring
- **Drug-induced complete AVB**: Beta-blockers, calcium channel blockers (verapamil, diltiazem), digoxin, amiodarone can all cause complete AVB. Usually reversible after drug withdrawal. The agent should flag medication context if available
- **Post-surgical complete AVB**: Following aortic valve replacement, septal myectomy, or catheter ablation near the AV node/His bundle. Often permanent if persisting >48-72 hours post-procedure
- **Artifact mimicking AV dissociation**: Movement artifact can create pseudo-P-waves at regular intervals. The agent must verify that identified P-waves have consistent morphology and physiologically plausible rates (40-120 bpm, not 200+ bpm)
- **Extreme bradycardia with escape failure**: Ventricular escape rate <20 bpm or intermittent asystolic pauses >3 seconds represent impending cardiac arrest. The agent should flag any R-R interval >3 seconds as an emergency finding independent of the complete AVB diagnosis
