## 1. Pathophysiology Root Cause

### 1.1 Underlying Mechanism
Regular intense aerobic and/or resistance training produces structural and autonomic adaptations in the heart. The autonomic component — chronically elevated vagal tone — drives most rhythm abnormalities (bradycardia, AV conduction slowing, junctional escapes). The structural component — eccentric (volume load) or concentric (pressure load) hypertrophy depending on sport type — drives voltage changes and repolarization shifts. These are physiological, reversible adaptations, not pathological remodeling.

### 1.2 Electrophysiological Consequence
High vagal tone: slows sinus node automaticity (bradycardia), prolongs AV nodal conduction (1° AVB, Wenckebach), allows junctional escape rhythms when the sinus rate falls below junctional rate. These all normalize or attenuate with exercise — a critical distinguishing feature from pathological AV block.
Structural hypertrophy: increased myocardial mass → larger electrical vectors → higher amplitude QRS in voltage leads. Dilation of the left ventricle shifts the cardiac axis. Altered repolarization from hypertrophy can produce ST elevation (early repolarization) or T-wave changes.

### 1.3 Ethnic Variation
African-Caribbean athletes show T-wave inversions in V1–V3 (and sometimes V4) at a significantly higher prevalence than White athletes (13% vs <1%). This represents a distinct repolarization pattern — not equivalent to arrhythmogenic right ventricular cardiomyopathy (ARVC), which it superficially resembles. The International Criteria (2017) explicitly classify this as a normal variant in African-Caribbean athletes. Misclassification as ARVC leads to unnecessary disqualification from sport.

## 2. ECG Presentation — Lead by Lead

### 2.1 Diagnostic Criteria

Normal training-related (ACCEPTABLE) ECG findings per International Criteria (2017):

| Finding | Threshold | Mechanism |
|---|---|---|
| Sinus bradycardia | ≥30 bpm (if asymptomatic) | Elevated vagal tone |
| 1° AV block | PR >200 ms | Increased AV nodal conduction time |
| Incomplete RBBB | QRS <120 ms, rsR' in V1 | RV volume load |
| Early repolarization | J-point elevation ≥1 mm, V4–V6 | Repolarization variant |
| LVH voltage (isolated) | Sokolow ≥35 mm or Cornell ≥28 mm | Structural hypertrophy |
| Mobitz I (Wenckebach) | At rest; resolves with exercise | Vagal tone |
| Junctional escape rhythm | At rest | Vagal tone; sinus slowing |
| T inversion V1–V2 | Any athlete | Normal repolarization variant |
| T inversion V1–V3 | African-Caribbean athletes | Ethnic repolarization variant |
| T inversion V1–V4 | African-Caribbean athletes <16 years | Juvenile pattern persisting |

Abnormal (UNACCEPTABLE) ECG findings in athletes — require further evaluation:

| Finding | Concern |
|---|---|
| T inversion V2–V4 (non-African-Caribbean) | ARVC, HCM, cardiomyopathy |
| T inversion inferior or lateral leads | Cardiomyopathy, ischemia |
| ST depression ≥0.5 mm in ≥2 leads | Ischemia, cardiomyopathy |
| Pathological Q waves | HCM, prior MI |
| LBBB | Structural disease |
| QTc ≥500 ms | LQTS |
| Brugada type 1 pattern | Brugada syndrome |
| Ventricular pre-excitation (delta waves) | WPW |
| 2° Mobitz II or 3° AV block | Structural conduction disease |
| Frequent PVCs | Cardiomyopathy |
| Non-sustained VT | Structural disease |

### 2.2 Lead-by-Lead Manifestation

| Lead | Normal Athlete Finding | Abnormal Finding (Requires Workup) |
|---|---|---|
| I | Normal or high-amplitude QRS | Negative P/QRS (suspect dextrocardia or lead reversal) |
| II | Normal | Pathological Q (>40 ms or >25% R) |
| III | Minor T inversion (positional, normal) | Persistent deep T inversion |
| aVR | Normal | |
| aVL | RaVL may be elevated (voltage) | RaVL >11 mm without detraining regression |
| aVF | Normal | Pathological Q wave |
| V1 | T inversion normal; incomplete RBBB common | Complete RBBB; Brugada type 1 pattern |
| V2 | T inversion normal (all); biphasic acceptable | T inversion V2 alone in non-African-Caribbean + symptoms |
| V3 | T inversion: normal in African-Caribbean | T inversion in White/Asian athlete = abnormal if V2–V4 |
| V4 | T inversion: normal in African-Caribbean <16 yr | T inversion V4 non-African-Caribbean = abnormal |
| V5 | Early repolarization J-point elevation common | Deep T inversion = abnormal |
| V6 | Early repolarization; high R amplitude | Deep T inversion = abnormal |

### 2.3 Key Leads
- **V1–V3:** Most important for T-wave inversion pattern — ethnicity-dependent interpretation
- **aVL:** Key for LVH voltage screening (R ≥11 mm criterion)
- **V5–V6:** Early repolarization and LVH voltage

### 2.4 Beat-by-Beat Considerations
In bradycardic athletes, QT interval appears prolonged because QT lengthens with slower rates. Rate-correct to QTc before flagging. At 40 bpm, QT of 600 ms → QTc ~490 ms (borderline). At 60 bpm, same QTc of 490 ms is clearly abnormal. Never report raw QT interval without QTc in bradycardic athletes.

## 3. Morphology Details

### 3.1 Voltage
LVH voltage criteria (Sokolow-Lyon, Cornell) may be positive in up to 50% of elite athletes as an isolated finding. Isolated voltage increase without repolarization abnormality, axis deviation, or ST-T changes = acceptable athlete finding. All positive voltage criteria should prompt checking for secondary repolarization changes before escalating.

### 3.2 Early Repolarization
J-point elevation in V4–V6 (and often V5 > V6 > V4) is present in up to 40–80% of athletes. Morphology matters: concave-up ("smiley face") ST elevation is benign. Horizontal or downsloping ST is not early repolarization — evaluate for ischemia or cardiomyopathy. Notching or slurring at the J-point is characteristic of benign early repolarization.

### 3.3 T-Wave Inversion — Ethnic Detail
African-Caribbean athletes: T inversion extending from V1 to V3 (or V4) with ST elevation preceding the inversion is classified as "early repolarization pattern with T-wave inversion" — a distinct, normal, and well-described variant. The ST segment is often elevated before the inversion, unlike the ST depression before inversion seen in ARVC or cardiomyopathy.
Non-African-Caribbean athletes: T inversion beyond V2 requires evaluation.

### 3.4 AV Conduction
Wenckebach (Mobitz I) is the only form of 2° AV block acceptable in athletes. It occurs at rest, resolves with exercise or atropine. Mobitz II (fixed PR then dropped beat) is always pathological. 3° AV block is always pathological.

### 3.5 QRS Duration
Incomplete RBBB (QRS 100–119 ms, rsR' in V1) is common in athletes (RV volume load). Complete RBBB (QRS ≥120 ms) is generally not a training adaptation — evaluate underlying cause.

### 3.6 Detraining Response (Diagnostic Test)
Training-related voltage increase, early repolarization, and mild bradycardia regress with 3 months of detraining. HCM-related hypertrophy does NOT regress. This is the gold-standard distinguishing test when HCM vs athlete's heart is uncertain on imaging.

### 3.7 Brugada Pattern
Type 1 Brugada (coved ST elevation ≥2 mm in V1–V2 with negative T) is NEVER a normal athlete variant. Must be evaluated regardless of athletic status.

## 4. Differential Diagnosis

### 4.1 Mimics

| Condition | ECG Overlap | Key Differentiator |
|---|---|---|
| HCM | LVH voltage, T inversions | Asymmetric septal hypertrophy on echo; small LV cavity; no detraining regression; pathological Q waves |
| ARVC | T inversions V1–V3 (or beyond), epsilon waves | Epsilon wave; RVEDV on CMR; family history; fibro-fatty replacement |
| Dilated cardiomyopathy | LVH voltage, poor R progression | Dilated, hypokinetic LV on echo; EF reduced |
| LQTS | QTc prolongation | QTc ≥500 ms not athlete; family history; prior syncope |
| WPW | Short PR, delta waves | Never normal; pre-excitation regardless of athlete status |
| Brugada syndrome | RBBB pattern in V1 | Type 1 coved pattern never athlete variant |

### 4.2 Coexisting Conditions
Athletes can have pathological conditions concurrently. An athlete's ECG showing LVH voltage AND T inversions V2–V5 cannot be dismissed as "athlete variant." Each abnormal finding must be evaluated on its own merits. The Seattle/International Criteria provide a systematic framework specifically to avoid over-calling athlete variants while not missing true pathology.

## 5. STAT Classification

| Presentation | Classification | Rationale |
|---|---|---|
| Acceptable athlete variants only | **Routine / No action** | Normal physiological adaptation |
| Unacceptable finding (T inversion V2–V4, pathological Q, LBBB) | **Urgent — further evaluation** | Cardiomyopathy screening required |
| QTc ≥500 ms | **STAT** | Malignant arrhythmia risk |
| Brugada type 1 pattern | **STAT** | Sudden cardiac death risk |
| 3° AV block | **STAT** | Complete heart block — structural |
| VT or VF on monitor | **STAT** | Immediate intervention |

## 6. Reasoning Complexity Analysis

### 6.1 Pattern Recognition Challenge
Requires storing two sets of normal ranges: general population vs. athlete, and within athlete, White/Asian vs. African-Caribbean. A finding that is STAT in a general patient (T inversion V1–V3) is normal in an African-Caribbean athlete. Demographic context is mandatory for correct classification.

### 6.2 Criteria System Selection
Three criteria systems in common use: Seattle Criteria (2013), Refined Seattle Criteria (2014), International Criteria (2017). The International Criteria are current standard. The system must know which criteria to apply and flag when criteria versions differ in their classification.

### 6.3 Temporal/Situational Reasoning
Wenckebach and junctional rhythms are context-dependent (at rest vs. exercise). The ECG is a resting snapshot — the system must annotate that exercise-provoked behavior cannot be assessed from a resting ECG.

### 6.4 Detraining as Diagnostic Tool
The detraining test is a 3-month clinical process. The system can recommend it but cannot perform it. It must clearly recommend "serial ECGs after 3 months detraining if LVH voltage + any borderline repolarization finding."

### 6.5 Integration with Non-ECG Data
Echo (LV cavity size, septal thickness, diastolic function) and CMR are required for HCM vs. athlete's heart distinction when ECG is borderline. The system must prompt these studies.

### 6.6 Difficulty Score

| Dimension | Score (1–5) | Rationale |
|---|---|---|
| Pattern recognition | 3 | Many variants, but each has clear morphological features |
| Demographic weighting | 5 | Ethnicity is mandatory for T-wave interpretation |
| Criteria system complexity | 4 | Multiple overlapping criteria systems; International 2017 is current |
| Differential complexity | 4 | HCM and ARVC are serious differentials with overlapping features |
| Temporal/contextual reasoning | 3 | Resting vs. exercise; detraining test |
| Data integration | 3 | Echo/CMR prompt required for borderline cases |
| Arrhythmia vigilance | 2 | Most athlete findings are benign; exceptions are flagged by criteria |
| **Overall** | **4.0** | **High complexity due to ethnicity-dependent interpretation** |

## 6A. Agent Assignment

**Architecture:** Option E — 3 Parallel Phase 1 Specialists + 1 Sequential CDS (Node 2.1 Gate Passed 2026-03-27)

| Agent | Role for Athlete's Heart | Key Features Used |
|-------|--------------------------|-------------------|
| **RRC** (Rhythm/Rate/Conduction) | Supporting | Sinus bradycardia (<60bpm, normal athletic adaptation), first-degree AV block (PR >200ms, normal in athletes), junctional escape rhythm, early repolarization pattern; all normal athletic adaptations requiring context |
| **IT** (Ischemia/Territory) | Supporting | ST elevation from early repolarization pattern in inferior/lateral leads — must not classify as STEMI; flags for CDS cross-domain resolution |
| **MR** (Morphology/Repolarization) | Primary | High voltage (Sokolow-Lyon >35mm, Cornell criteria — voltage criteria without LVH pathology), early repolarization J-point elevation in inferior/lateral leads (concave morphology), T-wave inversions in V1-V4 (may be normal in some athletes, particularly Black athletes, but requires assessment), incomplete RBBB (RSR' pattern in V1 — common athlete finding) |
| **CDS** (Cross-Domain Synthesis) | Required — CRITICAL: distinguishes normal athlete ECG findings from pathological red flags (ARVC, HCM) | Integrates sinus bradycardia + high voltage + early repolarization (all normal) vs deep T-wave inversions extending beyond V3 + pathological Q-waves + QTc prolongation (ARVC/HCM red flags); applies International Criteria for ECG Interpretation in Athletes |

### Primary Agent
**MR** — Athlete's heart is primarily characterized by repolarization and voltage morphology features (high voltage, early repolarization pattern, T-wave inversion distribution) within MR's domain.

### Cross-Domain Hints
- MR → IT: `cross_domain_hint: "ST elevation with J-point notching in inferior/lateral leads — IT must not classify as STEMI; athlete early repolarization pattern; CDS to confirm with rate context (bradycardia = athlete)"`
- MR → CDS: `cross_domain_hint: "T-wave inversions detected beyond V1-V2 [specify leads] — CDS must determine if within expected athlete range (V1-V3/V4 in some populations) vs ARVC/HCM red flag (extending to V5/lateral or deep >2mm)"`
- RRC → CDS: `cross_domain_hint: "Sinus bradycardia with first-degree AV block — if MR shows high voltage + early repolarization without pathological T-wave inversions, combined picture = normal athlete ECG; CDS to confirm no red flags"`

### CDS Specific Role
CDS performs critical pathological red flag screening for athlete's heart, applying the International Criteria for ECG Interpretation in Athletes (Seattle Criteria / International Criteria 2017). CDS integrates normal athletic adaptations (sinus bradycardia, first-degree AVB, early repolarization, high voltage, incomplete RBBB — all identified by RRC and MR) and applies the rule: these findings alone = normal athlete ECG. Red flags requiring further evaluation include T-wave inversions extending beyond V3 (ARVC pattern), T-wave inversions in inferior leads, ST depression, pathological Q-waves, left bundle branch block, QTc >500ms, epsilon waves (ARVC), or delta waves (WPW). CDS generates either a "normal athlete ECG findings" conclusion or an "athlete ECG with red flags — further evaluation required" output identifying the specific concerning feature.

## 9. Edge Cases and Pitfalls

- **Never dismiss T inversions in V2–V4 in a non-African-Caribbean athlete as "athletic variant."** This is a common and dangerous error.
- **African-Caribbean athletes with T inversions extending to V5–V6** are NOT covered by the normal variant designation — these lateral inversions require evaluation regardless of ethnicity.
- **Junctional rhythm at rest** is common in bradycardic athletes. If it accelerates appropriately with exercise, it is benign. Persistent junctional rhythm without rate response to exercise requires evaluation.
- **Athletes can have coronary artery disease.** Age-appropriate athletes (masters athletes >35 years) warrant different considerations than young athletes.
- **WPW is never a normal variant.** Delta waves cannot be attributed to athletic adaptation.
- **Epsilon waves** (small deflections after QRS in V1–V3) are never normal variants — they are specific for ARVC.
- **Prolonged QRS ≥120 ms (complete RBBB or LBBB)** is not an athletic adaptation — evaluate.
- **Type 1 Brugada pattern** provoked by fever in an athlete with previously "normal" athlete ECG is significant — Brugada syndrome can be unmasked by fever.
