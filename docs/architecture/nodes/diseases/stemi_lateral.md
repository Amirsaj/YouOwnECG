## 1. Pathophysiology Root Cause

### 1.1 What Goes Wrong (Anatomy/Physiology)
- **Culprit vessel**: Left circumflex artery (LCx) via obtuse marginal branches, or LAD diagonal branch (D1/D2)
- LCx supplies the lateral wall of the left ventricle — specifically the posterolateral and anterolateral segments
- Diagonal branches of the LAD supply the high anterolateral wall; occlusion produces the "high lateral STEMI" pattern confined to leads I and aVL
- Acute thrombotic occlusion causes transmural ischemia progressing to infarction of the lateral myocardial wall
- At cellular level: ATP depletion within minutes of total occlusion, loss of membrane potential, potassium efflux from ischemic cells, intracellular calcium overload, and ultimately irreversible myocyte death
- LCx territory has the LEAST direct ECG lead coverage of any major coronary territory — only leads I, aVL, V5, V6 face the lateral wall directly, and even these are not optimally oriented for all lateral segments

### 1.2 Electrical Consequence
- Transmural ischemia creates an "injury current" — ischemic myocytes have a more negative resting membrane potential and shortened action potentials
- During the ST segment (phase 2 plateau), injured cells are already partially repolarized while surrounding healthy tissue remains depolarized, creating a voltage gradient (current of injury)
- The injury vector points TOWARD the lateral wall (away from the endocardium, through the epicardium)
- In the "high lateral" pattern (diagonal occlusion), the injury vector is directed leftward and superiorly — projecting maximally onto leads I and aVL
- In the "low lateral" pattern (distal LCx/OM), the injury vector points leftward and slightly inferiorly — projecting onto V5 and V6
- In extensive lateral STEMI (proximal LCx), the vector is broadly leftward, affecting I, aVL, V5, and V6
- Reciprocal ST depression occurs in leads oriented OPPOSITE to the injury vector — primarily the inferior leads (II, III, aVF) which face 180 degrees away from the lateral wall

### 1.3 Why It Appears on ECG
- Lead I (0 degrees) and aVL (-30 degrees): oriented leftward and superiorly — directly face the high lateral wall. A laterally-directed injury vector projects positively onto these leads → ST elevation
- V5 (left anterior axillary line, 5th intercostal) and V6 (left midaxillary line, 5th intercostal): sit over the low lateral wall — a laterally-directed injury vector projects positively → ST elevation
- Leads II (+60), III (+120), aVF (+90): face the inferior wall, opposite to the lateral injury vector → reciprocal ST depression (the mirror image)
- aVR (+210/−150): highly variable — may show ST elevation as a reciprocal-of-reciprocal, or remain relatively unchanged
- Precordial leads V1-V4: generally uninvolved unless there is extension into the anterior wall (wraparound LAD) or posterior wall (proximal LCx)
- **Critical clinical point**: The LCx territory is the most commonly missed STEMI on 12-lead ECG because (1) the lateral wall has relatively few dedicated leads, (2) high lateral STEMI in I/aVL only produces small-amplitude ST changes that are easily overlooked, and (3) LCx dominant circulation supplying the posterior wall may show changes only as reciprocal ST depression in V1-V3

## 2. ECG Presentation — Lead by Lead

### 2.1 Diagnostic Criteria (2025 AHA/ESC Guidelines)

| Criterion | Threshold | Source |
|-----------|-----------|--------|
| ST elevation in lateral leads | >=1 mm in >=2 contiguous lateral leads (I, aVL, V5, V6) | 2023 ESC STEMI Guidelines / 2025 AHA Update |
| Contiguous lead definition — lateral group | I + aVL (high lateral) OR V5 + V6 (low lateral) | Universal Definition of MI (4th) |
| Contiguous lead definition — extended lateral | I, aVL, V5, V6 as a contiguous group (any 2) | AHA/ESC Joint Statement |
| Gender-specific threshold (V5-V6) | >=1 mm for both men and women in limb leads; V5-V6 >=1 mm | ESC 2023 |
| New or presumed new LBBB | Consider STEMI equivalent — apply Sgarbossa criteria | AHA/ACC 2013, reaffirmed 2025 |
| Reciprocal changes | ST depression in II, III, aVF increases specificity for true STEMI | Expert consensus |
| Isolated aVL elevation | >=1 mm ST elevation in aVL with reciprocal ST depression in III ± aVF | European Heart Journal 2019 |

### 2.3 Key Leads (Most Diagnostic)
- **aVL**: Single most sensitive lead for high lateral / diagonal STEMI. Isolated aVL elevation with reciprocal ST depression in III is a recognized pattern for D1 occlusion and is one of the most frequently missed STEMI presentations
- **Lead I**: Second key lead for high lateral pattern — changes in I + aVL together significantly increase diagnostic confidence
- **V5, V6**: Key leads for low lateral (OM branch) STEMI
- **Lead III**: Most important reciprocal lead — prominent ST depression in III with elevation in aVL is highly specific for lateral STEMI
- **Earliest changes**: Hyperacute T-waves in aVL and/or V5-V6 may precede measurable ST elevation by minutes to hours

### 2.4 Beat-by-Beat Considerations
- ST elevation in lateral STEMI is generally constant across beats (not intermittent)
- In the hyperacute phase (first 30-60 minutes), ST elevation may fluctuate as the thrombus undergoes cycles of partial occlusion and re-occlusion
- Transient normalization ("pseudonormalization") may occur with spontaneous reperfusion — this does NOT indicate resolution of the emergency
- Arrhythmias: lateral wall STEMI may cause atrial arrhythmias (atrial fibrillation, atrial flutter) due to LCx supply of the left atrial branch; these may cause beat-to-beat variability in ST segment appearance
- In the setting of AF with lateral STEMI, measure ST changes on beats with similar R-R intervals

## 3. Morphology Details (What the Agent Must See)

### 3.1 P-wave Changes
- Morphology: Usually normal in early lateral STEMI
- If LCx supplies the left atrial branch (common), left atrial ischemia may cause P-wave notching (P-mitrale pattern) or atrial fibrillation
- Duration change: May prolong (>120 ms) with left atrial ischemia
- Axis change: Normal unless atrial ischemia shifts the P-wave axis

### 3.2 PR Interval Changes
- Duration: Usually normal — AV node is supplied by the RCA (85%) or LCx (15%)
- In left-dominant circulation where LCx supplies the AV node, lateral STEMI may cause PR prolongation or AV block
- Pattern: Constant PR interval unless AV nodal ischemia occurs

### 3.3 QRS Complex Changes
- Duration: Narrow in early STEMI; may widen if extensive lateral infarction involves the left bundle fascicles
- Morphology: Q-waves develop in I, aVL, V5-V6 as infarction becomes transmural (typically >6-12 hours)
  - Pathological Q-wave: >=30 ms duration or >=25% of R-wave amplitude in I, aVL
  - Q-waves in V5-V6: >=30 ms and >=1 mm deep
- Amplitude: May show loss of R-wave amplitude in V5-V6 (R-wave regression) as lateral wall becomes electrically silent
- Axis: Left axis deviation may develop if the left anterior fascicle is involved (proximal LCx territory)
- QRS fragmentation (fQRS) in lateral leads indicates heterogeneous conduction through ischemic tissue

### 3.4 ST Segment Changes
- Direction: **Elevation** in I, aVL (high lateral) and/or V5-V6 (low lateral); **depression** (reciprocal) in II, III, aVF
- Morphology progression:
  - **Hyperacute phase**: Straightening of ST segment, loss of normal concavity, subtle elevation with hyperacute T-waves
  - **Acute phase**: Convex ("tombstone") ST elevation in severe transmural injury; concave upward elevation in less severe or early presentations
  - **Subacute phase**: ST elevation begins to resolve, T-wave inversion develops
- Measurement point: J-point; confirm at J+60ms that the elevation persists
- Gender-specific: In lateral leads (I, aVL, V5, V6), the >=1 mm threshold applies to both men and women
- **Subtle STEMI warning**: In high lateral STEMI, ST elevation in aVL may be only 1-1.5 mm — this small-amplitude change is diagnostically critical. Do NOT dismiss it.

### 3.5 T-wave Changes
- **Hyperacute T-waves**: The earliest sign — tall, peaked, broad-based T-waves in I, aVL, V5-V6 that are disproportionately large relative to the QRS
- Direction: Initially hyperacute (tall, upright), then gradually invert as the STEMI evolves
- Amplitude: Hyperacute T-waves often exceed 50% of QRS amplitude in the same lead
- Symmetry: Hyperacute T-waves are broad-based and asymmetric; later, inverted T-waves become deep and symmetric (indicating completed infarction)
- Terminal T-wave inversion in I, aVL, V5-V6 in subacute phase
- Reciprocal T-wave changes: Tall, upright T-waves in III, aVF (mirror image of lateral T-wave inversion)

### 3.6 QT/QTc Changes
- QT interval may prolong acutely during STEMI (injured myocardium has altered repolarization)
- QTc >500 ms during acute STEMI increases arrhythmia risk (VT/VF)
- Heterogeneous repolarization (different QT intervals across leads) — a marker of transmural ischemia and arrhythmic substrate
- Not primary diagnostic criterion but supports the diagnosis

### 3.7 Other Features
- **Reciprocal changes are essential**: ST depression in II, III, aVF significantly increases specificity for lateral STEMI vs mimics (pericarditis and early repolarization do NOT produce reciprocal changes)
- **Loss of R-wave amplitude**: Progressive decrease in R-wave height in V5-V6 during evolving STEMI
- **New S-waves**: Development of S-waves in I, aVL may indicate loss of lateral forces
- **J-point notching**: May be present but is not specific; distinguish from early repolarization pattern

## 4. Differential Diagnosis

### 4.1 Mimics (What Looks Like This But Isn't)

| Mimic Condition | Shared Features | Distinguishing Features |
|----------------|-----------------|----------------------|
| LVH with strain pattern | ST elevation in I, aVL; ST depression in V5-V6 (strain) can overlap | LVH has voltage criteria (Sokolow-Lyon, Cornell), strain pattern is asymmetric ST depression/T inversion with slow downstroke in V5-V6 (NOT elevation). In lateral STEMI the ST elevation is in I/aVL, not in the same leads as LVH strain. LVH strain is chronic and stable. |
| Acute pericarditis | Diffuse ST elevation may include lateral leads | Pericarditis shows diffuse ST elevation in MULTIPLE territories (not confined to lateral), PR depression in II/aVF, PR elevation in aVR, NO reciprocal ST depression (key distinguisher), Spodick's sign (downsloping TP segment) |
| Early repolarization (benign) | ST elevation in V5-V6, occasionally I/aVL | Early repolarization shows J-point notching/slurring, concave-up ST morphology, concordant T-waves, NO reciprocal changes, stable over serial ECGs, common in young men |
| Takotsubo cardiomyopathy | ST elevation in lateral leads possible | Takotsubo typically affects anterior + lateral leads (apical ballooning pattern), extends beyond a single coronary territory, QTc markedly prolonged, and troponin elevation is modest relative to ECG changes. Coronary angiography is normal. |
| Normal variant — high takeoff in aVL | Slight ST elevation in aVL | Amplitude <1 mm, no reciprocal changes in III/aVF, stable on serial ECGs, asymptomatic patient |
| LBBB with secondary ST changes | ST changes in lateral leads | LBBB has wide QRS >=120 ms with typical morphology (broad R in I, aVL, V5-V6). ST changes are expected (discordant to QRS). Apply Sgarbossa criteria if STEMI suspected. |

### 4.2 Coexisting Conditions
- **LVH + lateral STEMI**: LVH voltage criteria may already show ST/T changes in lateral leads (strain pattern). Acute STEMI superimposed on LVH is diagnosed by: dynamic changes on serial ECGs, concordant ST elevation (same direction as QRS — abnormal in LVH), and clinical context. Ratio of ST elevation to S-wave depth >25% suggests STEMI over strain.
- **LBBB + lateral STEMI**: LBBB inherently produces discordant ST changes in V5-V6. Use Smith-modified Sgarbossa criteria: concordant ST elevation >=1 mm or discordant ST elevation with ST/S ratio >25%.
- **Prior lateral MI + acute re-infarction**: Existing Q-waves in I, aVL from old MI. Acute re-infarction may show ST re-elevation in leads with established Q-waves. New or recurrent ST elevation over Q-waves is diagnostic.
- **Atrial fibrillation + lateral STEMI**: LCx occlusion may cause AF due to left atrial ischemia. AF makes ST assessment harder (varying baselines). Measure ST changes on beats following similar R-R intervals.

## 5. STAT Classification

| Priority | Criteria |
|----------|----------|
| **STAT** | YES — Lateral STEMI is an acute coronary occlusion requiring emergent reperfusion. Minutes of delay = myocardial death. "Time is muscle." |
| **Time-sensitive** | Door-to-balloon <90 min (PCI-capable center) or door-to-needle <30 min (fibrinolysis). Every 30 min delay increases 1-year mortality by 7.5%. |
| **Clinical action** | Activate cardiac catheterization lab immediately. Dual antiplatelet therapy (aspirin + P2Y12 inhibitor), heparin, emergent PCI to LCx or diagonal branch. If PCI not available within 120 min, administer fibrinolytic therapy. |

### 6.1 Reasoning Domains Required to Detect This Condition
- **ST segment measurement**: Precise J-point and J+60ms measurement in lateral leads (I, aVL, V5, V6) and inferior leads (II, III, aVF) for reciprocal changes
- **Lead-group correlation**: Must identify that ST elevation is CONFINED to the lateral lead group (I, aVL +/- V5, V6) to localize the territory
- **Reciprocal change recognition**: ST depression in inferior leads (especially III) must be identified as reciprocal — this is a critical confirmatory step
- **Morphology assessment**: Convex vs concave ST morphology, hyperacute T-wave detection, Q-wave development
- **Voltage criteria exclusion**: Must rule out LVH (which produces ST changes in overlapping leads) before diagnosing lateral STEMI
- **Temporal pattern analysis**: Serial ECG comparison to detect dynamic changes (hyperacute → acute → subacute evolution)
- **Cross-domain reasoning required**: YES — must integrate ischemia analysis (ST/T changes) with structural knowledge (LVH exclusion) and morphology assessment (T-wave characteristics)
- **Sequential reasoning required**: YES — "IF ST elevation in I/aVL, THEN check for reciprocal depression in III/aVF, THEN exclude LVH strain, THEN assess T-wave morphology for hyperacute features"

### 6.2 Feature Dependencies
- **ESSENTIAL features**:
  - ST segment amplitude at J-point in I, aVL, V5, V6 (must be >=1 mm in >=2 contiguous leads)
  - ST segment amplitude at J-point in II, III, aVF (reciprocal depression)
  - QRS morphology in I, aVL, V5, V6 (to exclude LBBB, delta waves)
  - T-wave amplitude and morphology in lateral leads (hyperacute T detection)
- **SUPPORTING features**:
  - QRS voltage in lateral and precordial leads (LVH screening — Sokolow-Lyon, Cornell)
  - Q-wave presence/duration in I, aVL, V5, V6 (infarction evolution)
  - QRS axis (left axis shift may indicate lateral wall involvement)
  - PR interval (AV block in left-dominant circulation)
  - ST morphology (concavity/convexity — helps distinguish from pericarditis, early repolarization)
- **EXCLUDING features**:
  - Wide QRS >=120 ms with LBBB morphology → shifts to Sgarbossa criteria pathway
  - Diffuse ST elevation in all territories → consider pericarditis, NOT single-territory STEMI
  - PR depression in II + PR elevation in aVR → pericarditis pattern
- **Per-beat vs aggregate**: ST measurement should be aggregate (median across multiple beats), but if rhythm is irregular (AF), per-beat analysis with R-R interval matching is needed

### 6.3 Cross-Condition Interactions
- **LVH masks lateral STEMI**: LVH strain in V5-V6 produces ST depression that may partially cancel out or obscure acute ST elevation. Agent must apply voltage criteria first, then assess whether ST changes exceed expected strain.
- **LBBB masks lateral STEMI**: LBBB produces expected ST discordance in lateral leads. Sgarbossa/Smith-modified criteria are the pathway.
- **Pericarditis mimics lateral STEMI**: If only looking at lateral leads, pericarditis ST elevation can appear identical. The absence of reciprocal changes and presence of diffuse elevation across territories distinguishes pericarditis.
- **Lateral STEMI + inferior STEMI**: Proximal LCx occlusion can produce both lateral and inferior changes (inferolateral STEMI). The agent must recognize this as a combined territory, not two separate events.
- **Lateral STEMI + posterior STEMI**: LCx wraps to supply the posterior wall. ST depression in V1-V3 (posterior reciprocal) + ST elevation in lateral leads = lateral + posterior STEMI (LCx culprit).

### 6.4 Reasoning Chain Sketch
**Minimum reasoning chain** (fewest steps to high confidence):
1. Detect ST elevation >=1 mm in >=2 of {I, aVL, V5, V6}
2. Confirm reciprocal ST depression in >=1 of {II, III, aVF}
3. Exclude LBBB (QRS <120 ms or no LBBB morphology)
4. → **Lateral STEMI — STAT alert**

**Full reasoning chain** (complete evidence assembly):
1. Measure ST amplitude at J-point in all 12 leads
2. Identify ST elevation >=1 mm in lateral lead group (I, aVL, V5, V6)
3. Determine sub-pattern: high lateral (I, aVL only) vs low lateral (V5, V6) vs extensive (all four)
4. Assess reciprocal ST depression in inferior leads (II, III, aVF) — severity of III depression correlates with aVL elevation
5. Check for posterior extension: ST depression in V1-V3 with tall R waves → posterior involvement
6. Assess T-wave morphology: hyperacute (tall, broad) → very early STEMI; inverted → evolving/subacute
7. Screen for LVH voltage criteria — if positive, evaluate whether ST changes exceed expected strain pattern
8. Check QRS for LBBB — if present, apply Sgarbossa criteria
9. Evaluate PR segment for pericarditis pattern (PR depression II, PR elevation aVR)
10. Check for diffuse vs localized ST changes — diffuse suggests pericarditis
11. Assess ST morphology: convex (tombstone) = high confidence STEMI; concave = possible but consider alternatives
12. Document Q-wave status: absent (early/acute) vs present (evolving/completed)
13. → Final classification with confidence score and territory mapping

### 6.5 Confidence Anchors
- **HIGH confidence features**:
  - Convex ("tombstone") ST elevation in I + aVL >=2 mm with deep reciprocal ST depression in III
  - Hyperacute T-waves in aVL with reciprocal T-wave changes in inferior leads
  - Dynamic progression on serial ECG (increasing ST elevation over minutes-hours)
  - New Q-waves developing in I, aVL with associated ST elevation
- **LOWER confidence if absent**:
  - No reciprocal changes in inferior leads → consider pericarditis, early repolarization, LVH
  - ST elevation only 1 mm with concave morphology → could be normal variant or early repolarization
  - Stable ST changes on serial ECGs → less likely acute STEMI
- **Pathognomonic combination**:
  - ST elevation >=1 mm in I + aVL + reciprocal ST depression in III + aVF + hyperacute T-waves in aVL + acute chest pain = near-100% specific for lateral STEMI (clinical context assumed available)
  - ECG-only pathognomonic: Convex ST elevation in I + aVL with deep reciprocal ST depression in III and hyperacute T-waves → specificity >95%
- **Classification thresholds**:
  - **Possible**: ST elevation >=1 mm in aVL alone, no reciprocal changes, concave morphology
  - **Probable**: ST elevation >=1 mm in >=2 lateral leads with reciprocal changes in >=1 inferior lead
  - **Definite**: ST elevation >=1 mm in >=2 lateral leads with reciprocal changes + hyperacute T-waves or convex ST morphology + absence of LBBB/LVH/pericarditis confounders

### Primary Agent
**IT** — lateral STEMI is a territorial ischemia diagnosis defined by ST elevation in lateral leads with coronary territory mapping (LCx or diagonal branch of LAD), which is the IT agent's core domain.

### Cross-Domain Hints
- IT emits `cross_domain_hint: "Lateral ST elevation with inferior ST depression — CDS must confirm whether inferior depression is reciprocal or represents independent inferior ischemia"` when ST elevation in I/aVL is accompanied by ST depression in II/III/aVF.

### CDS Specific Role
CDS integrates the lateral ST elevation pattern from IT with inferior lead findings to determine if inferior ST depression is a reciprocal mirror of the lateral injury current (expected) or an independent ischemic territory (concurrent inferior STEMI). CDS applies vector analysis: high lateral injury vector points away from inferior leads, producing obligate reciprocal depression — if depression is disproportionate or accompanied by independent inferior T-wave morphology changes, CDS flags for dual-territory consideration.

## 9. Edge Cases and Pitfalls

- **Isolated aVL elevation (most commonly missed STEMI)**: Diagonal branch (D1) occlusion may produce >=1 mm ST elevation ONLY in aVL with reciprocal depression ONLY in lead III. This is easily dismissed as "nonspecific" but represents a true acute coronary occlusion. The agent MUST flag isolated aVL elevation with inferior reciprocal changes as a high-priority finding.
- **LCx STEMI with near-normal 12-lead ECG**: In patients with a dominant LCx supplying the posterior wall, the only ECG sign may be ST depression in V1-V3 (reciprocal of posterior ST elevation) with no lateral lead changes. The standard 12-lead ECG lacks direct posterior leads. This is the most commonly missed STEMI territory — the agent should flag ST depression in V1-V3 with a recommendation for posterior leads (V7-V9).
- **LVH with superimposed lateral STEMI**: Patients with pre-existing LVH may have baseline ST/T changes in lateral leads. Acute STEMI produces DYNAMIC changes superimposed on the chronic pattern. Serial ECG comparison is essential. Concordant ST elevation (ST segment in same direction as QRS) is abnormal in LVH and suggests STEMI.
- **Female patients**: Women are more likely to present with atypical symptoms and may have smaller-amplitude ST changes. Apply the same >=1 mm threshold but maintain a high index of suspicion with smaller elevations (0.5-1 mm) in the setting of clinical concern.
- **Elderly patients**: Baseline ECG abnormalities are more common (LVH, prior MI, conduction delays). Dynamic ST changes on serial ECGs become even more important for diagnosis.
- **Subtle lateral STEMI in the setting of sinus tachycardia**: Rapid heart rates cause baseline wander and make ST segment assessment difficult. Additionally, the shortened diastolic interval may cause rate-related ST depression that confounds interpretation.
- **Pericariditis vs lateral STEMI**: Both can elevate ST segments in lateral leads. Key distinguishing features: pericarditis is diffuse (elevation in >1 coronary territory), has PR depression, has NO reciprocal changes, and shows Spodick's sign. A lateral STEMI confined to I/aVL with reciprocal depression in III/aVF is NOT pericarditis.
- **Lead misplacement artifact**: Arm lead reversal (LA-RA) swaps leads I and II and swaps aVL and aVR. This can create apparent ST changes in "aVL" that are actually aVR. Check for P-wave axis consistency to exclude lead reversal.
- **Post-cardiac arrest ECG**: Widespread ST changes after resuscitation can include lateral leads. These may represent true STEMI (culprit lesion causing the arrest) or post-arrest myocardial stunning. Emergent catheterization is still indicated if STEMI criteria are met.
