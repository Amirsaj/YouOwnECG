## 1. Pathophysiology Root Cause

### 1.1 Why STEMI Equivalents Exist
Standard STEMI criteria (≥1 mm ST elevation in ≥2 contiguous leads — or ≥2 mm in V1–V3) were derived from typical anterior and inferior MI presentations. They fail to detect acute coronary occlusion (ACO) in several anatomical and electrical scenarios:
- **Posterior wall:** ECG leads face the anterior chest; posterior injury current is projected as an anterior mirror image (ST depression V1–V3 rather than elevation)
- **Very early occlusion:** Before full ST elevation develops, hyperacute T waves or de Winter pattern precede it
- **Conduction disease masking:** LBBB and paced rhythms cause baseline ST/T changes that bury ST elevation
- **LAD occlusion with specific patterns:** Some proximal LAD occlusions produce upsloping ST depression and tall T waves (de Winter), not elevation
- **Recovery phase (Wellens):** The dangerous phase of LAD stenosis can appear ECG-normal or show T-wave changes in a pain-free patient

All STEMI equivalents share one feature: they represent **acute coronary occlusion with ongoing myocardial infarction** and carry the same time-critical urgency as standard STEMI. Door-to-balloon time must not be delayed because standard STEMI criteria are not met.

### 1.2 The ACO-MI Framework
The preferred modern concept is ACO-MI (Acute Coronary Occlusion Myocardial Infarction) vs. Non-Occlusion-MI (NOMI). STEMI criteria identify only a subset of ACO-MI. All STEMI equivalents are ACO-MI by definition — they require same-urgency cath lab activation.

### 1.3 Importance for YouOwnECG
ER clinicians frequently encounter patients with chest pain and non-standard ECG patterns. Missing a STEMI equivalent is a sentinel clinical error. This node provides the comprehensive reference for all 9 recognized STEMI equivalent patterns.

## 2. ECG Presentation — Lead by Lead

### 2.1 Diagnostic Criteria — All STEMI Equivalents

| Pattern | Mechanism | Key Leads | Urgency |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1. Posterior STEMI | Posterior wall injury current projects anteriorly inverted | V1–V3 (depression), V7–V9 (elevation) | STAT — same as STEMI |
| 2. De Winter T-waves | LAD occlusion; non-classic injury current | V1–V5 (upsloping depression + tall T) | STAT |
| 3. Wellens Syndrome Type A | Reperfused/critical LAD stenosis | V2–V3 (biphasic T) | STAT/Urgent |
| 4. Wellens Syndrome Type B | Reperfused/critical LAD stenosis | V2–V3 (deep symmetric T) | STAT/Urgent |
| 5. aVR + diffuse depression | LMCA or proximal LAD occlusion | aVR elevation + ≥6 leads depression | STAT |
| 6. Sgarbossa positive (LBBB) | LAD/RCA occlusion masked by LBBB | Concordant lead(s) | STAT |
| 7. Sgarbossa positive (paced) | Occlusion masked by ventricular pacing | Concordant lead(s) | STAT |
| 8. Hyperacute T-waves | Earliest phase of STEMI | Affected territory leads | STAT |
| 9. Isolated aVL ST depression | Reciprocal to early inferior STEMI | aVL (depression); II/III/aVF subtle | STAT |

### 2.2 Lead-by-Lead Manifestation — Per Pattern

**Pattern 1: Posterior STEMI**

| Lead | Finding |
|---|---|
| V1 | ST depression ≥0.5 mm (primary finding); tall R wave (mirror of posterior Q wave); tall upright T (mirror of posterior T inversion) |
| V2 | ST depression ≥0.5 mm; tall R; tall T |
| V3 | ST depression; transitional |
| V7 | ST elevation ≥0.5 mm (posterior lead — left posterior axillary line) |
| V8 | ST elevation ≥0.5 mm (posterior lead — left midscapular line) |
| V9 | ST elevation ≥0.5 mm (posterior lead — left paraspinal) |
| R/S in V1 | R/S ratio >1 in V1 (mirror of posterior Q wave; tall R represents posterior wall that is not depolarizing) |

**Pattern 2: De Winter T-waves**

| Lead | Finding |
|---|---|
| V1–V4 | Upsloping ST depression ≥1 mm at J-point; TALL, peaked, symmetric T waves following the depression |
| V5–V6 | May have similar but milder pattern |
| aVR | ST elevation 1–2 mm (reciprocal finding — present in ~75% of de Winter cases) |
| Key feature | Static pattern — does NOT evolve to classic ST elevation over time |

**Pattern 3 & 4: Wellens Syndrome**

| Lead | Type A (Biphasic) | Type B (Deep Symmetric) |
|---|---|---|
| V2 | Biphasic T wave: positive deflection → negative deflection | Deep symmetric T-wave inversion (≥2 mm) |
| V3 | Same as V2 | Same as V2 |
| V1, V4 | May be mildly involved | May be involved |
| ST segment | Near-isoelectric or minimal elevation | Near-isoelectric |
| Context | Patient pain-free at time of ECG; history of recent chest pain | Same |
| Danger | If stress test performed: may precipitate massive anterior MI | Same |

**Pattern 5: aVR Elevation + Diffuse ST Depression**

| Lead | Finding |
|---|---|
| aVR | ST elevation ≥1 mm |
| V1 | ST elevation ≥1 mm (often accompanies aVR elevation) |
| I, II, aVL, V4–V6 | ST depression (≥1 mm in ≥6 leads) |
| Inferior leads | ST depression |
| Pattern | Elevation only in aVR (±V1); depression everywhere else = global subendocardial ischemia |

**Patterns 6 & 7: Sgarbossa in LBBB / Paced — see pacemaker_rhythms.md (Node 2.7.106)**

**Pattern 8: Hyperacute T-waves**

| Lead | Finding |
|---|---|
| Affected territory | Tall (amplitude ≥6 mm precordial), BROAD-BASED, SYMMETRIC T waves; height out of proportion to QRS amplitude |
| Key feature | Precedes ST elevation by minutes to hours; may be the only finding on a very early ECG |
| Context | Patient with acute chest pain; "the T wave you cannot ignore" |

**Pattern 9: Isolated aVL ST Depression (early inferior STEMI)**

| Lead | Finding |
|---|---|
| aVL | ST depression ≥0.5 mm (reciprocal to inferior STEMI) |
| II, III, aVF | Subtle ST elevation (may be <1 mm — not meeting standard STEMI threshold) |
| V5–V6 | Subtle ST depression sometimes |
| Key feature | aVL changes can precede visible inferior lead elevation by 5–15 minutes |

### 2.3 Key Leads Summary by Pattern

| Pattern | Primary Leads | Confirmatory Leads |
|---|---|---|
| Posterior | V1–V3 (depression) | V7–V9 (elevation) |
| De Winter | V1–V5 (upsloping depression + tall T) | aVR (elevation) |
| Wellens | V2–V3 (T morphology) | V1, V4 |
| aVR pattern | aVR (elevation) | All other leads (depression) |
| Hyperacute T | Affected territory | Serial ECG evolution |
| Isolated aVL depression | aVL | II, III, aVF |

### 2.4 Beat-by-Beat Considerations
De Winter T-waves: static — look the same beat to beat. No dynamic evolution within a single ECG.
Wellens: pain-free window — the ECG shows pathological T waves without active ischemia on that beat.
Hyperacute T: evolves on the order of minutes. Serial ECGs every 15 minutes are essential.

## 3. Morphology Details

### 3.1 Posterior STEMI — Morphology
The mirror-image concept: imagine the V1–V3 waveforms flipped upside down. The resulting pattern should look like a normal STEMI in V7–V9. Tall R in V1 (the flip of a deep Q wave), horizontal ST depression (the flip of ST elevation), tall upright T (the flip of an inverted T). The R/S ratio in V1 ≥1 is equivalent to the "Q wave" of posterior MI. The system should compute R/S ratio in V1 and flag ≥1 with horizontal ST depression as posterior STEMI until proven otherwise.

### 3.2 De Winter T-waves — Morphology
The ST segment DOES NOT elevate. Instead, it depresses 1–3 mm with an UPSLOPING morphology (slopes upward from J-point to the T-wave peak). The T wave is unusually tall, peaked, and symmetric — resembles hyperkalemia T waves but is restricted to anterior precordial leads V1–V5. This upsloping-depression-into-tall-T pattern is pathognomonic. The system must distinguish this from the downsloping ST depression of posterior STEMI and from the benign J-point depression of normal repolarization variants.

### 3.3 Wellens — Morphology Detail
Type A (biphasic): the T wave starts positive then inverts sharply — a terminal negativity. The transition from positive to negative in the middle of the T wave is distinctive.
Type B (deep symmetric): both limbs of the T-wave inversion are equal. Deep typically ≥2 mm in V2–V3. ST segment near isoelectric. Symmetry distinguishes Wellens Type B from asymmetric T inversion of non-ischemic causes (strain, early repolarization).

### 3.4 aVR Elevation — Morphology
ST elevation in aVR is always present to some degree in diffuse subendocardial ischemia. The magnitude matters: ≥1 mm with ≥6 leads showing concurrent depression = LMCA/proximal LAD/multivessel. The anatomical basis: aVR looks at the inflow tract and septal region — when the entire subendocardium is ischemic (LMCA pattern), aVR captures the residual injury current.

### 3.5 Hyperacute T-waves — Morphology
Key measurable features:
- Height: ≥6 mm in precordial leads (some authorities use ≥8 mm)
- Base width: broader than normal T wave (tall AND wide — differentiates from peaked hyperkalemia T which is narrow-based)
- Symmetry: symmetric (both limbs equal height)
- Proportion: T-wave amplitude exceeds 50% of R-wave amplitude in the same lead

### 3.6 Isolated aVL Depression — Morphology
aVL ST depression ≥0.5 mm as the only initial abnormality. The key is the absence of competing explanations (LVH strain, digoxin effect, rate-related changes). In a patient with chest pain, isolated aVL depression demands assessment of inferior leads for subtle changes and serial ECGs every 10–15 minutes.

## 4. Differential Diagnosis

### 4.1 Mimics

| Pattern | Main Mimic | Key Differentiator |
|---|---|---|
| Posterior STEMI | RVH, WPW Type A, posterior r progression | Clinical context; V7–V9; echo wall motion |
| De Winter T-waves | Hyperkalemia (peaked T only) | Hyperkalemia: tall T in all leads, QRS widening, no upsloping depression restricted to V1–V5 |
| Wellens Type B | Cardiomyopathy T inversions, RVH, ARVC | Pain history; troponin; echo; context (not athlete, not ARVC distribution) |
| aVR elevation pattern | Demand ischemia (Type 2 MI), global pericarditis | Pericarditis: diffuse saddle-back elevation not depression; PR depression; no reciprocal aVR isolation |
| Hyperacute T | Normal male variant (tall T V2–V3) | Symptoms; serial ECG evolution; height/width proportion |
| Isolated aVL depression | LVH strain (aVL depression common) | LVH: voltage; strain pattern (downsloping vs. horizontal); no acute pain correlation |

### 4.2 Coexisting Conditions
Posterior STEMI commonly coexists with inferior STEMI (RCA dominance or left circumflex wrapping inferior wall). The combined pattern: inferior ST elevation (II, III, aVF) + V1–V3 ST depression. The posterior component worsens prognosis significantly and is missed if V7–V9 are not recorded.

## 5. STAT Classification

| Pattern | Classification | Action |
|---|---|---|
| Posterior STEMI | **STAT — ACO-MI** | Activate cath; obtain V7–V9 immediately |
| De Winter T-waves | **STAT — ACO-MI** | Activate cath immediately; do not wait for serial ECGs |
| Wellens (Type A or B) | **STAT/Urgent** | No stress test; admit; urgent cath (hours); do not discharge |
| aVR + diffuse depression | **STAT — ACO-MI** | Highest-risk pattern; immediate cath for LMCA/proximal LAD |
| Sgarbossa positive (LBBB/paced) | **STAT — ACO-MI** | Same as STEMI; activate cath |
| Hyperacute T-waves | **STAT** | Serial ECGs every 10–15 min; presumed ACO until serial ECG/troponin clarifies |
| Isolated aVL depression + pain | **URGENT** | Serial ECGs every 10–15 min; if inferior ST evolves → STAT |

## 6. Reasoning Complexity Analysis

### 6.1 Pattern Recognition Challenge
Each pattern requires a different morphological recognition algorithm:
- Posterior: R/S ratio V1, ST depression slope (horizontal/downsloping)
- De Winter: upsloping ST depression + T-wave height/width in V1–V5
- Wellens: T-wave morphology (biphasic vs. symmetric deep) in pain-free context
- aVR: elevation amplitude + count of depressed leads
- Hyperacute T: amplitude + width + proportion criteria
- Isolated aVL: amplitude threshold + clinical context + inferior lead assessment

Six distinct measurement and classification tasks, each requiring different feature extraction from ECGdeli output.

### 6.2 Clinical Context Dependency
Wellens is a clinical ECG diagnosis — the patient must be pain-free at time of ECG with a recent chest pain history. The ECG in isolation (Type B deep T inversions) has multiple causes; Wellens designation requires context. The system must either have pain status input or flag "Wellens pattern morphology — clinical pain status required for classification."

### 6.3 Serial ECG Importance
Hyperacute T-waves and isolated aVL depression demand serial ECG comparison. A single ECG snapshot may be ambiguous. The system must compute and display lead-by-lead ST and T-wave changes between serial ECGs with directional trend indicators.

### 6.4 V7–V9 Activation
Posterior STEMI can only be confirmed with V7–V9 leads. The system must detect horizontal ST depression V1–V3 with R/S ≥1 in V1 and immediately recommend: "Obtain V7–V9 leads — posterior STEMI cannot be excluded." If V7–V9 data is present in the ECGdeli input, analyze it.

### 6.5 De Winter Static Nature
De Winter does NOT evolve. A single ECG with de Winter pattern is the indication for cath activation. This differs from hyperacute T or aVL depression where serial ECGs are recommended before activation. The system must categorize de Winter as "single-ECG activation trigger" — not a "watch and repeat" pattern.

| Dimension | Score (1–5) | Rationale |

| Agent | Role for STEMI Equivalent Patterns | Key Features Used |
|-------|--------------------------|-------------------|
| **RRC** (Rhythm/Rate/Conduction) | Supporting | Heart rate (de Winter: rate obscures ST morphology), LBBB detection (triggers Sgarbossa pathway for LBBB+ischemia equivalent), QRS duration and morphology for each pattern |
| **IT** (Ischemia/Territory) | Primary | de Winter: upsloping ST depression ≥1 mm + hyperacute T-waves in V1–V6; Wellens: symmetric T-wave inversion V2–V3 without STE; posterior STEMI: horizontal ST depression V1–V3 (mirror image); Brugada-STEMI overlap: ST changes in V1–V2 requiring differentiation; coronary territory for each pattern |
| **MR** (Morphology/Repolarization) | Supporting | de Winter T-wave morphology (narrow-based symmetric peaked); Wellens T-wave subtype (Type A biphasic vs Type B deep symmetric); posterior STEMI: dominant R-wave in V1 (R/S ≥1); Brugada: coved ST morphology (downsloping) vs STEMI convex |
| **CDS** (Cross-Domain Synthesis) | Required — CRITICAL for each STEMI equivalent | Each equivalent requires specific cross-domain resolution: de Winter (IT ST + MR T-wave morphology); Wellens (IT negative criteria + MR morphology subtype); posterior STEMI (IT V1–V3 depression + MR R/S ratio); Brugada-STEMI (IT must not call STEMI when MR confirms coved pattern) |

### Primary Agent
**IT** is primary for all ischemic STEMI equivalents (de Winter, Wellens, posterior STEMI) because all involve ischemic ST/T changes in coronary territories. **MR** is co-primary or primary for Brugada-overlap patterns where morphology is the defining feature.

### Cross-Domain Hints
Each STEMI equivalent pattern emits specific cross-domain hints — see the individual disease files for exact hint text:
- de Winter: IT emits hint requesting MR T-wave morphology confirmation
- Wellens: IT emits hint requesting MR subtype classification
- Posterior STEMI: IT emits hint requesting MR R/S ratio; MR emits hint requesting IT ST depression assessment
- Brugada-STEMI overlap: IT emits hint suppressing anterior STEMI call when V1–V2 isolated ST changes are detected

### CDS Specific Role
As a reference file, this entry documents the CDS logic framework for STEMI equivalents as a class. CDS must apply pattern-specific resolution algorithms rather than generic STEMI territory logic. For each STEMI equivalent, CDS receives the pattern-specific cross-domain data from Phase 1 agents, applies the appropriate algorithm (de Winter composite, Wellens negative criteria, Sgarbossa for LBBB, morphology override for Brugada), and generates a STEMI equivalent classification with the specific equivalent type labeled in the output. All STEMI equivalents that are confirmed by CDS generate the same urgency tier as confirmed STEMI.

## 9. Edge Cases and Pitfalls

- **Never apply Wellens diagnosis to a patient who is currently in pain.** Wellens is a post-ischemic repolarization pattern in a pain-free patient. During active ischemia, deep T inversions have different implications.
- **De Winter patterns can revert to STEMI:** Although de Winter is described as static, individual patients have been reported to transition to classic STEMI. Do not assume the de Winter pattern is stable — cath urgency is unchanged.
- **Posterior STEMI without inferior involvement:** Isolated posterior STEMI (circumflex occlusion, non-dominant RCA) produces ONLY V1–V3 depression with no inferior ST changes. This pattern is particularly prone to missed diagnosis.
- **Hyperacute T waves in hyperkalemia:** Hyperkalemia produces peaked, narrow-based T waves that can look like hyperacute T waves. Hyperkalemia: tall T in all leads, narrow peaked, associated with PR prolongation and wide QRS as potassium rises. Hyperacute T: restricted to the ischemic territory, broad-based. Always check potassium in a patient with diffuse peaked T waves.
- **Wellens Type B and cardiomyopathy:** Deep symmetric T inversions in V2–V4 can occur in dilated cardiomyopathy, RVH, and other conditions. Wellens is a diagnosis requiring troponin rise-and-fall pattern, coronary anatomy, and clinical pain history — not ECG alone.
- **aVR elevation in other conditions:** Diffuse ST depression + aVR elevation is also seen in: severe hypokalemia, digoxin toxicity, diffuse myopericarditis, and demand ischemia (Type 2 MI). The pattern requires clinical context for correct diagnosis.
- **Isolated aVL depression with normal inferior leads:** If the clinician dismisses aVL depression as LVH strain and sends the patient home, an early inferior STEMI may be missed. In the setting of chest pain, ANY aVL ST depression ≥0.5 mm requires serial ECGs.
