## 1. Pathophysiology Root Cause

### 1.1 Vaughan Williams Classification Framework
The Vaughan Williams classification groups antiarrhythmic drugs by their primary ion channel target. Each class produces predictable ECG changes based on which channel is affected.

| Class | Primary Target | Primary ECG Effect |
|---|---|---|
| IA | Na+ channel block (intermediate kinetics) + K+ block | QRS widening + QT prolongation |
| IB | Na+ channel block (fast kinetics) | Minimal QRS change; QT shortening |
| IC | Na+ channel block (slow kinetics, use-dependent) | Significant QRS widening |
| II | Beta-adrenergic receptor block | Sinus bradycardia, PR prolongation |
| III | K+ channel block (primarily IKr/IKs) | QT prolongation |
| IV | L-type Ca2+ channel block (AV nodal) | PR prolongation, sinus bradycardia |

### 1.2 Cellular Basis
- **Na+ channel block:** Slows the rate of phase 0 depolarization (upstroke velocity, dV/dt) → slows conduction velocity → QRS widening. Degree of QRS widening reflects degree of Na+ block.
- **K+ channel block:** Prolongs phase 3 repolarization → prolongs APD → QT prolongation. The IKr channel is the primary target for most Class IA and III drugs.
- **Ca2+ channel block (L-type):** Slows AV nodal conduction (Ca2+-dependent conduction in the AV node) → PR prolongation. Does not affect ventricular muscle significantly (fast Na+-dependent conduction).
- **Use-dependence:** Some Na+ channel blockers (especially Class IC) bind more avidly at faster heart rates — greater QRS widening at fast rates. This is "use-dependent" block: the channel is blocked more when it is used more (at faster rates). At slow rates, the drug partially dissociates and QRS is narrower.

### 1.3 Proarrhythmic Mechanism
Antiarrhythmics can cause the very arrhythmias they treat:
- QRS widening (Class I) → facilitates reentry → VT
- QT prolongation (Class IA, III) → TdP risk
- Class IC in structural heart disease → increased mortality (CAST trial — post-MI patients on flecainide/encainide had higher mortality from proarrhythmia)
- Class IC-induced flutter with 1:1 conduction → hemodynamic collapse

## 2. ECG Presentation — Class by Class

### 2.1 Class IA (Quinidine, Procainamide, Disopyramide) — Diagnostic Criteria

| ECG Finding | Mechanism | Clinical Significance |
|---|---|---|
| QRS widening (>120 ms) | Na+ channel block → reduced phase 0 velocity | Degree proportional to drug level |
| QT prolongation (QTc >500 ms) | K+ channel (IKr) block → prolonged phase 3 | TdP risk — dose-dependent |
| U wave prominence (quinidine) | Purkinje repolarization prolongation | Quinidine U waves can be very large |
| PR prolongation | AV nodal slowing | Mild; less than Class II/IV |
| T-wave widening | Prolonged repolarization | Broad T waves with U waves |

**Quinidine-specific:** Large U waves + QT prolongation + "quinidine syncope" (TdP). Quinidine also causes paradoxical acceleration of ventricular rate in AF (anticholinergic effect on AV node) when used alone — must combine with AV nodal blocking agent.

**Procainamide:** Similar to quinidine; also produces NAPA (active metabolite) which is primarily a K+ channel blocker → additive QT prolongation.

**Disopyramide:** Strong anticholinergic effects (urinary retention, dry mouth, visual blurring) in addition to Class IA ECG changes.

### 2.2 Class IB (Lidocaine, Mexiletine) — Diagnostic Criteria

| ECG Finding | Mechanism | Clinical Significance |
|---|---|---|
| Minimal QRS change | Fast kinetics — drug dissociates rapidly | QRS widening minimal even at therapeutic levels |
| QTc shortening or unchanged | Shortened APD in ventricular tissue | QT may slightly shorten |
| No T-wave changes | Minimal effect on repolarization gradient | ECG often appears essentially normal |

Class IB drugs have fast channel kinetics — they bind and unbind the Na+ channel rapidly (within one action potential cycle). This means:
- At normal heart rates, significant channel block does not accumulate
- Primarily affects ischemic/diseased tissue (where channels are partially inactivated and drug binding is preferred)
- Essentially no proarrhythmic QRS widening at therapeutic doses
- No clinical ECG monitoring parameters of use

### 2.3 Class IC (Flecainide, Propafenone) — Diagnostic Criteria

| ECG Finding | Mechanism | Clinical Significance |
|---|---|---|
| Significant QRS widening | Slow kinetics, use-dependent strong Na+ block | QRS widening correlates with rate; more widening at fast rates |
| PR prolongation | AV nodal Na+ channel contribution | May cause first-degree AV block |
| Minimal QT change | Na+ block dominates; limited K+ block | QT change is from QRS widening; JT interval (T wave only) unchanged |
| Rate-dependent QRS widening | Use-dependence: more block at faster rates | During exercise or tachycardia, QRS may widen significantly |
| Flutter with 1:1 conduction (dangerous) | AF slows to flutter rate (200 bpm); AV node conducts 1:1 | Hemodynamic emergency — must use with AV nodal agent |

**Critical IC danger — atrial flutter with 1:1 conduction:**
Class IC drugs slow atrial flutter rate from ~300 bpm to ~200 bpm. The AV node, which was blocking many impulses at 300 bpm, can now conduct all impulses at 200 bpm → 1:1 conduction → ventricular rate 200 bpm with wide QRS (Na+ block) → VT-appearing tachycardia → hemodynamic collapse. Prevention requires concurrent AV nodal blocking agent (beta-blocker or Ca2+ channel blocker).

**CAST trial (post-MI contraindication):** Flecainide and encainide increased mortality in post-MI patients with asymptomatic PVCs despite suppressing PVCs. Class IC drugs are contraindicated in patients with structural heart disease (reduced EF, previous MI).

### 2.4 Class II (Beta-Blockers) — Diagnostic Criteria

| ECG Finding | Mechanism | Clinical Significance |
|---|---|---|
| Sinus bradycardia | Beta-1 blockade → reduced SA node automaticity | Expected; dose-dependent |
| PR prolongation | Beta-1 blockade → slowed AV nodal conduction | Mild; PR typically 180–240 ms |
| No QRS change | No Na+ channel effect | QRS unchanged |
| No QT change | No K+ channel effect | QTc unchanged |
| Reduced heart rate variability | Blunted sympathetic response | Noted on long-term monitoring |

Beta-blockers are unique in that they produce essentially normal ECGs at therapeutic doses beyond rate slowing. They have the most favorable safety profile of all antiarrhythmics. They do not cause TdP.

### 2.5 Class III (Amiodarone, Sotalol, Dofetilide, Ibutilide) — Diagnostic Criteria

| Drug | ECG Finding | TdP Risk | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| Amiodarone | Sinus bradycardia + PR prolongation + QT prolongation + T-wave notching/widening | LOW despite long QT | Multi-channel effects; rarely causes TdP despite very long QTc |
| Sotalol | QT prolongation (dose-dependent) | HIGH | QTc >500 ms = significant TdP risk; dose must be reduced |
| Dofetilide | QT prolongation | HIGH | Pure IKr blocker; strict QTc monitoring required; hospitalized initiation |
| Ibutilide | QT prolongation (transient) | MODERATE | IV use only; monitored for 4 hours post-infusion |

**Amiodarone — the exception:**
Amiodarone blocks Na+, K+, Ca2+ channels AND beta-adrenergic receptors. Despite causing the longest QTc of any antiarrhythmic, it has paradoxically LOW TdP risk because:
1. Uniform QT prolongation (reduces transmural dispersion of repolarization)
2. Na+ channel block (reduces triggered activity amplitude)
3. Sympatholytic effect (reduces catecholamine-driven afterdepolarizations)

Amiodarone-specific ECG findings:
- Sinus bradycardia (beta-blocking effect)
- PR prolongation (Ca2+ channel block on AV node)
- QT prolongation (K+ channel block) — QTc 500–600 ms is common and NOT a contraindication
- T-wave notching: characteristic bifid/notched T waves or "humped" T waves
- Corneal microdeposits and thyroid effects are non-ECG toxicity

**Sotalol:** Has both beta-blocking (Class II) and K+-blocking (Class III) properties. The K+ block is dose-dependent. At doses >320 mg/day, QTc monitoring is mandatory. Sotalol is renally cleared — renal impairment dramatically increases QTc and TdP risk.

### 2.6 Class IV (Verapamil, Diltiazem) — Diagnostic Criteria

| ECG Finding | Mechanism | Clinical Significance |
|---|---|---|
| PR prolongation | L-type Ca2+ block → slowed AV nodal conduction | Rate control in AF/flutter |
| Sinus bradycardia | SA node Ca2+-dependent automaticity slowed | Dose-dependent |
| No QRS change | Ventricular myocardium uses fast Na+ channels | QRS unchanged |
| No QT change | No K+ channel effect | QTc unchanged |

**CRITICAL — WPW + AF + Class IV contraindication:**
In Wolff-Parkinson-White syndrome with AF, verapamil or diltiazem is ABSOLUTELY CONTRAINDICATED. Mechanism:
1. AF → impulses trying to conduct via AV node and accessory pathway simultaneously
2. Class IV blocks the AV node → all impulses routed through accessory pathway
3. Accessory pathway has no decremental conduction → conducts AF at 200–300 bpm
4. Result: very rapid irregular wide-complex tachycardia → VF

## 3. Morphology Details

### 3.1 QRS Widening — Class I Assessment
- Baseline QRS should be documented before initiating any Class I agent
- Therapeutic monitoring: QRS should not widen >25% from baseline
- QRS widening >150 ms with Class IC = significant proarrhythmic risk
- Use-dependent widening: compare QRS at rest vs. during exercise or faster rates for Class IC

### 3.2 QT Prolongation Assessment
- Bazett QTc: QTc = QT/√RR; appropriate for resting rates
- Fridericia QTc: QTc = QT/RR^(1/3); preferred at extremes of rate
- Drug-induced TdP risk thresholds: QTc >500 ms or QTc increase >60 ms from baseline
- Exception: amiodarone — QTc 500–600 ms acceptable; TdP risk low
- Sotalol, dofetilide: QTc ≥500 ms = dose reduction or discontinuation

### 3.3 T Wave Morphology by Drug
- Amiodarone: notched, humped, or bifid T waves — characteristic amiodarone-effect appearance
- Class IA: broad, wide T waves with large U waves (quinidine)
- Class IC: T wave morphology generally preserved; QRS widening is the main change

### 3.4 PR Interval Monitoring
- Class II: PR 180–250 ms expected
- Class IV: PR 180–260 ms expected; second-degree AV block = excessive dose
- Amiodarone: PR 200–280 ms common; complete AV block rare

## 4. Differential Diagnosis

### 4.1 Mimics

| Drug Effect | Overlapping Condition | Distinguishing Feature |
|---|---|---|
| Class IA/IC QRS widening | LBBB, RBBB | BBB: fixed, independent of rate; drug: acquired, may be rate-dependent |
| Class IA/III QT prolongation | Congenital LQT, hypokalemia | Drug history; electrolytes; QT normalized after drug cessation |
| Beta-blocker bradycardia | Sick sinus syndrome | Drug history; resolves with drug withdrawal |
| Class IV PR prolongation | First-degree AV block (intrinsic) | Drug history; resolves with dose reduction |
| Amiodarone T notching | LQT2 notched T waves | Amiodarone history; LQT2 genetic; no amiodarone use |

### 4.2 Drug-Drug Interactions (ECG-Relevant)
- Amiodarone + digoxin → digoxin toxicity (amiodarone reduces digoxin clearance ~50%)
- Amiodarone + sotalol/dofetilide → additive QT prolongation → TdP
- Class IC + AV nodal agents (beta-blocker/CCB) → required combination to prevent 1:1 flutter conduction
- Quinidine + verapamil → hypotension; quinidine + amiodarone → additive QT

## 5. STAT Classification

| Finding | Drug Class | Action |
|---|---|---|
| QRS widening >25% from baseline | Class I (any) | Reduce dose; check level; consider discontinuation |
| QTc >500 ms | Class IA, III (sotalol, dofetilide) | Reduce dose or hold; electrolytes; continuous monitoring |
| QTc >500 ms on amiodarone | Amiodarone | Monitor; acceptable QTc if no TdP; check thyroid |
| TdP | Any QT-prolonging drug | Hold drug; IV MgSO4; correct electrolytes |
| Wide-complex regular tachycardia on IC | Class IC | Suspect flutter with 1:1 or proarrhythmic VT — STAT |
| Sinus bradycardia <40 bpm | Class II or IV | Reduce dose; atropine if symptomatic |
| Complete AV block | Class IV or II | Hold drug; transcutaneous pacing if symptomatic |
| WPW + AF with rapid wide complex | Class IV given | STAT — defibrillation; procainamide is treatment of choice |

## 6. Reasoning Complexity Analysis

### 6.1 Multi-Drug Complexity
This node covers 4 classes and ~10 individual drugs. Each has distinct ECG signatures. The reasoning system must first identify the drug class (from medication history) and then map expected ECG changes to the actual findings.

### 6.2 Proarrhythmia Recognition
The most complex reasoning task: distinguishing therapeutic ECG change from proarrhythmic ECG change. QRS widening on a Class IC drug at baseline = therapeutic; QRS widening during tachycardia = proarrhythmic. QTc 530 ms on amiodarone = expected; QTc 530 ms on sotalol = dangerous.

### 6.3 Context-Dependent Risk Assessment
Drug-specific TdP risk assessment requires integration of: drug identity, dose, QTc value, concurrent drugs, electrolyte status, renal function, heart rate. This is inherently a multi-variable clinical reasoning task.

### 6.4 WPW Contraindication
The WPW + AF + Class IV rule is high-stakes, infrequent, and counterintuitive. Rate-slowing drugs worsen WPW-AF by forcing conduction through the accessory pathway. The treating team must know which drugs to AVOID in WPW — not just which drugs to give.

### 6.5 Amiodarone Exception
Amiodarone violates the general rule that QT prolongation = TdP risk. The reasoning system must encode the amiodarone exception explicitly rather than applying a blanket QTc threshold rule.

### 6.6 Difficulty Score

| Dimension | Score (1–5) | Rationale |
|---|---|---|
| Pattern recognition | 3 | Multiple drug-specific patterns to identify |
| Measurement precision | 3 | QRS and QTc monitoring; rate-dependent QRS changes |
| Differential diagnosis | 3 | Drug effect vs. intrinsic conduction disease |
| Multi-condition interaction | 4 | Drug-drug, drug-electrolyte interactions |
| Severity stratification | 4 | Drug-specific risk thresholds (amiodarone exception) |
| Treatment implication | 4 | Class IC contraindication post-MI; WPW contraindication; TdP management |
| Arrhythmia recognition | 4 | Flutter with 1:1; bidirectional VT; proarrhythmic VT |
| **Overall** | **3.5** | **High complexity — reference node** |

## 6A. Agent Assignment

**Architecture:** Option E — 3 Parallel Phase 1 Specialists + 1 Sequential CDS (Node 2.1 Gate Passed 2026-03-27)

| Agent | Role for Antiarrhythmic Drug Effects | Key Features Used |
|-------|--------------------------|-------------------|
| **RRC** (Rhythm/Rate/Conduction) | Primary | QTc prolongation (Class Ia: quinidine, procainamide, disopyramide; Class III: amiodarone, sotalol, dofetilide), QRS widening ≥120ms (Class Ic: flecainide, propafenone), PR prolongation (Class Ia/III/IV), bradycardia (beta-blockers, Class IV), use-dependent QRS widening with Class Ic |
| **IT** (Ischemia/Territory) | Not involved | No ischemic ST/T-wave changes from antiarrhythmic drug effects |
| **MR** (Morphology/Repolarization) | Supporting | QRS morphology changes from Class Ic (wide QRS mimicking BBB), T-wave changes with Class III (broad/peaked T with amiodarone), QT morphology assessment |
| **CDS** (Cross-Domain Synthesis) | Standard integration only | Integrates QTc prolongation + QRS widening + rate/rhythm findings; flags drug-induced QTc prolongation >500ms as high-risk; identifies Class Ic QRS widening pattern |

### Primary Agent
**RRC** — Antiarrhythmic drug effects manifest primarily as conduction interval changes (PR, QRS duration, QTc) and rate/rhythm alterations, all core RRC domain features.

### Cross-Domain Hints
- RRC → MR: `cross_domain_hint: "QRS widening detected — MR should characterize morphology to distinguish Class Ic drug effect (wide QRS without typical BBB notching) from true BBB pattern"`
- RRC → CDS: `cross_domain_hint: "QTc prolonged in context of antiarrhythmic drug effect — CDS should identify drug class from combined QTc + QRS + PR pattern and flag TdP risk if QTc >500ms"`

### CDS Specific Role
CDS performs standard integration for antiarrhythmic drug effects, combining interval changes across RRC and morphology changes from MR to identify the drug class responsible. Class Ia shows QTc prolongation + QRS widening + PR prolongation; Class Ic shows marked QRS widening without significant QTc change; Class III shows QTc prolongation (sometimes marked, as with sotalol) with T-wave broadening. CDS flags drug-induced QTc >500ms as a high-risk finding requiring clinical escalation and TdP risk stratification.

## 7. RAG Knowledge Requirements

### 7.1 Essential Knowledge Chunks
- Vaughan Williams classification: classes I-IV, primary mechanisms, ECG effects
- Class IA: QRS + QT prolongation; quinidine U waves; TdP risk
- Class IB: minimal ECG change; no proarrhythmia at therapeutic doses
- Class IC: use-dependent QRS widening; flutter with 1:1 danger; CAST trial; post-MI contraindication
- Class II: bradycardia + PR prolongation; no TdP risk
- Class III: QT prolongation; amiodarone exception (low TdP despite long QT); sotalol/dofetilide = high TdP risk
- Class IV: PR prolongation; WPW-AF contraindication
- Drug monitoring parameters: QRS change threshold, QTc thresholds, amiodarone exception

### 7.2 Reference Values
- QRS widening threshold: >25% increase from baseline = concerning
- QTc for TdP risk (non-amiodarone): >500 ms or >60 ms increase from baseline
- Amiodarone acceptable QTc: up to 600 ms (no standard upper limit if no TdP)
- Sotalol/dofetilide: hold if QTc ≥500 ms

## 9. Edge Cases and Pitfalls

1. **Class IC flutter with 1:1 conduction presenting as VT:** Wide-complex regular tachycardia at rate 180–230 bpm in a patient on flecainide for AF = flutter with 1:1 conduction until proven otherwise. This is not VT — the treatment is IV procainamide (not lidocaine or amiodarone) and AV nodal blockade. Electrical cardioversion is definitive.

2. **Amiodarone QTc >550 ms — don't panic:** Many clinicians reflexively hold amiodarone when QTc exceeds 500 ms. This is inappropriate. Amiodarone-related QTc 520–580 ms is expected and TdP risk is low due to multi-channel effects. TdP on amiodarone is rare and usually indicates a concurrent precipitant (hypokalemia, additional QT-prolonging drug).

3. **Proarrhythmic VT vs. treated arrhythmia:** Wide-complex VT appearing after initiation of antiarrhythmic therapy = proarrhythmia until proven otherwise. Class IC drugs can convert AF to a monomorphic VT that is hemodynamically tolerated. Clinical deterioration after starting an antiarrhythmic → drug is the suspect.

4. **Sotalol dose reduction with renal impairment:** Sotalol is renally cleared. AKI in a patient on therapeutic sotalol → drug accumulates → QTc lengthens → TdP. Sotalol dosing must be adjusted for GFR. QTc monitoring is mandatory.

5. **Rate-dependent QRS widening on Class IC:** A patient on flecainide may have a narrow QRS at rest (60 bpm) and a wide QRS during exertion or AF (150 bpm). This is use-dependent block in action. Exercise treadmill testing may be used to unmask class IC toxicity at therapeutic levels.

6. **Procainamide NAPA accumulation:** Procainamide is metabolized to N-acetylprocainamide (NAPA), a pure K+ channel blocker with significant TdP risk. Slow acetylators (genetic polymorphism) accumulate NAPA disproportionately. Monitoring both procainamide AND NAPA levels is required.
