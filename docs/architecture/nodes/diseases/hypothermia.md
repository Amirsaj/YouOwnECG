## 1. Pathophysiology Root Cause

### 1.1 Cellular Mechanism
Hypothermia globally slows all ion channel kinetics in a temperature-dependent manner. The cardiac action potential depends on precise timing of:
- Phase 0: fast Na+ channel activation
- Phase 1: rapid inactivation of Na+ channels + Ito activation
- Phase 2: L-type Ca2+ channel plateau
- Phase 3: IKr/IKs K+ channel activation and repolarization
- Phase 4: diastolic depolarization (pacemaker current, If)

When temperature falls below 37°C, all of these processes slow. The net effects:
1. Slower phase 0 → slower conduction → QRS widening
2. Prolonged phase 2 and 3 → prolonged APD → QT prolongation (OPPOSITE of hypercalcemia)
3. Slowed phase 4 → reduced automaticity → sinus bradycardia
4. In the AV node: slower Ca2+-dependent conduction → PR prolongation

### 1.2 Osborn Wave (J Wave) Mechanism
The Osborn wave is the pathognomonic ECG finding of hypothermia. Its mechanism:

Hypothermia creates a heterogeneous effect on epicardial vs. endocardial/midmyocardial cells. Specifically:
- Epicardial cells have a prominent Ito (transient outward K+ current) that creates a notch in phase 1 of the action potential
- At low temperatures, Ito in epicardial cells is enhanced relative to its counterpart in endocardial cells
- This creates a transmural voltage gradient at the end of phase 1 / beginning of phase 2 (at the J-point)
- The epicardium repolarizes slightly before the endocardium at this early phase → generates a positive deflection on the ECG at the J-point

This J-point positive deflection is the Osborn wave. The same mechanism explains Osborn waves in other settings (hypercalcemia, early repolarization) where Ito is relatively enhanced.

### 1.3 Temperature Thresholds and Severity
- Normal: 37°C (98.6°F)
- Mild hypothermia: 32–35°C — sinus bradycardia, early PR/QT changes, shivering
- Moderate hypothermia: 28–32°C — Osborn waves appear, atrial fibrillation (very common), junctional rhythms
- Severe hypothermia: <28°C — high VT/VF risk, wide-complex rhythms, asystole possible
- Critical: <20°C — asystole, cardiac arrest

### 1.4 The Fundamental Rule — "Not Dead Until Warm and Dead"
Severe hypothermia can cause complete cardiac arrest with absent pulse and absent ECG activity. However, the cardiac muscle is metabolically preserved by the cold (same principle as cardioplegia in cardiac surgery). If the patient can be rewarmed (ideally via ECMO or cardiopulmonary bypass), full cardiac recovery is possible — even after prolonged arrest. Death is not confirmed in a hypothermic patient until core temperature is ≥35°C and cardiac function has not returned.

## 2. ECG Presentation — Lead by Lead

### 2.1 Diagnostic Criteria by Temperature

| Temperature | ECG Finding | Clinical Significance |
|---|---|---|
| 35–37°C | Normal or sinus bradycardia | Borderline; monitor |
| 32–35°C (mild) | Sinus bradycardia, PR and QT prolongation, T-wave flattening | Active rewarming |
| 28–32°C (moderate) | Osborn waves, AF, junctional rhythms, further interval prolongation | STAT — active rewarming |
| <28°C (severe) | Wide-complex VT/VF risk, Osborn waves, all intervals prolonged | STAT — rewarming + arrhythmia management |
| <20°C | Asystole, flat line possible | ECMO if available; do not declare death |

### 2.3 Key Leads
**V4-V6** and **II, III, aVF** are the primary leads for Osborn wave detection. The Osborn wave is typically most prominent in lateral precordial leads in hypothermia, with significant inferior lead involvement as well.

This distribution (both inferior and lateral) distinguishes hypothermia Osborn waves from Brugada syndrome (right precordial V1-V3 only) and helps differentiate from early repolarization (similar but benign, no temperature correlation).

### 2.4 Beat-by-Beat Considerations
- Bradycardia is uniform — all beats slow, sinus P waves present (initially)
- PR, QRS, and QT all prolong uniformly
- Osborn waves appear on all beats — fixed, temperature-dependent
- AF in hypothermia: characteristically irregular, no distinct P waves — but the AF is typically benign and self-limited (reverts spontaneously with rewarming)
- Shivering artifact: irregular, rapid-frequency baseline tremor distinct from cardiac signal — must not be mistaken for AF or VF

## 3. Morphology Details

### 3.1 Osborn Wave (J Wave) — Detailed Morphology
- **Location:** J-point — the junction between the end of the QRS and the beginning of the ST segment
- **Polarity:** Positive (upright) in the same leads as the QRS R wave; appears as a "bump" or "hump" rising off the descending limb of the R wave before the ST segment
- **Shape:** Rounded dome, camel-hump, or notched appearance; smooth contour
- **Amplitude:** Roughly proportional to degree of hypothermia; 1–3 mm in mild-moderate, >5 mm in severe
- **Width:** 30–80 ms
- **Best leads:** V4-V6, II, III, aVF (lateral and inferior)
- **Distinguishing from early repolarization:** Early repolarization J-point notching is most prominent in V2-V4, is present at normothermia, and correlates with high vagal tone. Hypothermia Osborn waves are more diffuse, correlate with temperature, and occur in a broader distribution including inferior leads.

### 3.2 PR Interval
- Progressively prolongs with decreasing temperature
- Normal at 35°C → mild prolongation at 32°C → 280–320 ms at 28°C → first-degree and higher AV block in severe disease
- The PR prolongation reflects slowed AV nodal Ca2+-dependent conduction

### 3.3 QRS Duration
- Progressively widens
- <110 ms in mild hypothermia
- 110–130 ms in moderate hypothermia
- >130 ms in severe (<28°C)
- The Osborn wave may be confused with QRS widening (late QRS deflection) — careful analysis separates QRS end from Osborn onset

### 3.4 QT Interval
- QT is prolonged in hypothermia (opposite of hypercalcemia)
- This is the critical distinguishing fact: hypercalcemia shortens QT; hypothermia prolongs QT
- The QT prolongation in hypothermia is from prolonged phase 2 and 3 (slowed Ca2+ channel kinetics and K+ channel kinetics)
- QTc may appear spuriously normalized by the bradycardia correction — absolute QT measurement is informative

### 3.5 Shivering Artifact
- Irregular baseline undulation at 5–15 Hz from skeletal muscle activity
- Can mimic AF baseline fibrillatory activity
- KEY distinguishing feature: shivering artifact baseline is regular in frequency (muscle tremor) vs. AF baseline which is irregular in amplitude and frequency
- Shivering disappears in moderate-severe hypothermia (shivering mechanism suppressed below 30°C) — the patient stops shivering just when Osborn waves appear

### 3.6 Hypothermia-Associated AF
- Very common at core temperature 28–32°C
- Mechanism: slowed and heterogeneous atrial conduction → reentry → AF
- Special property: hypothermia AF is electrically stable and does NOT require cardioversion in most cases — it reverts spontaneously with rewarming
- Antiarrhythmics are relatively ineffective in hypothermia (drug kinetics also slowed)
- Aggressive rate control or cardioversion of hypothermia AF before rewarming is not recommended

## 4. Differential Diagnosis

### 4.1 Mimics

| Condition | Overlapping Feature | Distinguishing Feature |
|---|---|---|
| Hypercalcemia | Osborn waves (J waves) | Hypercalcemia: QT SHORTENED; hypothermia: QT PROLONGED; temperature normal in hypercalcemia |
| Early repolarization (benign) | J-point notching, Osborn-like | ER: V2-V4 predominant; normothermic; no PR/QRS widening; young healthy patient |
| Brugada syndrome | Right precordial J-point changes | Brugada: V1-V3 coved ST elevation; no Osborn in lateral/inferior; normothermic; no bradycardia |
| LBBB | QRS widening | LBBB: fixed morphology; normothermic; specific criteria |
| Hyperkalemia | QRS widening, bradycardia | Hyperkalemia: peaked T waves, no Osborn waves; K+ elevated |
| Drug-induced bradycardia | Sinus bradycardia, PR prolongation | Drug history; normothermic; no Osborn waves |

### 4.2 Conditions Precipitating Hypothermia
- **Alcohol intoxication:** Most common in ER; peripheral vasodilation → heat loss; also impairs shivering response
- **Trauma:** Exposure after injury; massive transfusion (unwarmed blood)
- **Sepsis/critical illness:** Impaired thermoregulation
- **Hypothyroidism:** Myxedema coma → profound hypothermia; ECG also shows low voltage, bradycardia
- **Drowning/submersion:** Cold water immersion
- **Psychiatric illness/homelessness:** Environmental exposure

### 4.3 Osborn Wave Differential Summary
| Osborn Wave Context | QT | Temperature | Other Features |
|---|---|---|---|
| Hypothermia | Prolonged | Low | PR/QRS widened; bradycardia |
| Hypercalcemia | Shortened | Normal | ST abbreviation |
| Early repolarization | Normal | Normal | Young, athletic; V2-V4; benign |
| Brugada syndrome | Normal/slightly long | Normal | V1-V3 coved ST elevation |

## 6. Reasoning Complexity Analysis

### 6.1 Osborn Wave Recognition
Moderate difficulty. The Osborn wave is visually distinctive once recognized — a "bump" at the J-point. The challenge is:
1. Not confusing it with QRS widening (terminal QRS slurring vs. true J-point Osborn)
2. Recognizing small Osborn waves (1–2 mm) in mild hypothermia
3. Not missing Osborn waves due to focus on the rate and intervals

### 6.2 Temperature-ECG Correlation
The key intellectual task: integrating ECG findings with body temperature to create a severity assessment. ECG is not used to diagnose hypothermia (clinical temperature measurement does that) but to assess cardiac severity and arrhythmia risk.

### 6.3 AF Management Nuance
The instruction "do not cardiovert hypothermia AF" is a non-obvious clinical rule. The reasoning system must flag hypothermia-associated AF as a specific variant that requires different management from other AF presentations.

### 6.4 Death Declaration Restriction
"Not dead until warm and dead" is a critical clinical rule with medico-legal implications. Any patient presenting with cardiac arrest and hypothermia should not be declared dead until rewarming has been attempted.

### 6.5 Osborn Wave Differential
Osborn waves have 4 distinct causes with different clinical significance (hypothermia, hypercalcemia, early repolarization, Brugada). The temperature + QT combination is the primary discriminator. The reasoning system must apply temperature and QT as joint discriminating variables.

### 6.6 Difficulty Score

| Dimension | Score (1–5) | Rationale |
|---|---|---|
| Pattern recognition | 2 | Osborn waves are visually distinctive; bradycardia obvious |
| Measurement precision | 3 | QT measurement at slow rates; Osborn amplitude measurement |
| Differential diagnosis | 3 | Osborn wave differential requires temperature + QT integration |
| Multi-condition interaction | 2 | Hypothermia + AF + shivering artifact to integrate |
| Severity stratification | 3 | Temperature-based stratification; AF management nuance |

### Primary Agent
**MR** — The Osborn/J-wave is a distinctive repolarization morphology feature at the J-point, directly within MR's domain.

### Cross-Domain Hints
- MR → CDS: `cross_domain_hint: "Osborn/J-waves detected at J-point in lateral/inferior leads — CDS should integrate with RRC rate and interval data for J-wave differential: hypothermia (bradycardia + prolonged intervals + lateral/inferior J-waves) vs Brugada (normal rate + V1-V2 coved) vs early repolarization"`
- RRC → CDS: `cross_domain_hint: "Bradycardia with prolonged QTc and widened QRS — if MR confirms Osborn waves in lateral/inferior distribution, hypothermia pattern; CDS should integrate and flag arrhythmia risk at severe hypothermia (<30°C)"`

### CDS Specific Role
CDS resolves the J-wave differential diagnosis for hypothermia by integrating Osborn wave characteristics from MR with rate and interval findings from RRC. The key discriminators: hypothermia produces J-waves in lateral and inferior leads with global bradycardia and prolonged PR/QRS/QTc, whereas Brugada produces a coved ST pattern restricted to V1-V2 with normal rate and intervals, and early repolarization shows J-point elevation with normal rate and intervals. CDS uses the distribution of J-waves (lateral + inferior = hypothermia), the rate (bradycardia = hypothermia), and the amplitude trend (larger J-waves = lower temperature) to confirm hypothermia and flag the risk of ventricular fibrillation in severe hypothermia (core temperature <30°C).

- Moderate hypothermia: 28–32°C; Osborn waves appear ~30°C
- Severe hypothermia: <28°C; VF risk high below 28°C

## 9. Edge Cases and Pitfalls

1. **Shivering artifact misread as AF or VF:** Shivering produces a high-frequency baseline tremor at 5–15 Hz. This can obscure P waves and mimic the chaotic baseline of AF or (rarely at extreme amplitude) VF. Key: the QRS complexes remain clearly identifiable and regular in shivering artifact. In VF, there are no discrete QRS complexes. In AF, the QRS complexes are irregularly irregular with no identifiable P waves but QRS morphology is preserved.

2. **Osborn wave amplitude not correlating linearly with temperature:** While Osborn wave amplitude roughly correlates with hypothermia severity, the relationship is not precise. Small Osborn waves can occur with moderate hypothermia, and large Osborn waves can occur at relatively higher temperatures in some patients. Temperature measurement (rectal, esophageal, or bladder) is required for definitive severity assessment.

3. **Osborn waves in normothermic patient:** Osborn waves in a patient without hypothermia should trigger evaluation for hypercalcemia (check ionized Ca2+). Early repolarization and Brugada syndrome are also in the differential. Context: a young athletic male with small V2-V4 J-point elevation and normal QT → early repolarization. An older patient with no prior history of J waves + QT shortening + lethargy → check calcium.

4. **Cardioversion of hypothermia AF causing VF:** Cardioversion of AF in severe hypothermia (<28°C) can precipitate VF. The cold myocardium is electrically unstable and vulnerable to arrhythmia induction. AF should not be cardioverted until temperature is above 30°C.

5. **Hypothermia masking ischemia:** The widespread QT prolongation, ST-T changes, and QRS widening of hypothermia can mask concurrent myocardial ischemia (e.g., STEMI). If there is clinical concern for ischemia in a rewarmed or mildly hypothermic patient, serial ECGs during rewarming are essential. ST elevation appearing or worsening with rewarming suggests primary ischemic pathology.

6. **Drug kinetics in hypothermia:** Antiarrhythmic drugs (amiodarone, lidocaine, procainamide) have reduced efficacy and unpredictable kinetics in hypothermia — drug metabolism is slowed proportionally to temperature. Arrhythmias in severe hypothermia are best treated with rewarming, not pharmacologic agents. Defibrillation attempts before temperature reaches 28–30°C have low success rates; continue CPR and rewarm.

7. **ECMO in hypothermic arrest:** Extracorporeal membrane oxygenation (ECMO) or cardiopulmonary bypass provides simultaneous circulatory support and active core rewarming. This is the gold standard for rewarming in cardiac arrest from hypothermia. Survival after hypothermic cardiac arrest with ECMO rewarming is documented even after >60 minutes of arrest.
