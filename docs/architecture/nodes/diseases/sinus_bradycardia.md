## 1. Pathophysiology Root Cause

### 1.1 What Goes Wrong (Anatomy/Physiology)

The SA node fires at a rate below 60 bpm. The mechanism may be physiologic (appropriate slowing) or pathologic (impaired automaticity). Common causes by category:

- **Increased vagal tone**: athletes (physiologic), vasovagal episodes, inferior STEMI (Bezold-Jarisch reflex via RCA → SA nodal artery), carotid sinus hypersensitivity, vomiting, Valsalva, sleep
- **Medications**: beta-blockers (reduce phase-4 depolarization slope), non-dihydropyridine calcium channel blockers (verapamil, diltiazem), digoxin (increased vagal tone), amiodarone, ivabradine, lithium, cholinergic agents
- **Structural/intrinsic SA node disease**: sick sinus syndrome (SSS) — fibrosis of the SA node from aging, prior MI, infiltrative disease (amyloid, sarcoid), surgical damage
- **Metabolic/systemic**: hypothyroidism (reduces adrenergic sensitivity and SA node automaticity), hypothermia (slows all pacemaker activity), hyperkalemia (depresses automaticity), increased intracranial pressure (Cushing reflex)
- **Ischemia**: RCA occlusion → SA node ischemia (SA nodal artery from RCA in 60%, LCX in 40%)

The critical clinical distinction: benign sinus bradycardia (athlete, sleeping, vagal) vs. pathologic sinus bradycardia requiring intervention.

### 1.2 Electrical Consequence

Slowed SA node phase-4 diastolic depolarization increases the time between action potentials. Once the SA node fires, the impulse propagates through the atria, AV node, His-Purkinje, and ventricles via the identical pathway as normal sinus rhythm. Therefore:
- P-wave morphology and axis are preserved (sinus origin confirmed)
- PR interval normal or slightly prolonged (increased vagal tone affects AV node too)
- QRS morphology unchanged
- The sole ECG change is rate: RR interval >1000 ms

### 1.3 Why It Appears on ECG

Identical conduction pathway as NSR — only rate differs. A rate <60 bpm means RR interval >1000 ms. If P-waves are present with normal sinus morphology and the rate is slow, the diagnosis is sinus bradycardia until proven otherwise. The challenge is distinguishing sinus bradycardia with a dropped beat (2nd degree AV block) from sinus bradycardia alone, and from junctional escape rhythm.

## 2. ECG Presentation — Lead by Lead

### 2.1 Diagnostic Criteria (2025 AHA/ESC Guidelines)

1. Rate <60 bpm (RR interval >1000 ms)
2. P-wave present before every QRS with constant PR interval
3. P-wave morphology: upright in I, II, aVF; negative in aVR (confirms sinus origin)
4. P-wave axis: 0°–75°
5. PR interval: 120–200 ms (may be at upper end if co-existing first-degree AV block)
6. QRS: narrow (<120 ms) unless preexisting BBB or aberrancy
7. Every P followed by QRS (1:1 conduction — rules out high-degree AV block)

### 2.2 Lead-by-Lead Manifestation

| Lead | Expected Finding | Why (Vector Explanation) | Sensitivity |
|------|-----------------|--------------------------|-------------|
| I | Upright P (small), normal QRS, upright T | Sinus P has leftward component; lead I detects it | Moderate |
| II | Tall upright P (most diagnostic), normal QRS, upright T | Lead II aligned with sinus P-axis — largest P amplitude | High |
| III | Upright or biphasic P; variable QRS morphology | Partially aligned with P-axis | Moderate |
| aVR | Inverted P, inverted QRS, inverted T | Opposes all sinus vectors — P must be negative here | High |
| aVL | Small or biphasic P; variable QRS | Perpendicular to typical sinus P-axis | Low–Moderate |
| aVF | Upright P, normal QRS, upright T | Inferior lead; aligned with inferior P-wave component | High |
| V1 | Biphasic P (positive then negative terminal); narrow rS or QS | Atrial depolarization spreads toward then away from V1 | Moderate |
| V2 | Upright P; rS pattern | P visible but less diagnostic than limb leads | Moderate |
| V3–V6 | Upright P; normal QRS morphology | P present but R:S ratio increases toward V5–V6 | Low |

### 2.3 Key Leads (Most Diagnostic)

- **Lead II**: Single best lead — large upright P confirms sinus origin; rate easily measured by PP interval
- **aVR**: P must be negative — if upright, the rhythm is not sinus (junctional, retrograde, ectopic atrial)
- **V1**: Biphasic P morphology helps confirm sinus P; also useful for detecting coexisting conduction disease (RBBB, incomplete RBBB)

### 2.4 Beat-by-Beat Considerations

- RR intervals are regular in simple sinus bradycardia; slight variation is normal due to autonomic modulation
- In sick sinus syndrome, RR intervals may be irregular — bradycardia alternating with tachycardia (tachy-brady syndrome)
- Every P must be followed by a QRS; if any P fails to conduct, 2nd degree AV block must be diagnosed in addition to or instead of sinus bradycardia
- Junctional escape beats may emerge if the sinus rate slows enough (<40 bpm) — these lack preceding P-waves or have retrograde inverted P-waves immediately before or after the QRS

## 3. Morphology Details (What the Agent Must See)

### 3.1 P-wave Changes

- Morphology identical to the patient's own NSR P-waves — this is the critical confirmatory feature
- Amplitude and duration unchanged from NSR
- At very slow rates (<40 bpm), junctional or ventricular escape may occur — these escape beats may have absent or retrograde P-waves, indicating the sinus is not reliably capturing the rhythm
- In sick sinus syndrome, P-wave amplitude may decrease over time (SA node fibrosis) or disappear altogether in advanced disease

### 3.2 PR Interval Changes

- Normal range: 120–200 ms
- May be at the upper end (180–200 ms) when co-existing first-degree AV block is present (increased vagal tone affects both SA and AV nodes simultaneously — classic beta-blocker or digoxin effect)
- Constant PR beat to beat (variable PR = consider 2nd degree AV block or wandering atrial pacemaker)
- Short PR (<120 ms) with sinus bradycardia: consider pre-excitation (WPW) with coincidental slow rate

### 3.3 QRS Complex Changes

- Narrow (<120 ms) in absence of preexisting BBB
- Wide QRS may indicate: preexisting RBBB or LBBB; rate-related aberrancy (rare at slow rates); or a junctional/ventricular escape beat (not true sinus bradycardia)
- Escape beats have different morphology from sinus-conducted beats — essential to distinguish

### 3.4 ST Segment Changes

- Normal in benign sinus bradycardia
- ST elevation in inferior leads (II, III, aVF) + sinus bradycardia → RCA occlusion (inferior STEMI) driving vagal bradycardia — STAT
- ST depression during bradycardia: may reflect subendocardial ischemia, reciprocal changes, or baseline abnormality
- Bradycardia in the context of any ST changes demands immediate coronary evaluation

### 3.5 T-wave Changes

- Normal in benign sinus bradycardia
- Tall, peaked T-waves + bradycardia: consider hyperkalemia (which also depresses SA node)
- Hypothermia: Osborn J-waves (positive deflection at QRS-ST junction) + bradycardia; also prolongs QT
- T-wave inversion in inferior leads + bradycardia: inferior STEMI or posterior MI

### 3.6 QT/QTc Changes

- QT interval lengthens at slower rates (expected — longer RR interval allows longer repolarization)
- QTc (rate-corrected) should remain 350–460 ms
- Prolonged QTc in the context of bradycardia increases Torsades de Pointes risk — clinically important combination
- Hypothyroidism causes bradycardia + prolonged QTc (both due to reduced metabolic rate)

### 3.7 Other Features

- **Escape rhythms**: If sinus rate drops below junctional escape rate (~40–60 bpm), junctional escape beats may appear. If sinus rate drops below ventricular escape rate (~20–40 bpm), wide ventricular escape beats appear. These are protective, not pathological.
- **Pause**: A sudden pause in sinus bradycardia (sinus arrest or SA exit block) may precede the onset of a junctional or ventricular escape rhythm
- **Tachy-brady syndrome**: In sick sinus syndrome, the bradycardia alternates with paroxysmal SVT or AF. The transition from tachycardia to bradycardia often reveals prolonged pauses (sinus node not recovering promptly)

## 4. Differential Diagnosis

### 4.1 Mimics

| Mimic Condition | Shared Features | Distinguishing Features |
|----------------|----------------|------------------------|
| Junctional rhythm | Slow rate (40–60 bpm); narrow QRS | No sinus P-wave (or retrograde P negative in II/III/aVF immediately before or after QRS); P-axis deviated |
| 2:1 AV block | Slow ventricular rate; sinus P-waves visible | Every other P-wave is NOT followed by QRS; atrial rate normal (~60–100 bpm); ventricular rate exactly half atrial |
| Complete (3rd degree) AV block | Slow ventricular rate; P-waves present | P-waves and QRS complexes completely dissociated (no fixed PR interval); ventricular rate independent of atrial rate |
| Sinus arrest / SA exit block | Pause on ECG | PP intervals during the pause are exact multiples of basic sinus PP (SA exit block Type II); complete absence of P-waves during pause |
| Slow AF | Slow irregular ventricular rate | No discrete P-waves; irregularly irregular baseline; no constant PR |
| Wandering atrial pacemaker | Slow or normal rate; variable P-wave morphology | P-wave morphology changes beat to beat (≥3 distinct P morphologies); PR interval varies |

### 4.2 Coexisting Conditions

- **Inferior STEMI**: RCA occlusion → SA nodal ischemia → sinus bradycardia + inferior ST elevation. The bradycardia may be the first sign of an inferior STEMI; always examine inferior leads when bradycardia is found.
- **Complete AV block in inferior STEMI**: Sinus bradycardia progressing to 1st, 2nd, and 3rd degree AV block is a recognized sequence in inferior STEMI due to RCA occlusion of both SA and AV nodal arteries.
- **Hyperkalemia**: May cause sinus bradycardia, P-wave widening/disappearance, wide QRS, and peaked T-waves — potentially fatal combination.
- **Hypothyroidism**: Bradycardia + low voltage + prolonged QT + flat T-waves + effusion pattern (electrical alternans).
- **Beta-blocker/CCB toxicity**: Sinus bradycardia + hypotension + PR prolongation — may require glucagon, calcium, or high-dose insulin therapy.

## 5. STAT Classification

| Priority | Criteria |
|----------|----------|
| STAT RED — Immediate | Sinus bradycardia + hemodynamic instability (systolic BP <90, altered mentation, syncope, shock) |
| STAT RED — Immediate | Sinus bradycardia + inferior ST elevation (RCA occlusion until proven otherwise) |
| STAT RED — Immediate | Sinus bradycardia + complete AV block (escape rhythm <40 bpm, dissociated P and QRS) |
| STAT RED — Immediate | Drug toxicity (beta-blocker, CCB, digoxin) with bradycardia + hemodynamic compromise |
| STAT ORANGE — Urgent | Symptomatic bradycardia (presyncope, near-syncope, chest pain) without hemodynamic instability |
| STAT ORANGE — Urgent | Sinus bradycardia + new LBBB or other significant conduction change |
| STAT YELLOW — Time-sensitive | Asymptomatic bradycardia in a patient without athletic conditioning and no known cause |
| NOT STAT | Asymptomatic sinus bradycardia in a well-conditioned athlete or sleeping patient |
| NOT STAT | Known beta-blocker therapy producing mild bradycardia (rate >40 bpm) without symptoms |

## 6. Reasoning Complexity Analysis (Feeds Into Node 2.1)

NOTE: Does NOT pre-assign agents. Documents reasoning complexity for Node 2.1 to analyze.

### 6.1 Reasoning Domains Required

- Rate calculation and confirmation <60 bpm
- P-wave presence and morphology verification (is every QRS preceded by a sinus P?)
- PR interval constancy (PR block co-diagnosis?)
- Escape beat recognition and differentiation from sinus-conducted beats
- Clinical context integration: athlete vs. patient, symptoms, medications
- Coexisting ST changes: inferior STEMI driving bradycardia?
- Drug toxicity pattern recognition

### 6.2 Feature Dependencies (ESSENTIAL vs SUPPORTING vs EXCLUSION)

**ESSENTIAL (required for diagnosis)**:
- Rate <60 bpm (per beat)
- Sinus P-wave present before every QRS (upright in II, inverted in aVR)
- Constant PR interval (per beat and aggregate)

**SUPPORTING (strengthen confidence)**:
- Clinical context: athlete, medications, vagal trigger
- Normal QRS width
- Absence of AV dissociation
- No ST changes

**EXCLUSION (rule out if present)**:
- AV dissociation → rules out simple sinus bradycardia, adds complete AV block
- Absent P-waves → rules out sinus bradycardia (junctional or AF with slow rate)
- Non-sinus P morphology (negative in II) → rules out sinus mechanism
- Dropped beats (P without QRS) → adds 2nd degree AV block diagnosis

### 6.3 Cross-Condition Interactions (Masking, Dependencies, Dangerous Combinations)

- **Inferior STEMI + bradycardia**: The bradycardia may distract from ST changes or vice versa. Both must be identified simultaneously. RCA-mediated bradycardia is often the first ECG sign of inferior STEMI — examining ST changes is mandatory in all bradycardia.
- **Complete AV block masquerading as sinus bradycardia**: If the junctional escape rate happens to be regular and slow (~40–50 bpm), and retrograde P-waves occur at a fixed interval after QRS, it may superficially resemble sinus bradycardia. PP interval measurement is critical.
- **Hyperkalemia masking P-waves**: Progressive hyperkalemia first widens then obliterates P-waves while causing bradycardia. A "sinoventricular rhythm" (broad QRS without P-waves at 50–60 bpm) may be mistaken for junctional rhythm.
- **Hypothermia + bradycardia + Osborn waves**: Hypothermia produces sinus bradycardia, QT prolongation, J-waves, and shivering artifact — the combination is pathognomonic but requires careful signal analysis.

### 6.4 Reasoning Chain Sketch

**Minimum chain:**
```
1. Confirm rate <60 bpm
2. Identify P-wave before every QRS with sinus morphology (upright II, inverted aVR)
3. Confirm constant PR ≥120 ms
4. Diagnose: Sinus Bradycardia at [rate] bpm
```

**Full chain:**
```
1. Confirm rate <60 bpm (measure ≥3 RR intervals)
2. P-wave present before every QRS?
   - Yes, with sinus morphology → sinus mechanism confirmed
   - No P before some QRS → check for AV block
   - No P or retrograde P → junctional or escape rhythm
3. PR interval: constant? Length?
   - Variable PR → 2nd degree AV block? Wandering pacemaker?
   - PR >200 ms → 1st degree AV block co-diagnosis
4. QRS width: narrow or wide?
   - Wide → preexisting BBB? Escape beat?
5. Inspect inferior leads for ST elevation (II, III, aVF) → RCA occlusion?
6. Inspect all leads for ST/T changes → ischemia, hyperkalemia, hypothyroidism?
7. Identify escape beats if present (different morphology, no preceding P)
8. Clinical context: athlete? medications? symptomatic?
9. Final diagnosis: Sinus Bradycardia at [rate] bpm + [any co-diagnoses]
10. STAT flag if: hemodynamic instability, inferior STEMI, complete AV block, drug toxicity
```

### 6.5 Confidence Anchors

- **HIGH confidence**: Clear sinus P-waves, rate 45–59 bpm, constant PR, known athlete or beta-blocker use, no symptoms, no ST changes
- **LOWER confidence**: Rate <40 bpm (escape beats likely), atypical P morphology, new onset without explanation, presence of ST changes in inferior leads
- **Pathognomonic combination**: Sinus bradycardia + inferior ST elevation + normal or slightly prolonged PR → RCA occlusion with vagal bradycardia (Bezold-Jarisch reflex)
- **Possible**: Rate 50–59 bpm, sinus P confirmed, no symptoms, no structural concern
- **Probable**: Rate 40–49 bpm, confirmed sinus mechanism, mild symptoms, no ST changes
- **Definite**: Rate <40 bpm + sinus P confirmed → requires explanation and monitoring regardless of symptoms

| Dimension | Score (1–5) | Notes |
|-----------|-------------|-------|
| Signal clarity needed | 2/5 | P-waves visible at slow rates; identification is easier than at high rates |
| Number of leads required | 2/5 | Lead II + aVR sufficient for basic diagnosis; II/III/aVF needed to exclude inferior STEMI |
| Cross-domain reasoning | 2/5 | Rate and P-wave detection straightforward; co-diagnoses add complexity |

CDS performs standard integration. ## 9. Edge Cases and Pitfalls

- **Inferior STEMI presenting as sinus bradycardia**: The Bezold-Jarisch reflex (vagal surge from right ventricular/inferior myocardial ischemia) causes profound sinus bradycardia before ST elevation is obvious. Always check inferior leads in any bradycardia. Atropine is appropriate acutely; definitive therapy is reperfusion.
- **Complete AV block misidentified as sinus bradycardia**: If a junctional escape rhythm fires at 50 bpm with a P-wave visible nearby (even retrograde), it may superficially mimic sinus bradycardia. Key discriminator: is the PR interval truly constant across all beats, and is the P always BEFORE (not after) the QRS?
- **Hyperkalemia obliterating P-waves**: As K+ rises, P-wave amplitude decreases. At K+ ~6.5–7 mEq/L, P-waves may disappear entirely, leaving only QRS complexes at a slow rate. This "sinoventricular rhythm" looks like junctional or sinus bradycardia without P-waves. Context (renal failure, peaked T-waves, wide QRS) is critical.
- **Rate <40 bpm**: At very slow sinus rates, junctional or ventricular escape beats may appear between sinus beats. Misidentifying an escape beat as a PAC or PVC changes the clinical interpretation entirely.
- **Treating sinus bradycardia without looking for the cause**: Atropine addresses the rate but not the cause. Atropine in inferior STEMI is a temporizing measure — the patient needs cath lab activation, not just rate management.
- **Athlete misidentified as sick sinus**: Highly trained athletes commonly have resting heart rates of 35–50 bpm with near-complete vagal dominance. Long PP pauses during sleep (>2.5 s) are documented in athletes and do not require intervention. Clinical context is mandatory.
- **Drug toxicity**: Beta-blocker and CCB overdose causing severe bradycardia require specific antidotes (glucagon, calcium gluconate, high-dose insulin euglycaemic therapy, lipid emulsion for lipophilic drugs). Atropine alone is often insufficient.
- **Tachy-brady syndrome**: In sick sinus syndrome, the ECG may capture only the bradycardic phase. The history of palpitations or documented prior tachyarrhythmia + bradycardia is essential for SSS diagnosis. A 12-lead snapshot showing only bradycardia may underestimate the diagnosis.
