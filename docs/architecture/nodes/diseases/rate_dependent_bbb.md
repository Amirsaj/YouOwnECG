## 1. Pathophysiology Root Cause

### 1.1 Anatomic Substrate

The bundle branches have intrinsic refractory periods — after each depolarization, they require a recovery period before they can conduct again. If the next impulse arrives before the bundle branch has fully recovered, conduction through that branch fails and the impulse must travel cell-to-cell through ventricular myocardium instead — producing a wide, aberrant QRS with bundle branch block morphology. This phenomenon is rate-dependent: it appears or disappears based on the cycle length (heart rate) determining whether the next impulse arrives within or outside the refractory window.

### 1.2 Phase 3 Block (Tachycardia-Dependent Aberrancy)

Phase 3 of the cardiac action potential is the rapid repolarization phase — still partially refractory. Phase 3 block occurs when a premature or tachycardic impulse reaches the bundle branch during phase 3, while the branch is still refractory from the previous depolarization. As heart rate increases, cycle lengths shorten, and successive impulses arrive during the still-refractory phase 3 — producing sustained rate-dependent BBB. Phase 3 block is the most common form of aberrant conduction.

The right bundle branch has a longer refractory period than the left bundle branch. Therefore, phase 3 block most commonly produces RBBB morphology at elevated heart rates (rate-dependent RBBB). This is clinically significant: SVT with rate-dependent RBBB is the most common cause of a regular wide-complex tachycardia that is NOT VT and NOT antidromic AVRT.

### 1.3 Phase 4 Block (Bradycardia-Dependent Aberrancy)

Phase 4 of the action potential is the resting diastolic phase. In normal Purkinje tissue, the resting membrane potential is stable. In diseased Purkinje tissue, spontaneous diastolic depolarization (phase 4 automaticity) can cause the membrane potential to drift toward threshold during prolonged diastole. When the next impulse arrives after a long pause, the diseased bundle branch is partially depolarized (closer to threshold), fast sodium channels are partially inactivated, and conduction fails — producing BBB at SLOW rates. Phase 4 block implies more significant underlying conduction disease than phase 3 block and is far less common.

### 1.4 The Ashman Phenomenon

Named after Ashman (1947), this describes aberrant conduction specifically in the context of a long-short cycle sequence. The physiological basis: the refractory period of the bundle branch is proportional to the preceding cycle length (longer preceding cycle = longer refractory period). When a long cycle is followed by a short cycle (e.g., in AFib due to random cycle variation), the short-cycle beat arrives during the prolonged refractory period set by the long cycle — producing aberrant (usually RBBB) conduction. The Ashman phenomenon is NOT a sign of VT in AFib — it is physiologic aberrancy.

## 2. ECG Presentation — Lead by Lead

### 2.1 Diagnostic Criteria (2025 AHA/ESC Guidelines)

Rate-dependent BBB (aberrant conduction) is characterized by:
1. Wide QRS beats (≥120ms) with RBBB or LBBB morphology that appear ONLY at specific heart rates or after specific cycle length patterns
2. Narrow QRS beats present at other rates (or after different cycle lengths)
3. The wide beats must have a preceding P-wave consistent with sinus or supraventricular origin (distinguishing from PVC)
4. The transition from narrow to wide QRS must correlate with a rate threshold or cycle length change (not random occurrence)
5. Wide beats during tachycardia: qualify as SVT with aberrant conduction if: preceding P-wave present, narrow QRS transitions to wide at a specific rate, classic BBB morphology

### 2.2 Lead-by-Lead Manifestation

| Lead | Expected Finding | Why | Sensitivity |
|------|-----------------|-----|-------------|
| I | Terminal S-wave on aberrant beats (RBBB morphology) OR absent septal q + broad R (LBBB morphology) | Depends on which bundle branch is refractory | High (depends on BBB type) |
| II | Normal P-wave before each wide beat (critical finding) | Confirms supraventricular origin of aberrant beats | Very high |
| III | Morphology mirrors RBBB or LBBB pattern in wide beats | Same as BBB morphology rules | Moderate |
| aVR | Terminal R in RBBB morphology; QS in LBBB morphology | Standard BBB morphology rules | Moderate |
| aVL | qR pattern with LBBB or wide slurred R | Left lateral BBB morphology | Moderate |
| aVF | Variable; follows BBB morphologic rules | Inferior lead | Low (non-specific) |
| V1 | rSR' in wide beats (RBBB aberrancy — most common) | Right bundle refractory → RBBB aberrancy manifests in V1 | Very high |
| V2 | rsR' or rSR' in RBBB aberrancy | Confirms right precordial BBB pattern | High |
| V3 | Transitional | Transition zone | Low |
| V4 | Transitional | Mid-precordial | Low |
| V5 | Terminal S-wave in RBBB aberrancy | Left precordial: lateral terminal S from RBBB | High |
| V6 | Terminal S-wave in RBBB aberrancy; absent in LBBB aberrancy | Left precordial: key terminal S marker for RBBB | Very high |
| Rhythm strip (any lead) | Rate threshold where wide QRS appears/disappears; long-short sequence (Ashman) | Key diagnostic feature: rate dependence visible across multiple beats | Very high |

### 2.3 Key Leads

- **V1**: RBBB aberrancy produces rSR' in V1 — same morphology as fixed RBBB but rate-dependent
- **II (long strip)**: Best lead for confirming P-wave before each aberrant beat and observing rate-cycle pattern
- **V6**: Terminal S-wave in RBBB aberrancy — confirms RBBB morphology
- **V1 morphology in tachycardia**: rSR' with initial r ≥ terminal R' favors SVT with aberrancy over VT (Josephson's criteria); monophasic R or QR favors VT

### 2.4 Beat-by-Beat Considerations

This is the most temporally complex analysis in conduction disorders. The critical observations are:

**Rate-dependent onset/offset**: Identify the rate at which QRS widens. Wide QRS appears above a threshold rate (phase 3) or below a threshold rate (phase 4). This requires analyzing multiple consecutive beats across a range of rates.

**Ashman sequence identification**: Identify a long-short RR cycle pair preceding the wide QRS beat. The longer the preceding cycle, the more likely the next beat is aberrant if it arrives early.

**P-wave mapping**: In tachycardia, P-waves may be buried in preceding T-waves. Use multiple leads; V1 often shows P-waves as notches in T-waves best. Every aberrant beat MUST have an identifiable supraventricular P-wave to differentiate from PVC.

**Compensatory pause**: After an aberrant beat in sinus rhythm, the pause is INCOMPLETE (sinus node resets) — different from a PVC which has a COMPLETE compensatory pause. This is a key differentiator.

## 3. Morphology Details

### 3.1 QRS Width in Aberrant Beats

Typically 120–150ms, same as fixed BBB. QRS >160ms during aberrancy is unusual and should raise concern for additional factors (drug effect, hyperkalemia adding to the conduction delay).

### 3.2 Morphology Criteria Favoring SVT with Aberrancy vs. VT

(Wellens criteria and Josephson modifications for wide-complex tachycardia):

**Favors SVT with RBBB aberrancy:**
- rSR' in V1 with initial r smaller than terminal R' (triphasic V1)
- QRS duration <140ms
- Normal QRS axis or only mild deviation
- RS complexes present in precordial leads
- P-waves identifiable before each QRS

**Favors VT:**
- Monophasic R or biphasic QR in V1
- QS or rS in V6 during tachycardia
- QRS >160ms
- Extreme axis deviation (northwest axis: negative in I and aVF)
- AV dissociation (P-waves march through independently of QRS)
- Fusion beats (QRS morphology intermediate between sinus and tachycardia beats)
- Capture beats (a narrow QRS during tachycardia when a sinus impulse briefly captures the ventricle)
- Concordance: all precordial leads QRS deflections in same direction (all positive or all negative)

### 3.3 Ashman Beat Morphology

Ashman beats in AFib have RBBB morphology in ~80% of cases (right bundle has longer refractory period). Identify: long RR cycle followed by shorter RR cycle → aberrant beat. Critical: multiple consecutive aberrant beats in AFib from Ashman phenomenon can mimic a run of wide-complex tachycardia superimposed on AFib.

### 3.4 P-Wave Relationship

In rate-dependent BBB: P-wave precedes each wide QRS with a consistent, normal PR interval (may be minimally shortened if rate is fast). The P-wave morphology matches sinus (upright in II, aVF). This is the single most important feature distinguishing aberrant conduction from PVC.

### 3.5 QRS Onset

Aberrant beats have the SAME QRS onset timing relative to the P-wave as the narrow beats (same PR interval). PVCs have no preceding P-wave and may have a variable relationship to any preceding P-wave that is present by coincidence.

### 3.6 Transition to Wide QRS

In phase 3 block, the transition from narrow to wide is usually abrupt at a specific cycle length threshold (critical rate). In some patients, there is a "warm-up" period of borderline QRS widening just before the full BBB morphology appears.

### 3.7 Rate at Which Aberrancy Resolves (Hysteresis)

Rate-dependent BBB often shows hysteresis: aberrancy appears at a higher rate and resolves at a lower rate (not the same threshold in both directions). This is because the diseased bundle branch has a longer refractory period once it has been used at fast rates (accommodation phenomenon). Clinically: BBB may persist for several beats after rate slows below the threshold of onset.

## 4. Differential Diagnosis

### 4.1 Mimics

| Mimic | How to Distinguish |
|-------|--------------------|
| Premature Ventricular Contraction (PVC) | PVC: no preceding P-wave, complete compensatory pause, no rate-dependent pattern; aberrancy: preceding P-wave, incomplete compensatory pause, rate-dependent |
| Ventricular Tachycardia (sustained) | VT: AV dissociation, fusion/capture beats, specific V1/V6 morphology criteria; SVT with aberrancy: P-waves tracking QRS, no AV dissociation |
| Fixed BBB (permanent) | Fixed BBB: wide QRS at ALL rates; rate-dependent: narrow at some rates |
| Antidromic AVRT (WPW) | Antidromic AVRT: short PR in sinus rhythm, delta wave during sinus, accessory pathway identified; aberrancy: no delta wave, normal PR in sinus |
| Pre-excited AFib | Irregular + very fast wide complex; rate-dependent RBBB in AFib is Ashman (RBBB morphology, rate-related, long-short); pre-excited AFib: shortest RR <250ms, delta waves vary beat-to-beat |
| Accelerated idioventricular rhythm | Wide complex, no P-wave, rate 60–100 bpm; occurs at slow-normal rates without preceding P-waves |

### 4.2 Coexisting Conditions

- Existing first-degree AV block + rate-dependent RBBB: together suggest bundle branch system disease
- AFib: Ashman phenomenon is extremely common; recognition prevents unnecessary VT diagnosis
- SVT (AVNRT, AT): rate-dependent RBBB commonly develops at tachycardia onset — the most clinically important context
- Electrolyte abnormalities: hyperkalemia lowers rate threshold for aberrancy (bundle branches become more prone to block at lower rates)

## 5. STAT Classification

| Priority | Criteria |
|----------|---------|
| NOT STAT — Isolated finding | Isolated rate-dependent RBBB during mild tachycardia, no symptoms |
| HIGH — Urgent evaluation | Rate-dependent LBBB during tachycardia (LBBB aberrancy is less common; suggests more significant left-sided disease) |
| HIGH — Cannot exclude VT | Wide complex tachycardia that does NOT meet clear SVT-with-aberrancy criteria — treat as VT |
| STAT — Treat as VT | Any doubt about VT vs. aberrancy: TREAT AS VT |
| ROUTINE | Documented prior history of rate-dependent RBBB, same pattern, no change |

**Key clinical safety rule:** When uncertain whether a wide-complex tachycardia is VT or SVT with aberrancy — TREAT AS VT. Treating VT as SVT (giving verapamil for presumed SVT in actual VT) can cause hemodynamic collapse. The reverse error (treating SVT as VT) is safe.

## 6. Reasoning Complexity Analysis

### 6.1 Reasoning Domains Required

- Temporal pattern analysis: observing QRS width change across a rate or cycle length continuum (multi-beat analysis)
- P-wave mapping: identifying P-waves before each wide QRS across multiple leads including during tachycardia
- Morphology analysis: applying Wellens/Josephson VT vs. aberrancy criteria to V1 and V6
- Compensatory pause measurement: complete vs. incomplete pause after aberrant beat
- Ashman cycle detection: long-short RR pair identification in AFib context
- Risk stratification: VT-vs-aberrancy decision with clear safety bias toward VT

### 6.2 Feature Dependencies

```
Wide QRS beat detected:
    → Preceding P-wave present with normal PR? → Favors aberrancy
    → No preceding P-wave → Favors PVC/VT
    → Does QRS width vary with rate? → Yes → rate-dependent BBB confirmed
    → Long-short cycle preceding wide beat in AFib? → Ashman phenomenon
    → In sustained tachycardia: apply full VT vs. SVT criteria:
        V1: rSR' triphasic? → SVT-aberrancy
        V6: terminal S? → SVT-aberrancy
        AV dissociation? → VT
        QRS >160ms? → VT more likely
        Fusion/capture beats? → VT
    → Cannot definitively classify? → Treat as VT
```

### 6.3 Cross-Condition Interactions

- AFib + Ashman: must not misclassify Ashman beats as VT runs superimposed on AFib
- WPW: must confirm delta waves absent before attributing wide QRS during tachycardia to aberrancy (not antidromic AVRT)
- Hyperkalemia: can lower the rate threshold for aberrancy — unexpected aberrancy should trigger potassium check
- TCA or Na-channel blockers: pharmacologic QRS widening does not produce rate-dependent pattern — constant regardless of rate

### 6.4 Reasoning Chain Sketch

```
1. Identify wide QRS beat (≥120ms)
2. Check for preceding P-wave in multiple leads (II, V1, V5)
   → P-wave present with normal PR → proceed to aberrancy analysis
   → No P-wave → PVC or VT
3. Assess rate/cycle length context:
   → Rate increased before wide beat appeared? → Phase 3 block
   → Rate decreased before wide beat appeared? → Phase 4 block
   → Long cycle before short cycle in AFib? → Ashman
4. Measure wide QRS duration → <160ms favors aberrancy
5. Evaluate V1 morphology: rSR' triphasic? R < R'?
6. Evaluate V6: terminal S present? (RBBB morphology)
7. Check for AV dissociation, fusion beats, capture beats (VT indicators)
8. If persistent tachycardia: apply full Brugada/Wellens algorithm
9. Assign confidence: high (clear SVT+P-waves), moderate, or "treat as VT"
10. Check compensatory pause after aberrant beat if in sinus context
```

### 6.5 Confidence Anchors

- High confidence: narrow QRS in same recording at slower rate + wide QRS at faster rate + P-wave before each beat = rate-dependent BBB with high confidence
- High confidence: Ashman beat in AFib with RBBB morphology after long-short sequence = physiologic aberrancy
- Moderate confidence: triphasic rSR' V1, terminal S V6, QRS <140ms in tachycardia = SVT with aberrancy (VT not excluded)
- Low confidence (require VT treatment): AV dissociation present, QRS >160ms, extreme axis deviation, no clear P-waves — even if some aberrancy features present

| Dimension | Score (1–5) | Notes |
|-----------|-------------|-------|
| Signal clarity needed | 3 | P-wave identification in tachycardia requires good signal quality; buried P-waves in T-waves are challenging |
| Number of leads required | 3 | V1, V6, II (long strip), multiple leads for P-wave search — full 12-lead or at least 6 leads needed |
| Cross-domain reasoning | 3 | Must combine rate pattern analysis, P-wave tracking, morphology criteria, and clinical safety logic |

## 6A. Agent Assignment

**Architecture:** Option E — 3 Parallel Phase 1 Specialists + 1 Sequential CDS (Node 2.1 Gate Passed 2026-03-27)

| Agent | Role for Rate-Dependent Bundle Branch Block | Key Features Used |
|-------|--------------------------|-------------------|
| **RRC** (Rhythm/Rate/Conduction) | Primary | Heart rate at which BBB appears and disappears (critical cycle length threshold — Ashman phenomenon); QRS duration measurement per beat; confirms normal QRS duration at slower rates and wide QRS at faster rates; preceding P-waves with consistent PR intervals (confirms supraventricular origin, not PVC) |
| **IT** (Ischemia/Territory) | Not involved | No ischemia-specific features; rate-dependent BBB is a functional conduction phenomenon |
| **MR** (Morphology/Repolarization) | Supporting | BBB morphology when present: RBBB pattern (rSR' in V1, wide S in I/V6) or LBBB pattern (broad R in I/V5-V6, QS in V1-V3); confirms specific BBB type during aberrant conduction; confirms return to normal morphology at slower rates |
| **CDS** (Cross-Domain Synthesis) | Required — resolves rate-dependent vs fixed BBB and ischemia assessment validity | Integrates RRC rate/cycle length context with MR morphology to confirm BBB is rate-dependent (not fixed); determines whether ischemia assessment is valid (at normal rates without BBB: standard IT criteria apply; during BBB beats: appropriate suppression or Sgarbossa application based on BBB type) |

### Primary Agent
**RRC** — rate-dependent BBB is a conduction phenomenon defined by heart rate context; the rate threshold at which aberration appears and the per-beat QRS duration measurement are the defining features, both in the RRC domain.

### Cross-Domain Hints
- RRC emits: `cross_domain_hint: "Variable QRS width correlated with rate change detected — MR must identify BBB morphology type during wide beats; CDS must determine if BBB is rate-dependent vs fixed and adjust ischemia interpretation accordingly"`
- MR emits: `cross_domain_hint: "RBBB/LBBB morphology present only in wide QRS beats — CDS must confirm rate-dependency from RRC cycle length data and determine if IT ischemia assessment applies to narrow-QRS beats only"`

### CDS Specific Role
CDS determines whether the BBB is rate-dependent by correlating RRC rate/cycle length data with MR morphology appearance: if BBB morphology is absent at slower rates and present above a rate threshold, CDS confirms rate-dependent BBB (not fixed). It then manages the ischemia assessment split: during narrow-QRS beats (rate below threshold), standard IT criteria are fully applicable; during wide-QRS beats (rate above threshold), CDS applies the appropriate BBB-specific ischemia interpretation (Sgarbossa for LBBB morphology beats; standard criteria for non-V1-V3 leads in RBBB morphology beats). CDS also flags the distinction between rate-dependent RBBB (benign, common in the Ashman phenomenon during atrial fibrillation) and rate-dependent LBBB (more clinically significant, may indicate ischemia-mediated conduction disease).

## 9. Edge Cases and Pitfalls

**The most dangerous error: treating VT as SVT with aberrancy.** Administering verapamil or adenosine to a VT patient can cause irreversible hemodynamic collapse. The safety bias must always be: wide-complex tachycardia = VT until proven otherwise. The aberrancy diagnosis should only be made with explicit evidence (confirmed P-waves, incomplete pause, rate dependence) — never by exclusion of VT criteria alone.

**Ashman beats can cluster.** In AFib with a series of long cycles followed by multiple short cycles, consecutive Ashman beats can produce 3–5 wide beats in a row. This can look exactly like a run of VT. Differentiators: each beat has an irregular RR interval tracking the underlying AFib fibrillatory activity; the wide beats have RBBB morphology; rate of wide-complex beats matches the overall AFib rate (not suddenly 150–180 bpm independent of background).

**Phase 4 block is rare and implies serious disease.** Bradycardia-dependent BBB means the patient's Purkinje tissue is spontaneously depolarizing during long diastolic pauses — this is a marker of diseased, automatic Purkinje fibers. Do not dismiss it as benign. These patients require thorough evaluation for ischemia, cardiomyopathy, and conduction system disease.

**Right bundle vs. left bundle aberrancy asymmetry.** Phase 3 block producing LBBB is less common than RBBB because the left bundle has a shorter refractory period. Rate-dependent LBBB implies more significant left-sided disease and should prompt evaluation for left heart pathology.

**P-wave mimics in tachycardia.** T-wave notching, artifact, or baseline wander can be mistaken for P-waves. Use multiple leads to confirm P-wave identification. V1 and lead II are most useful for seeing P-waves separately from T-waves during tachycardia.

**Aberrancy resolution may not be immediate.** Due to hysteresis, once RBBB aberrancy is established at fast rates, it may persist for several beats even after the rate drops below the original threshold. This transient persistence can confuse beat-by-beat analysis. Look at the broader trend over 5–10 beats rather than each beat in isolation.
