## 1. Pathophysiology Root Cause

### 1.1 Underlying Mechanism
Unstable Angina (UA) sits at the severe end of the ACS spectrum, distinct from NSTEMI only by the absence of myocardial necrosis. The fundamental lesion is a vulnerable atherosclerotic plaque that undergoes partial disruption — fissuring, erosion, or rupture — triggering platelet aggregation and thrombus formation WITHOUT complete coronary occlusion. The resulting thrombus is non-occlusive, causing intermittent or partial reduction in coronary blood flow.

Because there is no cell death, cardiac biomarkers (high-sensitivity troponin) remain within normal reference limits across serial measurements. This is the defining distinction between UA and NSTEMI. Both conditions share identical ECG patterns; troponin kinetics alone separate them.

### 1.2 Subendocardial Vulnerability
During ischemic episodes, the subendocardium — the innermost layer of the ventricular wall — is selectively vulnerable. The subendocardium is furthest from epicardial coronary supply, bears the highest wall tension, and has the highest metabolic demand. Partial occlusion preferentially starves this layer, producing transmembrane potential changes that manifest as ST depression on the surface ECG (injured subendocardium has a lower resting potential and a faster phase 3 repolarization, which shifts the ST vector toward the endocardium and away from the recording electrode on the chest).

### 1.3 Dynamic vs. Fixed Changes
The hallmark of UA is dynamic ECG change: ST or T-wave abnormalities that appear during ischemic episodes and partially or fully resolve with reperfusion (spontaneous, nitrates, or anti-ischemic therapy). This dynamic nature distinguishes active UA from a fixed baseline abnormality. Serial ECGs — ideally during and after chest pain — are the core diagnostic tool.

## 2. ECG Presentation — Lead by Lead

### 2.1 Diagnostic Criteria (2025 AHA/ESC Guidelines)

Per the 2025 ESC ACS guidelines and AHA NSTEMI/UA update:

- **New horizontal or downsloping ST depression ≥0.5mm** in ≥2 contiguous leads, occurring at rest or with minimal exertion
- **Transient ST elevation <20 min duration** that resolves (if >20 min, manage as STEMI until proven otherwise)
- **T-wave inversion ≥1mm** in ≥2 contiguous leads with dominant R-wave
- **Dynamic change**: ≥1mm shift (either direction) in ST segment compared to prior or post-pain ECG constitutes a significant dynamic change
- **Normal ECG does not exclude UA**: ~50% of patients with confirmed UA have a normal or near-normal admission ECG
- Troponin-negative (both values below 99th percentile URL on 0/1h or 0/2h algorithm) is required to maintain the UA designation; if troponin rises, reclassify as NSTEMI

### 2.2 Lead-by-Lead Manifestation

| Lead | Expected Finding in Active UA | Notes |
|------|-------------------------------|-------|
| I | ST depression or T-wave flattening | Lateral wall subendocardial ischemia |
| II | ST depression or T-wave flattening/inversion | Inferior-lateral ischemia |
| III | Mild ST depression or normal | Less sensitive than II |
| aVR | ST elevation (reciprocal to diffuse depression) | >1mm elevation suggests LMCA/proximal LAD |
| aVL | T-wave inversion or flattening | High lateral involvement |
| aVF | ST depression or normal | Inferior subendocardial |
| V1 | ST depression; T-wave inversion if Wellens | Anterior subendocardial |
| V2 | ST depression; deep T inversion if Wellens Type B | LAD critical stenosis marker |
| V3 | ST depression or T-wave changes | Anterior subendocardial |
| V4 | ST depression (often maximal here in demand ischemia) | Anterior/apical subendocardial |
| V5 | ST depression | Lateral subendocardial |
| V6 | ST depression or T flattening | Lateral subendocardial |

**Normal ECG (50%)**: Approximately half of all UA presentations have a completely normal ECG at time of initial recording. This does not exclude the diagnosis and mandates serial ECGs.

### 2.3 Key Leads

- **V4-V6 + II**: Most common territory for subendocardial ST depression in demand ischemia
- **V1-V4 T-wave inversions (post-pain)**: Raise concern for Wellens syndrome — critical LAD stenosis (see Section 9)
- **aVR ST elevation**: When present alongside diffuse ST depression, suggests proximal LAD or LMCA involvement — upgrade urgency immediately
- **Reciprocal pairs**: ST depression in V1-V3 may represent true posterior STEMI (not UA) — must correlate

### 2.4 Beat-by-Beat Considerations

- Rate and rhythm must be assessed first. Tachycardia (AF with RVR, SVT, sinus tachycardia in sepsis or pain) can cause demand ischemia with ST depression that is secondary, not primary coronary ACS.
- ST depression depth and morphology should be measured at the J-point + 80ms (per standard criteria).
- Horizontal or downsloping ST depression carries higher specificity for ischemia than upsloping ST depression (the latter may be a normal tachycardia response).
- Pseudo-normalization: If a patient with known T-wave inversions presents with "normal" upright T-waves during pain — this is actually ST/T normalization representing active ischemia (the ischemia masks pre-existing inversion). This is a pitfall.

## 3. Morphology Details

### 3.1 ST Segment Shape
- **Horizontal depression**: flat ST segment parallel to baseline, ≥0.5mm below; highest specificity for subendocardial ischemia
- **Downsloping depression**: ST slopes downward after J-point; also highly specific
- **Upsloping depression**: less specific; may be normal variant or rate-related; only concerning if ≥1.5mm at J+80ms
- During active ischemia, depression is dynamic and can shift in morphology over minutes

### 3.2 T-Wave Changes
- **Flattening**: loss of normal upright T-wave amplitude; nonspecific but common
- **Inversion**: negative T-wave in leads normally upright (I, II, V3-V6); ≥1mm is significant
- **Biphasic T-waves** (upright then terminal negative deflection) in V2-V3: Wellens Type A pattern — see Section 9
- **Deep symmetric T inversions** in V1-V4: Wellens Type B — see Section 9

### 3.3 Q Waves
No new pathological Q waves in UA (no necrosis). Absence of new Q waves in a patient with symptoms is supportive of UA/NSTEMI over STEMI.

### 3.4 QRS Complex
QRS morphology is generally unchanged unless LBBB or RBBB pre-exists. New LBBB in context of ACS should trigger STEMI pathway consideration (Sgarbossa criteria).

### 3.5 PR Interval
Usually normal. Prolonged PR or AV block can occur if ischemia involves AV nodal territory (RCA-dependent), but this is more characteristic of inferior STEMI.

### 3.6 QTc Interval
May be mildly prolonged during active ischemia. Profound QT prolongation raises consideration of electrolyte disturbance as a contributing factor. Prolonged QTc with T-wave changes: vigilance for torsade de pointes risk.

### 3.7 Amplitude Considerations
No specific voltage criteria. Low voltage (QRS <5mm in limb leads, <10mm in precordial leads) suggests pericardial effusion, COPD, or obesity — differential to consider if diffuse ST changes are present.

## 4. Differential Diagnosis

### 4.1 Mimics
| Mimic | Distinguishing Feature |
|-------|------------------------|
| NSTEMI | Identical ECG; elevated troponin separates — cannot distinguish on ECG alone |
| Demand ischemia (Type 2 MI) | Identifiable trigger (tachycardia, anemia, hypotension, sepsis); troponin may rise |
| Wellens syndrome | T-wave inversions V2-V3 POST-pain with pain-free ECG; represents LAD critical stenosis |
| Posterior STEMI | ST depression V1-V3 + tall R in V1 = mirror image of posterior STEMI; needs posterior leads |
| LVH strain | Fixed ST depression/T-wave inversion in lateral leads; voltage criteria for LVH present |
| Digoxin effect | Scooped "reverse tick" ST depression; diffuse; medication history |
| Hypokalemia | Prominent U-waves, flat T-waves, apparent ST depression; check electrolytes |
| Early repolarization | ST elevation (not depression); J-point notching; benign variant |
| Pericarditis | Diffuse concave ST elevation + PR depression; not depression |
| Takotsubo | Deep T inversions + QT prolongation post-stress; regional wall motion abnormality on echo |

### 4.2 Coexisting Conditions
- **Pre-existing LBBB**: ST depression is expected as normal in LBBB (discordant ST-T changes); new ST depression in LBBB is very difficult to interpret — Sgarbossa criteria primarily address elevation; UA interpretation in LBBB requires clinical judgment
- **Pre-existing ST depression**: Baseline ECG comparison essential; dynamic change above baseline is meaningful
- **Paced rhythm**: Similarly masks ischemic changes; modified Sgarbossa may apply but is less validated for ischemia assessment
- **WPW**: Delta waves distort ST segments throughout — difficult to interpret ischemic changes

## 6. Reasoning Complexity Analysis

### 6.1 Pattern Recognition Difficulty
UA is one of the most diagnostically challenging conditions in ECG interpretation because the ECG may be entirely normal. A normal ECG in a patient with typical rest pain and ACS risk factors is still a high-risk scenario. The pattern-recognition task is not "find an abnormality" but rather "confirm absence of STEMI, identify if dynamic changes are present, and flag high-risk features."

### 6.2 Dynamic Change Detection
The most important finding — dynamic change — requires comparison with a prior ECG. Without a baseline, new ST depression cannot be distinguished from chronic baseline depression (e.g., LVH strain). The system must support serial ECG comparison and flag delta changes.

### 6.3 Clinical Context Dependency
ECG interpretation in UA is inseparable from clinical context: symptom timing, pain character, risk factors, vital signs, and troponin trajectory all modify the probability of UA vs. NSTEMI vs. non-cardiac ST change. An AI system reading only the ECG cannot make the final UA determination.

### 6.4 Wellens Identification
Wellens syndrome T-wave changes are often subtle (biphasic T in V2-V3) and appear in the PAIN-FREE state. They may be dismissed as nonspecific T-wave changes. The system must actively look for Wellens patterns in any patient with prior chest pain.

### 6.5 aVR Elevation Recognition
The critical LMCA/proximal LAD pattern (diffuse ST depression + aVR elevation ≥1mm) is frequently missed because aVR is often discounted in routine reading. The system must specifically evaluate aVR in the presence of diffuse ST depression.

### 6.6 Difficulty Score

| Dimension | Score (1–5) | Rationale |
|-----------|-------------|-----------|
| Pattern Recognition | 3 | ECG may be normal or show subtle changes; no single pathognomonic finding |
| Dynamic Change Detection | 4 | Requires serial ECG comparison; single ECG insufficient |
| Differential Complexity | 3 | Multiple mimics share identical static appearance |
| Wellens Detection | 4 | Subtle biphasic/inverted T-waves in pain-free state; high-stakes miss |
| aVR Interpretation | 3 | Requires integration of aVR with other leads; easily overlooked |
| Clinical Context Requirement | 4 | Cannot finalize diagnosis without troponin and symptom timing |
| **Overall Difficulty** | **3.0** | Dynamic, context-dependent, with high-stakes subtypes |

| **CDS** (Cross-Domain Synthesis) | Standard integration; flags for dynamic change tracking | Integrates IT's territorial ST/T findings with MR's T-wave morphology; performs standard Wellens subtype identification; flags that dynamic changes are the key diagnostic feature — serial ECG comparison is clinically required; notes that static single-ECG assessment has limited sensitivity for UA |

### Primary Agent
**IT** — unstable angina ECG findings are defined by transient territorial ST depression and dynamic T-wave changes in ischemia territories, which is the IT agent's core domain.

### Cross-Domain Hints
No cross-domain hints required — UA ECG findings are within IT's primary domain with MR providing T-wave morphology support. CDS performs standard integration.

### CDS Specific Role
CDS performs standard integration of Phase 1 outputs for unstable angina. CDS identifies Wellens syndrome subtype when IT detects characteristic T-wave changes in V2–V3 without ST elevation and MR confirms morphology. CDS explicitly flags the dynamic-change dependency: the single-ECG sensitivity for UA is low (~50%), and CDS notes this limitation in its output to prevent false confidence. If serial ECGs are provided, CDS prioritizes dynamic ST/T changes over static findings.

## 9. Edge Cases and Pitfalls

**1. Normal ECG does not exclude UA.** Approximately 50% of presentations have a normal initial ECG. Normal ECG with classic ACS symptoms requires serial ECGs, continuous monitoring, and troponin algorithm — NOT reassurance and discharge.

**2. Pseudo-normalization of T-waves.** A patient with chronic T-wave inversions (from prior MI, LVH, or Wellens) may show "normal" upright T-waves during active ischemia. The ST elevation vector during ischemia partially corrects the baseline inversion, creating apparent normalization that is actually active ischemia. This is one of the most dangerous pitfalls.

**3. Wellens syndrome in pain-free state.** Wellens T-wave changes characteristically appear BETWEEN episodes of pain, not during pain. An ECG taken during active pain may show ST elevation or depression. The Wellens pattern (biphasic or deep T inversions V2-V3) on the pain-free ECG represents the reperfusion injury signature of critical proximal LAD stenosis. Stress testing in Wellens syndrome is contraindicated — risk of precipitating anterior STEMI.

**4. ST depression V1-V3 as posterior STEMI.** Isolated ST depression maximal in V1-V3 with tall, broad R in V1 is NOT UA/NSTEMI — it is a posterior STEMI (mirror image of ST elevation that would be seen in posterior leads V7-V9). This must trigger STEMI pathway, not NSTEMI. Obtain V7-V9 leads.

**5. aVR elevation as LMCA pattern.** ST elevation in aVR ≥1mm with widespread ST depression is NOT reciprocal change in a localized territory — it reflects global subendocardial ischemia from LMCA stenosis, 3-vessel disease, or proximal LAD. This carries mortality equivalent to STEMI and requires urgent catheterization discussion.

**6. Rate-related ST depression in tachycardia.** Sinus tachycardia from any cause (pain, fever, hypovolemia, thyrotoxicosis) can produce upsloping ST depression that mimics ischemia. Rate-related ST depression typically resolves as heart rate normalizes. Downsloping or horizontal depression is less rate-dependent.

**7. LVH strain mimicry.** LVH produces fixed lateral ST depression and T-wave inversion in I, aVL, V5-V6. If the patient also has voltage criteria for LVH, the baseline may already show "ischemic" changes. Dynamic changes above this baseline are meaningful; the baseline itself is not acute ischemia.

**8. Digoxin effect.** Digoxin produces characteristic scooped (reverse-tick) ST depression that is diffuse. This is a drug effect, not ischemia, but can obscure true ischemic changes. Medication reconciliation is required.
