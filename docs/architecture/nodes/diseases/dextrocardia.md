## 1. Pathophysiology Root Cause

### 1.1 True Dextrocardia
The heart is structurally positioned in the right side of the chest. The cardiac apex points rightward instead of leftward. Two main forms:
- **Situs inversus totalis:** All thoracic and abdominal organs are mirror-imaged. The heart, liver, and stomach are all on the opposite side. ECG is the mirror image of normal. Associated with Kartagener syndrome (primary ciliary dyskinesia: bronchiectasis, sinusitis, infertility).
- **Isolated dextrocardia (situs solitus with dextrocardia):** Heart is on the right, but other organs are in normal position. More likely to have associated congenital heart defects (VSD, transposition, etc.).

The key ECG consequence: all cardiac vectors that normally point leftward now point rightward. Standard limb lead electrodes assume leftward cardiac axis. When the heart is on the right, lead I — which measures rightward voltage as negative — will show inverted (negative) P, QRS, and T waves. The precordial leads, placed on the left chest, record decreasing signal as they move progressively away from the right-sided heart.

### 1.2 Accidental Limb Lead Reversal (RA-LA Swap)
The most common ECG "artifact" in clinical practice. When the right arm (RA) and left arm (LA) electrodes are switched, lead I records the mirror image of itself (P, QRS, T all inverted), and aVR and aVL are swapped. This appears nearly identical to dextrocardia in the limb leads. However — critically — the precordial electrodes are placed correctly on the chest and record normal R-wave progression, because the chest lead positions are unchanged.

### 1.3 Electrophysiological Consequence
In true dextrocardia, the heart's electrical axis is reversed. The initial septal depolarization — normally left-to-right (producing small Q in I, small r in V1) — is now reversed: it goes right-to-left, producing small Q in aVR and positive deflection in leads oriented to the right. R-wave amplitude in precordial leads decreases from V1 to V6 because the electrodes are moving progressively farther from the right-sided cardiac apex.

## 2. ECG Presentation — Lead by Lead

### 2.1 Diagnostic Criteria

| Feature | True Dextrocardia | RA-LA Lead Reversal |
|---|---|---|
| Lead I | Inverted P, QRS, T (globally negative) | Inverted P, QRS, T |
| aVR | Upright P, positive QRS (looks like "normal lead I") | Appears like normal aVL |
| aVL | Abnormal (appears like normal aVR) | Appears like normal aVR |
| aVF | Usually normal or minimally changed | Usually normal |
| Precordial R progression | Decreasing V1→V6 (abnormal) | Normal V1→V6 (key differentiator) |
| P axis | Inverted in I, positive in aVR | Inverted in I, positive in aVR |
| QRS axis | Right axis deviation (extreme rightward) | Right axis deviation apparent |
| Chest X-ray correlation | Cardiac apex on right side | Normal cardiac position |

**Key diagnostic rule:**
- Dextrocardia: BOTH limb leads AND precordials abnormal
- RA-LA swap: limb leads abnormal, precordials NORMAL

### 2.2 Lead-by-Lead Manifestation

| Lead | True Dextrocardia | RA-LA Reversal | Normal (Reference) |
|---|---|---|---|
| I | Inverted P, negative QRS, inverted T | Inverted P, negative QRS, inverted T | Upright P, positive QRS, upright T |
| II | Appears like normal III | Appears like normal III | Normal |
| III | Appears like normal II | Appears like normal II | Normal |
| aVR | Appears like normal aVL (upright P, positive QRS) | Appears like normal aVL | Inverted P, negative QRS |
| aVL | Appears like normal aVR | Appears like normal aVR | Normal |
| aVF | Often normal or minimally affected | Normal | Normal |
| V1 | May have dominant R (closest to right-sided heart) | Normal for V1 (rsR' or rS) | rS or rsR' |
| V2 | R decreases from V1 | Normal progression | R grows |
| V3 | Continued R decrease | Normal progression | R grows |
| V4 | Minimal R | Normal progression | Dominant R |
| V5 | Near-absent R | Normal progression | Dominant R |
| V6 | Absent R / QS pattern | Normal progression | Dominant R |

### 2.3 Key Leads
- **Lead I:** The single most important lead. If globally negative (P, QRS, T all inverted), immediately suspect dextrocardia or RA-LA reversal.
- **aVR:** If aVR shows a "normal-looking" upright P and positive QRS, RA-LA reversal or dextrocardia is confirmed.
- **Precordial leads (V1–V6):** The differentiating feature. Normal R progression = lead reversal only. Decreasing R = true dextrocardia.

### 2.4 Beat-by-Beat Considerations
P-wave axis is inverted in lead I and upright in aVR in both conditions. This implies the P-wave axis is rightward — approximately +120° to +150° — which is the mirror image of normal sinus rhythm. Each beat will show this consistent pattern. Rhythm interpretation (rate, regularity) is unaffected by dextrocardia or lead reversal.

## 3. Morphology Details

### 3.1 The "Global Negativity" Rule for Lead I
In dextrocardia and RA-LA reversal, every waveform in lead I is inverted: P wave is negative, QRS is predominantly negative, T wave is negative, and even the U wave if present is negative. This "global negativity" in a single limb lead is pathognomonic of one of these two conditions. No ischemic or structural condition simultaneously inverts all waveforms in lead I while leaving other leads relatively normal.

### 3.2 aVR as "Normal Lead I"
In situs inversus or RA-LA reversal, aVR effectively records what lead I normally records. It will show an upright P wave (normal sinus origin), a predominantly positive QRS, and an upright T wave. This mirrors the expected "normal" appearance of lead I. This is the classic clue that something is wrong with the lead orientation.

### 3.3 R-Wave Progression (Precordial — Critical)
Normal R-wave progression: R amplitude increases from V1 to V5/V6 as electrodes approach the left ventricular mass. In dextrocardia: the right-sided heart is closest to V1 (leftward electrodes are moving AWAY from the apex), so R amplitude decreases V1→V6. In RA-LA reversal: chest leads are correctly placed, so R progression is normal.

### 3.4 Inferior Lead Changes
In situs inversus totalis, II and III are effectively swapped, as are their mirror images. aVF, which records inferiorly, is relatively preserved because the inferior-superior axis is not mirror-reversed in pure left-right situs inversion.

### 3.5 Corrected ECG for True Dextrocardia
To obtain a diagnostically useful ECG in dextrocardia:
1. Reverse limb leads: swap RA and LA electrode connections
2. Place precordial electrodes on the RIGHT chest (V1R position = normal V2; V2R = normal V1; V3R–V6R mirror the normal positions on right side)
The corrected ECG will then show normal sinus P waves, normal QRS axis, and normal R-wave progression in right-sided precordials.

### 3.6 Congenital Heart Disease Association
Isolated dextrocardia (without situs inversus) has a high association with congenital heart defects. Any dextrocardia diagnosis should prompt echocardiography to evaluate for VSD, transposition, heterotaxy. The ECG itself cannot detect associated structural defects.

### 3.7 Kartagener Syndrome
Dextrocardia + bronchiectasis + chronic sinusitis = Kartagener syndrome (primary ciliary dyskinesia). ECG appearance is identical to other forms of situs inversus. Clinical history is the differentiator.

## 4. Differential Diagnosis

### 4.1 Mimics

| Condition | ECG Overlap | Key Differentiator |
|---|---|---|
| RA-LA lead reversal | Identical limb lead pattern | Normal precordial R progression |
| Other limb lead reversals (RA-LL, LA-LL) | Altered limb leads | Different pattern; different leads affected; usually not globally negative in I |
| Extreme right axis deviation from other causes | Negative QRS in I | P and T waves not inverted; precordial progression normal |
| Ectopic atrial rhythm (inferior P) | Inverted P in some leads | P inverted only, not QRS and T |
| Junctional rhythm | Inverted or absent P | QRS upright in I; T wave not inverted |

### 4.2 Coexisting Conditions
- Dextrocardia patients require ECG leads placed in right-sided positions for all clinical ECGs. If a standard left-sided ECG is obtained, STEMI, LVH, and arrhythmia diagnoses are all unreliable.
- If the ECG system encounters "negative lead I" and the standard STEMI algorithm fires, it will produce false positives. The system must detect dextrocardia/reversal first, before applying any diagnostic algorithm.

| Presentation | Classification | Rationale |
|---|---|---|

| Suspected dextrocardia (new, unexplained) | **Urgent** | Requires echo; rule out associated CHD; check CXR |
| Dextrocardia with suspected acute coronary occlusion | **STAT** | Must obtain corrected ECG immediately; standard STEMI criteria do not apply |

## 6. Reasoning Complexity Analysis

### 6.1 Pattern Recognition Challenge
Global negativity in lead I is highly specific and easy to recognize. The challenge is distinguishing true dextrocardia from RA-LA reversal — which requires inspecting precordial leads, not limb leads.

### 6.2 Downstream Algorithm Integrity
Every downstream diagnostic algorithm (STEMI, LVH, axis, arrhythmia) is invalid on a dextrocardia ECG recorded with standard electrode positions. The system must gate all other diagnoses on the prior detection of dextrocardia/reversal. This is a dependency relationship that must be enforced in the reasoning chain.

### 6.3 Corrected ECG Requirement
The system must output a clear recommendation: "Corrected ECG required. Place limb leads reversed and precordials on right chest for dextrocardia, or correct RA-LA reversal and repeat."

### 6.4 Clinical Context Dependence
A known dextrocardia patient presenting with chest pain needs a correctly placed ECG for STEMI evaluation. The system must retrieve prior ECG context (known dextrocardia flag) and apply appropriate electrode guidance.

### 6.5 Automated Detection Reliability
Automated detection of negative-I-globally is straightforward algorithmically (flag if P amplitude in I < −0.1 mV and QRS amplitude in I predominantly negative). Distinguishing reversal from dextrocardia requires examining V1–V6 R-wave slope — also computable from waveform amplitudes.

### 6.6 Difficulty Score

| Dimension | Score (1–5) | Rationale |
|---|---|---|
| Pattern recognition | 2 | Global negativity in I is highly distinctive |
| Lead-reversal vs. dextrocardia distinction | 3 | Requires precordial R-wave slope analysis |
| Downstream algorithm gating | 4 | All other diagnoses must be suppressed until ECG corrected |
| Clinical urgency assessment | 3 | Standard ECG inadequate; corrected ECG urgency depends on context |
| Congenital/structural association | 2 | Recognition of need for echo in new diagnosis |
| Demographic context | 1 | No demographic-dependent interpretation needed |
| Data integration | 2 | CXR and echo confirm; ECG detection is standalone |
| **Overall** | **2.5** | **Moderate complexity** |

| Agent | Role for Dextrocardia | Key Features Used |
|-------|--------------------------|-------------------|
| **RRC** (Rhythm/Rate/Conduction) | Co-Primary | Global negative QRS in lead I (negative P-QRS-T in lead I — "negative lead I" sign), right axis deviation, aVR appears like normal lead I (upright P and QRS), loss of normal lead I upright morphology |
| **IT** (Ischemia/Territory) | Supporting | All territorial analysis is reversed — must apply mirror-image territory mapping; normal ischemia territory rules are inverted in dextrocardia; flags that standard coronary territory mapping cannot be applied without correction |
| **MR** (Morphology/Repolarization) | Co-Primary | Reverse R-wave progression V1-V6 (R-wave decreases from V1 to V6 instead of increasing), absent R-wave in V5-V6 ("absent lateral R-wave progression"), mirror-image precordial lead pattern |
| **CDS** (Cross-Domain Synthesis) | Required — CRITICAL: applies mirror-image correction to all territorial assessment; prevents false ischemia and conduction misdiagnosis | Integrates RRC global lead I negativity + MR reverse R-progression → dextrocardia diagnosis; applies mirror-image correction to IT territorial mapping; flags that all ischemia assessment requires reversed territory rules (e.g., V1-V3 changes may represent lateral wall in dextrocardia) |

### Primary Agent
**RRC and MR are co-primary** — Dextrocardia is identified by the combination of RRC's global lead I negativity (rhythm/axis finding) and MR's reverse R-wave progression (morphology finding); neither alone is sufficient without the other.

### Cross-Domain Hints
- RRC → MR: `cross_domain_hint: "Global negative QRS in lead I detected — MR should assess R-wave progression V1-V6 for reversal (decreasing R-wave V1→V6); combined findings confirm dextrocardia"`
- RRC → CDS: `cross_domain_hint: "Global lead I negativity detected — CDS must apply dextrocardia diagnosis and flag IT that all coronary territory mapping requires mirror-image correction; standard territory rules do not apply"`
- MR → CDS: `cross_domain_hint: "Reverse R-wave progression V1-V6 detected — if RRC confirms global lead I negativity, dextrocardia confirmed; CDS must correct all territorial analysis with mirror-image mapping"`
- IT → CDS: `cross_domain_hint: "Standard coronary territory mapping may be unreliable — if dextrocardia confirmed by RRC/MR, CDS must apply reversed territory rules before any ischemia interpretation"`

### CDS Specific Role
CDS plays a critical systemic correction role for dextrocardia: once RRC detects global lead I negativity and MR confirms reverse R-wave progression, CDS confirms the dextrocardia diagnosis and then applies mirror-image territory correction to all IT findings. In dextrocardia, the anatomic relationships of ECG leads to coronary territories are reversed: what appears as anterior ST changes in V1-V3 may represent lateral wall ischemia, and inferior territory findings map to different leads than standard. CDS flags dextrocardia prominently in the output so that all downstream interpretation — ischemia assessment, axis determination, bundle branch block classification — is performed with the corrected anatomic reference. CDS also distinguishes true dextrocardia from lead reversal (reversal produces negative lead I but normal R-wave progression in precordial leads), which is a key clinical differentiation with major management implications.

## 9. Edge Cases and Pitfalls

- **RA-LL reversal (right arm and left leg swapped):** Produces a different pattern — lead II becomes inverted (looks like lead III), lead III looks like lead II, aVF is inverted. Lead I appears relatively normal. Less commonly recognized than RA-LA swap.
- **All four limb leads swapped:** Various patterns depending on which pair. The system should detect any limb lead that shows globally negative waveforms and flag for potential reversal.
- **Dextrocardia + STEMI:** If a right-sided MI is suspected (inferior STEMI with right heart involvement), right-sided leads (V3R–V6R) are needed regardless — which happens to be the correct position for dextrocardia ECG. One setup serves both purposes.
- **CXR correlation:** The cardiac silhouette on CXR pointing rightward confirms dextrocardia. If CXR is available in the data pipeline, cross-reference.
- **Known dextrocardia patient in the database:** Future YouOwnECG sessions with this patient should flag "known dextrocardia — right-sided leads expected." Prior ECG comparison requires corrected ECGs from both sessions.
- **Pediatric dextrocardia:** Neonates and infants with complex congenital heart disease often have variant situs. The system must apply same detection logic in pediatric ECGs.
