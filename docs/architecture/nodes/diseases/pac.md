## 1. Pathophysiology Root Cause

### 1.1 What Goes Wrong

A PAC arises from an ectopic focus in the atrial myocardium (outside the sinoatrial node) that achieves sufficient automaticity or is triggered to fire before the next expected sinus beat. The mechanism may be:

- **Enhanced automaticity**: An atrial cell depolarizes spontaneously before the sinus node fires (caffeine, sympathetic stimulation, atrial stretch from volume overload or structural disease, electrolyte disturbance, hypoxia, or idiopathic)
- **Triggered activity**: Early or delayed afterdepolarizations reaching threshold — common in digitalis toxicity, hypokalaemia
- **Micro-reentry**: Small reentrant circuit within atrial tissue

The ectopic focus may reside anywhere in either atrium: high right atrium near sinus node, low right atrium near AV node, left atrium, pulmonary vein ostia (particularly relevant for AF initiation), or inter-atrial tissue.

### 1.2 Electrical Consequence

The ectopic focus discharges and spreads through both atria via normal atrial myocardial conduction. Because the wavefront originates at a different location than the sinus node, the P-wave vector differs from the normal sinus P-wave. The impulse reaches the AV node and, if AV node is not refractory, conducts normally through His-Purkinje to the ventricles — producing a narrow, morphologically normal QRS. The PAC partially or fully discharges the sinus node, resetting its timing cycle.

### 1.3 Why It Appears on ECG

- **Early P-wave**: PAC fires before the next expected sinus P → shorter than normal PP interval preceding the PAC
- **Altered P morphology**: Different atrial activation vector → P-wave shape differs from sinus P in one or more leads
- **Incomplete compensatory pause**: Sinus node reset → the RR interval surrounding the PAC is less than 2 normal sinus RR intervals
- **Conducted normally**: QRS is narrow unless bundle branches are refractory (aberrant conduction)
- **Blocked PAC**: If AV node is refractory when PAC arrives → P-wave without QRS → apparent pause on rhythm strip

## 2. ECG Presentation — Lead by Lead

### 2.1 Diagnostic Criteria (2025 AHA/ESC Guidelines)

1. Premature P-wave occurring before the next expected sinus P-wave
2. P-wave morphology differs from sinus P-wave in at least one lead
3. PR interval of PAC differs from sinus PR (may be longer if ectopic focus near AV node; shorter if low atrial origin with direct AV nodal proximity)
4. QRS morphology: usually identical to sinus beats (narrow, normal); may be wide if aberrant conduction (Ashman phenomenon)
5. Incomplete compensatory pause: post-PAC RR + pre-PAC RR < 2 normal sinus RR intervals
6. Blocked PAC: premature P-wave without following QRS, followed by pause

### 2.2 Lead-by-Lead Manifestation

| Lead | Finding |
|------|---------|
| I | P-wave morphology may differ from sinus P; upright, isoelectric, or inverted depending on ectopic origin |
| II | KEY: most visible P-wave changes — high RA PAC similar to sinus; low RA or AV junction PAC inverted (negative) |
| III | Ectopic P-wave morphology variable; helps localize atrial origin |
| aVR | Sinus P normally negative; PAC P-wave may be less negative or positive depending on origin |
| aVL | P-wave morphology altered; left atrial PAC may produce negative P in aVL |
| aVF | Low atrial PAC produces inverted P in aVF (retrograde-like activation) |
| V1 | Useful for left vs right atrial origin: left atrial PAC → biphasic or negative P in V1; right atrial PAC → positive P in V1 |
| V2 | P-wave morphology aids origin localization |
| V3–V6 | QRS morphology normal; P-wave typically not reliably visible in lateral precordial leads |

### 2.3 Key Leads (Most Diagnostic)

- **Lead II**: Best single lead for P-wave visibility and morphology comparison against sinus P
- **V1**: Differentiates right vs left atrial origin (left atrial PAC: negative or biphasic P in V1)
- **Long rhythm strip (Lead II or V1)**: Essential for identifying PAC pattern (bigeminy, trigeminy) and burden estimation

### 2.4 Beat-by-Beat Considerations

- PAC interrupts otherwise regular sinus rhythm — the early beat breaks the RR pattern
- Bigeminy: every other beat is a PAC (sinus–PAC–sinus–PAC)
- Trigeminy: every third beat is a PAC (sinus–sinus–PAC pattern)
- PAC runs: two consecutive PACs = atrial couplet; three or more = atrial tachycardia
- Blocked PAC: the PAC P-wave must be actively sought — it deforms the T-wave of the preceding beat, creating a "notched T" or "bifid T" — easily missed, misread as sinus pause or 2nd degree AV block

## 3. Morphology Details

### 3.1 P-wave Changes

The P-wave is the defining feature — it must differ from the sinus P. Characteristics:

- **Morphology**: varies with ectopic origin location
  - High right atrium (near SA node): P resembles sinus P closely — most difficult to identify
  - Low right atrium or coronary sinus: inverted P in II/III/aVF (negative), short PR interval
  - Left atrium: negative or deeply biphasic P in V1; may be broad or notched
  - Pulmonary vein ostia: often narrow, sharp, monophasic P in V1
- **Timing**: premature — TP interval shorter than normal sinus cycle
- **Axis**: deviated from normal sinus axis (0° to +75°) depending on ectopic origin

### 3.2 PR Interval Changes

- PR interval of PAC can be shorter or longer than normal sinus PR:
  - Short PR (<120 ms): ectopic focus close to AV node (low atrial), minimal AV nodal delay
  - Long PR (>200 ms): PAC arrives during relative refractory period of AV node → AV node conduction slowed
  - Normal PR: ectopic focus at normal distance from AV node
- Blocked PAC: no PR interval — P-wave not followed by QRS

### 3.3 QRS Complex Changes

- **Normal (narrow) QRS**: most common — AV node, His-Purkinje fully recovered, normal ventricular conduction
- **Aberrant conduction (wide QRS)**: PAC arrives while one bundle branch is still refractory from the preceding beat
  - **Ashman phenomenon**: especially after a long RR interval preceding the PAC — right bundle branch has longer refractory period than left → RBBB-pattern aberrancy is most common
  - Aberrantly conducted PAC mimics PVC — key distinction: preceding ectopic P-wave present before aberrant QRS
- **Blocked PAC**: QRS absent — P-wave without conduction

### 3.4 ST Segment Changes

- ST segment of PAC-conducted beats is identical to sinus beats (normal conduction path)
- No primary ST changes attributable to the PAC itself
- Rate changes after PAC may transiently alter ST morphology in subsequent beats

### 3.5 T-wave Changes

- T-wave of the beat immediately preceding a blocked PAC is deformed — the PAC P-wave is superimposed on the T-wave
- This T-wave deformity (notching, peaking, increased amplitude) is the key to identifying blocked PACs
- Always compare the T-wave morphology before a suspected pause against all other T-waves on the strip

### 3.6 QT/QTc Changes

- QT interval of PAC-conducted beats: not significantly different from sinus beats
- Post-PAC beat may show QT prolongation if the PAC follows a longer than usual pause (cycle-length dependency)

### 3.7 Other Features

- **Compensatory vs non-compensatory pause**:
  - Incomplete (non-compensatory) pause: pre-PAC RR + post-PAC RR < 2 × sinus RR — caused by sinus node reset
  - Full compensatory pause: occasionally seen if PAC does not reset sinus node — less common than with PVCs
  - Interpolated PAC: very rare — PAC sandwiched between two normal sinus beats without pause
- **PAC burden**: high-burden PACs (>500/day or >10% of beats on Holter) associated with increased AF risk and should be documented

## 4. Differential Diagnosis

### 4.1 Mimics

| Condition | Key Differentiator |
|-----------|-------------------|
| Sinus arrhythmia | Gradual PP variation with respiration; P morphology constant; no premature P-wave |
| 2nd degree AV block (Mobitz I) | Progressive PR prolongation before dropped QRS; no premature P-wave; PP intervals constant |
| 2nd degree AV block (Mobitz II) | Sudden P-wave without QRS; P-waves march through at constant rate; no deformed T-waves |
| PVC | No preceding P-wave; full compensatory pause; wide bizarre QRS; discordant T-wave |
| Junctional escape | Occurs late (escape), not early; negative P in inferior leads; rate lower |
| SA exit block | Sudden absent P-wave; PP interval doubles; no deformed T-wave preceding pause |

### 4.2 Coexisting Conditions

- **Atrial fibrillation initiation**: PACs from pulmonary vein ostia are the dominant trigger for AF. High-burden PACs, particularly those with short coupling intervals, warrant AF monitoring.
- **Structural heart disease**: Frequent PACs may reflect atrial enlargement, elevated filling pressures, or mitral valve disease.
- **Digitalis toxicity**: PAC bigeminy is a classic digitalis toxic rhythm.
- **Aberrant PAC vs PVC**: Ashman phenomenon produces wide QRS — must actively look for preceding ectopic P-wave to avoid misclassifying as PVC.

## 5. STAT Classification

**NOT STAT — isolated PACs are benign in most clinical contexts.**

Contextual escalation required when:
- **High PAC burden on continuous monitoring**: >10% of beats suggests underlying atrial pathology, medication effect, or electrolyte disturbance — warrants workup, not STAT
- **PAC triggering runs of atrial tachycardia or AF**: document and assess hemodynamic impact
- **Blocked PAC causing symptomatic pauses**: if patient is symptomatic with blocked PAC-related pauses, escalate to cardiology review
- **Digitalis toxicity pattern** (PAC bigeminy + GI symptoms + visual changes): STAT — check digoxin level, hold digoxin, monitor
- **Frequent PACs in acute MI setting**: may herald AF or atrial flutter — flag for monitoring

## 6. Reasoning Complexity Analysis

### 6.1 Reasoning Domains Required

1. PP interval regularity analysis (detecting early P-wave)
2. P-wave morphology comparison between ectopic and sinus beats (multi-lead)
3. PR interval classification (short/normal/long)
4. QRS width assessment (aberrant vs normal conduction)
5. Compensatory pause measurement and classification
6. Blocked PAC detection (T-wave deformity analysis)
7. Pattern recognition (bigeminy, trigeminy, couplets)

### 6.2 Feature Dependencies

- P-wave morphology comparison requires reliable identification of the sinus P-wave template
- Ashman phenomenon detection requires preceding RR interval measurement (long-short sequence)
- Blocked PAC detection requires T-wave shape comparison across multiple beats
- PAC burden estimation requires multi-beat rhythm strip (not just standard 12-lead snapshot)

### 6.3 Cross-Condition Interactions

- Aberrantly conducted PAC easily confused with PVC — P-wave search is mandatory for all wide QRS beats
- Blocked PAC mimics 2nd degree AV block — has direct clinical management consequence (PAC = benign; Mobitz II = pacemaker)
- High-burden PACs in context of acute presentation may mask paroxysmal AF on 12-lead snapshot
- PAC bigeminy at rapid sinus rates may superficially resemble ventricular bigeminy

### 6.4 Reasoning Chain Sketch

```
Early beat detected (RR interval shorter than sinus cycle)?
  └── Yes → Is there a P-wave before the early QRS?
        ├── No P visible → PVC (proceed to PVC protocol)
        └── P present → Is P morphology same as sinus P?
              ├── Same morphology → sinus early beat? (very rare) — reconsider
              └── Different morphology → PAC confirmed
                    ├── QRS narrow → conducted PAC
                    ├── QRS wide → aberrant PAC (Ashman) — look for preceding long RR
                    └── No QRS → blocked PAC — check T-wave deformity of preceding beat
  └── Pause without preceding QRS → search for buried P in preceding T-wave
        ├── P found → blocked PAC (NOT 2nd degree block)
        └── No P found → sinus pause or SA exit block
```

### 6.5 Confidence Anchors

- High confidence PAC: early beat with distinct P morphology, narrow QRS, incomplete compensatory pause
- High confidence blocked PAC: T-wave deformity at site of expected P, followed by pause, PP interval preserved or shorter than sinus
- Uncertainty trigger: wide QRS after early beat → mandatory P-wave search before labeling PVC
- Uncertainty trigger: pause on strip → mandatory T-wave comparison to exclude blocked PAC

### 6.6 Difficulty Score

| Dimension | Score |
|-----------|-------|
| Signal clarity needed | 3/5 — P-wave morphology comparison requires clean signal; blocked PAC requires T-wave analysis |
| Number of leads required | 2/5 — lead II + V1 usually sufficient; multi-lead helps localize origin |
| Cross-domain reasoning | 2/5 — primarily rate/morphology/timing; aberrancy adds complexity |
| Temporal pattern complexity | 2/5 — bigeminy/trigeminy patterns are regular and recognizable |
| Differential complexity | 3/5 — blocked PAC vs 2nd degree block is clinically critical |
| Rarity in PTB-XL | 2/5 — PACs are common; well-represented in dataset |
| **Overall difficulty** | **2.0/5** |

## 6A. Agent Assignment

**Architecture:** Option E — 3 Parallel Phase 1 Specialists + 1 Sequential CDS (Node 2.1 Gate Passed 2026-03-27)

| Agent | Role for PAC | Key Features Used |
|-------|--------------------------|-------------------|
| **RRC** (Rhythm/Rate/Conduction) | Primary | Premature P-wave with morphology different from sinus P-wave, early beat timing, incomplete compensatory pause (post-PAC RR < 2× sinus RR), blocked PAC (premature P-wave not followed by QRS) |
| **IT** (Ischemia/Territory) | Not involved | ST/T assessment not relevant to PAC classification |
| **MR** (Morphology/Repolarization) | Supporting | Aberrant conduction QRS morphology (RBBB or LBBB pattern) when PAC conducts with aberrancy |
| **CDS** (Cross-Domain Synthesis) | Standard integration only | Confirms PAC timing (RRC) with aberrant morphology if present (MR); flags frequent PACs with short coupling interval as AF initiation risk |

### Primary Agent
**RRC** — PAC diagnosis is defined by premature P-wave timing and morphology difference from sinus P; all core criteria are RRC-domain measurements.

### Cross-Domain Hints
- RRC → MR: `cross_domain_hint: "premature_beat_with_wide_QRS — confirm aberrant conduction morphology (RBBB/LBBB pattern) vs PVC"`

### CDS Specific Role
CDS integrates RRC's premature P-wave detection with MR's QRS morphology to confirm PAC with aberrancy vs PVC (the key differential). When RRC detects frequent PACs (>6/min or runs), CDS flags elevated AF initiation risk. Short coupling interval PACs are specifically noted as a trigger for paroxysmal AF.

## 9. Edge Cases and Pitfalls

1. **Blocked PAC misread as 2nd degree AV block**: The most clinically significant PAC pitfall. Management differs completely (blocked PAC = reassurance; Mobitz II = urgent pacemaker evaluation). Key: look for a deformed T-wave before every pause. A notched, peaked, or asymmetric T-wave preceding a pause strongly suggests blocked PAC.

2. **Aberrantly conducted PAC misread as PVC**: Wide QRS PAC has preceding ectopic P-wave (often hidden in preceding T-wave). Search for P-wave before assuming ventricular origin. Ashman pattern (long-short RR preceding the wide beat) supports aberrancy.

3. **High RA PAC indistinguishable from sinus beat**: If PAC origin is near the sinus node, P morphology may be nearly identical. Diagnosis depends on timing (early) alone. Check multiple leads.

4. **PAC triggering AF or atrial flutter**: A PAC with short coupling interval may initiate AF on the recording. The PAC itself may be visible just before the irregular baseline begins.

5. **Digitalis-toxic PAC bigeminy**: Every other beat is a PAC — may superficially resemble a regular rhythm if the alternating beats are not carefully analyzed. Context (digitalis use, clinical symptoms) is critical.

6. **PAC in atrial flutter**: Ectopic beats may disrupt flutter cycle temporarily. Do not dismiss an apparent flutter irregularity without checking for atrial ectopy.

7. **PR interval prolongation after PAC**: Long PR following a PAC does not indicate AV block — it reflects AV nodal refractoriness to the early ectopic beat. Compare PR only between sinus beats.
