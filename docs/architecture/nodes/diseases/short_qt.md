## 1. Pathophysiology Root Cause

### 1.1 Molecular Basis
Short QT Syndrome (SQTS) is a rare inherited channelopathy characterized by abnormally accelerated ventricular repolarization. The underlying defect is gain-of-function mutations in cardiac ion channels that increase outward potassium current or decrease inward depolarizing current, shortening the action potential duration (APD). Three primary genetic subtypes:

- **SQT1 (KCNH2 / hERG):** Gain-of-function in the rapid delayed rectifier K+ current (IKr). hERG channel normally activates slowly during plateau and repolarizes the cell; gain-of-function accelerates repolarization dramatically. The mutant channel does not inactivate normally, allowing greater outward K+ flow throughout plateau.
- **SQT2 (KCNQ1 / KvLQT1):** Gain-of-function in the slow delayed rectifier K+ current (IKs). Less common than SQT1. IKs normally contributes to late repolarization; accelerated IKs shortens APD.
- **SQT3 (KCNJ2 / Kir2.1):** Gain-of-function in inward rectifier K+ current (IK1). IK1 governs terminal repolarization and resting membrane potential. Gain-of-function accelerates phase 3 repolarization.

Additional rarer subtypes involve CACNA1C, CACNB2, SCN5A loss-of-function, all converging on reduced APD.

### 1.2 Electrophysiological Consequence
Shortened APD directly shortens the QT interval. More critically, because different myocardial layers (epicardium, M-cells, endocardium) have different baseline APDs, disproportionate shortening in some layers increases transmural dispersion of repolarization (TDR). Elevated TDR creates substrate for re-entrant arrhythmias: short refractory periods allow premature impulses to find excitable tissue and initiate VF. Atrial tissue is similarly affected, predisposing to AF in structurally normal hearts.

### 1.3 Clinical Phenotype
- Familial sudden cardiac death, often at young age (<40 years)
- Syncope or documented VF/VT — often the first clinical event
- Atrial fibrillation in young patients with no structural disease
- Structurally normal heart on echo; normal QRS morphology
- Symptoms often precipitated by rest or sleep (high vagal tone), unlike exercise-triggered channelopathies

## 2. ECG Presentation — Lead by Lead

### 2.1 Diagnostic Criteria (2025 AHA/ESC Guidelines)

| Criterion | Threshold | Diagnostic Weight |
|---|---|---|
| QTc ≤340 ms | Highly specific cutoff | Diagnostic alone if confirmed on serial ECGs |
| QTc 340–360 ms + symptoms | Syncope, documented VF, or SCD survivor | Diagnostic with clinical context |
| QTc 340–360 ms + family history | First-degree relative with confirmed SQTS or unexplained SCD <40 years | Diagnostic with genetic/familial context |
| Confirmed pathogenic mutation | KCNQ1, KCNH2, KCNJ2, CACNA1C, CACNB2 gain-of-function | Genetic diagnosis independent of QTc |
| Tall, peaked T waves | Narrow T waves with near-absent ST segment | Supportive morphological criterion |

QTc correction: Bazett formula (QT / √RR) remains standard but overestimates QTc at high HR in SQTS. Fridericia (QT / RR^0.33) may be more accurate. Measure in lead II or V5.

### 2.2 Lead-by-Lead Manifestation

| Lead | Expected Finding | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| I | Short QT; tall narrow T | T wave may appear disproportionately tall relative to QRS |
| II | Short QT; tall peaked T | Best lead for QT measurement |
| III | Short QT; variable T amplitude | |
| aVR | Short QT; inverted T (normal) | QT shortening visible |
| aVL | Short QT; tall T | May show peaking |
| aVF | Short QT; tall T | Inferior distribution prominent |
| V1 | Short QT; tall T or biphasic | Precordial leads show most striking changes |
| V2 | Very short QT; tall peaked T; minimal ST | ST segment virtually absent; T wave begins immediately after QRS |
| V3 | Very short QT; tall peaked T; minimal ST | Most prominent morphologic change in precordial leads |
| V4 | Short QT; tall peaked T | |
| V5 | Short QT; tall T | Preferred measurement lead by some guidelines |
| V6 | Short QT; tall T | Lateral leads often show tall narrow T |

### 2.3 Key Leads
- **V2–V3**: Most dramatic morphology — T wave appears to begin immediately after S wave with minimal or no ST segment. T wave is tall, narrow, symmetric-to-asymmetric depending on subtype.
- **II and V5**: Standard measurement leads for QT interval. QT appears markedly compressed relative to the RR interval.
- **Lateral leads (I, aVL, V4–V6)**: Tall T waves often most prominent here; useful for confirming generalized repolarization acceleration.

### 2.4 Beat-by-Beat Considerations
- Heart rate adaptation: QT shortening persists even after HR correction. At fast rates the QT/RR relationship is abnormal — the QT fails to lengthen appropriately with bradycardia (blunted rate adaptation).
- RR dependency: In long-short-long sequences preceding VF initiation, the short QT provides insufficient time for the preceding beat's refractory period to recover.
- AF episodes: Irregularly irregular rhythm with normal QRS may be the presenting finding; absence of structural disease + young age + family history should trigger QT scrutiny on rhythm strip.

## 3. Morphology Details

### 3.1 ST Segment
Virtually absent. The ST segment (isoelectric phase between QRS end and T-wave onset) represents phase 2 of the action potential. Because phase 2 (the plateau) is dramatically shortened or eliminated in SQTS, the ECG transition from QRS to T wave occurs abruptly. This is the most visually striking feature: the T wave appears to "sprout" directly from the end of the QRS complex.

### 3.2 T-Wave Morphology
Tall, narrow, peaked. Rapid upstroke and downstroke. In SQT1 (KCNH2 mutations) the T wave is characteristically tall and narrow with near-symmetric morphology. In SQT3 (KCNJ2 mutations) the T wave may show asymmetric morphology with a particularly steep terminal descent due to accelerated terminal repolarization. T-wave amplitude commonly exceeds 5–8 mm in precordial leads.

### 3.3 QRS Complex
Normal duration, normal morphology, normal axis. SQTS does not affect depolarization. QRS width is not prolonged. This distinguishes it from conditions where both QRS and QT are altered (LBBB, hyperkalemia).

### 3.4 PR Interval
Typically normal. No conduction system involvement in most SQTS variants.

### 3.5 P Wave
Normal morphology and duration unless coexisting AF is present, in which case no discrete P waves are seen.

### 3.6 U Wave
Not typically prominent. Absence of prominent U waves helps distinguish SQTS from hypokalemia where tall T waves and prominent U waves can mimic short QT.

### 3.7 Interval Measurements
- QTc ≤340 ms diagnostic; 340–360 ms suspicious
- QT absolute value often <300 ms at normal heart rates (60–80 bpm)
- JT interval (from J point to T end): disproportionately short
- The ratio QT/QTp (predicted QT for given RR) useful: ratio <0.88 suggestive

## 4. Differential Diagnosis

### 4.1 Mimics

| Condition | Differentiating Feature |
|---|---|
| Hypercalcemia | Short QT due to shortened ST segment; T wave not typically tall/peaked; calcium level confirms; rarely as short as SQTS |
| Digitalis effect | Shortens QT modestly; characteristic scooped ST depression; drug history |
| Hyperkalemia | Short QT possible; also has peaked T waves but wide QRS, PR prolongation, P-wave flattening |
| Acidosis | Mild QT shortening; metabolic context; no tall narrow T |
| Normal variant | Some individuals have QTc 340–360 ms without symptoms; family history and genetic testing essential |
| Vagal effect / high vagal tone | Rate-dependent QT shortening; normalizes with increased HR |

### 4.2 Coexisting Conditions
- **Atrial fibrillation**: Common first presentation; SQTS is a cause of AF in structurally normal young heart
- **WPW**: Can shorten apparent QT via delta wave; measure QT from end of delta wave, not QRS onset
- **Early repolarization**: J-point elevation may create confusion with steep T-wave onset; distribution and morphology differ

## 6. Reasoning Complexity Analysis

### 6.1 Reasoning Domains Required
- Interval measurement: accurate QT measurement and rate-correction
- Morphology recognition: absent ST segment, peaked narrow T waves
- Clinical integration: symptoms, family history, age
- Differential exclusion: metabolic causes, drugs, other channelopathies
- Statistical reasoning: rare condition — prior probability low in unselected ER population

### 6.2 Feature Dependencies
- QT measurement accuracy depends on correct identification of T-wave end (often difficult when T wave is tall/peaked or when U wave merges)
- Rate correction formula selection affects diagnostic threshold; Bazett overcorrects at extremes
- Single-lead measurement may miss lead-specific variation; multi-lead confirmation required

### 6.3 Cross-Condition Interactions
- SQTS + AF: AF makes QT measurement unreliable (variable RR intervals); measure on longest available RR cycle
- SQTS + Brugada overlap: rare cases described; evaluate V1–V3 separately from QT assessment
- Hypercalcemia mimicry: metabolic panel essential before diagnosing SQTS

### 6.4 Reasoning Chain Sketch
1. Detect short QT interval (automated or manual measurement)
2. Confirm in multiple leads (II, V5)
3. Apply rate correction (Bazett or Fridericia)
4. Assess T-wave morphology: peaked, narrow, absent ST
5. Rule out metabolic causes (Ca²⁺, K⁺, Mg²⁺, digitalis)
6. Integrate clinical context: age, symptoms, family history
7. If QTc ≤340 ms → flag as SQTS candidate, escalate
8. If QTc 340–360 ms → flag as suspicious, require clinical correlation

### 6.5 Confidence Anchors
- QTc ≤340 ms with absent ST segment + tall peaked T: high confidence SQTS
- QTc ≤340 ms with normal T morphology + metabolic abnormality: low confidence, investigate metabolic cause first
- QTc 340–360 ms with no symptoms or family history: low confidence, normal variant possible

### 6.6 Difficulty Score

| Dimension | Score (1–5) | Rationale |
|---|---|---|
| Morphology Recognition | 2 | Peaked T with absent ST is visually distinct |
| Interval Measurement | 3 | T-wave end identification difficult on peaked T; rate correction formula matters |
| Differential Diagnosis | 2 | Metabolic causes excluded by labs; limited DDx |
| Clinical Integration | 3 | Symptoms and family history essential but not on ECG |
| Rarity / Prior Probability | 3 | Very rare; easy to over-diagnose normal variants |
| Arrhythmia Risk Stratification | 3 | Distinguishing diagnostic vs suspicious QTc range |
| **Overall** | **2.5** | Primarily a measurement and threshold problem |

## 6A. Agent Assignment

**Architecture:** Option E — 3 Parallel Phase 1 Specialists + 1 Sequential CDS (Node 2.1 Gate Passed 2026-03-27)

| Agent | Role for Short QT Syndrome | Key Features Used |
|-------|--------------------------|-------------------|
| **RRC** (Rhythm/Rate/Conduction) | Supporting | QT/QTc measurement (markedly shortened, <340ms diagnostic); RR interval for rate correction; arrhythmia detection (VF susceptibility) |
| **IT** (Ischemia/Territory) | Supporting | Peaked T-waves in precordial leads — must not classify narrow-based peaked T-waves as hyperacute ischemic T-waves; distinction requires CDS |
| **MR** (Morphology/Repolarization) | Primary | QTc <340ms (diagnostic), peaked T-waves with narrow base (T-wave occupies unusually small portion of RR interval), absent or minimal ST segment, T-wave morphology characterization by lead |
| **CDS** (Cross-Domain Synthesis) | Required — resolves peaked narrow T-waves differential: Short QT vs hyperacute ischemia vs hyperkalemia | Integrates MR narrow-based peaked T-waves + IT non-territorial distribution + RRC QTc <340ms → Short QT syndrome; hyperacute ischemia has territorial distribution and normal QTc; hyperkalemia has diffuse peaked T-waves with broad base and normal/prolonged QTc |

### Primary Agent
**MR** — Short QT syndrome is defined by its repolarization morphology: markedly shortened QTc with narrow-based peaked T-waves are repolarization features within MR's domain.

### Cross-Domain Hints
- MR → IT: `cross_domain_hint: "Narrow-based peaked T-waves detected — IT must not classify as hyperacute ischemic T-waves; narrow base + short QTc = Short QT syndrome pattern; CDS to resolve differential"`
- MR → CDS: `cross_domain_hint: "QTc markedly shortened with narrow-based peaked T-waves — CDS should apply Short QT differential: confirm non-territorial distribution (IT) and rule out hypercalcemia (ST abbreviation without peaked T) and digitalis"`
- RRC → CDS: `cross_domain_hint: "QTc <340ms measured — CDS should integrate MR T-wave morphology (narrow-based = Short QT syndrome) vs MR ST abbreviation (hypercalcemia) for specific diagnosis"`

### CDS Specific Role
CDS resolves the peaked T-wave differential for short QT syndrome by applying three key discriminators: Short QT syndrome produces narrow-based peaked T-waves with QTc <340ms in a non-territorial distribution; hyperacute ischemia produces broad-based peaked T-waves with normal QTc in a territorial distribution with reciprocal changes; hyperkalemia produces symmetric tent-like peaked T-waves with normal or prolonged QTc diffusely across all leads. CDS uses MR's T-wave base width characterization, IT's territorial assessment, and RRC's QTc measurement to generate the specific diagnosis and flag the arrhythmia risk (Short QT syndrome carries significant VF/SCD risk requiring cardiology referral).

## 9. Edge Cases and Pitfalls

1. **QT measurement error in peaked T waves**: Automatic algorithms often misidentify the T-wave end when T is tall and peaked, under-measuring QT. Manual verification required for QTc ≤360 ms.
2. **QU confusion**: Hypokalemia creates tall T + prominent U, making the QU interval look like a very long QT. In SQTS, U waves are absent or unremarkable. If apparent "QT" is long but QTc is short, re-examine for U wave merge.
3. **Atrial fibrillation masking**: In AF, variable RR intervals make QTc unreliable. Measure on the longest cycle (smallest HR artifact). SQTS-associated AF in a young patient without structural disease warrants QT scrutiny on return to sinus rhythm.
4. **Normal variant overlap**: QTc 340–360 ms may be seen in healthy young males without SQTS. Clinical context (symptoms, family history, genetic testing) essential before diagnosing.
5. **Digitalis in elderly patients**: Digitalis shortens QT and produces scooped ST changes; drug history mandatory in any short QT case.
6. **Rate-dependent QT**: Sinus tachycardia shortens QT physiologically; confirm shortening persists after adequate rate correction and is not rate-artifact.
