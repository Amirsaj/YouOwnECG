## 1. Pathophysiology Root Cause

### 1.1 What Goes Wrong (Anatomy/Physiology)

NSTEMI is an acute myocardial infarction in which the occluding thrombus is either non-occlusive (partial occlusion) or transiently occlusive with spontaneous partial reperfusion. In contrast to STEMI, where a complete occlusion cuts off all flow distally, NSTEMI typically involves a ruptured or eroded plaque with a platelet-rich (white) thrombus that reduces — but does not eliminate — antegrade flow. Collateral vessels may also provide partial perfusion to the at-risk territory.

The functional result is ischemia confined predominantly to the subendocardium — the innermost layer of myocardium that is most vulnerable because it:
- Has the highest oxygen demand (greatest wall tension)
- Is furthest from the epicardial coronary supply
- Is compressed during systole, further reducing perfusion

Because the injury does not extend fully through the myocardial wall (not transmural), NSTEMI does not produce the same transmural injury current that causes ST elevation in STEMI. Instead, subendocardial ischemia generates an injury vector directed toward the endocardium — pointing away from all surface leads — which manifests as ST depression on the ECG.

Importantly, in ~40% of NSTEMI cases the 12-lead ECG is entirely normal at presentation. This is the single most important clinical fact about NSTEMI ECG interpretation: a normal ECG does NOT exclude NSTEMI. Diagnosis requires troponin elevation with a rise-and/or-fall kinetic pattern consistent with acute myocardial injury.

### 1.2 Electrical Consequence

The subendocardial ischemia creates an injury current that is fundamentally opposite in direction to the STEMI injury current:

**STEMI injury current**: directed from healthy myocardium toward the epicardial injury zone (outward from the heart surface) → ST elevation in overlying leads.

**NSTEMI/subendocardial injury current**: directed inward (toward the endocardium, away from all surface leads) → ST depression in surface leads, because the injury vector points away from all positive electrodes.

At the cellular level:
- Subendocardial cells have shortened action potential duration due to ATP depletion and K+ channel opening (IK-ATP)
- The shorter action potential in the inner layer reverses the normal endocardium-to-epicardium repolarization gradient
- This reversed gradient produces ST depression and T-wave inversions in leads overlying the ischemic territory
- Diastolic injury current: the depolarized resting potential of subendocardial cells creates a current that depresses the TQ segment, which (as with STEMI) appears as apparent ST depression when the TQ is used as the isoelectric baseline

### 1.3 Why It Appears on ECG

Because the subendocardial injury vector points inward (away from all surface electrodes), no single group of leads shows ST elevation — all leads overlying the ischemic zone show ST depression. The magnitude of depression reflects the extent and severity of ischemia.

**aVR is the exception**: aVR has its positive pole oriented toward the right shoulder (roughly opposite to the mean cardiac vector). The inward injury vector has a small component that projects positively onto aVR, explaining why aVR frequently shows ST elevation during subendocardial ischemia or diffuse ischemia. Marked aVR elevation (≥1 mm) in the setting of diffuse ST depression is a high-risk pattern suggesting left main or proximal LAD disease.

**T-wave inversions**: When the subendocardial ischemia causes delayed or reversed repolarization in the innermost layers, the T-wave vector shifts away from the ischemic territory → T-wave inversions in those leads. These may persist after chest pain resolution as post-ischemic T-wave changes and can represent the only ECG finding on arrival if the acute episode has partially resolved.

## 2. ECG Presentation — Lead by Lead

### 2.1 Diagnostic Criteria (2025 AHA/ESC Guidelines)

| Criterion | Threshold | Source |
|-----------|-----------|--------|
| ST depression (horizontal or downsloping) | ≥0.5 mm in ≥2 contiguous leads | ESC 2023 NSTE-ACS Guidelines; 2025 AHA/ACC |
| T-wave inversion | ≥1 mm in ≥2 contiguous leads with dominant R wave | ESC 2023 NSTE-ACS Guidelines |
| New LBBB in ischemic context | New or presumed new — raises suspicion; use Sgarbossa | 2025 AHA/ACC |
| Transient ST elevation | <20 minutes duration, then normalizes or converts to depression | ESC 2023 — indicates vasospasm or subtotal occlusion |
| Normal ECG | Does NOT exclude NSTEMI | ESC 2023; ~40% of NSTEMI have normal initial ECG |
| Troponin kinetics required | Rise AND/OR fall with at least one value above 99th percentile URL | 2018 Fourth Universal Definition of MI (Thygesen) |
| Serial ECGs | Repeat at 0h, 1h (or 0h/2h), 3h — dynamic changes increase diagnostic yield | ESC 0h/1h algorithm |

### 2.2 Lead-by-Lead Manifestation

| Lead | Expected Finding | Why (Vector Explanation) | Sensitivity |
|------|-----------------|--------------------------|-------------|
| I | ST depression (if lateral ischemia) | Faces lateral LV wall; subendocardial injury vector projects negatively | Moderate |
| II | ST depression (inferior territory) | Faces inferior wall at ~60°; subendocardial vector points away | Moderate |
| III | ST depression or normal | Faces inferior wall at ~120°; less reliable | Low |
| aVR | ST elevation ≥0.5–1 mm | Positive pole opposite to main cardiac vector; inward injury vector projects positively | High (if present, high-risk marker) |
| aVL | ST depression or T-wave inversion (lateral ischemia) | Faces high lateral wall; injury vector points away | Moderate |
| aVF | ST depression (inferior ischemia) | Faces inferior wall; subendocardial vector projects negatively | Moderate |
| V1 | ST depression; T-wave inversion | Faces RV septum/posterior LV; posterior ischemia can mirror here | Moderate |
| V2 | ST depression; T-wave inversion | Anterior septal lead; subendocardial LAD territory ischemia | Moderate–High |
| V3 | ST depression; T-wave inversion | Anterior wall; same as V2 | Moderate–High |
| V4 | ST depression; T-wave inversion | Mid-anterior wall; LAD territory | Moderate |
| V5 | ST depression or normal | Lateral wall; circumflex territory | Low–Moderate |
| V6 | ST depression or normal | Lateral wall; circumflex territory | Low–Moderate |

### 2.3 Key Leads (Most Diagnostic)

- **V2-V4**: Most commonly affected in anterior subendocardial ischemia (LAD territory); ST depression and T-wave inversions here are the most sensitive precordial finding.
- **II, aVF**: Inferior subendocardial ischemia (RCA/circumflex territory).
- **aVR**: ST elevation in aVR is the single highest-risk ECG marker in NSTEMI — elevation ≥1 mm in aVR with diffuse ST depression in ≥6 leads should trigger immediate cardiology consultation for possible left main or proximal LAD occlusion.
- **V1**: Horizontal ST depression in V1 may represent posterior STEMI equivalence — always check posterior leads (V7-V9) if V1 depression is isolated or predominant.

### 2.4 Beat-by-Beat Considerations

- NSTEMI findings are typically constant across all beats (not rate-dependent, not alternating) during active ischemia.
- Dynamic changes across serial ECGs are the hallmark: worsening ST depression during chest pain, improvement or resolution after pain resolves or after nitrates/anti-ischemic therapy.
- Post-NSTEMI T-wave inversions (after resolution of the acute event) may appear in the hours following the episode and can persist for days to weeks — this pattern (anterior T-wave inversions in V2-V4 with preserved R waves) is the classic "post-NSTEMI" or "post-ischemic" ECG signature.
- Rate-related ST changes (ST depression worsening with tachycardia) may be present during demand ischemia triggers.

## 3. Morphology Details (What the Agent Must See)

### 3.1 P-wave Changes

- P-waves are normal in uncomplicated NSTEMI.
- If the ischemia extends to or involves the atria (rare), P-wave morphology changes may occur.
- Sinus tachycardia is common as a reflex response to pain, anxiety, or hemodynamic compromise — P-wave rate increases but morphology remains normal.

### 3.2 PR Interval Changes

- PR interval is typically normal in NSTEMI.
- First-degree AV block may coexist in inferior territory NSTEMI (RCA territory includes AV nodal artery in 80-90% of individuals).
- New PR changes in the context of ischemia should raise concern for ischemic AV nodal disease.

### 3.3 QRS Complex Changes

- QRS is typically narrow and normal in early NSTEMI (no completed infarction).
- As myocardial necrosis progresses over hours, pathological Q waves may begin to appear — Q wave development indicates the NSTEMI is evolving toward transmural infarction or that significant necrosis has occurred.
- The absence of Q waves does NOT mean the ECG is normal; NSTEMI characteristically does NOT produce Q waves in the acute phase (hence "non-Q-wave MI" was the older terminology).
- New LBBB or RBBB may complicate the picture if the infarct involves the conduction system.

### 3.4 ST Segment Changes

- Horizontal or downsloping ST depression ≥0.5 mm at J+80ms in ≥2 contiguous leads.
- Downsloping ST depression is more specific for ischemia than upsloping ST depression.
- Upsloping ST depression (≤1 mm) can be a normal finding especially during tachycardia and should not alone trigger a NSTEMI diagnosis.
- Transient ST elevation (<20 minutes) that resolves may represent vasospasm or a very brief period of complete occlusion (variant/Prinzmetal angina overlapping with NSTEMI).
- ST depression measured at J-point + 60-80 ms (J+60 or J+80 ms) per current guidelines.

### 3.5 T-wave Changes

- T-wave inversions ≥1 mm in ≥2 contiguous leads with a dominant R wave.
- Symmetric, deep T-wave inversions are more specific for ischemia than asymmetric inversions.
- Wellens-type T-wave inversions (deep, symmetric inversions in V2-V4 with preserved R waves) represent a specific post-ischemic pattern indicating a critical LAD lesion after a resolved episode of chest pain — this is a high-risk finding requiring urgent catheterization even if the patient is currently pain-free.
- Biphasic T-waves (Wellens Type A) represent an earlier stage.
- Hyperacute T-waves (tall, peaked, symmetric, broad-based) may be present in the very early phase before ST changes develop.
- Flattened T-waves (isoelectric) represent a non-specific change that may indicate early or resolving ischemia.

### 3.6 QT/QTc Changes

- QTc may be prolonged during active ischemia — the shortened action potential in ischemic cells can paradoxically affect the global QT depending on extent of involvement.
- Significant QTc prolongation (>500 ms) raises torsades risk; monitor closely especially if QT-prolonging medications are used.
- QTc shortening is not expected in NSTEMI.

### 3.7 Other Features

- **Pseudo-normalization**: In a patient with known prior T-wave inversions, acute ischemia can paradoxically make the T-waves appear "upright" (pseudo-normalization) — this is a dangerous pitfall where a "normal-looking" ECG actually represents a new ischemic event.
- **R-wave amplitude changes**: Acutely developing R-wave reduction in precordial leads may indicate evolving anterior infarction, even before Q waves appear.

## 4. Differential Diagnosis

### 4.1 Mimics (What Looks Like This But Isn't)

| Mimic Condition | Shared Features | Distinguishing Features |
|----------------|-----------------|------------------------|
| Posterior STEMI | ST depression V1-V3, upright T waves in V1-V3 | Posterior STEMI: tall R wave V1-V2, upright prominent T wave V1-V2; confirm with posterior leads V7-V9 showing ST elevation ≥0.5 mm |
| LVH strain pattern | ST depression V4-V6, T-wave inversions lateral leads | LVH: voltage criteria met, strain pattern in leads with tallest R waves, no dynamic change, chronic history |
| LBBB (new or old) | ST changes secondary to LBBB (expected discordance) | LBBB: broad QRS, LBBB morphology; secondary ST changes are expected and do not indicate ischemia unless Sgarbossa criteria met |
| Digoxin effect | Downsloping ST depression ("reverse tick") V5-V6 | Digoxin: characteristic scooped/downsloping morphology, therapeutic drug level, no troponin rise |
| Hypokalemia | ST depression, T-wave flattening/inversion, U waves | Hypokalemia: prominent U waves, prolonged QU interval, electrolyte levels confirm |
| Pulmonary embolism | ST depression, T-wave inversions V1-V3 | PE: S1Q3T3 pattern, sinus tachycardia, right heart strain, V1-V3 T-wave inversions; clinical context, D-dimer, echo |
| Takotsubo cardiomyopathy | ST depression or T-wave inversions in anterior leads | Takotsubo: often women post-stress event, diffuse T-wave inversions evolving over hours, apical ballooning on echo, troponin mildly elevated |
| Early repolarization | ST "elevation" variant | Early repolarization: concave upward ST, notched J-point, young patients, no dynamic change, no symptoms |

### 4.2 Coexisting Conditions

- **LBBB**: Completely masks ischemic ST changes; requires Sgarbossa/Smith-modified criteria (→ Node 2.7.66).
- **LVH**: Strain pattern may obscure or exaggerate ischemic ST depression in lateral leads.
- **Prior MI with T-wave inversions**: A new NSTEMI on top of prior T-wave inversions may show pseudo-normalization (dangerous pitfall).
- **Paced rhythm**: Right ventricular pacing creates LBBB-like pattern; ischemia detection requires Sgarbossa-equivalent analysis.
- **Atrial fibrillation**: Baseline may be noisy, making precise ST measurement harder; rate-related ST changes from fast ventricular response may mimic ischemia.

### 6.1 Reasoning Domains Required to Detect This Condition

- **ST segment quantification**: precise measurement of J-point deviation (horizontal vs. downsloping vs. upsloping morphology) in multiple leads simultaneously
- **Lead-group correlation**: identifying which anatomical territory is affected based on lead groupings (anterior/inferior/lateral)
- **Pattern recognition**: distinguishing ischemic ST depression from secondary ST changes (LBBB, LVH strain, digoxin)
- **Contextual reasoning**: NSTEMI cannot be diagnosed from ECG alone — the system must flag the ECG as "consistent with NSTEMI" and explicitly state that troponin data is required
- **Temporal reasoning**: serial ECG comparison to detect dynamic changes
- **High-risk pattern recognition**: aVR elevation + diffuse ST depression = left main equivalent
- **Differential exclusion**: posterior STEMI must be actively excluded when V1-V3 ST depression is present

### 6.2 Feature Dependencies

**Essential features:**
- J-point deviation magnitude per lead (ST depression in mm)
- ST morphology classification (horizontal / downsloping / upsloping)
- Lead grouping (which contiguous leads are affected)
- QRS width and morphology (to detect LBBB/LVH that would confound interpretation)

**Supporting features:**
- T-wave amplitude and symmetry
- R-wave amplitude (to confirm dominant R wave for T-wave inversion significance)
- aVR ST elevation
- Rate and rhythm (tachycardia lowers specificity of ST changes)
- Prior ECG comparison (dynamic change detection)

**Exclusionary features:**
- LBBB present → standard ischemia criteria do not apply; switch to Sgarbossa
- QRS >200 ms → artifact or conduction disorder first
- Rate >150 bpm → rule out SVT/AFL with rate-related ST changes

### 6.3 Cross-Condition Interactions

- **STEMI**: If ST depression is actually posterior STEMI (V1-V3 depression with tall R waves), this is a STEMI-equivalent requiring catheterization — the system must flag this and recommend posterior leads.
- **Sgarbossa (Node 2.7.66)**: If LBBB present, NSTEMI assessment is replaced by Sgarbossa analysis; NSTEMI cannot be detected in the presence of LBBB.
- **Wellens Syndrome (Node 2.7.X)**: Wellens pattern is a post-ischemic NSTEMI pattern — T-wave inversions V2-V4 with preserved R waves after chest pain resolution. This is a specific subtype of NSTEMI ECG presentation.
- **Subendocardial Ischemia (Node 2.7.69)**: The broad category; NSTEMI is a subset of subendocardial ischemia where troponin elevation confirms myocardial injury has occurred.
- **Unstable Angina (Node 2.7.X)**: Identical ECG findings to NSTEMI; differentiation is by troponin only — the ECG-reading system cannot distinguish UA from NSTEMI.

### 6.4 Reasoning Chain Sketch

**Minimum chain (high confidence):**
1. Detect ST depression ≥0.5 mm in ≥2 contiguous leads with horizontal or downsloping morphology
2. Confirm QRS is narrow (no LBBB confound) OR apply Sgarbossa if LBBB present
3. Classify territory (anterior/inferior/lateral/diffuse)
4. Check aVR for elevation
5. Flag as "ECG consistent with NSTEMI/subendocardial ischemia — troponin required"

**Full chain (complete evidence assembly):**
1. Rhythm and rate classification
2. QRS width and morphology assessment (exclude LBBB, LVH)
3. Measure J-point deviation in all 12 leads
4. Classify ST morphology in each affected lead
5. Check V1-V3 for tall R waves (posterior STEMI exclusion)
6. T-wave analysis in all leads
7. Compare with prior ECG if available (dynamic change)
8. Synthesize territory, severity, and high-risk markers
9. Provide structured output: territory, risk level, note troponin dependency

### 6.5 Confidence Anchors

- **HIGH confidence**: New horizontal ST depression ≥1 mm in ≥4 leads + aVR elevation ≥1 mm in a patient with chest pain and normal QRS → near-certain ischemia
- **MODERATE confidence**: Isolated T-wave inversions V2-V4, new from prior ECG → probable post-ischemic change (Wellens pattern)
- **LOW confidence**: ST depression 0.5 mm in 2 leads only, upsloping morphology → non-specific; may be normal variant or demand ischemia
- **Pathognomonic**: No single ECG finding is pathognomonic for NSTEMI — diagnosis REQUIRES troponin; ECG provides supporting risk stratification
- **Possible**: Any new ST depression in correct clinical context
- **Probable**: Horizontal/downsloping ST depression ≥1 mm in ≥2 contiguous leads, new from baseline
- **Definite**: Only with troponin elevation — ECG cannot achieve "definite NSTEMI" alone

**Architecture:** Option E — 3 Parallel Phase 1 Specialists + 1 Sequential CDS (Node 2.1 Gate Passed 2026-03-27)

| Agent | Role for NSTEMI | Key Features Used |
|-------|--------------------------|-------------------|
| **RRC** (Rhythm/Rate/Conduction) | Supporting | Heart rate, rhythm (sinus vs ectopic), any arrhythmia as a complication (AF, VT), QRS duration (if LBBB present — modifies ST interpretation) |
| **IT** (Ischemia/Territory) | Primary | Horizontal or downsloping ST depression ≥0.5 mm in ≥2 contiguous leads, ischemic T-wave inversions (symmetric, ≥1 mm) in ≥2 contiguous leads, ST depression distribution and coronary territory mapping (LAD territory: V1–V5; RCA/LCx: II/III/aVF or I/aVL), transient ST elevation <20 min (if visible), absence of persistent ST elevation |
| **MR** (Morphology/Repolarization) | Supporting | T-wave morphology — symmetric inversion depth and lead distribution; distinguishes ischemic symmetric inversion from LVH strain (asymmetric), Wellens pattern (V2–V3 specific), or CNS T-waves (diffuse, very deep) |
| **CDS** (Cross-Domain Synthesis) | Required — integrates territorial ST/T findings; resolves NSTEMI vs other ST depression causes | Integrates IT's territorial ST depression with MR's T-wave morphology assessment; resolves differential: NSTEMI vs posterior STEMI mirror (horizontal V1–V3 depression — CDS checks R/S from MR), vs pericarditis (concave ST from MR), vs LVH strain (asymmetric T from MR), vs Wellens (V2–V3 specific pattern), vs digoxin (scooped ST morphology) |

### Primary Agent
**IT** — NSTEMI is defined by ischemic ST depression and/or T-wave changes in a coronary territory, which is the IT agent's core ischemia/territory domain.

### Cross-Domain Hints
- IT emits `cross_domain_hint: "Horizontal ST depression in V1–V3 — posterior STEMI mirror image possible; MR R/S ratio in V1 and RRC QRS assessment required before NSTEMI classification is finalized"` when horizontal depression is prominent in V1–V3.
- MR emits `cross_domain_hint: "T-wave inversion morphology in V2–V3 is symmetric and deep — Wellens syndrome pattern possible; IT must evaluate for absence of ST elevation and Q-waves"` when Wellens-morphology T-waves are detected.

### CDS Specific Role
CDS integrates IT's territorial ST/T findings with MR's T-wave morphology analysis to generate the final NSTEMI diagnosis while resolving four key differentials: (1) posterior STEMI — if IT's V1–V3 horizontal depression is accompanied by MR's dominant R in V1, CDS reclassifies as posterior STEMI equivalent; (2) Wellens — if MR identifies symmetric V2–V3 T-wave inversion with IT confirming absence of ST elevation and Q-waves, CDS classifies as Wellens syndrome; (3) LVH strain — if MR reports asymmetric T-wave changes with known hypertrophy voltage, CDS reduces ischemia confidence; (4) pericarditis — if MR shows concave ST morphology with diffuse distribution, CDS flags the competing diagnosis.

## 9. Edge Cases and Pitfalls

- **Normal ECG in NSTEMI (~40%)**: The most dangerous pitfall. A completely normal ECG does not exclude NSTEMI. Any patient with chest pain and risk factors requires serial troponins regardless of ECG findings.
- **Pseudo-normalization**: A patient with prior T-wave inversions due to old MI may develop an "upright" T-wave during an acute ischemic event, making the ECG look deceptively normal or improved. Always compare with the most recent available baseline ECG.
- **Posterior STEMI masquerading as NSTEMI**: ST depression in V1-V3 may be the reciprocal of a posterior STEMI — which IS a complete occlusion requiring emergent catheterization. Look for: tall, broad R waves in V1-V2, prominent upright T-waves in V1-V2. Always obtain posterior leads (V7-V9) when V1-V3 ST depression is the dominant finding.
- **Left main/proximal LAD as NSTEMI equivalent**: Diffuse ST depression in ≥6 leads + aVR elevation ≥1 mm should be treated as an emergent left main equivalent, even in the absence of classical ST elevation. This is a common teaching gap — these patients require immediate catheterization comparable to STEMI urgency.
- **LBBB masking ischemia**: In the presence of LBBB (new or old), standard ischemia criteria are invalid. The secondary ST-T changes of LBBB can completely obscure NSTEMI changes. Apply Sgarbossa criteria (Node 2.7.66) and maintain a high clinical suspicion.
- **Rate-related ST depression**: Sinus tachycardia or SVT with rates >120 bpm commonly produces upsloping ST depression that is rate-related and not ischemic. This resolves with rate control. The upsloping morphology (vs. horizontal/downsloping) is key to this distinction.
- **Wellens T-wave pattern**: When a patient is pain-free and ECG shows deep, symmetric T-wave inversions in V2-V4 with preserved R-wave progression, this is NOT a reassuring finding — it represents a high-risk post-ischemic pattern (Wellens Type B) indicating a critical proximal LAD stenosis. These patients need urgent catheterization even though the acute episode has resolved.
- **Takotsubo cardiomyopathy**: Can produce diffuse anterior T-wave inversions, QT prolongation, and modest troponin elevation — clinically identical to NSTEMI on ECG. Differentiation by echo (apical ballooning) and typically in older women after emotional or physical stress.
