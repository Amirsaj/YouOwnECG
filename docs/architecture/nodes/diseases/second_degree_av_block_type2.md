## 1. Pathophysiology Root Cause

### 1.1 What Goes Wrong

Mobitz Type II second degree AV block is an intermittent, unpredictable failure of AV conduction that occurs below the AV node — within the His bundle or the bundle branches. Unlike Type I (Wenckebach), the site of block is in the fast-response, sodium-dependent His-Purkinje tissue, not the slow-response calcium-dependent AV nodal cells.

The underlying mechanisms:
- **Bilateral bundle branch disease:** Conduction must succeed in BOTH right and left bundles to depolarize the ventricles. If both are diseased, a single beat may arrive when one or both are refractory, failing to conduct. This is why Type II almost always coexists with bundle branch block or bifascicular block.
- **Infranodal fibrosis:** Lev disease (fibrocalcific degeneration of the left-sided conduction system) and Lenègre disease (idiopathic fibrosclerosis of the His-Purkinje system) are the most common structural causes in older patients.
- **Acute anterior MI:** LAD territory infarction can destroy the bundle of His or both bundle branches, producing sudden infranodal block. This is far more dangerous than the AV nodal Wenckebach of inferior MI — the escape rhythm is ventricular (wide, slow, unreliable).
- **Cardiac surgery, TAVI:** Direct trauma to infranodal conduction.
- **Infiltrative disease:** Sarcoidosis, amyloidosis, Chagas disease.

The critical distinction: because the block is in the His-Purkinje system (fast-response cells with a sharp refractory period, not a graded one), there is NO gradual fatigue phenomenon. Conduction either succeeds completely or fails completely — producing the "all-or-nothing" appearance of constant PR with sudden dropped QRS.

### 1.2 Electrical Consequence

The conducted beats traverse the AV node normally (constant PR) and then travel normally through whichever infranodal pathway is still functional — but this pathway may be diseased, producing bundle branch block morphology. When a beat arrives during the refractory period of the diseased infranodal tissue, it fails entirely — the ventricles are not excited.

Because the failure is unpredictable (no graded fatigue):
- PR interval is CONSTANT in conducted beats (no progressive lengthening)
- QRS is typically WIDE (≥ 120ms) reflecting the underlying bundle branch disease
- The dropped beat occurs suddenly with no warning — no PR change precedes it
- The escape rhythm after a prolonged pause is junctional or ventricular — often slow and unreliable

### 1.3 Why It Appears on ECG

The surface ECG shows a regular sinus rhythm with constant PR intervals, then suddenly a P-wave appears without a QRS following it. The pause contains the non-conducted P-wave. After the pause, conduction resumes with the same (constant) PR. There is no PR lengthening sequence — the absent QRS is an abrupt event, not the culmination of a progressive process.

The wide QRS on conducted beats reflects the infranodal disease responsible for the block. A narrow QRS in a patient with apparent Mobitz Type II should prompt reconsideration of the diagnosis — true Type II with narrow QRS is very rare and implies block within the His bundle itself.

## 2. ECG Presentation — Lead by Lead

### 2.1 Diagnostic Criteria (2025 AHA/ESC Guidelines)

| Criterion | Threshold | Source |
|-----------|-----------|--------|
| PR interval in conducted beats | Constant (variation < 20ms beat-to-beat) | AHA/ESC 2018 |
| Dropped QRS | Sudden, without preceding PR lengthening | AHA/ESC 2018 |
| QRS duration | Wide (≥ 120ms) in most cases — LBBB or RBBB pattern | AHA/ESC 2018 |
| Conduction ratio | Any ratio except 2:1 (which cannot be typed alone) | AHA/ESC 2018 |
| Conducted beats | ≥ 2 consecutive conducted beats before each dropped beat required to verify constant PR | Clinical standard |
| PR reset after pause | Not applicable — PR is constant; no reset phenomenon | AHA/ESC 2018 |

**Warning:** If only 2:1 conduction is observed, the block cannot be definitively typed as Type I or II. A longer strip capturing 3:2 or higher ratio sequences is required.

### 2.2 Lead-by-Lead Manifestation

| Lead | Expected Finding | Why | Sensitivity |
|------|-----------------|-----|-------------|
| I | Wide QRS (RBBB or LBBB); constant PR; sudden dropped QRS | Lateral lead — QRS morphology well seen | Moderate |
| II | PRIMARY: constant PR; P-wave without QRS in pause; wide QRS | Best P-wave visibility; PR measurement most reliable | High |
| III | Wide QRS with inferior morphology; constant PR | Inferior lead — LBBB/RBBB patterns visible | Moderate |
| aVR | Inverted P and QRS; confirms sinus P-wave | Opposite to II — confirms atrial origin | Moderate |
| aVL | Wide QRS; LBBB shows broad monophasic R | Lateral lead — LBBB morphology visible | Moderate |
| aVF | Wide QRS; constant PR; inferior context | Inferior lead — LBBB QS or RBBB patterns | Moderate |
| V1 | Key for QRS morphology: RBBB = rSR'; LBBB = QS or rS; biphasic P visible | Right-sided lead — best for bundle branch classification | High |
| V2 | Wide QRS morphology; P-wave in baseline | Anterior — transitional for bundle branch patterns | Moderate |
| V3–V4 | Transitional QRS; PR constant | Precordial transition zone | Low–Moderate |
| V5 | Wide R (LBBB) or RS (RBBB); constant PR | Lateral | Low–Moderate |
| V6 | Monophasic R (LBBB) or qRS (RBBB); constant PR | Lateral — LBBB pattern completion | Moderate |

**Best leads for diagnosis:** II (constant PR and dropped beat), V1 (bundle branch morphology classification), rhythm strip at least 10 seconds.

### 2.3 Key Leads

- **Lead II:** Primary lead for demonstrating constant PR before and after the dropped beat. The non-conducted P-wave is visible as an isolated upright deflection in the pause.
- **V1:** Defines the bundle branch block pattern (RBBB vs. LBBB), which is clinically critical for establishing infranodal location and prognostic risk.
- **V6:** Completes the bundle branch block characterization; LBBB produces a broad monophasic R in V6.
- **Rhythm strip (10+ seconds):** Essential — must show at least 3 conducted beats with constant PR before concluding Type II.

### 2.4 Beat-by-Beat Considerations

Type II analysis requires:
1. Confirm sinus P-waves at regular PP intervals (constant atrial rate)
2. Identify all P-waves, including those in the pause
3. Measure PR on EVERY conducted beat — confirm no progressive lengthening
4. Confirm QRS width ≥ 120ms in conducted beats
5. Classify bundle branch block pattern (RBBB or LBBB)
6. Note the dropped beat: no PR change preceded it
7. Confirm PR is identical before and after the pause

The minimum strip requirement is at least one complete sequence showing 3 or more conducted beats with constant PR, followed by one dropped beat, followed by resumption with the same PR.

## 3. Morphology Details

### 3.1 P-Wave Morphology

Normal sinus P-waves: upright in I and II, inverted in aVR. Morphology is constant across all P-waves — both conducted and non-conducted. The non-conducted P-wave in the pause is morphologically identical to the conducted ones. P-wave abnormality (e.g., notched P suggesting atrial disease) does not alter Type II diagnosis but is an additional context feature.

### 3.2 PR Interval

The critical diagnostic feature: the PR interval is CONSTANT. This distinguishes Type II from Type I at a glance when multiple conducted beats precede the dropped beat. The constancy must be demonstrated in at least 2–3 consecutive conducted beats before the dropped one. If any PR variability is seen, consider Type I with atypical Wenckebach, rather than dismissing Type II.

Numerical range: may be normal (120–200ms), prolonged (>200ms — if first degree AV block coexists), or rarely short. The key is not the absolute value but the absence of progressive change.

### 3.3 QRS Complex

Wide (≥ 120ms) in the vast majority of Type II cases, reflecting infranodal disease:
- **LBBB morphology:** Broad notched R in I, aVL, V5, V6; QS or rS in V1; QS in V2 often; no septal Q in V5–V6. This is the more common pattern in degenerative conduction disease.
- **RBBB morphology:** rSR' in V1 (M-shaped); deep slurred S in I, V5, V6. May reflect incomplete right bundle disease with a diseased left fascicle also involved.
- **Bifascicular block pattern:** RBBB + LAHB (left axis deviation) or RBBB + LPHB (right axis deviation) are the most common pre-existing patterns that progress to Type II.

Narrow QRS in apparent Type II should raise doubt — consider: (a) block within the His bundle itself (rare true Type II with narrow QRS), or (b) misclassification of Type I with constant first PR of Wenckebach cycle (first-beat PR may appear stable if only 2 conducted beats seen).

### 3.4 ST Segment

Not altered by Type II block itself. Any ST abnormalities reflect the underlying etiology. In acute anterior MI with new Type II block, expect ST elevation in V1–V4 — this is a dual catastrophe requiring immediate intervention. In degenerative Type II, ST segments are typically isoelectric.

### 3.5 T-Wave

The T-waves in conducted beats may be discordant from the QRS (expected with LBBB or RBBB — secondary T-wave changes). The T-wave of the last conducted beat before the dropped beat is not elongated or altered by the Wenckebach mechanism (unlike Type I, there is no pause-dependent T-wave change in Type II).

### 3.6 QT/QTc

Not directly altered by Type II block. However, the underlying conditions causing Type II (e.g., sarcoidosis, anterior MI with reperfusion, antiarrhythmic drugs) may independently prolong QTc. Measure QTc on conducted beats using the widest QRS lead.

### 3.7 Other Features

- **Pauses:** The pause containing the dropped QRS has a specific duration: exactly 2× the PP interval (one PP + one non-conducted PP). This is different from Wenckebach (where the pause is < 2× RR) and helps confirm Type II.
- **Escape beats:** If the pause is prolonged (≥ 2.5–3 seconds), a junctional or ventricular escape beat may appear with a different morphology. This is not Type II itself but a consequence of it.

## 4. Differential Diagnosis

### 4.1 Mimics

| Condition | How it Mimics Type II | Distinguishing Feature |
|-----------|----------------------|----------------------|
| Mobitz Type I (Wenckebach) | Both have dropped QRS; short strips may show only 1–2 conducted beats | Type I: PR progressively lengthens; first PR after pause is shortest; QRS narrow |
| 2:1 AV Block | Cannot type from 2:1 ratio; one conducted beat per cycle | Requires longer strip with other ratios; narrow QRS → likely Type I; wide QRS → likely Type II |
| Nonconducted PAC | Sudden pause with visible early P-wave | PAC P-wave morphology differs; occurs EARLY (short coupling interval); preceding PR is normal |
| High-grade AV Block | Multiple consecutive dropped P-waves; looks like Type II × multiple | High-grade: ≥ 2 consecutive non-conducted P-waves; may include Type II as the underlying mechanism |
| Sinus exit block (Type II) | SA nodal block: PP pause = 2× PP | Sinus exit block: no P-wave in pause; Type II: P-wave present in pause but no QRS |
| AV dissociation | P-waves "walk" through QRS complexes | True AV dissociation: PP and RR independent rhythms; no consistent PR relationship |
| Hyperkalemia with conduction block | Wide QRS, variable conduction | Hyperkalemia: peaked T-waves, absent P-waves (sine wave); PR prolongation; context (renal failure, K+ level) |

### 4.2 Coexisting Conditions

- **Anterior STEMI:** New Type II block in anterior STEMI indicates bilateral bundle branch ischemia from LAD occlusion — this is a cardiac emergency. The escape rhythm is ventricular (not junctional as in inferior STEMI) and is unreliable. Immediate transcutaneous pacing and expedited reperfusion are indicated.
- **Bifascicular block:** RBBB + LAHB is the most common precursor pattern. In a patient with known bifascicular block who develops new syncope, Type II block in the remaining fascicle is the most likely cause — requires permanent pacemaker.
- **Trifascicular block:** RBBB + LAHB (or LPHB) + first degree AV block = disease in all three fascicles. This combination strongly predicts progression to complete heart block.
- **Sarcoidosis:** Can produce Type II, complete heart block, and ventricular tachycardia simultaneously. High suspicion in young patients with unexplained conduction disease.

## 5. STAT Classification

| Priority | Criteria |
|----------|----------|
| **STAT (RED)** | Any symptomatic Type II: syncope, presyncope, hemodynamic compromise — pacemaker urgently required |
| **STAT (RED)** | Type II + acute anterior STEMI — bilateral bundle branch ischemia; transcutaneous pacing immediately; expedited reperfusion |
| **STAT (RED)** | Type II + ventricular rate < 40bpm or long pauses (≥ 3 seconds) — risk of cardiac arrest |
| **URGENT (ORANGE)** | Newly discovered Type II, asymptomatic — cardiology consultation same day; admission for monitoring |
| **URGENT (ORANGE)** | Type II + bifascicular block + syncope — high-grade conduction disease; permanent pacemaker evaluation |
| **URGENT (ORANGE)** | Type II in known sarcoidosis/amyloidosis — ICD/pacemaker evaluation required |
| **MONITOR (AMBER)** | Incidental Type II without symptoms, stable rate — admit for monitoring and expedited electrophysiology evaluation |

**Default STAT level: URGENT (ORANGE) minimum. Type II is NEVER a benign finding. It predicts unpredictable progression to complete heart block. Pacemaker evaluation is indicated in ALL cases.**

## 6. Reasoning Complexity Analysis

> **NOTE**: This section documents reasoning complexity for Node 2.1 agent architecture research. It does not pre-assign agents.

### 6.1 Reasoning Domains Required

- **Interval measurement:** PR must be measured on EVERY conducted beat to confirm constancy
- **Pattern recognition (temporal):** Distinguish constant PR from subtly progressive PR (Type I vs. II)
- **QRS morphology analysis:** Bundle branch block classification (RBBB vs. LBBB) is integral to diagnosis
- **Rate and rhythm:** Identify all P-waves including non-conducted ones; calculate PP interval
- **Clinical context integration:** Anterior vs. inferior MI; drug history; known conduction disease
- **Differential reasoning:** Must exclude 2:1 block (untypable), Type I with short strip, blocked PAC

### 6.2 Feature Dependencies

Essential features:
- P-wave timing: all P-waves at constant PP interval (including in pauses)
- PR interval measured on each conducted beat (minimum 3 for constancy confirmation)
- QRS duration: wide (≥ 120ms) in conducted beats
- Dropped QRS: P without QRS, at expected timing, without preceding PR change

Supporting features:
- Bundle branch block morphology classification (RBBB vs. LBBB)
- Presence of bifascicular block pattern
- ST changes in anterior or inferior leads (contextual etiology)
- Heart rate and ventricular rate adequacy

Exclusion features:
- Progressive PR lengthening (would diagnose Type I instead)
- Early P-wave with different morphology (suggests blocked PAC rather than Type II)
- PR shortening after pause (Wenckebach reset — Type I)

### 6.3 Cross-Condition Interactions

- **2:1 AV Block (2.7.43):** Type II is the most dangerous cause of 2:1 block. The 2:1 ambiguity document (Node 2.7.43) cross-references Type II for clinical decision-making with wide QRS.
- **Complete AV Block (likely Node ~2.7.44):** Type II progresses to complete heart block unpredictably. The algorithm must flag this risk in the clinical output.
- **LBBB (2.7.X):** LBBB in the conducted beats is consistent with Type II infranodal block; it also masks anterior STEMI (Sgarbossa criteria required).
- **Anterior STEMI:** ST elevation in V1–V4 + new Type II = LAD catastrophe. These must be detected simultaneously.
- **Trifascicular block:** First degree AV block + bifascicular block pattern = trifascicular disease — must be co-reported.

### 6.4 Reasoning Chain Sketch

Minimum chain (high confidence Type II):
1. Confirm sinus P-waves at regular intervals
2. Identify ≥ 3 consecutive conducted P-QRS pairs
3. Measure PR on each: confirm constant (no progressive change)
4. Confirm QRS width ≥ 120ms in conducted beats
5. Identify non-conducted P-wave(s) at expected timing
6. Confirm no PR change precedes the dropped beat
7. Classify bundle branch pattern (supports infranodal localization)
→ Diagnosis: Mobitz Type II

Full chain adds:
- Exclude 2:1-only presentation (flag as "cannot type" if applicable)
- Check anterior/inferior ST changes for acute MI context
- Classify bifascicular block if present
- Assess ventricular rate adequacy and escape beat presence
- STAT assignment based on symptoms and etiology

### 6.5 Confidence Anchors

High confidence (near-pathognomonic):
- Constant PR (< 20ms variation) across ≥ 3 conducted beats + wide QRS + sudden dropped beat + PR unchanged after pause

Confidence lowering:
- Only 2 conducted beats visible before dropped beat (not enough to confirm constancy)
- QRS narrow (atypical; raises doubt)
- Any PR variation > 20ms (shifts toward Type I diagnosis)
- Short coupling interval of non-conducted P (suggests blocked PAC)

Minimum for "possible Type II":
- ≥ 2 conducted beats with similar PR + wide QRS + sudden dropped beat, AND longer strip unavailable

### 6.6 Difficulty Score

| Dimension | Score (1-5) | Notes |
|-----------|-------------|-------|
| Signal clarity needed | 3/5 | P-wave detection in pauses required; QRS onset for PR measurement on wide QRS is challenging |
| Number of leads required | 2/5 | II for PR measurement; V1+V6 for bundle branch classification |
| Cross-domain reasoning | 3/5 | PR constancy + QRS morphology + STEMI context + 2:1 ambiguity all required |
| Temporal pattern complexity | 3/5 | Multi-beat PR constancy check; strip length dependency for correct typing |
| Differential complexity | 3/5 | Type I with short strip, blocked PAC, 2:1 untypable all require active exclusion |
| Rarity in PTB-XL | 3/5 | Less common than Type I; fewer training examples; may require augmentation |
| **Overall difficulty** | **3.0/5** | **Infranodal block with high stakes; PR constancy + wide QRS are key features** |

| Agent | Role for Mobitz Type II | Key Features Used |
|-------|--------------------------|-------------------|
| **RRC** (Rhythm/Rate/Conduction) | Primary | Fixed PR interval on all conducted beats (no progressive lengthening), sudden dropped QRS without PR change, constant PP interval, QRS often wide (≥120 ms) in conducted beats, block ratio (2:1, 3:1) |
| **IT** (Ischemia/Territory) | Supporting | Anterior or inferior ST changes indicating acute MI as the etiology; Mobitz II in the setting of anterior STEMI indicates His-Purkinje ischemia |
| **MR** (Morphology/Repolarization) | Supporting | QRS duration and BBB morphology (RBBB or LBBB pattern) in conducted beats confirms His-Purkinje level block; wide QRS + sudden drop = Mobitz II until proven otherwise |
| **CDS** (Cross-Domain Synthesis) | Required — urgency escalation and ischemic cause detection | Integrates RRC's fixed-PR dropped-beat pattern with MR's wide QRS morphology to confirm Mobitz II; detects concurrent ischemia from IT to flag pacing urgency; flags high risk of progression to complete heart block |

### Primary Agent
**RRC** — Mobitz II is defined by fixed PR interval with sudden non-conducted P-waves; distinguishing it from Wenckebach requires beat-by-beat PR interval tracking, which is the core RRC function.

### Cross-Domain Hints
- RRC → MR: `cross_domain_hint: "sudden_dropped_QRS_fixed_PR — measure QRS duration and classify BBB morphology to confirm His-Purkinje level block"`
- RRC → IT: `cross_domain_hint: "mobitz_type_2_pattern — evaluate anterior and inferior ST elevation for acute MI etiology"`
- MR → CDS: `cross_domain_hint: "wide_QRS_in_conducted_beats — integrates with fixed PR drop to confirm infra-Hisian block level"`

### CDS Specific Role
CDS has a critical role for Mobitz II: it synthesizes RRC's conduction pattern with MR's QRS width to confirm infra-Hisian block level and applies high urgency classification given the risk of sudden progression to complete heart block. If IT concurrently reports anterior STEMI, CDS escalates to maximum urgency (large anterior MI + Mobitz II = high-grade block risk requiring urgent pacing). CDS explicitly notes that Mobitz II is never a benign finding and always requires escalation regardless of hemodynamic stability.

## 9. Edge Cases and Pitfalls

**Pitfall 1 — Short strip shows only 2 conducted beats before dropped beat:** With only 2 conducted beats, PR "constancy" cannot be reliably confirmed — a subtle Wenckebach cycle might have its first two beats with barely different PR (40ms increment then 20ms increment — both might appear "constant" on a 25mm/s strip). Always request longer strip. If unavailable, document as "probable Type II" with wide QRS context.

**Pitfall 2 — 2:1 AV block typed as Type II based on wide QRS alone:** Wide QRS in 2:1 block INCREASES the probability of Type II but does not confirm it. Some patients have LBBB + 2:1 Wenckebach (especially in inferior MI with pre-existing LBBB). The wide QRS is a risk flag, not a definitive classifier. Escalate STAT level for wide QRS 2:1 block regardless.

**Pitfall 3 — Misclassifying blocked PAC as Type II:** A blocked PAC bigeminy produces alternating conducted QRS and a pause with a P-wave — superficially similar to 2:1 Type II. Key distinction: the blocked P-wave in PAC bigeminy is EARLY (short coupling interval), has different morphology, and the preceding PR is not the same as the post-block beat. Type II: the blocked P-wave is ON TIME (same PP as all others).

**Pitfall 4 — LBBB masking anterior STEMI in Type II setting:** New Type II + LBBB in the context of chest pain = anterior STEMI until proven otherwise. Apply modified Sgarbossa criteria. Do not allow the Type II diagnosis to overshadow the concurrent STEMI assessment.

**Pitfall 5 — Isolated His bundle block (true Type II with narrow QRS):** Approximately 5% of Type II presentations are at the His bundle level with a narrow QRS. These patients have AV node intact (normal PR or slightly prolonged) and His-Purkinje intact BELOW the block, so conducted beats are narrow. This is clinically equivalent to infranodal Type II in prognosis and requires pacemaker. Diagnosis confirmed by His bundle electrogram.

**Pitfall 6 — Drug-induced Type II vs. structural disease:** Type II AV block caused by drugs (flecainide, propafenone, quinidine at toxic levels) affecting infranodal conduction exists but is rare. In drug-induced Type II, discontinuation of the offending agent may restore normal conduction. However, pacemaker protection should be considered during the acute phase regardless.

**Pitfall 7 — Vagal maneuvers worsening Type II:** Unlike Type I (which often improves with atropine or worsens with vagal maneuvers predictably), atropine may WORSEN Type II by increasing the atrial rate, causing more P-waves to arrive during infranodal refractoriness and increasing the proportion of blocked beats. Atropine is generally avoided in Type II; transcutaneous pacing is preferred.
