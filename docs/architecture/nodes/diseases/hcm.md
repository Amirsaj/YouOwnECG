## 1. Pathophysiology Root Cause

### 1.1 What Goes Wrong (Anatomy/Physiology)
Hypertrophic cardiomyopathy (HCM) is an autosomal dominant genetic cardiomyopathy caused by mutations in sarcomeric protein genes, most commonly:
- **MYBPC3** (cardiac myosin-binding protein C): ~40% of mutation-positive cases
- **MYH7** (beta-myosin heavy chain): ~35% of mutation-positive cases
- Other: TNNT2, TNNI3, TPM1, ACTC1

These mutations result in hypercontractile sarcomeres with dysregulated calcium handling, leading to:
- **Asymmetric septal hypertrophy** (most common morphology): interventricular septum disproportionately thickened relative to the posterior wall (septal/PW ratio >1.3)
- **Apical HCM (Yamaguchi variant)**: hypertrophy localized to the LV apex — near-pathognomonic ECG pattern
- **Concentric or other patterns**: less common but possible
- **LVOTO**: LV outflow tract obstruction from hypertrophied septum + systolic anterior motion (SAM) of mitral valve

Additional pathology:
- **Myocyte disarray**: chaotic, non-parallel arrangement of myocytes with fibrosis — the substrate for abnormal depolarization and arrhythmias
- **Microvascular dysfunction**: small vessel disease → ischemia → fibrosis
- **Diastolic dysfunction**: impaired LV relaxation from stiff, hypertrophied myocardium
- **Mitral regurgitation**: from SAM of mitral valve

### 1.2 Electrical Consequence
The hypertrophied, disorganized septum creates **abnormal septal depolarization**:
- Normal septal activation: left-to-right, producing small positive forces in V1 and small septal Q waves in lateral leads (I, aVL, V5-V6)
- HCM asymmetric hypertrophy: the hypertrophied septum generates **large, abnormal leftward and posterior depolarization vectors**
- These large septal vectors project as deep Q waves in lateral leads and may produce reciprocal tall R in V1
- Crucially, these Q waves are **narrow** (duration <40 ms) and **deep** — caused by augmented septal depolarization, NOT myocardial necrosis
- Myocyte disarray creates non-uniform conduction → repolarization abnormalities → deep T-wave inversions

**Apical HCM (Yamaguchi)**: the apical hypertrophy generates a very large apical depolarization vector; the apical endocardium depolarizes late and generates a massive, delayed negative repolarization force → **giant negative T waves** in the precordial leads V3-V6 ≥10 mm — nearly pathognomonic.

### 1.3 Why It Appears on ECG
- **High voltage**: increased myocardial mass → increased QRS amplitude; meets LVH voltage criteria in most cases
- **Septal Q waves**: abnormal, augmented septal depolarization → deep narrow Q in I, aVL, V5-V6; occasionally inferior leads
- **T-wave inversions**: abnormal repolarization from disarrayed myocytes and fibrosis; most dramatic in apical HCM
- **LAE**: chronic mitral regurgitation (from SAM) → elevated LA pressure → LA enlargement
- **QTc prolongation**: from abnormal repolarization in disarrayed myocardium

## 2. ECG Presentation — Lead by Lead

### 2.1 Diagnostic Criteria (2025 AHA/ESC Guidelines)

| Criterion | Threshold | Source |
|---|---|---|
| ECG abnormality prevalence | ~95% of HCM patients have abnormal ECG | ESC HCM Guidelines 2023; AHA/ACC HCM Guidelines 2020 |
| LVH voltage (Sokolow-Lyon) | SV1 + RV5 or RV6 ≥3.5 mV (35 mm) | AHA 2020 |
| Septal Q waves | Deep narrow Q (>3 mm depth, <40 ms duration) in I, aVL, V5-V6 and/or inferior leads | ESC 2023 HCM Guidelines |
| Apical HCM (Yamaguchi) giant T inversions | Negative T waves ≥10 mm in V3-V5/V6 + high QRS voltage | Yamaguchi 1979; ESC 2023 |
| T-wave inversions | Lateral and/or anterolateral T inversions | ESC HCM 2023 |
| QTc prolongation | QTc >440 ms (males), >460 ms (females) — common in HCM | AHA 2020 |
| LAE | P duration ≥120 ms or negative terminal V1 component ≥1 mm deep and ≥40 ms | AHA 2009 |

### 2.2 Lead-by-Lead Manifestation

| Lead | Expected Finding | Why | Sensitivity |
|---|---|---|---|
| I | Deep narrow Q wave; tall R; lateral T inversion | Abnormal septal vector (leftward) produces Q in lateral leads | High |
| II | Normal or T inversion; possible deep Q | Variable septal vector projection | Moderate |
| III | Deep Q wave (inferior Q — must distinguish from inferior MI) | Septal vector may project inferiorly in some morphologies | Moderate |
| aVR | Tall R or QR (reciprocal to septal Q in lateral leads) | Reciprocal to lateral Q waves | Moderate |
| aVL | Deep narrow Q wave; lateral T inversion | Lateral lead — high sensitivity for septal Q | High |
| aVF | May show Q or T inversion | Inferior projection of septal hypertrophy | Moderate |
| V1 | Tall R (reciprocal to septal Q); possible RVH-like tall R | Abnormal posterior depolarization vector projects anteriorly in V1 | Moderate |
| V2 | ST depression; T inversion or tall R | Posterior/septal forces | Moderate |
| V3 | Giant negative T waves (apical HCM); T inversion | Apical hypertrophy depolarization vector | High (apical HCM) |
| V4 | Giant negative T waves (apical HCM); T inversion | Apical forces maximize here | High (apical HCM) |
| V5 | Tall R (LVH voltage); T inversion or giant negative T | LV lateral high voltage; apical HCM T inversions extend here | High |
| V6 | Tall R (LVH voltage); deep Q; T inversion | LV lateral lead — septal Q + LVH voltage | High |

### 2.3 Key Leads (Most Diagnostic)
- **I, aVL, V5-V6**: Septal Q waves — deep, narrow, non-ischemic
- **V3-V5**: Giant T inversions ≥10 mm in apical HCM (Yamaguchi pattern) — near-pathognomonic
- **V5-V6**: High LVH voltage

### 2.4 Beat-by-Beat Considerations
- Septal Q waves and T inversions are fixed features (present on every beat)
- PVCs/NSVT may appear — important SCD risk markers; run of ≥3 VT beats on Holter = major SCD risk factor
- AF: develops in ~20–25% of HCM patients (late complication); stroke risk ×17; requires anticoagulation
- WPW co-exists in some HCM-related syndromes (e.g., PRKAG2 mutation-related HCM)

## 3. Morphology Details

### 3.1 P-wave Changes
- **LAE**: broad, notched P ≥120 ms; deep negative terminal V1 component; caused by chronic MR from SAM
- Normal or tall P if pulmonary hypertension develops in late-stage HCM
- P-wave changes may be the earliest sign before frank hypertrophy develops (in mutation-positive, phenotype-negative patients)

### 3.2 PR Interval Changes
- Usually normal
- Short PR with delta wave: if PRKAG2 or LAMP2 storage disease mimicking HCM — WPW co-exists
- First-degree AV block: occasional in late HCM with fibrosis involving conduction system

### 3.3 QRS Complex Changes
- **High LVH voltage**: Sokolow-Lyon ≥35 mm; Cornell ≥28 mm (males) / ≥20 mm (females)
- **Deep narrow Q waves** in I, aVL, V5-V6 (and sometimes II, III, aVF): amplitude >3 mm, duration <40 ms — the most specific HCM ECG marker
- Tall R in V1 (reciprocal to lateral Q waves / posterior septal forces)
- QRS duration: usually normal or borderline; may be slightly widened (105–115 ms) from septal disarray
- Left axis deviation: occasionally in significant septal hypertrophy

### 3.4 ST Segment Changes
- ST depression in lateral leads (I, aVL, V5-V6): LV strain pattern
- ST depression in precordial leads in apical HCM
- ST elevation is NOT a typical HCM feature unless LVOTO causes dynamic ischemia

### 3.5 T-wave Changes
- **Lateral T-wave inversions** (I, aVL, V5-V6): most common T-wave abnormality; from abnormal repolarization
- **Giant negative T waves in V3-V6 ≥10 mm** (apical HCM / Yamaguchi): nearly pathognomonic when combined with high voltage — this combination should immediately trigger HCM workup
- Inferior T inversions (II, III, aVF): may occur in variants with inferior hypertrophy
- Right precordial T inversions (V1-V3): can occur in LVOTO-related RV strain

### 3.6 QT/QTc Changes
- QTc commonly prolonged (>440 ms) due to abnormal repolarization from myocyte disarray
- QTc prolongation is an independent feature correlating with degree of hypertrophy and fibrosis
- Marked QTc prolongation (>500 ms) raises SCD risk — triggers arrhythmia monitoring

### 3.7 Other Features
- **NSVT on ambulatory monitoring**: major SCD risk factor — 3+ consecutive VT beats at ≥120 bpm
- **AF**: late complication; ECG may show AF without clear HCM features if P waves are lost
- **Atrial flutter**: less common; may occur in advanced disease
- **SCD trigger**: VF/VT — most common cause of SCD in young athletes with HCM
- **LGL pattern** (short PR, no delta): occasionally described in HCM, mechanism unclear

## 4. Differential Diagnosis

### 4.1 Mimics

| Mimic Condition | Shared Features | Distinguishing Features |
|---|---|---|
| Inferior MI | Deep Q waves in inferior leads | MI Q: wide (≥40 ms), in distribution of coronary territory (II/III/aVF); HCM Q: narrow, lateral/septal distribution |
| Lateral MI | Q waves + T inversions in I/aVL/V5-V6 | MI Q: wide (≥40 ms), associated ST elevation history; HCM: narrow Q, no ischemic context |
| Athlete's heart | LVH voltage, T inversions | Athlete: symmetric LVH, normal septal/PW ratio, no Q waves, T inversions resolve with deconditioning; HCM: asymmetric, Q waves, fixed T inversions |
| LVH from hypertension | High LVH voltage, strain pattern | HTN: no septal Q waves; symmetric LVH; no giant T inversions; septal/PW ratio normal |
| Dilated cardiomyopathy | High voltage or LBBB; Q waves | DCM: dilated, thin-walled LV; Q waves in non-septal distribution; LBBB common |
| WPW | Short PR, delta wave, high voltage | WPW: delta wave visible; PR <120 ms; pseudo-Q waves due to initial conduction delay |
| ARVC | T inversions in precordial leads | ARVC: T inversions V1-V3 (right precordial); epsilon wave; RV-origin VT (LBBB morphology); no septal Q |
| Takotsubo | T inversions in precordial leads | Takotsubo: dynamic, reversible; follows emotional/physical trigger; apical ballooning on echo; QTc often very prolonged |

### 4.2 Coexisting Conditions
- **AF**: occurs in ~20-25%; requires anticoagulation; raises stroke risk ×17
- **LVOTO**: dynamic gradient → syncope, SCD risk
- **Mitral regurgitation**: from SAM; worsens with dehydration, vasodilators
- **Diastolic heart failure**: impaired relaxation → elevated filling pressures

## 5. STAT Classification

| Priority | Criteria |
|---|---|
| STAT (Immediate) | Young patient with syncope or near-syncope + HCM ECG pattern (septal Q, LVH, T inversions) — SCD risk; new NSVT or VT in HCM patient |
| STAT (Immediate) | Apical HCM with giant T inversions in young athlete — SCD risk; requires immediate cardiology consult |
| Urgent | Newly recognized HCM ECG in asymptomatic patient — requires echo, genetic counseling, family screening |
| Urgent | New AF in known HCM — anticoagulation decision required |
| Routine | Known HCM on stable monitoring |

## 6. Reasoning Complexity Analysis (Feeds Into Node 2.1 — Agent Architecture Research)
> NOTE: Does NOT pre-assign agents. Documents reasoning complexity for Node 2.1.

### 6.1 Reasoning Domains Required
- Q-wave morphology analysis: narrow vs. wide, depth measurement, distribution mapping (lateral vs. inferior vs. coronary territory)
- Voltage analysis: LVH criteria across multiple leads
- T-wave quantification: depth measurement in V3-V5 for Yamaguchi detection
- Temporal context: athlete vs. non-athlete, acute vs. chronic, young patient context
- Risk stratification: SCD risk feature detection (NSVT, VT, syncope context)
- Differential exclusion: ischemic Q waves (wide) vs. HCM Q waves (narrow)
- Apical HCM pattern recognition: giant T inversions + high voltage = specific combination

### 6.2 Feature Dependencies
- Q-wave width measurement is critical — <40 ms = HCM; ≥40 ms = ischemic; requires precise onset/offset detection
- Voltage criteria require multi-lead amplitude measurements
- Giant T inversions require depth measurement in V3-V6 against baseline
- Age/athletic context modifies pre-test probability significantly

### 6.3 Cross-Condition Interactions
- **Athlete's heart**: most important differential in young patients — deconditioning test distinguishes
- **ARVC**: both can cause T inversions and arrhythmias — lead distribution (lateral vs. right precordial) differentiates
- **WPW mimicry**: pseudo-Q waves in WPW can mimic HCM septal Q waves
- **AF in HCM**: when AF is present, P-wave LAE features are lost; Q waves and voltage are the only remaining HCM markers

### 6.4 Reasoning Chain Sketch
1. Detect sinus rhythm (or note AF/arrhythmia)
2. Measure QRS voltage — flag if LVH criteria met
3. Analyze Q waves: presence, depth (>3 mm?), duration (<40 ms?), distribution (lateral? inferior? both?)
4. If Q waves narrow and deep in lateral leads → flag as possible HCM septal Q (not ischemic)
5. Measure T-wave depth in V3-V6 — flag if ≥10 mm negative
6. If giant T inversions V3-V6 + high voltage → raise apical HCM (Yamaguchi) alert
7. Assess P waves for LAE
8. Flag PVCs, NSVT, AF if present
9. Apply patient age/sex/context: young athlete → increase HCM prior
10. Differentiate Q width: if wide → ischemic; if narrow → septal/HCM
11. Assign confidence, STAT flag for SCD risk features

### 6.5 Confidence Anchors
- High confidence: deep narrow Q in lateral leads + LVH voltage + deep T inversions + no ischemic context + young patient
- Very high confidence for apical HCM: giant T inversions ≥10 mm V3-V5 + high voltage
- Moderate confidence: LVH voltage + T inversions without Q waves — needs echo to confirm
- Low confidence: isolated voltage with no Q or T changes — nonspecific LVH
- Uncertainty triggers: wide Q waves (may be ischemic); AF present (hides P-wave features); concurrent LBBB

## 6A. Agent Assignment

**Architecture:** Option E — 3 Parallel Phase 1 Specialists + 1 Sequential CDS (Node 2.1 Gate Passed 2026-03-27)

| Agent | Role for Hypertrophic Cardiomyopathy | Key Features Used |
|-------|--------------------------|-------------------|
| **RRC** (Rhythm/Rate/Conduction) | Supporting | Arrhythmia detection: non-sustained VT, supraventricular arrhythmias including AFib (HCM is the leading cause of SCD in young athletes — arrhythmias are the mechanism); left axis deviation common |
| **IT** (Ischemia/Territory) | Supporting — must NOT independently classify HCM Q-waves or apical T-wave inversions as ischemia | Detects pathological Q-waves in lateral/inferior leads and deep T-wave inversions; emits cross_domain_hint to CDS before classifying as myocardial infarction or Wellens syndrome; apical HCM T-wave inversions in V4-V6 detected as a finding |
| **MR** (Morphology/Repolarization) | Primary | LVH voltage criteria (Sokolow-Lyon ≥35mm, Cornell >28mm men); septal Q-waves in lateral leads (I, aVL, V5-V6): narrow (<40ms), deep (>3mm) septal Q-waves due to abnormal septal hypertrophy — the most specific HCM ECG feature; giant negative T-waves (deeply inverted, symmetric, >10mm) in V4-V6 for apical HCM variant; diffuse ST-T abnormalities |
| **CDS** (Cross-Domain Synthesis) | Required — CRITICAL: HCM Q-waves vs inferior/lateral MI; apical T-wave inversions vs Wellens | Receives IT Q-wave and T-wave inversion findings + MR LVH and septal Q-wave patterns; HCM septal Q-waves in lateral leads (narrow, deep, in I/aVL/V5-V6) + LVH morphology = HCM, not lateral infarction; apical giant T-wave inversions V4-V6 + LVH morphology + young patient = apical HCM, not Wellens; CDS applies the combined morphology + clinical context rule |

### Primary Agent
**MR** — HCM's most characteristic ECG features are morphology-based: septal Q-waves in lateral leads (narrow, deep ≥3mm) and giant T-wave inversions in apical HCM (≥10mm in V4-V6), combined with LVH voltage criteria; all are in the MR domain.

### Cross-Domain Hints
- MR emits: `cross_domain_hint: "Narrow deep Q-waves (>3mm, <40ms) in lateral leads I/aVL/V5-V6 with LVH voltage criteria detected — IT must not classify these as lateral infarction Q-waves; CDS must adjudicate: septal HCM Q-waves vs lateral MI"`
- MR emits (apical variant): `cross_domain_hint: "Giant symmetric T-wave inversions ≥10mm in V4-V6 with LVH detected — IT must not classify as Wellens syndrome; CDS must adjudicate: apical HCM vs Wellens pattern (requires anterior LAD territory context)"`
- IT emits: `cross_domain_hint: "Pathological Q-waves in lateral leads and/or deep T-wave inversions V4-V6 detected — CDS must apply HCM context rule: if MR confirms LVH + narrow lateral Q-waves or giant apical T inversions, suppress infarction/ischemia flag"`

### CDS Specific Role
CDS is the critical arbiter for HCM because its ECG features mimic high-stakes ischemic diagnoses. For Q-waves: CDS receives IT's lateral Q-wave detection and MR's septal Q-wave characterization (narrow <40ms, deep ≥3mm, in I/aVL/V5-V6) alongside LVH voltage criteria. If the Q-wave pattern matches HCM (narrow, lateral distribution, with LVH), CDS suppresses the lateral MI classification and generates an HCM Q-wave flag. For apical T-wave inversions: CDS receives IT's Wellens-pattern detection and MR's giant T-wave characterization (symmetric, deeply inverted ≥10mm in V4-V6, with LVH). If this matches apical HCM morphology, CDS suppresses the Wellens flag and generates an apical HCM suspicion flag. CDS also integrates RRC arrhythmia output: VT or AFib in a young patient with LVH and septal Q-waves elevates the HCM concern significantly.

**ER Nurse version:**
"The heart muscle — especially the dividing wall between the two lower chambers — is abnormally thick because of a genetic condition. This causes unusual deep dips in the ECG in the side leads, and in the apical type, dramatically inverted T waves across the chest leads. This condition can cause sudden cardiac death, especially in young patients who faint."

**Cardiologist version:**
"HCM ECG pattern: deep narrow Q waves in lateral leads (septal hypertrophy vector) with LVH voltage. T-wave inversions likely represent repolarization abnormality from myocyte disarray and fibrosis. Family history and genetic counseling indicated."

## 9. Edge Cases and Pitfalls

- **Narrow Q waves ≠ ischemia**: The most critical pitfall — HCM septal Q waves are narrow (<40 ms). Wide Q waves suggest MI. Misdiagnosis as inferior or lateral MI leads to incorrect treatment
- **Young athlete with LVH**: Always consider HCM vs. athlete's heart — athlete's heart is benign, HCM is SCD risk; echo differentiates (septal/PW ratio, filling pattern, response to deconditioning)
- **Apical HCM underrecognized**: Giant T inversions V3-V5 + high voltage is near-pathognomonic yet often missed; requires giant T measurement ≥10 mm
- **ECG normal at first**: ~5% of HCM patients have normal ECG — a normal ECG does NOT exclude HCM in a symptomatic young patient
- **MYBPC3 late-onset HCM**: In older patients, the ECG may be less dramatic; dominant LVH voltage without dramatic Q waves
- **Concurrent LBBB**: invalidates voltage criteria and Q-wave assessment; HCM may be undetectable on surface ECG
- **Storage diseases (Fabry, Pompe, DANON/LAMP2)**: mimic HCM ECG; DANON disease (LAMP2) shows extreme LVH voltage + WPW + HCM-like morphology — rare but critical diagnosis
- **SCD in exercise**: HCM is the most common cause of SCD in young competitive athletes in the US — context of syncope during exertion is a red flag
