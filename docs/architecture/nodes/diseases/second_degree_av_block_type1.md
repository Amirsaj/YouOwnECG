## 1. Pathophysiology Root Cause

### 1.1 What Goes Wrong

Wenckebach (Mobitz Type I) second degree AV block is a periodic failure of AV conduction that follows a predictable, progressive pattern. The underlying mechanism is decremental conduction within the AV node: each successive atrial impulse arrives at the AV node while it is in a progressively less recovered state, causing each subsequent conduction to take longer until one impulse arrives during absolute refractoriness and fails entirely.

This is an intrinsic property of AV nodal (slow-response, calcium-dependent) cells. Unlike His-Purkinje cells which have a sharp refractory cutoff, AV nodal cells show graded, rate-sensitive fatigue — the weaker each recovered state, the longer the transit time. This fatigue accumulates beat-by-beat until conduction fails, after which the AV node fully recovers and the cycle restarts.

Primary etiologies:
- **Inferior MI (RCA territory):** The AV nodal artery (branch of RCA in 90% of patients) is acutely ischemic. This is the most common serious cause in the ER.
- **Increased vagal tone:** Enhanced parasympathetic activity slows calcium channel opening in AV nodal cells. Common in athletes, during sleep, Bezold-Jarisch reflex (inferior MI).
- **Drug effect:** Beta-blockers (reduce AV nodal calcium influx indirectly); digoxin (enhances vagal tone on AV node); non-dihydropyridine CCBs (direct calcium channel block).
- **Post-cardiac surgery:** Direct AV nodal trauma or edema.
- **Myocarditis / Lyme carditis:** Inflammatory infiltration of AV nodal tissue.

### 1.2 Electrical Consequence

After the AV node fails to conduct (dropped QRS), it has a full recovery interval — the pause duration allows complete restitution. The first conducted beat of the next cycle travels through a fully recovered AV node at the fastest possible speed (shortest PR of the cycle). As subsequent beats arrive, the node is progressively less recovered, extending each PR interval. The increment of PR lengthening is typically DECREASING (each PR adds less than the previous addition), not linearly increasing — a subtle but important feature.

Because the block is at the AV node (supranodal to His-Purkinje):
- His-Purkinje system is unaffected → QRS is narrow
- The ventricular activation sequence is normal → QRS morphology is identical beat-to-beat

The conduction ratio describes the pattern: a 4:3 Wenckebach has 4 P-waves and 3 QRS complexes per cycle. The ratio can be 3:2, 4:3, 5:4, or any higher integer ratio.

### 1.3 Why It Appears on ECG

The progressive PR lengthening is directly visible as an increasing gap between each P-wave and its following QRS. The dropped beat creates a pause — a P-wave with no following QRS. After the pause, the PR resets to its shortest value, creating the signature grouped beating pattern.

A counterintuitive feature: the RR interval SHORTENS before the dropped beat. This occurs because each PR lengthens by a SMALLER increment than the previous beat. Although each P-wave is blocked more severely, the P-wave still "pulls" the next QRS closer relative to the last QRS. This RR shortening before the pause is a reliable secondary sign of Wenckebach.

## 2. ECG Presentation — Lead by Lead

### 2.1 Diagnostic Criteria (2025 AHA/ESC Guidelines)

- **Progressive PR interval lengthening** from beat to beat, with each increment typically decreasing
- **Sudden non-conducted P-wave** (P without QRS) terminating the cycle
- **PR reset** after the dropped beat — shortest PR is the first beat after the pause
- **RR interval shortening** immediately before the dropped beat (paradoxical but expected)
- **Narrow QRS** (< 120ms) in the conducted beats — block is at AV node
- **Grouped beating pattern** — visually, QRS complexes appear in clusters separated by pauses
- Conduction ratio must be stated: most common is 4:3 (4 P per 3 QRS) or 3:2

**Critical rule:** A 2:1 conduction ratio alone CANNOT be classified as Type I or Type II — the PR variability required to define Wenckebach cannot be observed with only one conducted beat per cycle. Longer strip required.

### 2.2 Lead-by-Lead Manifestation

| Lead | Expected Finding | Why | Sensitivity |
|------|-----------------|-----|-------------|
| I | Grouped QRS complexes; subtle PR lengthening visible | Lateral lead — good QRS visibility; P may be small | Moderate |
| II | PRIMARY LEAD: grouped beating, progressive PR, dropped QRS clear | Best P-wave visibility (upright, tall); PR measurement most reliable | High |
| III | Grouped beating; P-waves visible; inferior perspective | Inferior lead — useful in inferior MI context | Moderate |
| aVR | Inverted P grouped beats; confirms sinus P | Opposite to II — confirms atrial origin | Moderate |
| aVL | Variable P morphology; grouped QRS | Lateral — may show subtle PR changes | Low–Moderate |
| aVF | Grouped beating; inferior lead | Inferior lead — confirms sinus P-wave axis | Moderate |
| V1 | Best lead for seeing non-conducted P-waves | Biphasic P clearly distinct from T-wave; dropped P visible in isoelectric baseline | High |
| V2 | Visible P-waves in groupings | Anterior lead — P visibility variable | Moderate |
| V3–V4 | QRS grouping visible; P less distinct | Transitional leads | Low |
| V5–V6 | QRS grouped; tall R masks P detail | Lateral leads — QRS pattern most visible | Low |

**Best leads:** II (PR measurement, P-wave identification) and V1 (non-conducted P-wave identification).

### 2.3 Key Leads

- **Lead II:** Tallest, most upright P-wave. PR lengthening most visually obvious. Non-conducted P appears as an isolated upright deflection in the pause.
- **V1:** The biphasic P-wave in V1 is typically distinct from the preceding T-wave, making it the best lead to spot non-conducted P-waves during the pause. In leads with merged P-T deflections, the dropped P-wave is easily missed.
- **Rhythm strip (lead II or V1, 10+ seconds):** Single most important tool — at least 2 complete Wenckebach cycles needed for confident diagnosis.

### 2.4 Beat-by-Beat Considerations

The analysis must be performed sequentially, measuring PR on every single conducted beat:

1. Identify all P-waves (including non-conducted ones in pauses)
2. Label each P-wave and its relationship to the next QRS
3. Measure PR interval for each conducted P-QRS pair
4. Document the PR sequence: short → longer → longer → dropped → reset
5. Confirm RR shortening before the dropped beat
6. Calculate the conduction ratio (X:Y = P-waves:QRS per cycle)
7. Confirm QRS duration is narrow in conducted beats

Variable Wenckebach cycles (where the ratio changes from beat to beat, e.g., alternating 3:2 and 4:3) are common during inferior MI and drug effect — do not require all cycles to be identical.

## 3. Morphology Details

### 3.1 P-Wave Morphology
Sinus P-waves throughout — upright in I and II, inverted in aVR. P-wave morphology does NOT change within the Wenckebach cycle. Consistent P morphology distinguishes Wenckebach from PACs triggering AV block. The non-conducted P-wave has identical morphology to conducted ones — it is a sinus P that simply fails to propagate.

### 3.2 PR Interval
The diagnostic centerpiece. The first PR of each cycle is the shortest (may be normal, borderline, or overtly prolonged depending on baseline AV nodal function). Each subsequent PR is longer by a DECREASING increment. Example sequence: 180ms → 220ms → 245ms → dropped (40ms increment, then 25ms increment — decreasing increments).

The total PR lengthening across a cycle is often modest (40–80ms total) but is measurably progressive. In atypical Wenckebach (common), increments may be erratic.

### 3.3 QRS Complex
Narrow (< 120ms) in all conducted beats. QRS morphology is identical across all conducted beats — same axis, same duration, same morphology. Any QRS widening is due to a concurrent bundle branch block (which is independent of the Wenckebach block and does not alter the Wenckebach diagnosis).

### 3.4 ST Segment and T-Wave
Evaluated independently of the conduction disorder. In inferior MI, ST elevation in II, III, aVF coexists with Wenckebach. The dropped beat creates a longer RR interval before it — the T-wave of the last conducted beat before the pause may appear larger (longer recovery).

### 3.5 RR Interval Behavior
Progressive RR SHORTENING before the dropped beat is a mathematical consequence of decreasing PR increments. If RR is plotted across a cycle, the pattern is: relatively longer first RR → shorter → shortest before pause → long pause → restart. This is the opposite of what intuition suggests (longer PR should mean longer RR) and is pathognomonic for Wenckebach.

### 3.6 Pause Duration
The pause containing the dropped beat is LESS than twice the shortest RR interval. This distinguishes Wenckebach from sinus arrest (pause is a multiple of the PP interval). In Wenckebach, the pause = 1 PP interval + the pause itself = less than 2x RR.

### 3.7 Conduction Ratio
State the ratio explicitly: 3:2, 4:3, 5:4, 6:5, etc. Higher ratios (e.g., 8:7) may appear almost like first degree block with only subtle PR variation and infrequent dropped beats. Lower ratios (3:2) produce obvious grouped beating with frequent pauses.

## 4. Differential Diagnosis

### 4.1 Mimics

| Condition | How it Mimics Wenckebach | Distinguishing Feature |
|-----------|--------------------------|----------------------|
| Mobitz Type II | Sudden dropped QRS can look like Wenckebach if strip is short | Type II: PR is CONSTANT before the drop; no progressive lengthening |
| 2:1 AV Block | One conducted, one blocked — cannot see PR variability | 2:1 cannot be typed; look for variable ratio elsewhere in strip |
| Nonconducted PACs | Premature P-wave not conducted — pause in rhythm | PAC has different P morphology, occurs early (short coupling interval), no PR progression |
| Sinus arrhythmia with first degree AV block | Variable RR may suggest grouped beating | PP interval varies (sinus arrhythmia); no dropped beats; PR constant |
| High-grade AV block | Multiple consecutive non-conducted P-waves | Wenckebach: only ONE P dropped per cycle; high-grade: ≥ 2 consecutive dropped |
| AV dissociation (isorhythmic) | P-waves "walking through" QRS complexes | No PR progression; ventricular rate often matches or exceeds atrial rate; PP and RR independent |
| Blocked PAC bigeminy | Alternating QRS then P-QRS gap looks like 2:1 block | PAC P-wave morphology different; short coupling interval before the blocked P |

### 4.2 Coexisting Conditions

- **Inferior STEMI:** Wenckebach is the EXPECTED AV conduction complication of inferior STEMI. ST elevation in II, III, aVF with concurrent Wenckebach = AV nodal ischemia from RCA territory. Monitor for progression to complete heart block (occurs in ~5–10% of inferior STEMI with Wenckebach).
- **Digoxin toxicity:** Wenckebach + regularized atrial activity + PAT with block = classic digoxin toxicity triad. Serum level required.
- **Athletic heart:** High vagal tone in trained athletes produces Wenckebach during rest/sleep. Resolves with exercise (vagolytic). Distinguished from pathologic Wenckebach by context.
- **Lyme carditis:** Fluctuating degree of AV block including Wenckebach — can change hourly. Young patient, recent tick exposure, outdoor activity.

## 5. STAT Classification

| Parameter | Assessment |
|-----------|------------|
| Isolated Wenckebach, asymptomatic | MONITOR — not immediately STAT but requires documentation and monitoring |
| Wenckebach + inferior STEMI | STAT (RED) — RCA ischemia; STEMI activation takes priority; monitor for CHB |
| Wenckebach + hemodynamic compromise (hypotension, syncope) | STAT (RED) — atropine; transcutaneous pacing on standby |
| Wenckebach + digoxin toxicity suspected | URGENT (ORANGE) — digoxin level, consider digoxin-specific Fab |
| Wenckebach + Lyme carditis suspected | URGENT (ORANGE) — telemetry, IV antibiotics, consider temporary pacing if PR >300ms |
| Wenckebach + high ventricular rate loss (≥ 3:1 ratio) | URGENT (ORANGE) — effective ventricular rate may be inadequate |
| Asymptomatic nocturnal/athletic Wenckebach | LOW (GREEN) — physiologic variant; no treatment required |

**Default STAT level: AMBER (monitor), escalating per context above.**

## 6. Reasoning Complexity Analysis

### 6.1 Signal Clarity Needed
Requires precise P-wave detection across ALL beats in the strip, including the non-conducted P-wave in the pause. ECGdeli must reliably detect P-wave onsets at all PR intervals including very long ones (>300ms). Baseline noise and T-wave overlap with the non-conducted P are the main signal challenges. Temporal alignment across leads is critical for measuring PR accurately on every beat.

### 6.2 Number of Leads Required
Minimum 1 lead (rhythm strip) for diagnosis, but must be ≥ 10 seconds to capture at least 2 complete cycles. Practically: II and V1 together provide the best P-wave detection. Full 12-lead needed to assess:
- Inferior ST changes (STEMI context)
- QRS width (exclude concurrent bundle branch block)
- P-wave morphology (confirm sinus origin)

### 6.3 Cross-Domain Reasoning
Moderate cross-domain reasoning:
- Rate calculation at each segment of the Wenckebach cycle
- Inferior STEMI pattern recognition simultaneously
- Drug effect consideration (beta-blockers, digoxin)
- Distinction from Type II requires comparing PR stability across multiple beats
- Athletic context vs. pathologic context assessment

### 6.4 Temporal Pattern Complexity
Moderate-to-high. Wenckebach is fundamentally a temporal pattern — the diagnosis is made by tracking PR changes across a sequence of beats. Single-beat analysis is insufficient. The algorithm must:
- Detect all P-waves (including those hidden in T-waves)
- Track PR progression across a minimum of one complete cycle
- Identify the reset after the dropped beat
- Confirm RR shortening before the pause

### 6.5 Differential Complexity
Moderate. Primary differential is Mobitz Type II — the key distinction (constant vs. progressive PR) requires multi-beat analysis. The 2:1 ambiguity is clinically important. Non-conducted PAC bigeminy is another common trap.

### 6.6 Difficulty Score

| Dimension | Score | Rationale |
|-----------|-------|-----------|
| Signal clarity needed | 3/5 | Non-conducted P-waves in pauses require reliable P detection in isoelectric segments |
| Number of leads required | 2/5 | Rhythm strip minimum; II + V1 practical requirement |
| Cross-domain reasoning | 3/5 | STEMI context, drug context, Type I vs. II distinction all required |
| Temporal pattern complexity | 4/5 | Multi-beat sequential PR tracking is the core diagnosis; requires strip analysis |
| Differential complexity | 3/5 | Type II distinction, 2:1 ambiguity, blocked PAC all must be excluded |
| Rarity in PTB-XL | 2/5 | Represented in PTB-XL; sufficient training examples available |
| **Overall difficulty** | **2.5/5** | **Temporal pattern task requiring multi-beat analysis; moderate complexity** |

| **IT** (Ischemia/Territory) | Not involved | ST/T assessed independently; not part of Wenckebach classification |

### Primary Agent
**RRC** — Wenckebach is identified by beat-by-beat PR interval tracking, the dropped QRS, and grouped beating; all features are in the RRC conduction analysis domain.

### Cross-Domain Hints
- RRC → IT: `cross_domain_hint: "wenckebach_pattern_detected — evaluate inferior leads for ST elevation (RCA AV nodal ischemia)"`

### CDS Specific Role
CDS performs standard integration with one critical cross-domain check: when RRC identifies Wenckebach and IT reports inferior ST elevation (II/III/aVF), CDS flags RCA occlusion with AV nodal ischemia as the mechanism. This combination changes management from observation to emergent reperfusion. CDS also applies the 2:1 block caveat — if only 2:1 conduction is visible, CDS flags that Wenckebach vs Mobitz II cannot be distinguished without a longer rhythm strip.

## 9. Edge Cases and Pitfalls

**Pitfall 1 — Atypical Wenckebach:** PR increments are often NOT monotonically increasing in real patients. The first increment is typically the largest; subsequent increments may be smaller but occasionally erratic. Strict monotonic PR progression is a textbook simplification. In atypical Wenckebach, PR may appear to plateau before the drop. Always look for the overall TREND, not strict monotonic increase.

**Pitfall 2 — 2:1 Wenckebach vs. 2:1 Type II:** At 2:1 ratio, only one conducted beat exists per cycle — making it mathematically impossible to observe PR progression. In 2:1 block, narrow QRS favors Type I (Wenckebach), wide QRS favors Type II, but neither confirms the type. A longer strip capturing variable ratios (e.g., one cycle of 3:2 after a run of 2:1) resolves the ambiguity.

**Pitfall 3 — Blocked PAC bigeminy vs. 3:2 Wenckebach:** Both produce an alternating QRS / pause / QRS pattern. Key differentiator: in blocked PAC bigeminy, the non-conducted P-wave occurs EARLY (short coupling interval from prior QRS), has different morphology, and the preceding P-QRS has a NORMAL (not prolonged) PR. In 3:2 Wenckebach, the conducted beats have progressive PR, and the non-conducted P-wave occurs on time (normal PP interval from the prior P).

**Pitfall 4 — Non-conducted P-wave hidden in T-wave:** The last P-wave of the Wenckebach cycle (the dropped one) has the longest preceding PR — meaning this P-wave occurs later relative to the prior QRS, often landing directly on the T-wave. This can make the T-wave appear peaked or notched. In V1, the biphasic P is often distinguishable from the T-wave. Use multiple leads.

**Pitfall 5 — Inferior STEMI Wenckebach progressing to CHB:** In inferior STEMI, Wenckebach is common and often resolves with reperfusion. However, ~5–10% progress to complete heart block. The ER algorithm must flag for progressive monitoring — serial rhythm checks every 15–30 minutes in acute inferior STEMI + Wenckebach.

**Pitfall 6 — High vagal tone (overnight/post-prandial) in otherwise normal patients:** A Wenckebach found on a Holter at 2:00am in an otherwise healthy individual is almost certainly physiologic. Context (time of occurrence, heart rate, symptom correlation) is essential for STAT classification.
