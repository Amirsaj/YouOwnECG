## 1. Pathophysiology Root Cause

### 1.1 What Goes Wrong (Anatomy/Physiology)
- Acute thrombotic occlusion of the proximal-to-mid left anterior descending (LAD) artery, identical pathophysiology to anterior STEMI
- Transmural ischemia of the anterior wall, septum, and often the apex of the left ventricle
- Occurs in approximately 2% of acute LAD occlusions; the reason some patients produce this pattern rather than classic ST elevation is not fully understood but is hypothesized to relate to the degree/timing of transmural vs subendocardial ischemia gradient, collateral flow, or microvascular dynamics at the wavefront of ischemia
- The ischemic zone creates a persistent injury current, but the vector balance between subendocardial and epicardial injury produces a characteristic pattern that does NOT meet traditional STEMI ST-elevation criteria

### 1.2 Electrical Consequence
- Subendocardial ischemia creates a current of injury vector directed AWAY from the epicardial surface and TOWARD the endocardium (inward-directed vector)
- This produces ST depression at the J-point in precordial leads rather than the ST elevation expected with full transmural injury
- However, the ischemia IS transmural — the tall, peaked T-waves represent the hyperacute phase of myocardial injury with accelerated phase-3 repolarization in ischemic cells (shortened action potential duration, increased extracellular potassium)
- The injury current also projects toward aVR (rightward/superior vector from diffuse subendocardial ischemia), producing ST elevation in aVR
- Unlike typical STEMI evolution (hyperacute T → ST elevation → T inversion → Q waves), the de Winter pattern is STATIC — it does not evolve through these stages, persisting unchanged until reperfusion

### 1.3 Why It Appears on ECG
- The upsloping ST depression at J-point: the net injury vector points away from the anterior precordial leads at the J-point but transitions into a large positive T-wave vector (tall peaked T)
- The precordial leads (V1-V6) face the anterior wall directly — they register the full expression of the depressed J-point transitioning into the peaked T-wave
- aVR, which looks at the heart from a rightward-superior vantage, sees the reciprocal of diffuse ST depression in other leads, manifesting as ST elevation
- Limb leads may show subtle changes but the precordial leads carry the diagnostic pattern because they are closest to the LAD territory

## 2. ECG Presentation — Lead by Lead

### 2.1 Diagnostic Criteria (2025 AHA/ESC Guidelines)

| Criterion | Threshold | Source |
|-----------|-----------|--------|
| Upsloping ST depression at J-point in V1-V6 | >1 mm (0.1 mV) at the J-point | de Winter et al., NEJM 2008; 2023 ESC STEMI-equivalent recognition |
| Tall, symmetric, peaked T-waves in precordial leads | T-wave amplitude typically 5-15 mm, symmetric morphology | de Winter et al., NEJM 2008 |
| ST elevation in aVR | 0.5-1 mm (may exceed 1 mm) | de Winter et al., NEJM 2008 |
| All three criteria present simultaneously | All three required for diagnosis | Consensus |
| Static pattern (no temporal evolution) | Pattern persists without progressing to ST elevation | de Winter et al., NEJM 2008 |
| Clinical context | Acute chest pain or equivalent, troponin rise expected | AHA/ESC 2023 |

### 2.2 Lead-by-Lead Manifestation

| Lead | Expected Finding | Why (Vector Explanation) | Sensitivity |
|------|-----------------|------------------------|-------------|
| I | Mild ST depression possible; T-wave may be tall | Lead I has partial anterior wall orientation; lateral extension of ischemia projects modestly onto this lead | Low |
| II | Usually normal or mild nonspecific changes | Inferior lead, perpendicular to primary anterior injury vector | Very Low |
| III | Usually normal or mild nonspecific changes | Inferior lead, largely orthogonal to anterior ischemia vector | Very Low |
| aVR | ST elevation 0.5-1 mm (may reach 1-2 mm) | aVR faces the cavity; diffuse subendocardial ischemia vector points toward aVR; reciprocal of widespread precordial/limb ST depression | High — required criterion |
| aVL | Mild ST depression or normal; possible subtle T-wave peaking | High lateral lead with partial anterior wall view; changes depend on proximal vs mid LAD involvement | Low-Moderate |
| aVF | Usually normal or mild nonspecific changes | Inferior lead, orthogonal to anterior injury vector | Very Low |
| V1 | Upsloping ST depression >1 mm at J-point; tall peaked T-wave | Septal lead directly over LAD territory; sees full expression of the characteristic injury pattern | High — key diagnostic lead |
| V2 | Upsloping ST depression >1 mm at J-point; tall symmetric peaked T-wave (often most prominent) | Anterior septal lead, maximum proximity to proximal LAD territory; often shows most dramatic pattern | Very High — often most prominent |
| V3 | Upsloping ST depression >1 mm at J-point; tall symmetric peaked T-wave | Anterior lead overlying LAD territory; full expression of pattern | Very High |
| V4 | Upsloping ST depression >1 mm at J-point; tall symmetric peaked T-wave | Anterior-apical lead in LAD territory; typically shows prominent changes | Very High |
| V5 | Upsloping ST depression at J-point (may be less prominent); tall T-wave | Anterolateral transition; pattern may attenuate as lead moves away from direct LAD territory | High |
| V6 | Upsloping ST depression at J-point (may be mild); T-wave may still be tall | Lateral lead at edge of LAD territory; changes present but often less striking than V2-V4 | Moderate |

### 2.3 Key Leads (Most Diagnostic)
- **Most diagnostic:** V2-V4 — these leads consistently show the most prominent upsloping ST depression and tallest peaked T-waves
- **Required for diagnosis:** V1-V6 (precordial leads as a group) + aVR
- **Earliest and most reliable changes:** V2-V3 typically show the most dramatic pattern
- **Reciprocal changes:** aVR elevation is reciprocal to the diffuse precordial/limb depression pattern; no classic "reciprocal depression in inferior leads" as seen in anterior STEMI

### 2.4 Beat-by-Beat Considerations
- The pattern is STATIC — it is constant across all beats throughout the recording
- No beat-to-beat variability expected (unlike ischemic ST changes that may vary with heart rate)
- No temporal evolution on serial ECGs (this is a distinguishing feature vs hyperacute T-waves of early STEMI which evolve within minutes to hours)
- Every beat shows the same upsloping ST depression + peaked T morphology
- Pattern persists unchanged until the artery is reperfused, at which point it may convert to a typical STEMI pattern or normalize

## 3. Morphology Details (What the Agent Must See)

### 3.1 P-wave Changes
- Morphology: Normal sinus P-waves (no atrial pathology inherent to this condition)
- Duration change: Normal
- Axis change: Normal
- Note: Sinus tachycardia is common due to sympathetic activation from acute MI and pain; P-waves may be partially buried in preceding T-waves at higher rates

### 3.2 PR Interval Changes
- Duration: Normal (120-200 ms)
- Pattern: Constant
- Note: PR depression in aVR is NOT expected (that would suggest pericarditis). If AV block develops, it suggests extensive septal infarction with conduction system involvement

### 3.3 QRS Complex Changes
- Duration: Narrow (<120 ms) unless pre-existing bundle branch block
- Morphology: Normal initial activation; Q-waves are typically ABSENT early (their absence helps distinguish from completed MI)
- Amplitude: Normal to mildly increased (may reflect hyperacute phase)
- Axis: Normal unless pre-existing conduction disease
- Note: Development of new LBBB or RBBB during the event suggests proximal LAD occlusion with septal branch involvement — this is a grave sign

### 3.4 ST Segment Changes
- Direction: Depression at J-point in V1-V6; elevation in aVR
- Morphology: UPSLOPING from the depressed J-point — this is critical. The ST segment slopes upward from the depressed J-point into the tall T-wave peak, creating a characteristic "ski-slope" appearance. It is NOT horizontal or downsloping depression.
- Measurement point: J-point is the primary measurement point; the depression is maximal AT the J-point and diminishes as the segment ascends toward the T-wave peak
- Gender/lead-specific thresholds: >1 mm (0.1 mV) ST depression at J-point applies across genders. The aVR elevation threshold is >0.5 mm. Standard STEMI gender-specific thresholds do NOT apply because this is not ST elevation.

### 3.5 T-wave Changes
- Direction: Upright (tall) in V1-V6
- Amplitude: TALL — typically 5-15 mm in V2-V4, often exceeding normal T-wave amplitude by 2-3 fold
- Symmetry: SYMMETRIC — this is a key distinguishing feature. The ascending and descending limbs of the T-wave are mirror images, creating a peaked/tented appearance. Asymmetric T-waves (as in LVH or normal variant) are NOT the de Winter pattern.
- Specific patterns: These are hyperacute-type T-waves that do NOT evolve. They differ from typical hyperacute T-waves of STEMI in that: (1) they coexist with J-point depression, (2) they are static, (3) they have a more peaked/symmetric morphology.
- The T-wave base may be broad, but the peak is sharp and pointed

### 3.6 QT/QTc Changes
- QT interval: May appear prolonged due to the tall T-wave amplitude, but true QTc is usually normal or mildly prolonged
- Clinical significance: QT prolongation is not a primary diagnostic feature
- Torsades de Pointes risk: Not directly associated, though acute MI in general carries arrhythmia risk

### 3.7 Other Features
- No U-waves, Osborn waves, epsilon waves, or delta waves characteristic of this condition
- Loss of normal R-wave progression may develop as infarction progresses (poor R-wave progression in V1-V4)
- The pattern may CONVERT to classic STEMI pattern either spontaneously (brief reperfusion/reocclusion) or during catheterization — the cath lab team must be warned
- Reciprocal ST elevation in aVR is an essential component, not an "other" feature — it is diagnostic

## 4. Differential Diagnosis

### 4.1 Mimics (What Looks Like This But Isn't)

| Mimic Condition | Shared Features | Distinguishing Features |
|----------------|-----------------|----------------------|
| Hyperkalemia | Tall, peaked T-waves in precordial leads | Hyperkalemia T-waves are narrow-based and tent-shaped with normal or short QT; NO J-point depression; associated QRS widening, decreased P-wave amplitude, and sine-wave pattern at severe levels; check K+ level |
| LVH with strain pattern | ST depression in lateral/precordial leads, tall T-waves possible | LVH strain produces DOWNSLOPING ST depression (not upsloping); T-waves are asymmetrically inverted (not tall and peaked); meets voltage criteria for LVH; chronic finding |
| Demand ischemia (type 2 MI) | ST depression, tall T-waves possible | Usually horizontal or downsloping depression; T-waves typically not as tall or symmetric; occurs in setting of tachycardia, hypotension, or anemia; changes are dynamic and rate-dependent |
| Digoxin effect | ST depression with characteristic morphology | Digoxin produces "scooped" or "Salvador Dali mustache" ST depression; T-waves are flattened or inverted, NOT tall and peaked; typically diffuse across leads |
| Normal variant tall T-waves (early repolarization) | Tall T-waves in precordial leads | No J-point depression (J-point is elevated or at baseline); T-waves are asymmetric with gentle upslope and rapid downslope; patient is asymptomatic; pattern changes with heart rate |
| Hyperacute T-waves of early STEMI | Tall, peaked T-waves | Classic hyperacute T-waves EVOLVE to ST elevation within minutes to hours; no persistent J-point depression; de Winter pattern is static |
| Posterior STEMI | ST depression in V1-V3 | Posterior STEMI shows horizontal ST depression with upright T-waves (not peaked); tall R-waves in V1-V2 as reciprocal of posterior Q-waves; posterior leads (V7-V9) show ST elevation |

### 4.2 Coexisting Conditions
- **Pre-existing LVH:** May amplify voltage and confuse T-wave interpretation; the UPSLOPING J-point depression with symmetric T-waves is the key differentiator from LVH strain
- **Pre-existing LBBB:** Fundamentally alters ST/T morphology making de Winter pattern uninterpretable; apply Sgarbossa/Smith-modified criteria instead
- **Concurrent hyperkalemia:** May coexist with acute MI (renal failure); both produce peaked T-waves, but hyperkalemia shows narrow-based tenting, QRS widening, and the changes are diffuse (not just precordial)
- **Rate-related changes:** Sinus tachycardia (common during acute MI) may partially mask the ST depression by shortening the ST segment; the tall T-waves remain identifiable
- **Prior anterior MI:** Pre-existing Q-waves or poor R-wave progression may coexist; the de Winter pattern is an acute-on-chronic finding in this case

## 5. STAT Classification

| Priority | Criteria |
|----------|----------|
| **STAT** | YES — de Winter T-waves represent acute LAD occlusion, a STEMI-equivalent. Mortality and morbidity are identical to anterior STEMI. Every minute of delay increases infarct size and risk of cardiogenic shock, ventricular arrhythmia, and death. |
| **Time-sensitive** | IMMEDIATELY — door-to-balloon time targets apply (<90 minutes for PCI, <30 minutes for fibrinolysis if PCI unavailable). This pattern must be flagged at the same priority tier as ST-elevation anterior MI. The static nature of the pattern means serial ECGs will NOT show the expected STEMI evolution, risking dangerous delays if clinicians wait for "confirmation." |
| **Clinical action** | Activate cardiac catheterization lab STAT for emergent PCI. Administer dual antiplatelet therapy (aspirin + P2Y12 inhibitor), anticoagulation (heparin), and nitrates/morphine for pain. If hemodynamically unstable: intra-aortic balloon pump or mechanical support. If PCI not available within 120 minutes: fibrinolytic therapy. Continuous telemetry for ventricular arrhythmias. |

### 6.1 Reasoning Domains Required to Detect This Condition
- **Morphology comparison:** The agent must recognize upsloping ST depression morphology (not horizontal, not downsloping) — this requires shape analysis of the ST segment
- **Amplitude measurement:** J-point depression >1 mm must be quantified; T-wave amplitude and symmetry must be assessed
- **Lead-group correlation:** The pattern must be present across V1-V6 (precordial lead group) AND aVR must show elevation — this requires cross-lead reasoning
- **Pattern recognition:** The three-component pattern (J-point depression + tall symmetric T + aVR elevation) must be recognized as a unified diagnostic entity, not as three separate findings
- **Temporal pattern analysis:** The agent must recognize that the pattern is STATIC (not evolving) — this requires comparison across serial ECGs if available, or recognition within a single recording that the pattern is consistent beat-to-beat
- **Clinical context integration:** Acute chest pain + this ECG pattern = STEMI-equivalent; without clinical context, the pattern alone should still trigger a STAT alert
- Cross-domain reasoning IS required: morphology + amplitude + multi-lead correlation + temporal stability must converge

### 6.2 Feature Dependencies
- **ESSENTIAL features (from SDA-1):**
  - J-point amplitude measurement in each precordial lead (V1-V6) and aVR
  - ST segment slope classification (upsloping vs horizontal vs downsloping) in V1-V6
  - T-wave amplitude in V1-V6
  - T-wave symmetry index (ratio of ascending to descending limb duration) in V1-V6
  - ST elevation measurement in aVR
- **SUPPORTING features (increase confidence):**
  - Heart rate (sinus tachycardia expected in acute MI)
  - QRS duration (narrow expected; wide suggests conduction disease complicating interpretation)
  - Presence or absence of Q-waves (absence supports acute/early presentation)
  - R-wave progression in V1-V6
- **EXCLUDING features (if abnormal, reconsider diagnosis):**
  - ST elevation >1 mm in V1-V6 → this is STEMI, not de Winter
  - QRS >120 ms with LBBB morphology → cannot diagnose de Winter in LBBB
  - Downsloping or horizontal ST depression → suggests demand ischemia or LVH strain, not de Winter
  - T-wave inversion in V1-V6 → suggests Wellens or resolved ischemia, not de Winter
- **Per-beat vs aggregate:** Aggregate analysis sufficient — the pattern is static across all beats

### 6.3 Cross-Condition Interactions
- **Affects other conditions:** If de Winter is present, it may be misclassified as "non-STEMI" or "nonspecific ST changes" by algorithms looking only for ST elevation, causing dangerous misses
- **Requires ruling out:** Hyperkalemia (check T-wave base width and QRS), LVH strain (check voltage criteria and ST slope), demand ischemia (check clinical context and ST slope)
- **Condition combinations:** de Winter + new RBBB suggests very proximal LAD occlusion with septal branch involvement (worse prognosis). de Winter pattern may briefly convert to classic STEMI during spontaneous reperfusion-reocclusion cycles.

### 6.4 Reasoning Chain Sketch
- **Minimum reasoning chain (fewest steps to high confidence):**
  1. Detect J-point depression >1 mm with UPSLOPING morphology in V1-V6
  2. Detect tall, symmetric, peaked T-waves in V1-V6
  3. Detect ST elevation in aVR
  4. All three present → de Winter T-waves → STAT STEMI-equivalent alert

- **Full reasoning chain (complete evidence assembly):**
  1. Measure J-point amplitude in all 12 leads
  2. Classify ST segment slope in V1-V6 as upsloping (required)
  3. Measure T-wave amplitude in V1-V6; confirm >5 mm or significantly above baseline for that patient
  4. Calculate T-wave symmetry index; confirm symmetric morphology
  5. Confirm ST elevation in aVR (>0.5 mm)
  6. Confirm QRS is narrow (no bundle branch block confounding)
  7. Confirm no ST elevation in V1-V6 (exclude STEMI)
  8. Confirm pattern is consistent across all beats (static)
  9. Exclude hyperkalemia pattern (check T-wave base width, QRS duration, P-wave amplitude)
  10. Exclude LVH strain (check voltage criteria, ST slope direction)
  11. Assign diagnosis: de Winter T-waves → STAT STEMI-equivalent

### 6.5 Confidence Anchors
- **HIGH confidence features:**
  - Upsloping ST depression >1 mm at J-point in V1-V6 + tall symmetric peaked T-waves + aVR elevation — all three present simultaneously
  - Pattern is static across all beats and does not evolve on serial ECGs
  - Narrow QRS with no pre-existing conduction abnormality
- **LOW confidence (if absent):**
  - If ST depression is horizontal or downsloping → likely NOT de Winter (demand ischemia/strain)
  - If T-waves are asymmetric → likely NOT de Winter (LVH, normal variant)
  - If aVR does not show ST elevation → pattern is incomplete, lower confidence
  - If pattern evolves to ST elevation → this was hyperacute T-wave phase of STEMI, not de Winter
- **Pathognomonic combination:** Upsloping J-point depression + tall symmetric peaked T in V1-V6 + aVR elevation in a static pattern = near-100% specific for acute LAD occlusion
- **Classification thresholds:**
  - "Possible" de Winter: 2 of 3 criteria present OR all 3 present but mild
  - "Probable" de Winter: all 3 criteria present with clear morphology
  - "Definite" de Winter: all 3 criteria present, static pattern confirmed, mimics excluded

### 6.6 Difficulty Score
| Dimension | Score (1-5) | Notes |
|-----------|-------------|-------|
| Signal clarity needed | 3 | ST depression and T-wave morphology require reasonably clean signal; baseline wander or noise can obscure J-point measurement, but the pattern is large-amplitude |
| Number of leads required | 4 | Requires assessment of V1-V6 (6 leads) plus aVR; cannot be diagnosed from a single lead or small lead subset |
| Cross-domain reasoning | 4 | Must integrate ST segment slope analysis, T-wave morphology/symmetry, amplitude measurement, and multi-lead correlation simultaneously |
| Temporal pattern complexity | 2 | Pattern is STATIC — no beat-to-beat or temporal evolution to track; this actually simplifies detection once the morphology is recognized |
| Differential complexity | 4 | Must distinguish from hyperkalemia, LVH strain, demand ischemia, early STEMI, posterior STEMI, and normal variant — several common mimics |

| Agent | Role for de Winter T-waves | Key Features Used |
|-------|--------------------------|-------------------|
| **RRC** (Rhythm/Rate/Conduction) | Supporting | Heart rate (elevated rate may obscure upsloping ST morphology by causing T-P merging); emits cross_domain_hint if rate is high; QRS duration to exclude LBBB/RBBB artifacts |
| **IT** (Ischemia/Territory) | Primary | Upsloping ST depression ≥1 mm at J-point in V1–V6, tall symmetric peaked T-waves in precordial leads, absence of ST elevation in V1–V6 (critical negative criterion), coronary territory assignment (LAD occlusion), associated ST elevation in aVR ≥1 mm |
| **MR** (Morphology/Repolarization) | Supporting | T-wave morphology analysis — tall, symmetric, positive (hyperacute appearance); T-wave amplitude measurement across precordial leads; distinction from peaked hyperkalemia T-waves (narrow vs broad base) |
| **CDS** (Cross-Domain Synthesis) | Required — CRITICAL: integrates IT's ST morphology with MR's T-wave analysis | Confirms de Winter pattern by combining IT's upsloping ST depression with MR's hyperacute T-wave morphology; resolves differential with hyperkalemia (MR T-wave morphology: narrow-based de Winter vs broad-based hyperkalemia); confirms LAD occlusion territory; flags as STEMI equivalent |

### Primary Agent
**IT** — de Winter T-waves are a STEMI equivalent reflecting proximal LAD occlusion, characterized by upsloping ST depression with hyperacute T-waves in the LAD territory, which is the IT agent's ischemia/territory domain.

### Cross-Domain Hints
- RRC emits `cross_domain_hint: "Elevated heart rate (>100 bpm) — upsloping ST morphology in precordial leads may be distorted by T-P merging; IT ST measurement accuracy may be reduced"` when rate exceeds 100 bpm.
- IT emits `cross_domain_hint: "Upsloping ST depression with tall T-waves detected in precordial leads — MR T-wave morphology analysis required to confirm de Winter pattern vs hyperkalemia"` when this ST/T pattern is identified.

### CDS Specific Role
CDS performs the critical integration of IT's ST morphology findings with MR's T-wave analysis to confirm the de Winter STEMI equivalent pattern. CDS applies the full pattern recognition: upsloping ST depression ≥1 mm (IT) + symmetric tall positive T-waves (MR) + absence of ST elevation (IT) + aVR elevation (IT). CDS resolves the hyperkalemia differential using MR's T-wave base width analysis and lead distribution (de Winter: V1–V6 territorial; hyperkalemia: diffuse). Final output labels this as a STEMI equivalent requiring emergent cath activation.

## 9. Edge Cases and Pitfalls

- **The #1 pitfall:** Automated STEMI detection algorithms look for ST ELEVATION. The de Winter pattern has ST DEPRESSION, so automated algorithms will classify this as "non-STEMI" or "normal," causing catastrophic delays. The agent MUST have a dedicated de Winter detection pathway independent of the STEMI elevation algorithm.
- **Fine VF vs asystole analogy:** Just as fine VF can be missed by looking at one lead, the de Winter pattern can be missed by looking only at limb leads — the diagnostic information is in the precordial leads.
- **Atypical presentations:** Some patients show the pattern in V1-V4 only (not extending to V5-V6) if the LAD occlusion is mid-vessel rather than proximal. The agent should still flag this as possible de Winter if at least V1-V4 show the pattern.
- **Conversion to STEMI:** The pattern may abruptly convert to classic ST-elevation anterior STEMI, either spontaneously or during PCI. This does NOT mean the original de Winter diagnosis was wrong — it confirms acute LAD occlusion.
- **Age/sex variations:** The original de Winter series was predominantly male (83%) with mean age ~57. The pattern can occur in any demographic but index of suspicion should be highest in the typical ACS demographic.
- **Technical artifacts:** Baseline wander can artificially create apparent J-point depression. The agent must assess ST slope (upsloping is specific to de Winter) and T-wave symmetry to distinguish from artifact. High-pass filter settings >0.5 Hz can distort ST segments and create false J-point depression.
- **Masked by LBBB:** Pre-existing LBBB makes de Winter pattern unrecognizable. If LBBB is present, Sgarbossa/Smith criteria should be applied instead; the de Winter pathway should be bypassed.
- **Posterior lead extension:** Some authors report a "posterior de Winter" variant with similar morphology in V7-V9 for RCA occlusion — this is not well-validated and should be flagged as "atypical/uncertain" rather than definite de Winter.
- **Normalization during pain-free intervals:** If the patient has intermittent symptoms, the pattern may temporarily normalize during pain-free intervals and return with recurrent pain, mimicking "dynamic" changes and confusing the static-pattern criterion.
