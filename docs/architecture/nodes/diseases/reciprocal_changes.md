## 1. Pathophysiology Root Cause

### 1.1 What Goes Wrong (Anatomy/Physiology)
Reciprocal ST changes are not a primary pathology — they are an **electrophysiological consequence** of a primary injury (typically acute myocardial infarction or ischemia) viewed from the opposite electrical perspective. Understanding them requires understanding the geometry of the injury vector:

When a region of myocardium is acutely ischemic (as in a STEMI), the ischemic cells generate an **injury current** — a direct current flow from the ischemic zone to the surrounding normal myocardium. This injury current has a direction — it flows **toward the ischemic zone** (from normal toward ischemic tissue during diastole). On the ECG, this current manifests as:
- **ST elevation** in leads whose positive electrode faces the ischemic zone (leads "looking at" the injury)
- **ST depression** in leads whose positive electrode is directed **away** from the ischemic zone — pointing at the opposite side of the heart

This is the reciprocal change: not a second area of ischemia, not an independent finding, but the **same injury vector projected onto the opposite electrical axis**.

The key insight is geometrical: the 12 leads are distributed around the heart at different angles, and any electrical vector simultaneously projects positively onto some leads and negatively onto others. A large anterior injury vector projects positively onto V1-V4 (anterior leads) and negatively onto inferior leads (II, III, aVF).

### 1.2 Electrical Consequence
The injury current vector during acute STEMI is directional and large. In a 3-dimensional electrical field:
- Leads facing the ischemic zone (roughly <90° from vector direction): see ST elevation
- Leads facing away from the ischemic zone (roughly >90° from vector direction): see ST depression
- The degree of ST depression is proportional to the angle between the lead axis and the injury vector (cosine law of vector projection)

This means reciprocal changes are mathematically predictable given the anatomy of the infarct territory and the orientation of each lead.

### 1.3 Why It Appears on ECG
The 12 ECG leads provide 12 different angular views of cardiac electrical activity. When a large injury vector exists (as in STEMI), it simultaneously points toward certain leads and away from others. The classic anatomical pairings:
- **Inferior STEMI** (injury vector points inferiorly, toward II/III/aVF) → ST depression in I and aVL (which point superiorly/laterally, away from inferior injury)
- **Anterior STEMI** (V1-V4, injury vector anterior) → ST depression in II/III/aVF (pointing inferiorly, away from anterior injury)
- **Lateral STEMI** (I/aVL/V5-V6, injury vector leftward/lateral) → ST depression in inferior leads
- **Posterior STEMI** (posterior injury vector): the ST "depression" in V1-V3 IS the primary infarct territory viewed from the anterior — not technically reciprocal; it is the mirror image of the posterior ST elevation

## 2. ECG Presentation — Lead by Lead

### 2.1 Diagnostic Criteria (2025 AHA/ESC Guidelines)

| Criterion | Threshold | Source |
|---|---|---|
| Reciprocal ST depression | ≥1 mm ST depression in anatomically opposite leads from STEMI territory | AHA/ESC STEMI Guidelines 2023 |
| Absence of reciprocal changes in suspected STEMI | Absence of expected reciprocal changes should prompt reconsideration of STEMI diagnosis | Goldberger's; ECG Made Easy |
| Confirms ischemic etiology | Reciprocal depression distinguishes STEMI from pericarditis (pericarditis has no reciprocal depression, only aVR elevation) | ESC 2023 Pericarditis Guidelines |
| Posterior STEMI | ST depression V1-V3 that is the mirror image of posterior ST elevation (not truly reciprocal but managed identically) | AHA/ESC STEMI 2023 |

### 2.2 Lead-by-Lead Manifestation

| Lead | Expected Finding | Why | Sensitivity |
|---|---|---|---|
| I | ST depression in inferior STEMI and posterior STEMI | Lead I points leftward/superiorly — opposite of inferior and posterior injury vectors | High (inferior STEMI) |
| II | ST elevation in inferior STEMI; ST depression in anterior STEMI | Lead II points inferiorly — faces inferior injury; away from anterior injury | High |
| III | ST elevation in inferior STEMI (often most elevated); reciprocal depression in lateral STEMI | Lead III is most inferiorly oriented | High (inferior STEMI) |
| aVR | ST elevation in proximal LAD STEMI / global ischemia (left main equivalent); reciprocal to widespread ST depression | aVR points away from most LV surfaces; its elevation suggests widespread ST depression elsewhere | Moderate |
| aVL | ST depression (reciprocal) in inferior STEMI | aVL points superiorly/leftward — anatomically opposite to inferior injury vector | High (inferior STEMI) — most reliable reciprocal lead |
| aVF | ST elevation in inferior STEMI; ST depression in anterior or lateral STEMI | aVF is the most inferiorly directed limb lead | High |
| V1 | ST elevation in anterior STEMI; ST depression in posterior STEMI (mirror of posterior injury) | V1 faces anterior — elevated in anterior STEMI; posterior STEMI depression "looks through" to the posterior | High (posterior) |
| V2 | ST elevation in anterior STEMI; ST depression in posterior STEMI | V2 also faces anterior; posterior STEMI mirror most prominent here | High |
| V3 | ST elevation in anterior STEMI; ST depression in posterior STEMI | Anterior-facing lead; posterior STEMI mirror | High |
| V4 | ST elevation in anterior/apical STEMI | Anterior-apical facing | Moderate |
| V5 | ST elevation in lateral STEMI; may show reciprocal depression in right-sided STEMI (RCA) | Lateral LV facing | Moderate |
| V6 | ST elevation in lateral STEMI | Lateral LV | Moderate |

### 2.3 Key Leads (Most Diagnostic)
- **aVL**: The most reliable reciprocal lead for inferior STEMI — ST depression in aVL when II/III/aVF are elevated; its presence strongly confirms inferior STEMI
- **V1-V3**: The "reciprocal window" for posterior STEMI — horizontal ST depression (with upright T wave) in these leads should trigger posterior lead assessment
- **I/aVL**: Reciprocal to inferior STEMI (most commonly depressed)
- **II/III/aVF**: Reciprocal to anterior/lateral STEMI

### 2.4 Beat-by-Beat Considerations
- Reciprocal changes appear simultaneously with the primary ST elevation — they are part of the same injury current and evolve in parallel
- As the primary STEMI evolves (ST elevation rises further), reciprocal depression deepens proportionally
- After reperfusion (PCI or thrombolysis), as primary ST elevation resolves, reciprocal ST depression also resolves
- Dynamic reciprocal changes (appearing and disappearing with symptoms) strongly support coronary spasm (Prinzmetal angina) as the mechanism

## 3. Morphology Details

### 3.1 P-wave Changes
- Not directly altered by the ischemia causing reciprocal changes
- If ischemia involves the sinus node region (proximal RCA → sinus node artery), sinus bradycardia or sinus arrest may accompany inferior STEMI
- Atrial infarction (rare) can alter P-wave morphology — PR-segment changes in the primary infarcted territory

### 3.2 PR Interval Changes
- PR depression may occur in the lead with primary injury (a marker of atrial injury or pericarditis)
- In true pericarditis: PR depression is diffuse; reciprocal PR elevation in aVR — this pattern (PR depression + no reciprocal ST depression) distinguishes pericarditis from STEMI
- In inferior STEMI involving the AV node artery: AV block (1st, 2nd, or 3rd degree) may develop, particularly with proximal RCA occlusion

### 3.3 QRS Complex Changes
- During hyperacute phase: no QRS changes initially; early STEMI has ST changes before Q waves develop
- Pathological Q waves (QRS change) develop 1–6 hours into STEMI and indicate myocardial necrosis — these are in the primary infarct territory, NOT in the reciprocal leads
- Reciprocal leads do not develop Q waves from the reciprocal effect alone

### 3.4 ST Segment Changes
- **Primary finding in facing leads**: ST elevation ≥1 mm (limb leads) or ≥2 mm (precordial leads) in ≥2 contiguous leads
- **Reciprocal changes**: ST depression in anatomically opposite leads — horizontal or downsloping shape
- The reciprocal ST depression is typically horizontal or downsloping (not upsloping) — upsloping ST depression is less specific and may represent demand ischemia rather than true reciprocal change
- Morphology of reciprocal depression: often with tall, upright T waves (the mirror image of the T-wave inversion seen in the ischemic zone)

### 3.5 T-wave Changes
- **Hyperacute T waves**: tall, broad, symmetric T waves in the primary infarct territory — the very earliest sign of STEMI before ST elevation appears
- **Reciprocal T-wave depression**: in the opposite leads; or reciprocal tall upright T wave if the primary territory shows T-wave inversion
- Posterior STEMI: the mirror image in V1-V3 shows horizontal ST depression WITH tall upright T waves — the upright T is the mirror of the posterior T-wave inversion; this tall T + ST depression combo in V1-V3 is classic posterior STEMI

### 3.6 QT/QTc Changes
- QTc may be prolonged in acute STEMI from ischemia-related repolarization abnormality
- Not a primary feature of reciprocal changes specifically

### 3.7 Other Features
- **Posterior STEMI nuance**: The ST depression in V1-V3 in posterior STEMI is NOT truly reciprocal — it IS the primary infarct territory, viewed from the anterior. Placing V7-V9 leads (posterior leads) converts the depression to elevation, confirming posterior MI. Clinical management is identical to STEMI — emergent PCI
- **aVR elevation** in global ischemia: ST elevation in aVR (≥1 mm) + diffuse ST depression in I/II/III/aVF/V4-V6 = left main or proximal LAD occlusion / global subendocardial ischemia — not reciprocal to a single territory; represents a distinct high-risk pattern

## 4. Differential Diagnosis

### 4.1 Mimics

| Mimic Condition | Shared Features | Distinguishing Features |
|---|---|---|
| Pericarditis | Diffuse ST elevation | Pericarditis: diffuse ST elevation in multiple lead groups (anterior + inferior + lateral simultaneously); NO reciprocal depression (except aVR which is elevated); PR depression; saddle-shaped morphology |
| Demand ischemia (subendocardial) | ST depression | Demand ischemia: ST depression in the ischemic territory itself (not reciprocal); diffuse or horizontal; no concomitant ST elevation in a specific territory |
| Early repolarization | ST elevation in precordial leads | Early repolarization: benign; concave (smiley face) ST elevation; present in lateral leads, young patients; NO reciprocal depression; no dynamic change with symptoms |
| Takotsubo cardiomyopathy | Widespread ST elevation (can mimic anterior STEMI) | Takotsubo: apical ballooning; ST elevation often in V2-V5; may lack classical reciprocal pattern; follows emotional/physical trigger; troponin elevated |
| Left ventricular aneurysm | Persistent ST elevation | Chronic elevation; no acute symptoms; Q waves present; no dynamic change; no reciprocal depression (chronic pattern) |
| Hyperkalemia | Peaked T waves; QRS changes | Hyperkalemia: peaked T waves are diffuse (all leads); no specific ST elevation territory; PR prolongation; no reciprocal pattern |

### 4.2 Coexisting Conditions
- **RBBB or LBBB**: conduction disease can distort ST segments in both primary and reciprocal leads — use Sgarbossa criteria in LBBB; RBBB complicates anterior STEMI interpretation
- **LVH strain pattern**: chronic ST depression in lateral leads can confound reciprocal change detection in inferior STEMI
- **Pre-existing LBBB**: completely masks standard STEMI/reciprocal analysis — Sgarbossa required
- **Prior MI**: old Q waves with chronic ST changes can overlap with new reciprocal changes

## 5. STAT Classification

| Priority | Criteria |
|---|---|
| STAT (Immediate) | Any primary STEMI with reciprocal changes confirmed → catheterization lab activation; reciprocal changes confirm ischemic etiology and increase urgency |
| STAT (Immediate) | ST depression V1-V3 with upright T waves (posterior STEMI pattern) → posterior leads (V7-V9) must be placed immediately; if posterior STEMI confirmed → cath lab |
| STAT (Immediate) | aVR ST elevation ≥1 mm + diffuse ST depression → left main equivalent; emergent cardiac evaluation |
| Urgent | Expected reciprocal changes absent in suspected STEMI → question diagnosis; consider pericarditis, early repolarization, Takotsubo |
| Urgent | Dynamic reciprocal changes (appearing/resolving with symptoms) → likely Prinzmetal angina; urgent cardiology evaluation |

## 6. Reasoning Complexity Analysis (Feeds Into Node 2.1 — Agent Architecture Research)
> NOTE: Does NOT pre-assign agents. Documents reasoning complexity for Node 2.1.

### 6.1 Reasoning Domains Required
- Primary ST elevation detection: territory identification (anterior, inferior, lateral, posterior)
- Anatomical vector analysis: given primary territory → predict which leads should show reciprocal depression
- Reciprocal change verification: confirm depression is in expected leads, not additional territories
- Absence reasoning: absence of expected reciprocal changes challenges STEMI diagnosis
- Posterior STEMI pattern: recognize V1-V3 horizontal ST depression + upright T as posterior infarction (not reciprocal)
- aVR elevation pattern: left main equivalent recognition
- Pericarditis exclusion: diffuse ST elevation without reciprocal depression = pericarditis
- Morphology analysis: horizontal/downsloping depression vs. upsloping depression; reciprocal tall T vs. T inversion

### 6.2 Feature Dependencies
- Reciprocal change interpretation requires PRIOR identification of the primary STEMI territory
- aVL reciprocal depression is most reliable for inferior STEMI — must be assessed in context of inferior ST elevation
- Posterior STEMI detection requires recognition that V1-V3 depression IS the primary territory — needs posterior lead recommendation
- Pericarditis differential requires simultaneous multi-territory ST elevation assessment

### 6.3 Cross-Condition Interactions
- **STEMI + LBBB**: secondary ST changes of LBBB can mask reciprocal pattern; Sgarbossa criteria supersede standard reciprocal analysis
- **Inferior STEMI + RV infarction**: proximal RCA occlusion causes both inferior STEMI and RV infarction; ST elevation in V3R-V4R (right-sided leads) = RV involvement; reciprocal changes in aVL still present; critical because vasodilators (nitrates) are contraindicated in RV infarction
- **Anterior STEMI + posterior extension**: LAD wrap-around → both anterior ST elevation and posterior ST depression (which may appear as reciprocal but is actually co-infarction)
- **Pericarditis vs. STEMI**: the single most critical reciprocal change clinical decision — reciprocal depression = STEMI; absence of reciprocal depression = likely pericarditis

### 6.4 Reasoning Chain Sketch
1. Scan all 12 leads for ST elevation ≥1 mm (limb) or ≥2 mm (precordial) in ≥2 contiguous leads
2. If ST elevation found: identify territory (inferior, anterior, lateral, posterior window)
3. For inferior ST elevation (II/III/aVF): check aVL for reciprocal depression — most reliable confirmation
4. For anterior ST elevation (V1-V4): check II/III/aVF for reciprocal depression
5. For lateral ST elevation (I/aVL/V5-V6): check inferior leads for reciprocal depression
6. If ST depression in V1-V3 + upright T waves (no ST elevation elsewhere): flag posterior STEMI; recommend V7-V9 leads
7. If aVR elevation ≥1 mm + diffuse ST depression: flag left main equivalent
8. Check for expected reciprocal changes — if absent: challenge STEMI diagnosis; consider pericarditis
9. Assess morphology: horizontal/downsloping depression (specific) vs. upsloping (less specific)
10. Integrate with PR segment (PR depression → pericarditis); QRS changes (Q waves = established infarct)
11. Generate territory-specific STEMI alert with reciprocal confirmation status

### 6.5 Confidence Anchors
- High confidence STEMI: primary ST elevation in territory + expected reciprocal depression in opposite leads
- Very high confidence: aVL depression ≥1 mm + inferior ST elevation ≥1 mm (II/III/aVF) — one of the most reliable ECG pairs
- Moderate confidence STEMI: primary ST elevation without expected reciprocal changes — question diagnosis; check for conduction disease, pericarditis
- Posterior STEMI: V1-V3 horizontal ST depression + tall T without primary elevation territory — high suspicion for posterior STEMI; send posterior leads
- Uncertainty triggers: LBBB (Sgarbossa required); LVH strain (baseline ST abnormalities confound reciprocal assessment); early repolarization (mimics reciprocal patterns)

## 6A. Agent Assignment

**Architecture:** Option E — 3 Parallel Phase 1 Specialists + 1 Sequential CDS (Node 2.1 Gate Passed 2026-03-27)

| Agent | Role for Reciprocal Changes | Key Features Used |
|-------|--------------------------|-------------------|
| **RRC** (Rhythm/Rate/Conduction) | Supporting | Heart rate, rhythm; axis confirmation to validate expected vector relationships |
| **IT** (Ischemia/Territory) | Primary | ST elevation in primary territory (II/III/aVF for inferior; V1–V4 for anterior; I/aVL for high lateral), ST depression in non-ischemic leads (I/aVL for inferior STEMI; II/III/aVF for lateral STEMI; V1–V3 for posterior STEMI), vector relationship assessment between elevation and depression leads |
| **MR** (Morphology/Repolarization) | Not involved | — |
| **CDS** (Cross-Domain Synthesis) | Required — CRITICAL: identifies ischemic territory from elevation leads and confirms depression is reciprocal | Receives IT's elevation and depression lead maps; applies vector analysis to confirm depression leads are mirror-opposite to elevation leads; distinguishes true reciprocal depression from primary ischemia (e.g., if II/III/aVF shows depression alongside V1–V4 elevation, CDS determines if this is reciprocal to anterior STEMI or represents concurrent inferior ischemia) |

### Primary Agent
**IT** — reciprocal changes are ST depressions that mirror ST elevation in the opposite leads, which requires IT's full ischemia/territory analysis of both the elevation and depression distributions.

### Cross-Domain Hints
- IT emits `cross_domain_hint: "ST depression detected in leads without elevation — CDS must determine if depression is reciprocal to identified elevation territory or represents independent ischemia in a second territory"` whenever ST depression accompanies ST elevation in different lead groups.

### CDS Specific Role
CDS is the essential agent for reciprocal change interpretation — this condition is inherently cross-domain by definition. CDS receives IT's complete lead-by-lead ST map (elevation leads and depression leads) and applies vector geometry: the injury current vector points away from the ischemic zone, so the most depressed lead should be approximately opposite to the most elevated lead (e.g., III > II elevation → aVL maximum depression; V2–V3 elevation → aVF/III depression). If the depression leads align with the expected mirror image, CDS labels the depression as reciprocal and notes it as supportive evidence increasing STEMI specificity to >95%. If the depression leads are not mirror-opposite to the elevation leads, CDS flags potential dual-territory ischemia.

### 8.3 Clinician Explanation (Plain Language)

**ER Nurse version:**
"When one part of the heart is having a heart attack and the tracing shows ST elevation in those leads, the leads on the opposite side of the heart will show ST depression. This is called a reciprocal change. It is normal and expected during a heart attack and helps confirm that the ST elevation is real. If there are NO reciprocal changes where they should be, the pattern may not be a true heart attack."

**Cardiologist version:**
"Reciprocal ST depression: electrophysiological consequence of the injury current vector projecting negatively onto leads anatomically opposite to the infarct territory. Confirms ischemic etiology (pericarditis lacks reciprocal depression). aVL depression is the most reliable reciprocal marker for inferior STEMI — sensitivity ~80% for inferior STEMI when aVL depression ≥1 mm. Absence of expected reciprocal changes should prompt differential reconsideration. V1-V3 horizontal ST depression + tall T in the absence of primary elevation territory = posterior STEMI — posterior leads mandatory. Left main equivalent: aVR elevation ≥1 mm + diffuse ST depression — bypass/IABP consideration."

## 9. Edge Cases and Pitfalls

- **Posterior STEMI is the most missed STEMI**: V1-V3 horizontal ST depression is the only surface ECG clue; routinely overlooked because clinicians look for elevation; always place V7-V9 when horizontal ST depression + tall T in V1-V3 is present
- **Inferior STEMI without aVL depression**: ~20% of inferior STEMIs do not show aVL depression (especially right-dominant infarction with straight inferior vector); absence of aVL depression does not exclude inferior STEMI
- **Pericarditis misdiagnosis as STEMI**: pericarditis has diffuse ST elevation across multiple territories simultaneously; STEMI has territory-specific elevation with reciprocal depression; the presence of reciprocal depression is the key distinguishing feature
- **ST depression in the setting of SVT or tachycardia**: demand ischemia; not reciprocal; resolves with rate control; does not indicate STEMI unless a primary elevation territory is also identified
- **LBBB and reciprocal changes**: standard reciprocal analysis is invalid in LBBB; Sgarbossa's concordant ST elevation is the STEMI marker in this setting
- **RV infarction with inferior STEMI**: recognize by ST elevation in right-sided leads V3R-V4R; reciprocal changes in aVL still expected; give IV fluids, avoid nitrates and diuretics
- **"Reverse" reciprocal changes**: if a patient has primary ST depression (subendocardial ischemia) without a clear elevation territory, there may not be true reciprocal elevation — this is not the same as STEMI reciprocal changes
- **Early inferior STEMI with subtle elevation**: if II/III/aVF elevation is ≤1 mm, look at aVL for any depression — even subtle aVL depression (0.5 mm) in this context should raise high suspicion and trigger serial ECGs
- **Hyperacute T waves before ST elevation**: in the very early minutes of STEMI, before ST elevation develops, the opposite leads may show reciprocal T-wave flattening or inversion — a subtle early clue
