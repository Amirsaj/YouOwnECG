## 1. Pathophysiology Root Cause

### 1.1 Cardiovascular Physiology of Pregnancy
Pregnancy imposes progressive hemodynamic demands on the cardiovascular system:
- Blood volume increases 40–50% by the third trimester
- Cardiac output increases 30–50% (increased stroke volume + modest heart rate increase)
- Systemic vascular resistance decreases (progesterone-mediated vasodilation)
- Diaphragm elevation by the gravid uterus: physically displaces the heart upward and leftward in the thorax

These changes produce predictable ECG shifts that are physiological and expected.

### 1.2 Hormonal Effects on Cardiac Electrophysiology
Estrogen and progesterone modulate ion channel expression:
- Estrogen prolongs QT duration (reduces IKr — the rapid delayed rectifier potassium current)
- Progesterone increases sympathetic tone and may promote arrhythmia vulnerability
- Net effect: QT interval is modestly prolonged throughout pregnancy; pre-existing LQTS may be unmasked postpartum (estrogen withdrawal effect)
- Increased adrenergic sensitivity: PACs, PVCs, and SVT are more common during pregnancy, particularly in the second and third trimesters

### 1.3 Positional Changes
Diaphragm elevation physically shifts the cardiac axis leftward. This is a mechanical/positional effect — not structural remodeling. The shift is most pronounced in the third trimester and resolves after delivery. Q waves and T-wave changes in lead III reflect this axis shift — the heart is rotated such that lead III now captures inferior wall in a different geometric orientation.

## 2. ECG Presentation — Lead by Lead

### 2.1 Diagnostic Criteria — Normal Pregnancy ECG Changes

| Change | Trimester | Mechanism | Normal Range / Threshold |
|---|---|---|---|
| Sinus tachycardia | 1st–3rd | Increased cardiac output demand | HR up to 100 bpm; often 80–95 bpm |
| Left axis shift | 2nd–3rd | Diaphragm elevation, leftward cardiac rotation | Axis shift of −15° to −30° from pre-pregnancy |
| Small Q wave in III | 3rd | Positional; septal vector shift | Q <40 ms, <25% R — normal variant |
| T inversion in III | 2nd–3rd | Positional | Isolated III T inversion without symptoms — normal |
| ST depression / T flattening in III | 2nd–3rd | Positional | Mild, isolated to III — normal variant |
| QT prolongation | Throughout | Estrogen effect on IKr | QTc may reach 440–460 ms (upper normal in pregnancy) |
| PACs and PVCs | 2nd–3rd | Hormonal arrhythmia susceptibility | Isolated PACs/PVCs — normal; symptomatic or complex — evaluate |

### 2.2 Lead-by-Lead Manifestation

| Lead | Normal Pregnancy Change | Abnormal Finding (Requires Evaluation) |
|---|---|---|
| I | Usually unchanged; HR elevation | ST depression >1 mm; deep T inversion |
| II | Usually unchanged | ST depression; T inversion |
| III | Q wave (small), T inversion, ST depression | ST elevation; deep Q >40 ms or >25% R (inferior MI concern) |
| aVR | Usually unchanged | ST elevation >1 mm (LMCA/proximal LAD concern) |
| aVL | Left axis shift may enlarge R | ST depression, T inversion |
| aVF | Small changes with axis shift | ST depression or elevation; new Q wave |
| V1 | Unchanged; mild HR effect | RBBB, ST elevation (Brugada), T inversion beyond variant |
| V2 | Unchanged | ST depression, new T inversion |
| V3 | Unchanged | ST depression, new T inversion |
| V4 | Unchanged | ST depression |
| V5 | Unchanged | ST depression; ischemic T inversion |
| V6 | Unchanged | ST depression |

### 2.3 Key Leads
- **Lead III:** The lead most commonly showing normal pregnancy-related ST/T changes. Isolated III findings = positional variant. Multi-lead extension = evaluate.
- **aVR:** Any ST elevation in aVR with diffuse ST depression in pregnancy warrants urgent evaluation — same significance as outside pregnancy (LMCA/multivessel disease or proximal LAD occlusion).
- **V1–V6:** Pregnancy does not cause anterior, lateral, or posterior ST changes. New precordial ST/T changes in pregnancy are pathological until proven otherwise.

### 2.4 Beat-by-Beat Considerations
PACs are common in pregnancy — the system should not flag isolated PACs as requiring urgent evaluation in a pregnant patient unless they are frequent (>1000/24 h), symptomatic, or initiate SVT. PVCs in pregnancy are common; frequent monomorphic PVCs without structural heart disease are generally benign but should prompt echo if frequent. SVT in pregnancy: AVNRT is the most common, often first presenting in pregnancy. Vagal maneuvers and adenosine are safe in pregnancy.

## 3. Morphology Details

### 3.1 Left Axis Deviation
The axis shift is typically −15° to −30° from the patient's pre-pregnancy baseline. In absolute terms, the axis may reach −30° (borderline left axis deviation) in the third trimester. This is positional, not pathological. If the axis reaches ≤−45° (marked LAD), evaluate for LAFB.

### 3.2 Q Wave and T Inversion in Lead III
The small Q wave in III (septal vector, positional) is the same phenomenon as the normal Q in V5/V6 — simply altered by cardiac rotation. T inversion in III in isolation is benign in pregnancy. Key rule: if III shows changes AND inferior leads II and aVF show concordant changes (ST depression, new Q, T inversion), evaluate for inferior ischemia. Isolated III = variant. Multi-lead inferior = evaluate.

### 3.3 QTc in Pregnancy
Estrogen prolongs QT throughout pregnancy. QTc of 440–460 ms may be normal in late pregnancy. QTc ≥480 ms in pregnancy requires evaluation — LQTS may be unmasked. CRITICAL: QTc prolongation is most dangerous POSTPARTUM, when estrogen levels drop rapidly and the QT-prolonging protective/modulatory effects shift. Postpartum is the highest-risk period for LQTS-related cardiac events in women.

### 3.4 ST Changes in Precordial Leads
Pregnancy does not cause ST changes in the precordial leads. Any ST depression or elevation in V1–V6 in a pregnant patient should be evaluated as in any non-pregnant patient. Peripartum cardiomyopathy (PPCM) can produce LBBB, poor R progression, and LVH changes in precordial leads — this is a pathological ECG in a pregnant patient, not a normal variant.

### 3.5 Arrhythmia Frequency
Second and third trimester: increased PAC and PVC frequency (hormonal + hemodynamic). SVT is more common in pregnancy than in age-matched non-pregnant women. New onset atrial fibrillation in pregnancy is uncommon and should prompt structural and thyroid evaluation. Ventricular arrhythmias in pregnancy warrant evaluation for ARVC, LQTS, or catecholaminergic polymorphic VT (CPVT) as underlying substrate.

### 3.6 Peripartum Cardiomyopathy ECG Pattern
PPCM (last month of pregnancy to 5 months postpartum): dilated cardiomyopathy with reduced EF. ECG findings:
- LBBB (most specific ECG finding)
- Poor R-wave progression in precordial leads
- LVH voltage criteria
- Non-specific ST/T changes
- Atrial and ventricular arrhythmias
- Sinus tachycardia (sympathetic compensation for reduced CO)

### 3.7 Pre-eclampsia and Eclampsia
Pre-eclampsia (hypertension + proteinuria) can cause LVH from pressure overload. Eclampsia can produce an ECG pattern resembling Takotsubo cardiomyopathy (catecholamine surge from seizure). QTc prolongation is more severe in eclampsia. Hypomagnesemia (common in eclampsia treatment with magnesium sulfate infusion) can paradoxically normalize QTc — but magnesium toxicity causes widened PR, wide QRS, and ultimately cardiac arrest.

## 4. Differential Diagnosis

### 4.1 Mimics

| Condition | ECG Overlap | Key Differentiator |
|---|---|---|
| Inferior ischemia/STEMI | ST changes in III, T inversion | Multi-lead involvement (II, III, aVF); ST elevation not depression; troponin; symptoms |
| AVNRT | SVT in pregnancy | Regular narrow-complex tachycardia; abrupt onset/offset; responds to vagal maneuvers |
| Pulmonary embolism (PE) | Sinus tachycardia, S1Q3T3 | Right heart strain; S1Q3T3 pattern; hypoxia; ultrasound/CTPA |
| Peripartum cardiomyopathy | ST/T changes, arrhythmias, LBBB | LBBB new; reduced EF on echo; timing (peripartum) |
| Thyroid disease (hyperthyroidism) | Sinus tachycardia, AFib | TSH; persistent tachycardia beyond expected |
| Drug effects (tocolytics) | Tachycardia, QT changes | Terbutaline (sinus tachycardia); ritodrine (similar) — medication history |

### 4.2 Coexisting Conditions
Pregnant patients with structural heart disease (congenital, valvular) require individualized ECG interpretation — pregnancy-related ECG changes overlay their baseline abnormalities. Prior ECG comparison is essential. Patients with known LQTS entering pregnancy require intensive QTc monitoring especially in the postpartum period.

## 5. STAT Classification

| Presentation | Classification | Rationale |
|---|---|---|
| Isolated lead III ST/T changes in pregnancy | **Routine — document as variant** | Positional; no action if isolated |
| Sinus tachycardia HR 90–100 bpm, 3rd trimester | **Routine** | Expected hemodynamic adaptation |
| QTc ≥480 ms in pregnancy | **Urgent** | LQTS evaluation; monitor postpartum |
| New LBBB in pregnancy | **STAT** | Peripartum cardiomyopathy until proven otherwise |
| Multi-lead ST depression (inferior or precordial) | **STAT** | Ischemia in pregnancy — high-risk situation |
| STEMI criteria met in any lead | **STAT** | Same urgency as non-pregnant patient; cath team activation |
| Sustained SVT, AF, or VT | **STAT** | Hemodynamic compromise; fetal hypoperfusion risk |
| S1Q3T3 + hypoxia in pregnant patient | **STAT** | Pulmonary embolism — urgent CTPA (shielded) |

## 6. Reasoning Complexity Analysis

### 6.1 Pattern Recognition Challenge
The challenge is not recognizing pregnancy-related changes — these are few and specific (axis shift, lead III findings, mild tachycardia). The challenge is correctly NOT triggering ischemia workup for isolated III findings, while correctly TRIGGERING workup when the same-looking findings extend beyond III.

### 6.2 Lead III Isolation Rule
The core rule: if changes are isolated to lead III (with or without mild aVF involvement that could be positional), classify as normal variant. If changes involve II, aVF + III together, treat as inferior ischemia. This distinction requires measuring ST amplitude and T-wave direction in all three inferior leads simultaneously.

### 6.3 Postpartum Risk Window
The ECG system processes real-time data. The postpartum risk window (QTc monitoring obligation for known LQTS patients) is a temporal trigger — requires knowing delivery date and activating heightened QTc monitoring in the 0–5 months postpartum period.

### 6.4 Drug Interaction Awareness
Tocolytic medications (terbutaline, ritodrine) cause sinus tachycardia and may prolong QTc. Anti-nausea medications (ondansetron, promethazine) are QT-prolonging. The system should flag drug-induced QTc prolongation in pregnant patients as clinically relevant (additive risk).

### 6.5 PPCM Detection
New LBBB or marked deterioration in ECG quality (arrhythmias, voltage changes) in a patient in the peripartum period must trigger PPCM consideration and prompt echo recommendation.

### 6.6 Difficulty Score

| Dimension | Score (1–5) | Rationale |
|---|---|---|
| Pattern recognition | 2 | Normal changes are few and specific |
| Lead III isolation rule | 3 | Clear rule, but requires measuring all inferior leads |
| Postpartum temporal monitoring | 3 | Requires temporal trigger logic and delivery date input |
| Drug interaction awareness | 3 | Requires medication history parsing |
| PPCM detection | 3 | LBBB + peripartum timing = clear trigger |
| Differential complexity | 2 | Differentials are manageable |
| Clinical urgency calibration | 3 | Risk of over- or under-calling ischemia in pregnancy |
| **Overall** | **2.5** | **Moderate complexity** |

| Agent | Role for Pregnancy ECG | Key Features Used |
|-------|--------------------------|-------------------|
| **RRC** (Rhythm/Rate/Conduction) | Supporting | Sinus tachycardia (physiological in pregnancy, rate 90-100bpm common), increased PAC/PVC frequency (common normal variant in pregnancy), left axis shift from elevated diaphragm |
| **IT** (Ischemia/Territory) | Supporting | ST changes and T-wave inversions in lead III may mimic inferior ischemia — must not classify pregnancy-related isolated lead III changes as inferior STEMI or ischemia |
| **MR** (Morphology/Repolarization) | Primary | Left axis shift (elevated diaphragm repositions heart horizontally), T-wave inversion in lead III (normal variant in pregnancy), Q-wave in lead III (normal variant), ST-T changes from elevated diaphragm effects, QTc changes across trimesters |
| **CDS** (Cross-Domain Synthesis) | Required — resolves pregnancy normal variants vs pathological changes; flags when changes exceed expected physiological range | Integrates MR normal variant pattern (Q/T in III + left axis) + RRC sinus tachycardia → expected pregnancy ECG findings; escalates if changes extend beyond lead III, if ST depression is territorial, or if QTc is markedly prolonged (drug/electrolyte concern) |

### Primary Agent
**MR** — The key pregnancy ECG features (T-wave inversion in lead III, Q-wave in lead III, left axis shift from diaphragm elevation, ST changes) are morphology and repolarization findings within MR's domain.

### Cross-Domain Hints
- MR → IT: `cross_domain_hint: "T-wave inversion and/or Q-wave in lead III detected — IT must not classify as inferior ischemia; isolated lead III changes are normal pregnancy variant; CDS to confirm if changes are isolated vs extending to II/aVF"`
- IT → CDS: `cross_domain_hint: "ST/T changes in inferior leads detected — CDS should determine if isolated to lead III (pregnancy normal variant) vs involving II and aVF (potential inferior ischemia requiring evaluation)"`
- RRC → CDS: `cross_domain_hint: "Sinus tachycardia detected in pregnancy context — if MR shows left axis shift + isolated lead III changes, combined picture = expected physiological pregnancy findings; flag if rate >120bpm (may indicate pathological cause)"`

### CDS Specific Role
CDS resolves the pregnancy ECG interpretation challenge by applying the pregnancy normal variant rules: isolated Q-wave in lead III + T-wave inversion in lead III + left axis shift + sinus tachycardia together represent expected physiological findings of pregnancy (elevated diaphragm + increased cardiac output + autonomic changes). CDS flags pathological departures: T-wave inversions or ST depression extending to leads II and aVF (inferior ischemia cannot be dismissed as normal variant), ST elevation in any lead, QTc markedly prolonged (drug-induced or congenital LQTS — significant risk in peripartum period), or new bundle branch block. CDS generates a final output distinguishing "expected pregnancy ECG variant" from "findings requiring clinical evaluation in the context of pregnancy."

## 9. Edge Cases and Pitfalls

- **Never dismiss multi-lead inferior ST changes as "pregnancy variant."** The positional variant applies only to lead III. ST depression in II AND aVF AND III is inferior ischemia until proven otherwise — pregnancy does not modify this.
- **PPCM misdiagnosed as anxiety:** Tachycardia, dyspnea, and edema in the postpartum period are attributed to normal recovery. New LBBB or arrhythmias in a postpartum patient must prompt echo.
- **LQTS unmasking postpartum:** A woman with previously undiagnosed LQTS may have had a "protected" QTc during pregnancy (estrogen effect), with dangerous QTc lengthening after delivery. Any syncope or cardiac arrest in the postpartum period warrants QTc assessment.
- **Magnesium toxicity:** Patients receiving IV magnesium sulfate for pre-eclampsia or tocolysis may develop PR prolongation, widened QRS, and ultimately VF as toxicity progresses. ECG monitoring is required during infusion.
- **Thyrotoxicosis in pregnancy:** Hyperthyroidism during pregnancy causes sinus tachycardia, high voltage, and rarely AF. Common in hyperemesis gravidarum (hCG cross-reacts with TSH receptor). TSH must be checked.
- **Amniotic fluid embolism:** Rare, catastrophic. ECG shows sinus tachycardia, then right heart strain pattern, then arrhythmias and cardiac arrest. S1Q3T3 in a peripartum patient in collapse is amniotic fluid embolism until proven otherwise.
