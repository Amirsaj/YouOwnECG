## 1. Pathophysiology Root Cause

### 1.1 What Goes Wrong
- Fluid accumulates in the pericardial space (normally 15-50 mL of physiological fluid). Etiologies: inflammatory (pericarditis, myopericarditis), infectious, malignant (most common cause of large chronic effusions — lung, breast, lymphoma), traumatic (aortic dissection, post-cardiac surgery), renal (uremia), hypothyroid, autoimmune (lupus, rheumatoid arthritis), or idiopathic
- Cardiac tamponade occurs when intrapericardial pressure exceeds intracardiac diastolic pressure, impairing ventricular filling. This is determined by both the VOLUME of fluid AND the RATE of accumulation — a slowly accumulating 2L effusion may cause minimal hemodynamic compromise, while 200 mL accumulated rapidly (e.g., aortic dissection rupture into pericardium) can be immediately fatal
- Tamponade physiology: elevated intrapericardial pressure → impaired RV filling → interventricular septum shifts leftward → reduced LV filling → reduced stroke volume → compensatory sinus tachycardia → if untreated → cardiogenic shock → cardiac arrest
- The clinical triad of tamponade (Beck's triad): hypotension + elevated JVP + muffled heart sounds
- Pulsus paradoxus (>10 mmHg drop in systolic BP during inspiration): hallmark of tamponade — exaggerated normal respiratory variation due to fixed pericardial constraint and competitive ventricular filling

### 1.2 Electrical Consequence
- Large pericardial effusion creates a layer of electrically resistive fluid between the myocardium and the recording electrodes (skin-surface leads). Fluid attenuates the electrical signal — similar to recording through a resistive medium → reduced QRS amplitude (low voltage)
- The heart is no longer anchored normally by the pericardium — it literally floats and oscillates within the fluid. The oscillation frequency is approximately 1 oscillation per 2 cardiac cycles (0.5 Hz oscillation within a ~1 Hz heart rate), driven by the mechanical resonance of the heart-fluid system
- This oscillatory swinging of the whole cardiac mass within the fluid changes the electrical axis of the heart with each swing — the QRS vector points in one direction on beat 1, then swings back to a slightly different direction on beat 2, then back to beat 1 position, and so on → ELECTRICAL ALTERNANS
- The swinging is constant and periodic (every other beat), producing a perfectly alternating pattern: beat 1 and beat 3 look alike, beat 2 and beat 4 look alike — this alternating pattern affecting QRS, and sometimes P-wave and T-wave

### 1.3 Why It Appears on ECG
- Low voltage: fluid resistance between myocardium and skin electrodes attenuates all electrical signals uniformly → ALL QRS amplitudes reduced across all leads simultaneously
- Electrical alternans: periodic cardiac swinging → beat-to-beat axis change → QRS amplitude alternates between two values every other beat. This is visible on surface ECG because the axis change is large enough (often >20°) to produce measurable amplitude differences between alternating beats
- Sinus tachycardia: compensatory response to reduced stroke volume from impaired cardiac filling — the heart increases rate to maintain cardiac output (HR × SV = CO)
- The ECG TRIAD of tamponade: low voltage + electrical alternans + sinus tachycardia
- Not all three need be present simultaneously — electrical alternans is most specific; low voltage is most sensitive; sinus tachycardia is most common

## 2. ECG Presentation — Lead by Lead

### 2.1 Diagnostic Criteria (2025 AHA/ESC Guidelines)

| Criterion | Threshold | Notes |
|-----------|-----------|-------|
| **Low voltage (limb leads)** | QRS amplitude <5 mm (0.5 mV) in ALL limb leads | Both positive and negative deflection measured; must be present in all limb leads, not just some |
| **Low voltage (precordial leads)** | QRS amplitude <10 mm (1.0 mV) in ALL precordial leads | Alternative criterion; limb lead criterion is more specific |
| **Electrical alternans** | Beat-to-beat variation in QRS amplitude ≥1 mm in any lead | Most specific ECG sign of tamponade; may affect P-wave and T-wave also |
| **Total electrical alternans** | Alternation of P-wave + QRS + T-wave amplitude/morphology | Virtually pathognomonic for tamponade when present |
| **Sinus tachycardia** | HR >100 bpm | Compensatory; persistent and often proportional to hemodynamic compromise |
| **PR depression / ST elevation** | If concurrent pericarditis is present | May reflect underlying inflammatory process that caused the effusion |

**Clinical note (ESC 2015 Guidelines on Pericardial Diseases)**: ECG low voltage and/or electrical alternans in a patient with suspected effusion should prompt EMERGENT echocardiography. Echocardiography — not ECG — is the definitive diagnostic modality for pericardial effusion and tamponade.

### 2.2 Lead-by-Lead Manifestation

| Lead | Expected Finding | Why | Sensitivity |
|------|-----------------|-----|-------------|
| I | Low voltage QRS; alternating amplitude beat-to-beat | Fluid attenuates lateral-directed electrical signal; swinging axis changes projection onto I | High for low voltage |
| II | Low voltage QRS; alternating amplitude; sinus tachycardia; PR depression if concurrent pericarditis | Fluid attenuates inferolateral signal; best lead to see rhythm and PR segment | High for rhythm/rate; moderate for alternans |
| III | Low voltage QRS; alternating amplitude | Inferior projection — same attenuation mechanism | Moderate |
| aVR | Low voltage; reciprocal pattern to limb leads | Cavity-facing lead — same signal attenuation through fluid | Moderate |
| aVL | Low voltage; alternating amplitude | High lateral wall — same mechanism | Moderate |
| aVF | Low voltage; alternating amplitude | Inferior projection — same mechanism | Moderate |
| V1 | Low voltage; alternating amplitude; may show P-wave alternans | Anterior-facing; useful for detecting P-wave alternans when swinging affects atrial position | Moderate-High |
| V2 | Low voltage; alternating QRS amplitude | Anterior projection — attenuated by fluid | High |
| V3 | Low voltage; alternating QRS amplitude | Anterior-apical — same | High |
| V4 | Low voltage; alternating QRS amplitude | Anterior-apical — optimal for detecting alternans | High — V4 often shows clearest alternans |
| V5 | Low voltage; alternating QRS amplitude | Anterolateral — same | High |
| V6 | Low voltage; alternating QRS amplitude | Lateral — same | High |

### 2.3 Key Leads
- **Limb leads (I, II, III, aVR, aVL, aVF)**: Best for assessing LOW VOLTAGE — must check all 6 limb leads. QRS <5 mm in ALL limb leads = low voltage. If any single limb lead has QRS ≥5 mm, the limb lead criterion is not met
- **V4-V5**: Often show most prominent QRS alternation — the anterior-apical leads have the best view of the axis swing produced by cardiac swinging in the fluid
- **Lead II**: Best for rhythm strip, rate assessment, and PR depression detection. The rhythm strip should show clearly alternating QRS heights (taller-shorter-taller-shorter pattern)
- **V1**: Best for P-wave alternans detection — the small P-wave amplitude and distinct atrial position make P-wave axis changes visible here

### 2.4 Beat-by-Beat Considerations
- Electrical alternans is BY DEFINITION a beat-to-beat phenomenon — the entire diagnosis depends on accurate beat-to-beat measurement of QRS amplitude
- The alternating pattern is PERFECTLY PERIODIC — every odd beat is taller, every even beat is shorter (or vice versa). If the pattern is irregular or not perfectly alternating, consider other causes of QRS amplitude variation (respiratory variation, arrhythmia-induced aberrancy)
- Respiratory variation of QRS amplitude (normal) must be distinguished from true electrical alternans — respiratory variation follows breathing cycle (2-4 seconds), while tamponade alternans follows every heartbeat (half-second)
- In atrial fibrillation, the irregular RR intervals disrupt the regular oscillation of the heart in fluid — electrical alternans may be less apparent or absent despite tamponade, because the swinging motion is coupled to the mechanical rhythm
- T-wave alternans should also be checked separately from QRS alternans — T-wave alternans in the context of normal QRS represents arrhythmia risk from different mechanisms (see Node 2.7.87)

## 3. Morphology Details

### 3.1 P-wave Changes
- **Amplitude**: Reduced if effusion is large (fluid attenuates atrial signal as well as ventricular). Small P-waves may be difficult to identify on a low-voltage ECG
- **P-wave alternans**: When the heart swings sufficiently to change the atrial position relative to leads, P-wave axis/amplitude alternates between beats. Best seen in V1. P-wave alternans + QRS alternans = TOTAL electrical alternans (strongest ECG evidence for tamponade)
- **Rate**: P-waves occur at the sinus tachycardia rate — short PP intervals. Importantly, every P-wave conducts (1:1 AV conduction maintained), distinguishing tamponade-related tachycardia from AV block
- **Morphology in concurrent pericarditis**: Normal P-wave shape; the PR SEGMENT after the P-wave is depressed

### 3.2 PR Interval Changes
- **Duration**: Normal in isolated effusion/tamponade — the PR interval is not intrinsically affected by pericardial fluid
- **PR depression**: ONLY if concurrent pericarditis is present (inflammatory effusion). Many malignant effusions have no pericarditis and thus no PR depression
- **Diagnostic implication**: PR depression in effusion → underlying inflammatory process (pericarditis causing the effusion) → treat the underlying etiology (NSAIDs, colchicine) in addition to addressing the effusion

### 3.3 QRS Complex Changes
- **LOW VOLTAGE**: The cardinal QRS finding. All QRS amplitudes are reduced proportionally across all leads. The reduction is uniform (not lead-specific) because the fluid is a uniform resistive shell around the entire heart
- **Electrical alternans — QRS**: The key diagnostic finding. Measure R-wave amplitude (or the total QRS amplitude including any S-wave) in a consistent lead across multiple beats. If amplitude alternates by ≥1 mm between consecutive beats in a regular (every-other-beat) pattern → QRS electrical alternans
- **QRS duration**: Normal unless concurrent bundle branch block or prior conduction disease
- **Axis**: The mean QRS axis alternates beat-to-beat by definition in electrical alternans (typically ±20° or more). The axis swing causes the amplitude alternation as the projection of the QRS vector onto each lead changes
- **No Q-waves**: Pericardial effusion does not cause myocardial necrosis → no pathological Q-waves (unless underlying MI caused the effusion)

### 3.4 ST Segment Changes
- **Isolated effusion without pericarditis**: No ST changes — fluid compresses from outside without injuring the epicardium
- **With concurrent pericarditis**: Diffuse ST elevation (concave morphology) and PR depression — the pericarditis ECG pattern (see Node 2.7.100 pericarditis) PLUS low voltage and alternans from the effusion
- **Malignant effusion**: No ST changes unless tumor directly invades the pericardium (pericardial metastasis) causing local inflammation
- **Traumatic effusion (hemopericardium)**: Typically no ST changes unless the underlying injury involves the myocardium

### 3.5 T-wave Changes
- **Amplitude**: Reduced by the same fluid attenuation mechanism as QRS — flat T-waves are common in large effusions
- **T-wave alternans**: When present as part of total electrical alternans, T-waves alternate in amplitude between beats. This is different from T-wave alternans as a marker of arrhythmia risk (which is microvolt-level, not visible on standard ECG without signal averaging)
- **In tamponade context**: T-wave alternans visible on standard ECG (macroscopic T-wave alternans) in the setting of large effusion is strong evidence for tamponade

### 3.6 QT/QTc Changes
- **QTc**: May appear normal or mildly prolonged when corrected for the sinus tachycardia rate. The QT measurement may be technically challenging in low-voltage ECG (difficult to identify T-wave end)
- **TdP risk**: Not primarily increased by pericardial effusion alone; if concurrent myocarditis is present (myopericarditis with effusion), QTc may be prolonged from the myocardial injury

### 3.7 Other Features
- **ECG TRIAD — tamponade**: Low voltage (QRS <5 mm limb leads) + Electrical alternans (beat-to-beat QRS amplitude variation) + Sinus tachycardia (HR >100). This combination is highly specific for cardiac tamponade
- **Sensitivity of individual findings**: Sinus tachycardia alone has low specificity; low voltage alone is moderately sensitive but not specific (also seen in obesity, emphysema, large pericardial effusion without tamponade); electrical alternans is rare but highly specific for tamponade when present
- **The hierarchy**: Total electrical alternans (P + QRS + T) > QRS alternans alone > low voltage + tachycardia alone in terms of specificity for tamponade

## 4. Differential Diagnosis

### 4.1 Mimics

| Mimic Condition | Shared Features | Distinguishing Features |
|----------------|-----------------|------------------------|
| **Obesity / large body habitus** | Low voltage (signal attenuated by chest wall fat) | No electrical alternans; no tachycardia from cardiac cause; echocardiogram shows no effusion; low voltage is chronic and stable |
| **Emphysema / COPD** | Low voltage (hyperinflated lungs, increased air between heart and leads) | No electrical alternans; right heart strain pattern (RVH, S1Q3T3, RBBB); vertical heart position; clinical history |
| **Dilated cardiomyopathy** | Low voltage, possible tachycardia | No electrical alternans from swinging (heart is fixed in a dilated non-compliant pericardium); LBBB common; echocardiogram shows dilated non-compressing ventricle; no effusion |
| **Pleural effusion** | Can reduce voltage if very large bilateral effusions | Typically does not cause electrical alternans; echocardiogram normal pericardial space; chest X-ray shows pleural (not pericardial) fluid |
| **T-wave alternans (arrhythmia risk)** | Alternating T-waves | Arrhythmia-related TWA: only T-waves alternate, QRS does NOT alternate; seen with long QT, ischemia; no low voltage; mechanism is repolarization instability not cardiac swinging |
| **SVT** | Tachycardia | SVT: narrow complex with specific P-wave patterns (retrograde, absent, or in ST segment); no low voltage; no QRS alternans (unless with electrical alternans from aberrancy — different mechanism); responds to vagal maneuvers or adenosine |
| **Hypothyroidism** | Low voltage, sinus bradycardia | Hypothyroidism: bradycardia (not tachycardia); flat or inverted T-waves; may actually cause pericardial effusion (overlap possible); TSH/T4 testing diagnostic |

### 4.2 Coexisting Conditions
- **Pericarditis with effusion**: The most common inflammatory scenario — pericarditis ECG features (diffuse ST elevation, PR depression, Spodick's sign) PLUS effusion features (low voltage, alternans). The system must recognize BOTH sets of findings simultaneously and flag: "Pericarditis pattern with signs of effusion — assess for tamponade urgently"
- **Myocarditis with effusion**: Myocarditis can produce pericardial effusion (myopericarditis) — same dual-finding scenario as pericarditis but with troponin elevation and potentially arrhythmias
- **Malignancy**: Large chronic effusions from malignancy may be present for months without tamponade (slow accumulation allows pericardium to stretch). ECG may show only low voltage without alternans. When tamponade eventually develops, alternans appears
- **Aortic dissection with hemopericardium**: Rapid hemopericardium from Type A dissection → very rapid tamponade. ECG may show only sinus tachycardia initially, then rapid development of low voltage + alternans. This is a surgical emergency — pericardiocentesis without surgery may provoke re-bleeding
- **Post-cardiac surgery effusion**: Common 1-6 weeks post-cardiac surgery (post-pericardiotomy syndrome). ECG shows effusion findings; importantly, coagulation status affects management (drainage vs medical)

## 5. STAT Classification

| Priority | Criteria |
|----------|----------|
| **STAT** | YES — cardiac tamponade is a STAT emergency. ECG showing low voltage + electrical alternans + sinus tachycardia in a hypotensive patient = tamponade physiology = emergent bedside echocardiogram + preparation for pericardiocentesis. Time from recognition to drainage must be minimized |
| **Urgent** | YES — electrical alternans without hemodynamic compromise still requires emergent echocardiogram to characterize effusion size and assess for tamponade physiology (RV diastolic collapse, respiratory variation of mitral inflow) |
| **Time-sensitive** | YES — the window between ECG detection and hemodynamic decompensation may be minutes to hours depending on the rate of fluid accumulation |
| **Clinical action** | Immediate echocardiogram (bedside if hemodynamically unstable); continuous cardiac monitoring and telemetry; IV access (at least 2 large-bore IVs); fluid resuscitation as temporizing measure (caution — may worsen if RV is already compromised); urgent pericardiocentesis (ultrasound-guided preferred; surgical pericardial window if loculated or traumatic hemopericardium); AVOID positive pressure ventilation if possible (worsens venous return and tamponade physiology); emergent cardiac surgery consultation for traumatic or aortic dissection etiology |

### 6.1 Reasoning Domains Required
- **QRS amplitude measurement**: Precise beat-by-beat measurement of QRS amplitude in multiple leads — this is the core technical requirement. The system must measure R-wave height (or total QRS amplitude) for every beat across a sufficient number of beats to detect alternation
- **Low voltage detection**: Requires measurement of QRS amplitude across ALL limb leads simultaneously — criterion fails if any single lead exceeds the threshold
- **Electrical alternans detection**: Must identify the alternating (every-other-beat) pattern — this is a pattern recognition task requiring beat labeling (even vs odd) and amplitude comparison
- **Rate assessment**: Sinus tachycardia detection; P-wave identification at high rates
- **PR segment analysis**: Detection of PR depression if concurrent pericarditis
- **Rhythm analysis**: Must confirm 1:1 AV conduction and regular sinus mechanism — electrical alternans in an irregular rhythm needs different interpretation
- **Contextual escalation**: Recognizing that these findings together represent a potential emergency — this is the most critical reasoning step

### 6.2 Feature Dependencies
- **ESSENTIAL features**: QRS amplitude in all limb leads (low voltage criterion); beat-by-beat QRS amplitude pattern (alternans criterion); heart rate (tachycardia)
- **SUPPORTING features**: P-wave alternans (if detectable); T-wave alternans (if detectable); PR depression (concurrent pericarditis); ST elevation (concurrent pericarditis)
- **EXCLUSION features**: QRS variation synchronized with breathing cycle → respiratory variation, not alternans; irregular RR intervals with variable QRS → consider atrial fibrillation with rate-dependent aberrancy; QRS alternans in rapid SVT → SVT-related, not tamponade
- **Per-beat vs aggregate**: Primarily per-beat — alternans is a beat-level phenomenon. Low voltage is aggregate (consistent across all beats). The system needs both per-beat and aggregate analysis simultaneously

### 6.3 Cross-Condition Interactions
- **Pericardial effusion + pericarditis**: The most common pairing. The system must detect BOTH pericarditis features AND effusion features and synthesize: "Pericarditis with effusion — assess for tamponade"
- **Electrical alternans (Node 2.7.87) overlap**: Electrical alternans in tamponade is the primary clinical scenario for Node 2.7.87. These nodes are tightly coupled and should share detection logic. The distinction: tamponade alternans affects QRS (and possibly P and T); arrhythmia-risk alternans primarily affects T-wave
- **Low voltage + MI**: If patient had prior MI with Q-waves, and now has low voltage from effusion — the Q-waves may be attenuated by the fluid, reducing their amplitude below the threshold for detection. Missed Q-waves are a real risk in the low-voltage ECG
- **Low voltage mimics**: Obesity and emphysema produce low voltage without clinical urgency. The system must consider the combination of findings (alternans + tachycardia with low voltage) rather than low voltage alone

### 6.4 Reasoning Chain Sketch
**Minimum reasoning chain:**
1. Measure QRS amplitude in all limb leads → all <5 mm → low voltage
2. Check for beat-to-beat QRS amplitude alternation → present → electrical alternans
3. Confirm sinus tachycardia → HR >100
4. ESCALATE: "ECG triad of tamponade — emergent echocardiogram required"

**Full reasoning chain:**
1. Measure QRS amplitude (R-wave or total) in all 12 leads, beat-by-beat
2. Calculate mean QRS amplitude per lead → all limb leads <5 mm? → low voltage criterion met
3. Label even and odd beats; compare QRS amplitudes between groups → ≥1 mm difference in any lead, perfectly alternating? → electrical alternans
4. Measure heart rate → >100? → sinus tachycardia
5. Check P-wave morphology → each QRS preceded by normal P? → confirms sinus mechanism
6. Check P-wave amplitude per beat → P-wave alternans present? → total electrical alternans
7. Check T-wave amplitude per beat → T-wave alternans present? → part of total alternans
8. Check PR segment in lead II → depressed? → concurrent pericarditis (effusion is inflammatory)
9. Check ST segments → elevated (pericarditis pattern)? → concurrent pericarditis
10. Synthesize: (a) Low voltage alone → flag for effusion evaluation; (b) low voltage + alternans + tachycardia → tamponade ECG triad, STAT escalation; (c) concurrent pericarditis features → specify likely inflammatory etiology

### 6.5 Confidence Anchors
- **HIGH confidence for tamponade**: Low voltage (all limb leads <5 mm) + QRS electrical alternans (≥1 mm beat-to-beat variation) + sinus tachycardia = near-pathognomonic ECG triad. Specificity for tamponade very high
- **MODERATE confidence**: Low voltage alone + tachycardia without alternans — possible effusion without tamponade; possible obesity/emphysema; echocardiogram required
- **LOW confidence**: Tachycardia alone without low voltage or alternans — non-specific
- **Pathognomonic combination**: Total electrical alternans (P + QRS + T alternans) = virtually diagnostic of cardiac tamponade
- **Absent features**: Absence of electrical alternans does NOT exclude tamponade — many cases of tamponade never develop visible alternans (particularly with slow-accumulating effusions or atrial fibrillation)

| Dimension | Score (1-5) | Notes |

**MR** — Low voltage and electrical alternans are morphological amplitude measurements; both are detected by per-beat QRS amplitude analysis, which is the core MR domain function.

- MR → RRC: `cross_domain_hint: "beat_to_beat_QRS_amplitude_variation — confirm regular alternating pattern as electrical alternans vs arrhythmia"`

### CDS Specific Role
CDS assembles the three-component tamponade ECG triad: low voltage (MR), electrical alternans (MR confirmed by RRC), and sinus tachycardia (RRC). When all three are present, CDS issues a STAT tamponade alert with a recommendation for emergent echocardiography. When only low voltage is present without alternans, CDS flags as possible effusion (lower specificity). CDS also checks for concurrent pericarditis features (PR depression, saddle ST) to characterize the likely inflammatory etiology of the effusion.

## 9. Edge Cases and Pitfalls

- **Electrical alternans without tamponade**: QRS alternans can occur in SVT (especially with rate-dependent aberrancy) and in severe heart failure. In SVT, the alternation is related to alternating conduction patterns, not cardiac swinging. Key distinguishing features: SVT alternans is rate-dependent (resolves when SVT terminates); tamponade alternans is rate-independent and typically associated with low voltage. Echocardiogram resolves the distinction.
- **Tamponade without electrical alternans**: Only about 20% of cardiac tamponade cases demonstrate electrical alternans on standard ECG. The absence of alternans does NOT exclude tamponade. Low voltage + tachycardia alone should prompt echocardiogram.
- **Atrial fibrillation masking alternans**: The irregular RR intervals in AFib disrupt the periodic cardiac swinging — cardiac oscillation is coupled to mechanical rhythm, so irregular rhythm disrupts the regular alternation. Alternans may be absent or irregular in tamponade with AFib. Low voltage + irregular tachycardia without clear alternans → still requires echocardiogram.
- **Low voltage from non-cardiac causes**: Obesity (increased subcutaneous fat), emphysema (hyperinflated lungs as insulator), large pleural effusions, hypothyroidism, and anasarca all produce low voltage by the same physical principle (increased resistance between myocardium and skin leads). None of these produce true electrical alternans (no cardiac swinging in non-effusion causes). The combination of low voltage + alternans is specific for pericardial effusion with swinging.
- **Aortic dissection hemopericardium — critical pitfall**: Acute Type A aortic dissection can rupture into the pericardial space, causing rapid hemopericardium and tamponade. In this context, pericardiocentesis alone without surgery is DANGEROUS — removing the tamponade may relieve the tamponade effect that was tamponading the dissection site, leading to catastrophic re-bleeding. ECG in aortic dissection may show signs of tamponade (from hemopericardium) PLUS possible signs of acute MI if the dissection involves the coronary ostia. Always consider aortic dissection in a patient with tamponade ECG findings and tearing chest pain or pulse differential.
- **Post-cardiac surgery effusion**: Very common (15-30% of cardiac surgery patients develop pericardial effusion). Loculated effusions (after surgery, adhesions partition the pericardial space) may cause regional tamponade with atypical ECG findings. Echocardiography is essential; transesophageal echocardiography (TEE) may be needed if loculated.
- **Distinguishing respiratory QRS variation from electrical alternans**: Normal sinus arrhythmia causes QRS amplitude variation synchronized with breathing (QRS taller during expiration, shorter during inspiration, cycling every 2-5 seconds). True electrical alternans cycles every OTHER beat (0.5-1 second at normal heart rates). If the amplitude variation tracks with breathing on a long rhythm strip, it is respiratory variation, not tamponade alternans.
- **Very low voltage masking QRS detail**: In severe low voltage (QRS <3 mm), other ECG findings (ST changes, Q-waves, T-wave morphology) may be technically unmeasurable. The system should flag: "Extreme low voltage — additional ECG findings may not be measurable; clinical and echocardiographic evaluation essential."
