## 1. Pathophysiology Root Cause

### 1.1 What Goes Wrong (Anatomy/Physiology)
- The pericardium (visceral and parietal layers) becomes inflamed due to viral infection (most common: Coxsackie B, echovirus), bacterial infection, autoimmune disease (lupus, rheumatoid arthritis), post-MI (Dressler syndrome), uremia, malignancy, or idiopathic causes
- Inflammation involves the epicardial surface of the myocardium (the superficial layer directly beneath the visceral pericardium), producing a diffuse, superficial myocardial injury pattern
- Unlike MI, which affects a specific coronary territory, pericarditis affects the epicardium DIFFUSELY — the entire epicardial surface is inflamed, not just a region supplied by one artery
- Pericardial fluid may accumulate (effusion), and if rapid or large, can cause cardiac tamponade

### 1.2 Electrical Consequence
- Diffuse epicardial inflammation creates an injury current across the entire ventricular epicardium — the superficial (epicardial) layer is injured while the deeper (endocardial) layer is normal
- During ventricular repolarization (ST segment), the injured epicardium is relatively more positive than normal, creating a diffuse injury vector directed outward from the heart toward all epicardial-facing leads → widespread ST elevation
- The atrial epicardium is also inflamed, creating an atrial injury current that causes PR segment deviation: PR depression in most leads (atrial injury vector directed away from leads facing the atria) and PR elevation in aVR (atrial injury vector toward aVR)
- Because the inflammation is diffuse and superficial (NOT transmural or territorial), the ST elevation is widespread, NOT confined to a single coronary territory, and there are NO true reciprocal ST depressions (except in aVR and sometimes V1, which face away from the bulk of the epicardium)

### 1.3 Why It Appears on ECG
- Almost all limb and precordial leads face some portion of the epicardial surface → widespread ST elevation
- aVR faces the right atrial cavity (endocardial surface) — opposite to the epicardial injury vector → ST depression in aVR (reciprocal to the diffuse epicardial injury) and PR elevation (reciprocal to the diffuse atrial epicardial injury)
- V1 has variable findings because it faces the right ventricle and interventricular septum, which have less epicardial surface area oriented toward V1
- The four-stage evolution reflects the natural history of inflammation: acute injury (Stage 1) → resolution of injury current but not yet repolarization changes (Stage 2) → repolarization abnormalities from healing/fibrosis (Stage 3) → full recovery (Stage 4)

## 2. ECG Presentation — Lead by Lead

### 2.1 Diagnostic Criteria (2025 AHA/ESC Guidelines)

| Criterion | Threshold | Source |
|-----------|-----------|--------|
| **Diffuse ST elevation** | ST elevation in ≥6 leads (I, II, III, aVF, V2-V6), concave upward morphology | 2015 ESC Guidelines on Pericardial Diseases; 2024 ESC Update |
| **PR depression** | PR depression in lead II (most sensitive and specific ECG finding for pericarditis) | 2015 ESC Guidelines |
| **aVR reciprocal changes** | ST depression + PR elevation in aVR | 2015 ESC Guidelines |
| **Absence of reciprocal ST depression** | No ST depression in leads other than aVR and V1 (if present → consider STEMI) | Differential criterion |
| **ST/T ratio in V6** | ST elevation / T-wave amplitude > 0.25 in lead V6 → favors pericarditis over benign early repolarization | Ginzton & Laks 1982 |
| **Spodick's sign** | Downsloping TP segment (best seen in lead II or lateral leads) | Spodick 1974 |
| **No Q-wave development** | Absence of pathological Q-waves distinguishes from MI | Differential criterion |

**Four ECG Stages of Acute Pericarditis:**

| Stage | Timing | ECG Findings |
|-------|--------|-------------|
| Stage 1 (acute) | Hours to days | Diffuse concave-up ST elevation + PR depression; Spodick's sign |
| Stage 2 (evolution) | Days to ~1 week | ST segments normalize; T-waves flatten; PR normalizes |
| Stage 3 (T-wave inversion) | 1-3 weeks | Diffuse T-wave inversions (all leads that had ST elevation) |
| Stage 4 (resolution) | Weeks to months | ECG normalizes completely (or may have persistent T-wave changes) |

### 2.2 Lead-by-Lead Manifestation

| Lead | Expected Finding | Why (Vector Explanation) | Sensitivity |
|------|-----------------|------------------------|-------------|
| I | ST elevation (concave up, 0.5-2 mm); PR depression; T-wave upright in Stage 1, inverted in Stage 3 | Lead I faces the lateral epicardium — diffuse epicardial injury vector has a leftward component | Moderate |
| II | ST elevation (concave up, 1-3 mm); PR depression (most prominent and specific here); Spodick's sign (downsloping TP) | Lead II faces inferolateral epicardium — large epicardial surface projects toward II; atrial injury most visible here | High — PR depression in II is the most specific finding |
| III | ST elevation (concave up, 0.5-2 mm); PR depression (mild); may be less prominent than II | Lead III faces inferior epicardium — smaller projection of the diffuse vector | Moderate |
| aVR | ST depression (1-2 mm); PR elevation (specific finding) | aVR faces the endocardial/cavity side — RECIPROCAL to the diffuse epicardial injury and diffuse atrial injury; this is the "anchor lead" | High — aVR ST depression + PR elevation is highly specific for pericarditis |
| aVL | ST elevation (concave up, 0.5-1.5 mm); PR depression (mild); may be less prominent | aVL faces high lateral wall — smaller epicardial surface area projects toward aVL | Low-Moderate |
| aVF | ST elevation (concave up, 1-2 mm); PR depression | aVF faces the inferior epicardium — good projection of diffuse vector | Moderate |
| V1 | Variable: ST may be isoelectric, mildly elevated, or mildly depressed; PR elevation possible | V1 faces the right ventricular septum — the diffuse vector has minimal net projection toward V1; sometimes behaves like aVR (reciprocal) | Low — V1 is often the exception in pericarditis |
| V2 | ST elevation (concave up, 1-2 mm); PR depression | V2 faces the anterior RV/septal region — begins to pick up the diffuse epicardial injury vector | Moderate |
| V3 | ST elevation (concave up, 1-3 mm); PR depression | V3 faces the anterior septum/LV — increasing projection of the leftward-dominant injury vector | Moderate-High |
| V4 | ST elevation (concave up, 1-3 mm); PR depression | V4 faces the anterior LV — large epicardial surface projects toward V4 | High |
| V5 | ST elevation (concave up, 1-3 mm); PR depression | V5 faces the anterolateral LV — large epicardial surface; good for ST/T ratio measurement | High |
| V6 | ST elevation (concave up, 0.5-2 mm); PR depression; use for ST/T ratio calculation (>0.25 favors pericarditis) | V6 faces the lateral LV; ST/T ratio here helps differentiate from benign early repolarization | High — V6 ST/T ratio is a key discriminator |

### 2.3 Key Leads (Most Diagnostic)
- **Lead II**: PR depression is most prominent here — the single most specific ECG sign of pericarditis; also best lead for Spodick's sign
- **aVR**: ST depression + PR elevation — the reciprocal "anchor" that confirms diffuse epicardial process (not territorial)
- **V5-V6**: ST elevation typically most prominent; V6 used for ST/T ratio calculation (>0.25)
- **V1**: Important because it is often the EXCEPTION — absence of ST elevation in V1 with ST elevation in nearly all other leads strongly suggests pericarditis
- The PATTERN of involvement (widespread, non-territorial) is more important than any single lead

### 2.4 Beat-by-Beat Considerations
- Pericarditis ECG findings are CONSTANT across all beats in a given stage — no beat-to-beat variability expected
- The key temporal variability is across STAGES (days to weeks), not across beats
- If electrical alternans is seen (beat-to-beat QRS voltage alternation), suspect pericardial EFFUSION with possible tamponade — this is a complication, not typical uncomplicated pericarditis
- Sinus tachycardia is common (pain, fever, sympathetic activation) but the morphological findings are consistent beat-to-beat

## 3. Morphology Details (What the Agent Must See)

### 3.1 P-wave Changes
- **Morphology**: Usually normal sinus P-waves; may develop atrial fibrillation or atrial flutter if pericardial inflammation involves the atrial myocardium significantly
- **Duration change**: Normal unless LAE coexists from prior condition
- **Axis change**: Normal P-wave axis
- **Key finding**: The PR segment (not the P-wave itself) is the critical finding — PR depression reflects atrial epicardial injury current, not P-wave morphology change

### 3.2 PR Interval Changes
- **Duration**: Normal PR interval duration; it is the PR SEGMENT (between end of P-wave and onset of QRS) that is depressed, not the PR interval that is shortened
- **Pattern**: Constant PR depression across all beats; present in nearly all leads except aVR (where PR is elevated) and V1 (variable)
- **PR depression measurement**: Measure from the TP baseline to the PR segment; typically 0.5-1.5 mm depression in lead II
- **Clinical significance**: PR depression is present in ~80% of acute pericarditis cases and is the MOST SPECIFIC ECG finding (more specific than ST elevation, which can occur in many conditions)

### 3.3 QRS Complex Changes
- **Duration**: Normal (<120 ms) — pericarditis does NOT affect the His-Purkinje system or ventricular conduction
- **Morphology**: Normal QRS morphology; no Q-waves develop (unlike MI); no bundle branch block pattern
- **Amplitude**: Normal; if LOW voltage (<5 mm total in all limb leads or <10 mm in all precordial leads), suspect pericardial EFFUSION — fluid around the heart attenuates the electrical signal
- **Axis**: Normal; no axis deviation from pericarditis alone
- **Electrical alternans**: Beat-to-beat alternation of QRS amplitude — NOT a finding of pericarditis itself but indicates large pericardial effusion with the heart swinging in the fluid. This is an ominous sign suggesting possible tamponade.

### 3.4 ST Segment Changes
- **Direction**: ELEVATION in nearly all leads (I, II, III, aVF, V2-V6); DEPRESSION in aVR (and sometimes V1)
- **Morphology**: Concave upward ("saddle-shaped" or "smiley face") — this is a key differentiator from STEMI, where ST elevation is often convex upward or straight
- **Measurement point**: J-point for ST elevation measurement
- **Gender/lead-specific thresholds**: No specific gender thresholds (unlike STEMI criteria); the key is the PATTERN (diffuse, non-territorial) rather than absolute millimeters
- **Critical differentiator from STEMI**: (1) Pericarditis = diffuse (>6 leads), STEMI = territorial (specific vascular territory). (2) Pericarditis = concave up, STEMI = convex up or straight. (3) Pericarditis = no reciprocal depression (except aVR/V1), STEMI = reciprocal depression in opposing leads. (4) Pericarditis = PR depression present, STEMI = PR depression absent.

### 3.5 T-wave Changes
- **Stage 1**: T-waves upright and normal in all leads with ST elevation
- **Stage 2**: T-waves flatten (amplitude decreases)
- **Stage 3**: T-waves INVERT diffusely — inverted in the same leads that previously showed ST elevation. Importantly, T-wave inversions appear AFTER ST segments have normalized (unlike STEMI where T-wave inversion can occur while ST is still elevated)
- **Amplitude**: Normal in Stage 1; flattened in Stage 2; inverted (variable depth, typically 1-5 mm) in Stage 3
- **Symmetry**: T-wave inversions in Stage 3 are relatively symmetric (unlike LVH strain which is asymmetric)
- **Specific patterns**: The temporal sequence of ST elevation → normalization → T inversion → normalization is pathognomonic for pericarditis when diffuse

### 3.6 QT/QTc Changes
- **QTc**: Usually normal; may be mildly prolonged in Stage 3 (with diffuse T-wave inversions)
- **Clinical significance**: QT prolongation is not a primary feature of pericarditis; if prominent, consider other causes (myocarditis, electrolyte imbalance)
- **Torsades de Pointes risk**: Not increased by pericarditis alone

### 3.7 Other Features
- **Spodick's sign**: Downsloping TP segment — the baseline between the T-wave and the next P-wave slopes downward. Best seen in lead II and lateral leads. Reported in up to 80% of acute pericarditis but often subtle. Relatively specific for pericarditis.
- **Low voltage**: Suggests pericardial effusion (fluid attenuates signal). If low voltage + sinus tachycardia + electrical alternans → suspect tamponade (Beck's triad on ECG).
- **Electrical alternans**: Beat-to-beat alternation of QRS (and sometimes P and T) amplitude — indicates large effusion with cardiac swinging. Medical emergency if tamponade.
- **No pathological Q-waves**: Unlike MI, pericarditis does NOT cause myocardial necrosis (only superficial inflammation), so no Q-waves develop. Appearance of Q-waves should redirect toward MI or myocarditis with necrosis.

## 4. Differential Diagnosis

### 4.1 Mimics (What Looks Like This But Isn't)

| Mimic Condition | Shared Features | Distinguishing Features |
|----------------|-----------------|----------------------|
| **STEMI** | ST elevation | STEMI: territorial (specific leads for specific artery); convex or straight ST morphology; reciprocal ST depression in opposite leads; NO PR depression; Q-waves develop; evolves rapidly (minutes-hours). Pericarditis: diffuse (nearly all leads); concave ST; no reciprocal depression (except aVR); PR depression present; no Q-waves; evolves slowly (days-weeks) |
| **Benign early repolarization (BER)** | Diffuse ST elevation, concave morphology, young patient | BER: typically V3-V5 predominant; "fish-hook" or notched J-point; ST/T ratio <0.25 in V6; NO PR depression; no evolution through stages; stable over time. Pericarditis: more widespread; PR depression present; ST/T ratio >0.25 in V6; evolves through 4 stages |
| **Takotsubo (stress cardiomyopathy)** | Diffuse ST elevation, T-wave inversions | Takotsubo: ST elevation often in anterior leads (V1-V6); deep symmetric T-wave inversions with markedly prolonged QTc; wall motion abnormality on echo; troponin mildly elevated; triggered by emotional/physical stress |
| **Myocarditis** | Diffuse ST-T changes, chest pain | Myocarditis: may have identical ECG to pericarditis (myopericarditis); troponin elevation helps distinguish; more likely to have arrhythmias (VT, heart block); echocardiographic wall motion abnormalities; MRI shows myocardial edema/enhancement |
| **Normal variant/high vagal tone** | ST elevation in precordial leads, young patient | No PR depression; no Spodick's sign; stable over time; typically limited to V2-V4; tall upright T-waves |
| **LV aneurysm** | Persistent ST elevation | Fixed, non-evolving; present in leads with Q-waves (old infarct territory); no PR depression; history of prior MI |

### 4.2 Coexisting Conditions
- **Myopericarditis**: Pericarditis with myocardial involvement — ECG shows pericarditis pattern PLUS troponin elevation. ECG alone cannot reliably distinguish pericarditis from myopericarditis; troponin and cardiac MRI are needed.
- **Pericardial effusion**: May coexist at any stage. ECG shows pericarditis pattern PLUS low voltage PLUS possible electrical alternans. The system should flag: "Low voltage with diffuse ST elevation — consider pericardial effusion; assess for tamponade."
- **Uremic pericarditis**: Often lacks typical Stage 1 ECG findings — may present with diffuse low voltage and T-wave inversions without preceding ST elevation. BUN/creatinine context needed.
- **Post-MI pericarditis (Dressler syndrome)**: Occurs days to weeks after MI. May be difficult to distinguish new pericarditis ST elevation from evolving MI changes. Key: pericarditis changes are DIFFUSE (beyond the MI territory), whereas MI changes are territorial.

## 5. STAT Classification

| Priority | Criteria |
|----------|----------|
| **STAT** | No — uncomplicated pericarditis is NOT STAT. However, the PRIMARY role of this diagnosis in the ER is to DIFFERENTIATE FROM STEMI, which IS STAT. The system must confidently distinguish the two. Additionally, pericarditis with tamponade physiology (low voltage + electrical alternans + tachycardia) IS STAT. |
| **Time-sensitive** | Moderately — correct diagnosis prevents unnecessary emergent cardiac catheterization (if mistaken for STEMI). Treatment with NSAIDs + colchicine should begin promptly but is not minutes-critical. |
| **Clinical action** | NSAIDs (ibuprofen 600mg TID) + colchicine (0.5mg BID) for first episode; echocardiogram to assess for effusion; serial ECGs to confirm evolution through stages; avoid anticoagulation (risk of hemorrhagic pericardial effusion); restrict exercise until CRP normalizes and symptoms resolve. If tamponade suspected: emergent pericardiocentesis. |

### 6.1 Reasoning Domains Required to Detect This Condition
- **ST segment analysis**: Must assess ST elevation across all 12 leads for morphology (concave vs convex), distribution (diffuse vs territorial), and magnitude
- **PR segment analysis**: Must measure PR deviation — a specialized measurement not typically part of routine ECG analysis. Requires identifying the TP baseline and comparing to PR segment level
- **Lead-group correlation**: Must determine that ST elevation is DIFFUSE (non-territorial) — requires simultaneous assessment of multiple lead groups (anterior, inferior, lateral) and confirming that territorial reciprocal changes are ABSENT
- **Morphology comparison**: Must distinguish concave-up from convex-up ST elevation — a subtle shape distinction
- **Temporal pattern recognition**: If serial ECGs available, must recognize the 4-stage evolution
- **Ratio computation**: ST/T ratio in V6 requires measurement of both ST elevation and T-wave amplitude
- **Cross-domain reasoning**: Required — must integrate ST morphology + PR deviation + lead-group distribution + absence of reciprocal changes + absence of Q-waves to differentiate from STEMI

### 6.2 Feature Dependencies
- **ESSENTIAL features**: ST segment direction and morphology in all 12 leads; PR segment deviation in lead II and aVR; distribution of ST elevation (number and pattern of leads); absence of reciprocal ST depression (excluding aVR and V1)
- **SUPPORTING features**: ST/T ratio in V6; Spodick's sign (TP segment slope); QRS voltage (low voltage suggests effusion); electrical alternans (suggests effusion/tamponade); heart rate (sinus tachycardia common)
- **EXCLUSION features**: Territorial ST elevation with reciprocal depression → STEMI, not pericarditis. Pathological Q-waves → MI, not pericarditis. New LBBB → consider STEMI with Sgarbossa criteria.
- **Per-beat vs aggregate**: Aggregate (consistent across all beats); per-beat only for electrical alternans detection (complication)

### 6.3 Cross-Condition Interactions
- **Pericarditis vs STEMI**: The single most important differential in emergency cardiology. The system MUST correctly distinguish these because the clinical pathways are completely different (catheterization vs NSAIDs). Misclassifying pericarditis as STEMI leads to unnecessary invasive procedures; misclassifying STEMI as pericarditis is life-threatening.
- **Pericarditis vs BER**: Common clinical dilemma, especially in young men. PR depression and ST/T ratio >0.25 in V6 favor pericarditis. BER is stable on serial ECGs.
- **Pericarditis masking concurrent MI**: Rare but possible — if pericarditis and MI coexist (e.g., post-MI pericarditis), diffuse changes may obscure territorial STEMI. Serial ECGs and troponin are essential.
- **Pericarditis + atrial fibrillation**: Pericardial inflammation can trigger AFib; loss of P-waves eliminates PR depression as a diagnostic criterion, reducing confidence in pericarditis diagnosis.

### 6.4 Reasoning Chain Sketch
**Minimum reasoning chain:**
1. Detect ST elevation in multiple leads
2. Confirm ST elevation is DIFFUSE (non-territorial — present in ≥6 leads spanning multiple vascular territories)
3. Confirm NO reciprocal ST depression (except aVR and V1)
4. Detect PR depression in lead II → pericarditis

**Full reasoning chain:**
1. Measure ST segment in all 12 leads → identify leads with ST elevation
2. Classify ST morphology → concave upward (favors pericarditis) vs convex/straight (favors STEMI)
3. Map leads with ST elevation to vascular territories → if ≥2 territories AND no single territorial pattern predominates → diffuse process
4. Check for reciprocal ST depression → present in leads OTHER than aVR/V1 → favors STEMI; absent → favors pericarditis
5. Measure PR segment in lead II → depressed? → strong evidence for pericarditis
6. Check aVR → ST depression + PR elevation? → confirms reciprocal pattern of diffuse epicardial injury
7. Compute ST/T ratio in V6 → >0.25 favors pericarditis over BER
8. Look for Spodick's sign (downsloping TP) in lead II → supporting evidence
9. Check for Q-waves → present → MI, not pericarditis
10. Check QRS voltage → low → suggest effusion; check for electrical alternans → present → suggest tamponade
11. Synthesize → classify as pericarditis (Stage 1/2/3/4) or redirect to STEMI/BER/other

### 6.5 Confidence Anchors
- **HIGH confidence**: Diffuse concave-up ST elevation (≥6 leads, non-territorial) + PR depression in lead II + aVR ST depression/PR elevation + no reciprocal depression + no Q-waves = near-pathognomonic for acute pericarditis Stage 1
- **MODERATE confidence**: Diffuse ST elevation without clear PR depression (PR changes may be subtle or absent in ~20%)
- **LOW confidence**: ST elevation in 4-5 leads without PR depression — could be BER, early STEMI, or pericarditis
- **Pathognomonic combination**: Diffuse concave ST elevation + PR depression in II + PR elevation in aVR + Spodick's sign + ST/T ratio >0.25 in V6 = virtually diagnostic
- **Absent features that LOWER confidence**: Territorial ST elevation pattern → redirects to STEMI; reciprocal ST depression present (beyond aVR/V1) → redirects to STEMI; Q-waves → redirects to MI; no PR depression → reduces specificity (but does not exclude)

### 6.6 Difficulty Score
| Dimension | Score (1-5) | Notes |
|-----------|-------------|-------|
| Signal clarity needed | 3 | PR depression is subtle (0.5-1 mm) and requires clean baseline; Spodick's sign requires high-quality TP segment |
| Number of leads required | 5 | Must assess ALL 12 leads for distribution pattern; diagnosis depends on the PATTERN across leads, not individual lead findings |
| Cross-domain reasoning | 4 | ST morphology + PR analysis + lead-group distribution + territorial mapping + ratio computation |
| Temporal pattern complexity | 3 | Four-stage evolution is diagnostically useful if serial ECGs available; single-ECG diagnosis relies on Stage 1 features |
| Differential complexity | 5 | Must differentiate from STEMI (life-threatening if missed), BER (common mimic), Takotsubo, LV aneurysm — highest-stakes differential in ECG |
| Rarity in PTB-XL | 4 | Uncommon in PTB-XL database; limited examples for validation compared to LVH or STEMI |
| **Overall difficulty** | **4.0** | **High — the diagnosis requires multi-lead pattern recognition, subtle PR assessment, and the highest-stakes differential in emergency cardiology (STEMI vs pericarditis)** |

## 6A. Agent Assignment

**Architecture:** Option E — 3 Parallel Phase 1 Specialists + 1 Sequential CDS (Node 2.1 Gate Passed 2026-03-27)

| Agent | Role for Pericarditis | Key Features Used |
|-------|--------------------------|-------------------|
| **RRC** (Rhythm/Rate/Conduction) | Supporting | Sinus tachycardia rate (common in acute pericarditis due to pain/fever), rhythm regularity, PR interval for PR depression detection (subtle but specific finding) |
| **IT** (Ischemia/Territory) | Primary | Diffuse ST elevation across multiple non-contiguous lead groups (I/II/aVF + V2–V6), PR depression in multiple leads (especially II), PR elevation in aVR (reciprocal PR change), ST elevation distribution assessment (diffuse vs territorial), absence of true reciprocal ST depression in non-aVR leads |
| **MR** (Morphology/Repolarization) | Supporting | ST morphology — concave-up (saddle-shaped) vs convex (STEMI pattern); Stage 1 ST characteristics; sparing of aVR and V1 from ST elevation; T-wave morphology changes across pericarditis stages |
| **CDS** (Cross-Domain Synthesis) | Required — CRITICAL: pericarditis vs STEMI differentiation | Integrates IT's diffuse ST distribution with MR's concave ST morphology and RRC's PR depression finding; applies Spodick's ratio (ST/T amplitude in V6 ≥0.25 favors pericarditis); generates pericarditis vs STEMI differential with confidence scores |

### Primary Agent
**IT** — pericarditis is detected primarily through diffuse ST elevation with PR depression across multiple lead groups, which is in the IT agent's ST analysis domain; however, morphology differentiation from STEMI requires MR and CDS integration.

### Cross-Domain Hints
- IT emits `cross_domain_hint: "Diffuse ST elevation across non-contiguous lead groups — pericarditis pattern possible; MR must assess ST morphology (concave vs convex) before STEMI territory assignment"` when ST elevation is found in >2 non-contiguous lead groups.
- MR emits `cross_domain_hint: "Concave-up (saddle-shaped) ST morphology detected with diffuse distribution — strongly supports pericarditis over STEMI; forward morphology findings to CDS for differential resolution"` when saddle-shape morphology is identified.

### CDS Specific Role
CDS performs the highest-stakes differential in emergency ECG: pericarditis vs STEMI. CDS integrates: (1) IT's ST distribution (diffuse non-territorial = pericarditis; single territory = STEMI), (2) MR's ST morphology (concave-up = pericarditis; convex/tombstone = STEMI), (3) RRC's PR depression (present = pericarditis; absent = STEMI), (4) Spodick's ratio calculation (ST amplitude / T-wave amplitude in V6: ≥0.25 favors pericarditis). CDS generates a confidence-scored differential — if any territorial ST elevation exceeds the concave pattern, CDS does not suppress the STEMI call and requires clinician adjudication.

## 9. Edge Cases and Pitfalls

- **Subtle PR depression**: PR depression may be only 0.5 mm — easily missed with noisy baseline or low gain. The system must use high-precision PR segment measurement and flag even subtle depression when ST elevation is diffuse.
- **Stage 2-3 presentation**: If the patient presents after Stage 1 has passed, the classic diffuse ST elevation is gone. Stage 3 shows diffuse T-wave inversions, which can mimic diffuse ischemia, Wellens, or CNS T-wave changes. Clinical history (recent viral illness, pleuritic chest pain) becomes essential.
- **Pericarditis in the setting of prior MI**: Old Q-waves from prior MI coexist with diffuse ST elevation from pericarditis. The Q-waves are in a specific territory while the ST elevation is diffuse — this mismatch is the clue. Post-MI pericarditis (Dressler) typically occurs 2-10 weeks post-MI.
- **BER vs pericarditis in young men**: Both show diffuse concave ST elevation. BER is extremely common in young men (especially Black men). Key differentiators: (1) PR depression present → pericarditis, (2) ST/T ratio >0.25 in V6 → pericarditis, (3) Spodick's sign → pericarditis, (4) BER is stable on serial ECGs; pericarditis evolves through stages, (5) BER is typically limited to V3-V5; pericarditis involves limb leads too.
- **Pericarditis with concurrent atrial fibrillation**: Loss of organized atrial activity eliminates P-waves and thus eliminates PR depression — the most specific finding is lost. Diagnosis then relies entirely on diffuse ST elevation pattern without PR assessment, reducing confidence.
- **Localized pericarditis**: Rare — inflammation limited to one area (e.g., post-surgical) can produce regional ST elevation mimicking STEMI. This is a diagnostic trap; clinical context (recent cardiac surgery, localized pericardial inflammation on imaging) is essential.
- **Uremic pericarditis**: ECG findings may be atypical — often presents with diffuse low voltage and T-wave changes without classic Stage 1 ST elevation. The system should flag: "In the setting of renal failure, atypical pericarditis patterns are possible."
- **Tamponade**: Not a primary ECG diagnosis of pericarditis but a COMPLICATION. Low voltage + electrical alternans + sinus tachycardia = ECG triad of tamponade. The system should escalate urgency if these findings are present alongside pericarditis features.
- **Concomitant STEMI and pericarditis**: Extremely rare but possible (post-MI pericarditis developing while ongoing ischemia). If territorial ST changes are superimposed on diffuse changes, the system should flag BOTH possibilities and recommend urgent evaluation.
