## 1. Pathophysiology Root Cause

### 1.1 The Three-Fascicle Conduction System
The His-Purkinje conduction system beyond the AV node divides into three functional pathways:
1. **Right bundle branch (RBB)**: Single compact fascicle conducting to the right ventricle
2. **Left anterior fascicle (LAF)**: Conducts to the anterior and lateral left ventricular wall (LAD blood supply)
3. **Left posterior fascicle (LPF)**: Conducts to the inferior and posterior left ventricular wall (dual LAD/RCA blood supply)

Normally all three fascicles conduct simultaneously from the His bundle, producing a narrow, synchronous QRS complex. **Bifascicular block** occurs when two of these three fascicles fail to conduct, leaving only one fascicle to maintain ventricular activation.

### 1.2 The Two Combinations
**RBBB + LAFB** (most common, ~80% of bifascicular blocks):
- The right bundle and left anterior fascicle are both blocked
- Only the left posterior fascicle maintains His-Purkinje conduction
- The LV inferior-posterior territory activates rapidly via the intact LPF; the RV and anterior-lateral LV territory activate slowly via cell-to-cell spread
- ECG result: RBBB morphology + left axis deviation (the LPF activates first, producing inferior-rightward initial forces → subsequent spread to RV and anterior LV creates the RBBB terminal pattern while the initial axis reflects sole LPF activation)

**RBBB + LPFB** (less common, ~20% of bifascicular blocks):
- The right bundle and left posterior fascicle are both blocked
- Only the left anterior fascicle maintains His-Purkinje conduction
- The LV anterior-lateral territory activates rapidly via the intact LAF; the RV and inferior-posterior LV activate slowly
- ECG result: RBBB morphology + right axis deviation

### 1.3 Why Bifascicular Block Is Clinically Important
With two of three fascicles blocked, ventricular conduction depends entirely on one remaining fascicle. If that fascicle fails — transiently (from ischemia, rate-related failure, or metabolic stress) or permanently — the result is **complete heart block (third-degree AV block)**: the His bundle fires but no fascicle conducts, and the ventricles must rely on a slow escape rhythm (20–40 bpm ventricular escape or 40–60 bpm junctional escape). This can present as:
- Syncope (Stokes-Adams attack): abrupt complete block → ventricular standstill → syncope before escape rhythm establishes
- Sudden cardiac death: if no escape rhythm establishes quickly enough
- Progressive symptomatic bradycardia

In the setting of **acute anterior MI (LAD territory)**, bifascicular block acquires particularly high urgency. The LAD supplies the RBB, LAF, and often the proximal LPF. Bifascicular block in acute anterior MI indicates ischemia of the His-Purkinje system itself — a marker of massive ischemic territory and high risk for sudden progression to complete heart block within hours.

### 1.4 Causes
- **Coronary artery disease**: Most common. LAD ischemia damages RBB and LAF simultaneously. Requires involvement of both structures.
- **Lenegre-Lev disease**: Progressive idiopathic fibrosis of the conduction system. The most common cause in elderly patients without overt CAD.
- **Hypertensive heart disease and aortic stenosis**: Fibrocalcific involvement of the conduction system.
- **Dilated cardiomyopathy**: Diffuse involvement of the conduction system.
- **Infiltrative diseases**: Sarcoidosis, amyloidosis, Chagas disease.
- **Post-surgical**: After TAVR, septal myectomy, VSD repair — mechanical trauma to conduction tissue.

## 2. ECG Presentation — Lead by Lead

### 2.1 Diagnostic Criteria (2025 AHA/ESC Guidelines)

**RBBB + LAFB:**
| Criterion | Requirement |
|---|---|
| QRS Duration | ≥120ms (RBBB criterion) |
| rSR' in V1/V2 | RBBB morphology: bifid R with terminal R' in right precordial leads |
| Wide slurred S in I, V6 | Terminal broad S (rightward terminal vector from RBBB) |
| Frontal axis | −45° to −90° (left axis deviation from LAFB component) |
| qR in I, aVL | LAFB initial forces: small q, dominant R |
| rS in II, III, aVF | LAFB terminal forces: small r, deep S |

**RBBB + LPFB:**
| Criterion | Requirement |
|---|---|
| QRS Duration | ≥120ms (RBBB criterion) |
| rSR' in V1/V2 | RBBB morphology |
| Wide slurred S in I, V6 | Terminal broad S (RBBB) — BUT IMPORTANT: wide S in I from RBBB must be distinguished from deep S from LPFB |
| Frontal axis | +90° to +180° (right axis deviation from LPFB component) |
| rS in I, aVL | LPFB pattern; the terminal S in I is from both LPFB inferior forces AND RBBB rightward forces |
| qR in II, III, aVF | LPFB pattern |
| Exclusion | RVH, PE, lateral MI excluded before diagnosing RBBB + LPFB |

### 2.2 Lead-by-Lead Manifestation — RBBB + LAFB (most common)
| Lead | Expected Morphology | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| I | qR with broad terminal S | q from LAFB initial inferior vector; R from LAFB terminal superior vector; terminal S from RBBB rightward vector |
| II | rS with broad terminal S | rS from LAFB component; terminal S from RBBB |
| III | rS broad; deep S | LAFB: deep S in III; RBBB adds terminal slurring |
| aVR | QS or rS | Mirror of lateral leads |
| aVL | qR with terminal S | LAFB hallmark lead; RBBB adds terminal S |
| aVF | rS with terminal slurring | LAFB inferior leads |
| V1 | rSR': bifid R' | Classic RBBB morphology |
| V2 | rSR' or rsR' | Similar to V1 |
| V3 | Transitional | May show some terminal S |
| V4 | Transitional | |
| V5 | Broad terminal S | RBBB contribution in lateral precordial |
| V6 | Broad terminal S | Classic RBBB finding; LAFB does not affect V6 significantly |

### 2.3 Key Leads
- **V1**: rSR' confirms RBBB component — the fundamental structural requirement of bifascicular block diagnosis. The QRS must be ≥120ms.
- **Lead I**: Must show BOTH the qR morphology (LAFB) AND the terminal broad S (RBBB). The terminal S is what distinguishes this from isolated LAFB.
- **aVL**: qR morphology with tall R and terminal S — the LAFB-dominant lead.
- **Lead II (for axis)**: Net negative deflection (S > r) confirms left axis deviation ≥−45° for the LAFB component.
- **V6**: Terminal broad S confirms the RBBB component in the left lateral precordial leads.

### 2.4 Beat-by-Beat Considerations
- Morphology is fixed in sinus rhythm — not rate dependent.
- In acute anterior MI: watch for progressive PR prolongation (evolving first-degree AV block = third fascicle involvement). A new first-degree AV block appearing alongside bifascicular block in acute STEMI is the most urgent rhythm progression sign before complete block.
- If complete block occurs: QRS morphology becomes very wide ventricular escape (>140ms), completely different from bifascicular block morphology. The transition from bifascicular block to complete block may be abrupt.
- In atrial fibrillation: bifascicular block morphology may be misidentified as WCT. Irregular rhythm confirms AF with aberrancy; regular WCT with RBBB morphology + axis deviation raises VT concern.

## 3. Morphology Details

### 3.1 How Axis and RBBB Morphology Coexist
This is the conceptually challenging aspect. Complete RBBB produces:
- Terminal broad S in I (rightward terminal QRS vector from RV activation)
- rSR' in V1 (terminal R' from RV activation)

LAFB produces:
- Left axis deviation (qR in I with left-directed terminal R, rS in inferior leads with inferior-directed initial r)
- qR in I — but RBBB simultaneously adds a terminal S to the end of that R wave

The result in lead I for RBBB + LAFB is: q (LAFB initial inferior force) → R (LAFB terminal superior force — the dominant R wave) → terminal S (RBBB rightward terminal force from RV activation). This produces a qRS pattern in lead I: small q, dominant R, terminal broad S.

### 3.2 Axis Calculation in Bifascicular Block
The RBBB component does not affect the axis calculation because the RBBB terminal forces (rightward, late QRS) are excluded from the axis calculation. QRS axis is calculated from the net QRS deflection in the initial and middle portions of the QRS complex, before the terminal RBBB slurring. This is why bifascicular block preserves the axis contributions of the fascicular block component.

In practice: measure the frontal plane axis from the INITIAL QRS direction (before terminal RBBB slurring). If the net initial-to-mid QRS is directed at −45° to −90° → LAFB component confirmed alongside RBBB → bifascicular block RBBB + LAFB.

### 3.3 QRS Duration in Bifascicular Block
QRS duration is ≥120ms driven entirely by the RBBB component. The fascicular block component (LAFB or LPFB) contributes negligibly to QRS widening. If QRS is <120ms in a pattern that otherwise looks like RBBB + LAFB, this is likely incomplete RBBB + LAFB — still clinically significant but distinct from true bifascicular block.

### 3.4 PR Interval — The Trifascicular Alarm
The PR interval in bifascicular block should always be measured explicitly. A normal PR interval implies intact AV node + His bundle conduction. A prolonged PR interval (>200ms) in the context of bifascicular block indicates disease in the remaining conducting pathway — technically called **trifascicular block** (though this term is debated, it conveys high clinical risk). The AV delay may be in the AV node or in the diseased remaining fascicle itself.

Prolonged PR + RBBB + LAFB = all three fascicles functionally diseased = high risk for complete heart block = strong pacemaker indication.

### 3.5 The "New" Bifascicular Block in Acute Settings
Chronic bifascicular block carries a relatively low annual rate of progression to complete block (~1% per year in ambulatory patients). But **new bifascicular block** in the context of acute anterior MI is a different entity entirely:
- Risk of complete block within 24–48 hours is 20–30%
- Prophylactic temporary pacing should be considered
- This is a critical clinical-algorithmic decision point

### 3.6 Alternating Bundle Branch Block
If a patient's ECG shows RBBB on one recording and LBBB on another — "alternating bundle branch block" — this indicates disease in all three fascicles (at different times different combinations are failing). This is an urgent pacemaker indication.

### 3.7 RBBB + LPFB Morphological Complexity
In RBBB + LPFB, the terminal S wave in lead I has contributions from TWO sources: (a) the RBBB rightward terminal vector and (b) the LPFB inferior-rightward terminal vector. Both move away from lead I. The deep S in lead I and the qR in II/III/aVF confirm LPFB; the rSR' in V1 confirms RBBB. Right axis confirmation is essential. This combination requires excluding RVH and PE before diagnosis, as both also produce RBBB + right axis deviation from structural RV disease.

## 4. Differential Diagnosis

### 4.1 Mimics Table
| Condition | Differentiating Feature |
|---|---|
| Isolated RBBB | Wide QRS with RBBB morphology but NORMAL axis (no fascicular block component) |
| RBBB + RVH | Right axis but with dominant R in V1, right atrial enlargement, clinical RVH context; no qR in I/aVL |
| RBBB + lateral MI | Right axis from loss of lateral forces; Q waves in I/aVL distinguish from LAFB rS initial pattern |
| Complete LBBB | Wide QRS, broad monophasic R in I/V5/V6, no rSR' in V1; axis usually normal or slightly left |
| VT with RBBB morphology | Wide QRS (often >140ms), AV dissociation, fusion beats, concordance in precordial leads |
| WPW with RBBB-like pattern | Short PR, delta wave, different QRS onset morphology |
| Hyperkalemia | Broad bizarre QRS, no typical rSR', peaked T waves, clinical context |
| Paced rhythm (RBBB pacing mode) | Pacemaker spike before QRS |

### 4.2 Coexisting Conditions
- **First-degree AV block**: Adds trifascicular block risk. Always measure PR in bifascicular block.
- **Acute anterior STEMI**: Critical association. ST elevation V1-V4 + new bifascicular block = LAD territory ischemia involving His-Purkinje system → emergent reperfusion + cardiac monitoring.
- **Cardiomyopathy**: Bifascicular block may be the first sign. Echo is warranted.
- **Sarcoidosis/amyloidosis**: Infiltrative cardiomyopathy classically presents with progressive conduction disease. Bifascicular block in a young patient without obvious CAD should raise suspicion.
- **Chagas disease**: Important in endemic regions (Latin America). Progressive conduction disease + RBBB + LAFB is a classic Chagas presentation.

## 5. STAT Classification

| Category | Classification | Rationale |
|---|---|---|
| Chronic stable bifascicular block, asymptomatic | NOT STAT | ~1%/year progression; document, outpatient follow-up |
| New bifascicular block in acute anterior MI | STAT — CRITICAL | 20–30% risk of complete block in 24–48h; urgent |
| Bifascicular block + new first-degree AV block | HIGH ALERT | Trifascicular block; urgent pacemaker evaluation |
| Bifascicular block + syncope | HIGH ALERT | Likely Stokes-Adams attack; Holter/electrophysiology study |
| Bifascicular block + new in ER with no clear cause | MODERATE-HIGH ALERT | Rule out acute MI, myocarditis, toxicology |
| Alternating RBBB/LBBB on different ECGs | STAT — CRITICAL | Implies all fascicles diseased; urgent pacemaker |

**Overall STAT status: YES in acute MI context or new presentation. NOT STAT for chronic stable finding.**

## 6. Reasoning Complexity Analysis

### 6.1 Pattern Recognition Complexity
Bifascicular block diagnosis requires simultaneous recognition of TWO pattern components: the RBBB morphology (rSR' in V1, broad S in I/V6, QRS ≥120ms) AND the axis deviation (left for RBBB+LAFB, right for RBBB+LPFB). Neither alone is sufficient. The algorithm must perform parallel pattern-and-axis analysis and then combine them.

### 6.2 Axis Calculation in the Context of RBBB
Axis calculation in bifascicular block requires focusing on the initial and mid-QRS direction rather than the terminal QRS (which is dominated by the RBBB component). Automated axis algorithms that weight the full QRS duration may misclassify axis in bifascicular block. The system must calculate axis from initial QRS forces specifically.

### 6.3 RBBB + LPFB Exclusion Requirement
RBBB + LPFB requires the same exclusion cascade as isolated LPFB before the right axis can be attributed to LPFB: RVH must be excluded (no dominant R in V1 beyond RBBB morphology), PE must be excluded, lateral MI must be excluded. This adds multi-step reasoning.

### 6.4 PR Interval Analysis
Every bifascicular block detection must be followed by PR interval measurement. The PR measurement then triggers a conditional: normal PR = bifascicular block without trifascicular concern; prolonged PR = trifascicular block alert. This is a two-step reasoning chain.

### 6.5 Acute vs. Chronic Context
The clinical urgency of bifascicular block differs dramatically based on whether it is new or chronic, and whether acute MI is co-present. The algorithm must integrate STAT elevation logic based on these contextual triggers, not just the ECG morphology alone.

### 6.6 Difficulty Score

| Dimension | Score (1–5) | Rationale |
|---|---|---|
| Pattern recognition | 3 | Two simultaneous patterns (RBBB + axis); requires parallel analysis |
| Axis calculation in RBBB context | 3 | Must isolate initial QRS axis excluding terminal RBBB forces |
| RBBB + LPFB exclusion cascade | 3 | RVH/PE/MI exclusion needed for right axis component |
| PR interval trifascicular check | 2 | Straightforward PR measurement but critical clinical step |
| Acute MI context integration | 3 | Dramatically changes urgency; requires clinical context input |
| Combined classification logic | 3 | Two patterns AND clinical context AND PR → single STAT decision |
| Rarity/recognition challenge | 2 | RBBB + LAFB is common; RBBB + LPFB is rarer |
| **Overall Difficulty** | **2.8/5** | Most complex of the conduction disorders due to multi-component recognition and context-driven urgency |

## 6A. Agent Assignment

**Architecture:** Option E — 3 Parallel Phase 1 Specialists + 1 Sequential CDS (Node 2.1 Gate Passed 2026-03-27)

| Agent | Role for Bifascicular Block | Key Features Used |
|-------|--------------------------|-------------------|
| **RRC** (Rhythm/Rate/Conduction) | Primary | QRS duration ≥120ms (RBBB component); axis deviation for fascicular block type: left axis deviation −45° to −90° (RBBB + LAFB) or right axis deviation >+90° (RBBB + LPFB); PR interval measurement for trifascicular disease risk assessment |
| **IT** (Ischemia/Territory) | Not involved | No ischemia-specific features in isolated bifascicular block |
| **MR** (Morphology/Repolarization) | Supporting | rSR' or rsR' in V1-V2 confirming RBBB morphology component; wide slurred S in I/V6; secondary ST-T changes in V1-V3 from RBBB |
| **CDS** (Cross-Domain Synthesis) | Required — integrates axis + BBB + PR for complete heart block risk stratification | Synthesizes RRC (QRS duration + axis deviation + PR interval) with MR (RBBB morphology in V1-V2) to confirm bifascicular block type (RBBB + LAFB vs RBBB + LPFB); integrates PR interval from RRC: RBBB + fascicular block + PR >200ms = trifascicular disease — high-risk for progression to complete heart block |

### Primary Agent
**RRC** — bifascicular block is a conduction system diagnosis requiring two concurrent conduction findings: QRS duration ≥120ms for RBBB and axis deviation for the fascicular block component; both are RRC domain measurements.

### Cross-Domain Hints
- RRC emits: `cross_domain_hint: "QRS ≥120ms with axis deviation detected — MR must confirm RBBB morphology (rSR' in V1-V2, wide S in I/V6) to confirm bifascicular block; if PR prolonged, CDS must flag trifascicular disease risk"`
- MR emits: `cross_domain_hint: "RBBB morphology confirmed (rSR' in V1-V2) — RRC axis determines fascicular block type: LAD = RBBB + LAFB; RAD = RBBB + LPFB"`

### CDS Specific Role
CDS synthesizes the bifascicular block diagnosis by combining RRC QRS duration (≥120ms), RRC axis deviation (LAD for LAFB component, RAD for LPFB component), and MR RBBB morphology (rSR' in V1-V2). Its critical role is PR interval integration: if RRC reports PR >200ms alongside the bifascicular findings, CDS upgrades the classification to trifascicular disease and generates a high-risk flag for imminent complete heart block progression. For RBBB + LPFB specifically, CDS cross-references MR output to exclude RVH as a cause of the right axis deviation before confirming the fascicular block component.

## 9. Edge Cases and Pitfalls

**Pitfall 1 — Missing the axis deviation in RBBB**: When RBBB is identified, the axis must always be measured explicitly. A common error is to identify RBBB and stop without checking axis. Every RBBB should trigger an automated axis check to identify or exclude bifascicular block.

**Pitfall 2 — Terminal RBBB forces distorting axis calculation**: Automated axis algorithms using full QRS area may incorrectly calculate axis in RBBB because the terminal rightward forces (RV activation) pull the axis rightward artifactually. For axis calculation in RBBB, focus on the initial and mid-QRS direction, not the terminal portion.

**Pitfall 3 — RBBB + LPFB misdiagnosed as RBBB + RVH**: Both produce RBBB + right axis. The distinguishing features: RBBB + LPFB should have qR in II/III/aVF (small initial q from LAF activation) and no RVH-criteria R wave in V1 (the V1 R' in RBBB is the terminal R' of the bifid RBBB complex, not a dominant R from RVH). RBBB + RVH typically shows larger R' in V1, right atrial enlargement, and a clinical history of pulmonary disease.

**Pitfall 4 — New bifascicular block in acute MI undertriaged**: Bifascicular block in acute anterior MI is a sign of massive LAD ischemia involving the His-Purkinje system. It is a known precursor to complete heart block. In some ER workflows, the rhythm strip looks "stable" (sinus rhythm with wide QRS) and is not escalated. The algorithm must flag this combination explicitly as STAT.

**Pitfall 5 — Chronic bifascicular block not compared to prior ECGs**: The most important single clinical action when bifascicular block is identified is comparison with prior ECGs. Chronic stable bifascicular block is low risk. New bifascicular block is high risk. Without comparison, risk stratification is impossible.

**Pitfall 6 — PR interval missed in bifascicular block**: Given the wide QRS, clinicians sometimes focus on morphology and miss measuring the PR interval. Trifascicular block (bifascicular + prolonged PR) may carry an indication for prophylactic pacing. PR must be measured in every bifascicular block ECG.

**Pitfall 7 — VT with RBBB morphology and axis deviation called bifascicular block**: Monomorphic VT can produce RBBB morphology + axis deviation. Key distinguishers: QRS in VT is often >140–160ms (wider than typical RBBB); AV dissociation (P waves march through at a different rate); fusion beats; concordance in precordial leads (all positive or all negative). If in doubt and patient is hemodynamically unstable → treat as VT.

**Pitfall 8 — RBBB + LAFB in a young athlete mismanaged**: Bifascicular block in an athlete with an otherwise normal ECG and no symptoms may represent early conduction system disease but requires evaluation. It is NOT a normal athletic variant (unlike sinus bradycardia or LVH voltage criteria). Echo and cardiology referral are appropriate.
