## 1. Pathophysiology Root Cause

### 1.1 Mechanism of Scar Formation
A completed transmural anterior myocardial infarction results from prolonged, complete occlusion of the left anterior descending (LAD) artery or one of its major branches — typically the diagonal or septal perforators — without timely reperfusion. When cardiomyocytes in the anterior wall undergo irreversible necrosis (typically >40 minutes of total occlusion), they are replaced over days to weeks by fibrous scar tissue.

Scar is electrically inert. It neither depolarizes nor repolarizes. From an ECG standpoint, the scarred myocardium is a zone of electrical silence. The recording electrode overlying this territory detects only the spread of depolarization AWAY from it (unopposed vectors of opposite walls), producing a negative initial deflection — the pathological Q wave.

### 1.2 LAD Territory and Anterior Wall Anatomy
The LAD supplies the anterior interventricular septum, anterior wall of the left ventricle, and the apex. Depending on which segment of the LAD is occluded:

- **Proximal LAD** (before D1 and S1): Extensive anterior MI — septum, anterior wall, apex, lateral wall → Q waves V1-V6, often I, aVL
- **Mid LAD** (after D1): Anterior wall + apex → Q waves V2-V5
- **Distal LAD**: Apical → Q waves V4-V5 (smaller territory)
- **LAD-dominant wrapping around apex**: Inferior Q waves may accompany anterior Q waves

### 1.3 Chronic Phase ECG Changes
In the chronic (healed) phase of anterior MI, the acute injury and inflammation have resolved. The ST segment and T-waves normalize over weeks to months in most patients. What persists permanently as the fingerprint of prior infarction:

1. **Pathological Q waves** — permanent markers of scar; may diminish slightly over years but rarely disappear completely
2. **Poor or absent R-wave progression** in V1-V4 — loss of the normal anterior forces generated by healthy myocardium
3. **T-wave abnormalities** may persist as chronic repolarization abnormalities in the infarct zone

## 2. ECG Presentation — Lead by Lead

### 2.1 Diagnostic Criteria (2025 AHA/ESC Guidelines)

Per 2018 Fourth Universal Definition of Myocardial Infarction and 2022 ESC Chronic Coronary Syndrome guidelines:

**Pathological Q wave criteria** (any of the following, in ≥2 contiguous leads):
- **Duration ≥40ms (≥1 small square)** in any lead except aVR, III, and V1
- **Depth ≥25% of the amplitude of the following R wave** in the same lead
- **QS complex** (entirely negative deflection) in V2-V3
- In V1-V2: Any Q wave is abnormal
- **R-wave ≥40ms in V1-V2 combined with positive T-wave** without RBBB — possible posterior MI (not anterior)

For anterior MI specifically:
- Pathological Q waves in ≥2 of V1, V2, V3, V4
- QS pattern (no R wave at all) in V1-V3 is common in extensive anterior MI
- R-wave amplitude in V3 < R-wave amplitude in V4 (normal progression lost)

Note: An isolated small q in aVL or III is a normal variant. The 2-contiguous-lead requirement is critical.

### 2.2 Lead-by-Lead Manifestation

| Lead | Finding in Chronic Anterior MI | Significance |
|------|--------------------------------|--------------|
| I | Possible small q wave | Lateral extension of anterior MI |
| II | Usually normal | Not primary territory |
| III | Usually normal | Not primary territory |
| aVR | Normal | Not primary territory |
| aVL | Small q wave possible | High lateral involvement |
| aVF | Usually normal | Not primary territory |
| V1 | QS complex or pathological Q | Septal MI — common finding |
| V2 | QS or deep Q wave | Anterior wall — core territory |
| V3 | Pathological Q or poor R-wave | Anterior wall — core territory |
| V4 | Pathological Q or small R | Anterior/apical territory |
| V5 | Small Q or normal | Lateral extension |
| V6 | Usually normal or small Q | Lateral wall |

**QS complex**: When the entire QRS is negative (no R wave at all), termed QS complex. A QS in V1 alone is often a normal variant. QS in V1 AND V2 is abnormal. QS in V1-V4 indicates extensive anterior scar.

### 2.3 Key Leads
- **V1-V4** are the primary diagnostic leads for anterior MI
- **Minimum criteria**: Pathological Q in ≥2 contiguous leads from V1-V4
- **Extensive MI**: Q waves extending into V5-V6, I, aVL suggests proximal LAD occlusion
- **Isolated V1-V2**: Septal MI (septal perforators)
- **V3-V4 dominant**: Mid-LAD territory

### 2.4 Beat-by-Beat Considerations
- Q waves are measured in the first portion of the QRS complex (the initial vector)
- Width is measured from onset of Q to the Q-R transition (or Q-S nadir if QS)
- In sinus rhythm, measure Q width in the lead where it appears largest
- Rate does not affect Q-wave interpretation in chronic MI; however, in very high heart rates, narrow Q waves can be obscured
- Bundle branch blocks alter Q-wave interpretation: LBBB eliminates septal q waves normally and CAUSES absence of expected Q waves; RBBB in leads V1-V2 has its own initial R (do not confuse with loss of Q)

## 3. Morphology Details

### 3.1 Q Wave Morphology
- **Width**: The most important criterion. ≥40ms (one small square at 25mm/s paper speed) is pathological in most leads.
- **Depth**: ≥25% of subsequent R-wave amplitude. A 5mm R-wave with a 1.25mm Q = pathological Q. Note: if the R-wave is tiny, even a small absolute Q can be ≥25%.
- **Notching**: A notched or slurred initial QRS descent in a Q-wave lead can represent incomplete scar or peri-infarct slow conduction.
- **QS morphology**: Complete absence of R-wave. Most commonly V1-V2 in anterior MI. A QS in a single lead (especially V1) may be normal; QS across ≥2 contiguous leads is abnormal.

### 3.2 R-Wave Amplitude Pattern
- **Poor R-wave progression (PRWP)**: In healthy hearts, R-wave amplitude increases progressively from V1 to V4 (transition zone). After anterior MI, this growth fails because the anterior myocardium contributing to the R-wave forces is replaced by scar.
- **Reverse R-wave progression**: R-wave actually decreases in amplitude from V1 to V4 (R V4 < R V3 < R V2). Highly suggestive of anterior MI.
- **Absent R in V1-V3**: A QS pattern across V1-V3 with sudden appearance of R in V4 indicates that anterior scar territory extends to V3.

### 3.3 ST Segment in Chronic Phase
- ST segment has typically normalized by 6–12 weeks post-MI
- **Persistent ST elevation** (>3 months post-MI) in V1-V4 suggests left ventricular aneurysm formation at the infarct site — this is a distinct finding requiring echo correlation
- New ST elevation in leads with old Q waves = acute re-occlusion or aneurysm; never dismiss as "old changes"

### 3.4 T-Wave Changes in Chronic Phase
- T-waves may remain inverted (chronic repolarization abnormality of scar margin) or normalize
- Deep persistent T-wave inversions in V1-V4 months after MI may represent peri-infarct ischemia
- New T-wave changes in a stable prior-MI pattern deserve attention

### 3.5 QRS Duration
- Usually normal unless prior MI caused intraventricular conduction delay
- LBBB can develop after large anterior MI due to septal infarction affecting the left bundle branch
- Fascicular block: Left anterior fascicular block (LAFB) — left axis deviation — is common after anterior MI due to LAD involvement of anterior fascicle

### 3.6 Transition Zone
- Normal transition zone (where R-wave equals S-wave) is V3-V4
- Leftward transition (R>S already at V1-V2) = posterior MI or RBBB
- Rightward transition (R<S still at V5-V6) = anterior MI or LBBB

### 3.7 Axis
- Left axis deviation is common in prior anterior MI (LAFB from LAD territory involvement)
- Extreme axis deviation can occur with large anterior MI
- Right axis deviation is not expected unless concurrent right ventricular involvement or LPFB

## 4. Differential Diagnosis

### 4.1 Mimics
| Mimic | Distinguishing Feature |
|-------|------------------------|
| LBBB | QS in V1-V3 is EXPECTED in LBBB — cannot diagnose old anterior MI in LBBB |
| Normal variant QS in V1 | QS in V1 alone, normal R progression V2-V6; isolated and well-documented |
| WPW (pre-excitation) | Initial negative deflection (pseudo-Q) from delta wave; delta wave positive in other leads; short PR |
| COPD/emphysema | Poor R-wave progression from hyperinflation; vertical heart, low voltage; no pathological Q duration |
| Lead misplacement | QS in V1-V3 from electrodes placed too high; normalizes with correct placement; no clinical history |
| Dilated cardiomyopathy | Diffuse poor R-wave progression; may have pseudo-Q waves without coronary history |
| Hypertrophic CM | Deep septal Q waves in I, aVL, V5-V6 (not V1-V4); HCM pattern |
| Myocarditis | Diffuse ST-T changes; Q waves possible but less territorial; biomarker rise |
| Cardiac amyloid | Pseudo-Q waves (septal infiltration reduces septal forces) + low voltage |

### 4.2 Coexisting Conditions
- **LBBB + prior anterior MI**: LBBB completely masks Q-wave diagnosis. The QS pattern in V1-V3 is expected in LBBB and does NOT confirm prior MI. Imaging (echo, cardiac MRI) is required for diagnosis.
- **LVH + prior anterior MI**: LVH increases R-wave voltage, which can normalize the R:Q ratio and mask pathological Q waves.
- **Active anterior STEMI on top of old anterior MI**: Critically important — new ST elevation in leads with pre-existing Q waves = acute re-occlusion (re-MI) until proven otherwise. Compare carefully with oldest available ECG.
- **LAFB + prior anterior MI**: Both produce left axis deviation; LAFB alone does not cause Q waves in V1-V4 but does produce small Q in aVL and rS in inferior leads.

## 6. Reasoning Complexity Analysis

### 6.1 Pattern Recognition Difficulty
Pathological Q-wave recognition is one of the more straightforward tasks in ECG interpretation for a well-trained system. The challenge lies in: (1) distinguishing pathological from normal-variant Q waves, (2) interpreting Q waves in the presence of confounders (LBBB, WPW, lead misplacement), and (3) identifying when a prior MI pattern coexists with new acute changes.

### 6.2 LBBB Masking
LBBB is the most important confounding factor. In LBBB, septal activation is reversed — the septum activates right to left, eliminating the normal initial leftward septal forces and producing QS complexes in V1-V3 as part of the normal LBBB pattern. Therefore, Q waves in V1-V3 in the context of LBBB are expected and cannot be used to diagnose prior anterior MI without imaging correlation.

### 6.3 New Versus Old Assessment
A system reading a single ECG without prior context cannot determine if Q waves are new (acute) or old (chronic). New Q waves forming during an acute STEMI are a different finding from chronic scar Q waves. This is a fundamental limitation that mandates prior ECG comparison.

### 6.4 Concurrent Acute MI Recognition
The most dangerous scenario: a patient with known prior anterior MI presents with new chest pain. The baseline Q waves are already present. New ST elevation may be subtle if partially offset by the existing QS morphology. The system must flag any new ST changes above the established chronic baseline as potentially acute.

### 6.5 LV Aneurysm Differentiation
Chronic anterior MI with LV aneurysm produces persistent ST elevation in V1-V4 that looks like an "unresolved" STEMI. The ECG alone cannot reliably distinguish acute MI (needing immediate reperfusion) from LV aneurysm (no immediate intervention needed). Clinical history, troponin trend, and echo are required. The system should flag persistent ST elevation and recommend correlation.

### 6.6 Difficulty Score

| Dimension | Score (1–5) | Rationale |
|-----------|-------------|-----------|
| Q-Wave Identification | 2 | Clear morphological criteria; straightforward in clean ECG |
| LBBB Masking Handling | 4 | Requires LBBB detection first; Q-wave interpretation suspended |
| New vs. Old Determination | 3 | Requires prior ECG; single ECG cannot definitively determine chronicity |
| Concurrent Acute MI Detection | 4 | ST changes on top of old Q waves may be subtle |
| LV Aneurysm vs. Acute | 3 | Requires clinical correlation; ECG pattern similar |
| Confounders (WPW, COPD) | 2 | Detectable with careful analysis |
| **Overall Difficulty** | **2.0** | Well-defined criteria; main challenge is contextual determination |

## 6A. Agent Assignment

**Architecture:** Option E — 3 Parallel Phase 1 Specialists + 1 Sequential CDS (Node 2.1 Gate Passed 2026-03-27)

| Agent | Role for Old Anterior MI | Key Features Used |
|-------|--------------------------|-------------------|
| **RRC** (Rhythm/Rate/Conduction) | Supporting | Arrhythmia detection (PVCs, VT, ventricular arrhythmias arising from scar substrate); heart rate and rhythm as baseline |
| **IT** (Ischemia/Territory) | Primary | Pathological Q-waves in V1–V4 (≥40 ms duration, ≥25% of QRS amplitude, or ≥1 mm depth in ≥2 contiguous leads); poor R-wave progression V1–V4; LAD territory assignment; absent ST elevation (distinguishes old from acute); concurrent new ST elevation flagged as STEMI-on-old |
| **MR** (Morphology/Repolarization) | Supporting | R-wave amplitude loss across V1–V4 (quantified progression); anterior voltage reduction; LBBB detection (suspends Q-wave interpretation if present); QRS fragmentation |
| **CDS** (Cross-Domain Synthesis) | Standard integration only | Integrates IT's Q-wave territory with MR's voltage loss to confirm old anterior MI; distinguishes from other causes of poor R-wave progression; detects STEMI-on-old if new ST elevation added |

### Primary Agent
**IT** — Old anterior MI is defined by pathological Q-waves in V1–V4 assigned to the LAD territory; Q-wave detection, measurement, and coronary territory assignment are core IT functions.

### Cross-Domain Hints
- IT → MR: `cross_domain_hint: "Q_waves_V1_to_V4 — quantify R-wave amplitude progression to confirm anterior voltage loss"`
- IT → CDS: `cross_domain_hint: "anterior_Q_waves_without_ST_elevation — confirm old anterior MI; flag if new ST elevation appears (STEMI-on-old)"`

### CDS Specific Role
CDS integrates IT's Q-wave findings with MR's R-wave progression data to confirm old anterior MI and assess scar extent. CDS distinguishes old anterior MI from other causes of poor R-wave progression (LBBB, LVH, COPD, incorrect lead placement) by requiring both pathological Q-waves (IT) and anterior voltage loss (MR). If RRC identifies frequent PVCs or VT runs, CDS flags scar-related arrhythmia substrate.

## 9. Edge Cases and Pitfalls

**1. LBBB completely masks anterior Q-wave diagnosis.** In LBBB, the QS pattern in V1-V3 is an expected, normal consequence of reversed septal activation. Do not report "anterior MI" based on Q waves in V1-V3 when LBBB is present. This is a common false-positive and requires imaging (echo or cardiac MRI with late gadolinium enhancement) for definitive assessment.

**2. Lead misplacement simulates anterior MI.** Electrodes placed in the second intercostal space (too high) produce QS complexes in V1-V3 that mimic anterior MI. If a patient has no history of MI and QS is found, verify lead placement. The QS normalizes when leads are placed correctly. Note: standard ECG calibration records with no clinical markers to identify electrode position — clinical verification is required.

**3. LV aneurysm versus acute STEMI — both have ST elevation with Q waves.** LV aneurysm (scar with paradoxical systolic expansion) produces chronic persistent ST elevation in the infarct zone. This can be mistaken for acute STEMI. Key discriminators: (1) ST elevation in aneurysm is typically concave-up and less pronounced than acute STEMI; (2) clinical history of prior MI and stable symptoms; (3) echo showing dyskinetic segment; (4) flat troponin trend.

**4. Re-MI (new MI at old infarct site).** When a patient with prior anterior MI occludes the same territory again, new ST changes may be subtle because the pre-existing QS complex limits the amount of R-wave that can be "lost." The re-occlusion may manifest as modest ST elevation or hyperacute T-waves on a background of Q waves. Compare with the most recent prior ECG, not the most remote one.

**5. WPW pseudo-Q waves in lateral leads.** In WPW with negative delta waves in V1-V4, pseudo-Q waves can exactly mimic anterior MI Q waves. The short PR interval and delta wave in other leads are the discriminators. In atypical WPW patterns, this can be subtle.

**6. Cardiac amyloid and pseudo-Q waves.** Amyloid infiltration of the septum produces loss of septal forces, generating pseudo-Q waves (often called "pseudoinfarction" pattern). This occurs particularly in V1-V3. Key discriminators: low voltage in remaining leads, echocardiographic granular sparkling texture, clinical features of amyloidosis.

**7. Septal hypertrophy (HCM) — deep Q waves in I, aVL, V5-V6.** Hypertrophic cardiomyopathy produces deep narrow Q waves (septum is hypertrophied, so septal forces are exaggerated). These Q waves appear in lateral leads (I, aVL, V4-V6), not in V1-V3 — the reverse of anterior MI. Also: HCM Q waves are narrow (<40ms), anterior MI Q waves are wide.
