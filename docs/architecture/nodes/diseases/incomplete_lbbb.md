## 1. Pathophysiology Root Cause

### 1.1 Anatomy of the Left Bundle Branch
The left bundle branch (LBB) emerges from the bundle of His and fans out immediately into two major divisions: the left anterior fascicle (LAF), which carries conduction to the anterior and lateral walls of the left ventricle, and the left posterior fascicle (LPF), which carries conduction to the posterior and inferior walls. A third, septal fascicle is inconsistently present. The LBB as a whole serves the large, dominant mass of the left ventricle — the source of the QRS's majority of electrical forces.

### 1.2 What "Incomplete" Means
Complete LBBB results from total failure of left bundle conduction: the left ventricle is depolarized entirely via slow cell-to-cell spread from the right ventricle through the septum, reversing normal septal activation and creating a broad, monophasic QRS ≥120ms. **Incomplete LBBB** represents partial conduction delay — sufficient to produce the same directional abnormalities (reversed septal activation, broad lateral R waves) but with QRS duration 110–119ms. The underlying pathology and morphological fingerprint are identical to complete LBBB; the ventricle is just not fully dependent on cell-to-cell spread.

Mechanistically, incomplete LBBB may represent:
- Partial block in one of the two main fascicles with preserved (but slow) conduction through the other
- Bilateral fascicular slowing without complete block in either
- Disease at the proximal LBB trunk before it bifurcates

### 1.3 Causes and Clinical Context
Incomplete LBBB carries the same disease associations as complete LBBB — it is not a benign variant:
- **Hypertensive heart disease**: The most common cause. LV pressure overload leads to myocardial fibrosis that encroaches on the conduction system.
- **Dilated cardiomyopathy**: Diffuse myocardial disease disrupts fascicular conduction.
- **Aortic stenosis**: LV pressure overload with progressive conduction system fibrosis.
- **Coronary artery disease**: Ischemia to the proximal LAD territory (which supplies most of the LBB).
- **Lenegre-Lev disease**: Primary fibrotic degeneration of the conduction system; progressive widening over years.

Unlike incomplete RBBB, incomplete LBBB is **rarely a benign normal variant**. Its presence should prompt clinical evaluation for structural heart disease.

## 2. ECG Presentation — Lead by Lead

### 2.1 Diagnostic Criteria (2025 AHA/ESC Guidelines)
| Criterion | Requirement |
|---|---|
| QRS Duration | 110–119ms (borderline; <120ms distinguishes from complete LBBB) |
| Broad notched or slurred R in lateral leads | Broad, monophasic or notched R in I, aVL, V5, V6 |
| Absent septal Q waves | No normal septal Q waves in I, V5, V6 (reversed septal activation) |
| No rS in V1 | V1 may show QS or rS but without the classic narrow rS of normal; often broad |
| ST-T changes | Discordant: ST depression and T inversion in leads with dominant R (I, aVL, V5, V6); ST elevation in V1–V3 |

### 2.2 Lead-by-Lead Manifestation
| Lead | Expected Morphology | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| I | Broad monophasic R wave; no Q wave | Loss of septal Q is key |
| II | May be broad R; axis-dependent | |
| III | May show rS or broad R depending on axis | |
| aVR | QS or rS pattern | Mirror of lateral leads |
| aVL | Broad notched R; ST depression, T inversion | Often similar to I |
| aVF | Variable; axis-dependent | |
| V1 | QS or broad rS; ST elevation (discordant) | Reversed septal → initial away from V1 |
| V2 | QS or rS; ST elevation common | Secondary change, not ischemia |
| V3 | Transition; may show ST elevation | |
| V4 | Transitional morphology | |
| V5 | Broad notched R; ST depression, T inversion | KEY LEAD |
| V6 | Broad notched R; ST depression, T inversion | KEY LEAD |

### 2.3 Key Leads
- **I, V5, V6**: The lateral leads showing the broad notched or monophasic R wave. The absence of septal Q waves here is as diagnostically important as the broad R.
- **V1**: QS or rS pattern with secondary ST elevation — this is not STEMI; it is a secondary change from abnormal depolarization sequence.
- **aVL**: Broad R with discordant ST-T changes, often shows the lateral changes clearly.

### 2.4 Beat-by-Beat Considerations
- Morphology is consistent beat to beat in sinus rhythm (fixed delay, not rate-related).
- If LBBB morphology appears only at faster rates, this is **rate-related (functional) LBBB aberrancy** — a different entity with different implications.
- PR interval: normal to slightly prolonged. A prolonged PR + incomplete LBBB raises concern for multifascicular disease (His-Purkinje disease at multiple levels).
- In atrial fibrillation with rate-related aberrancy, LBBB morphology at fast ventricular rates must not be misread as VT — Ashman phenomenon.

## 3. Morphology Details

### 3.1 The Broad Notched R Wave Explained
Normal septal activation travels left-to-right (from the LBB septal branch). This creates the small septal Q wave in lateral leads (I, V5, V6) as the initial vector travels away from them. In LBBB (complete or incomplete), this septal activation is reversed — the septum is now activated right-to-left via right bundle conduction. The initial vector therefore moves toward the lateral leads, producing no Q wave and a broad, uninterrupted R wave. The "notch" in the R wave reflects the transition between septal activation (rightward spread from RBB) and lateral wall activation (slow cell-to-cell or fascicular spread).

### 3.2 QRS Duration Boundary
The 110–119ms zone is the defining feature of incomplete vs. complete LBBB. Accurate measurement is essential. Measure the widest QRS across all 12 leads simultaneously. Digital caliper measurement is preferred; automated measurements may underestimate QRS in lateral leads due to low-amplitude terminal forces.

### 3.3 Discordant ST-T Changes
The ST-T changes in LBBB (complete or incomplete) are **secondary** — caused by the abnormal depolarization sequence, not by primary repolarization disease. The rule of discordance: ST elevation occurs in leads with predominantly negative QRS (V1–V3), and ST depression with T inversion occurs in leads with predominantly positive QRS (I, aVL, V5, V6). These changes do NOT indicate ischemia in isolation. This is the Sgarbossa principle's baseline: pre-existing LBBB creates expected discordant ST changes.

### 3.4 Septal Q Wave Absence as a Diagnostic Feature
The absence of septal Q waves in I, V5, and V6 is often underappreciated. In many normal ECGs, tiny (1–2ms) septal Q waves are present laterally. Their complete absence is a red flag for LBBB even when QRS duration is borderline.

### 3.5 R Wave Notching
A notched or "M-shaped" R wave in I, V5, or V6 reflects the two-component activation: initial right-to-left septal forces (rising limb of R) followed by a brief delay before lateral wall forces (creating the notch) and then the main lateral R wave forces. This notching pattern distinguishes incomplete LBBB from simple voltage abnormalities.

### 3.6 V1 Morphology
The QS pattern in V1 results from reversed initial septal activation: initial forces are now directed away from V1 (right-to-left through septum, then left ventricular lateral wall activation — all moving away from V1). No positive deflection is expected. An R wave in V1 in the setting of LBBB morphology should raise concern for posterior MI or RV hypertrophy.

### 3.7 Incomplete vs. Complete: Practical Distinction
The morphological changes of incomplete LBBB are qualitatively identical to complete LBBB but quantitatively less severe. The notching may be subtler, the R wave not as broad, the ST-T changes not as exaggerated. The single differentiating measurement is QRS duration.

## 4. Differential Diagnosis

### 4.1 Mimics Table
| Condition | Differentiating Feature |
|---|---|
| Complete LBBB | QRS ≥120ms; morphologically identical |
| LVH with strain | Broad R in I/V5/V6 but septal Q waves typically PRESERVED; QRS usually <110ms unless voltage criteria met |
| LAFB (left anterior fascicular block) | Left axis deviation + qR in I/aVL but QRS <110ms; no broad monophasic R; septal Q waves may be present |
| Rate-related LBBB | Morphology only at faster rates; disappears at baseline HR |
| Wolff-Parkinson-White (left-sided pathway) | Delta wave; short PR; preexcitation pattern; LBBB-like morphology from right-to-left septal activation |
| LBBB pattern in VT | Wide QRS ≥120ms; AV dissociation; fusion beats; independent P waves |
| Hyperkalemia | Wide QRS but sinusoidal; peaked T waves; absent P waves at extreme levels |

### 4.2 Coexisting Conditions
- **Hypertension + LVH**: Voltage criteria for LVH are unreliable in LBBB because depolarization sequence alteration affects voltage distribution. Echocardiogram is needed.
- **Coronary artery disease**: Sgarbossa criteria apply for detecting superimposed STEMI on LBBB. In incomplete LBBB, Sgarbossa criteria may be harder to apply because baseline discordance is less pronounced.
- **Heart failure/cardiomyopathy**: LBBB in heart failure indicates mechanical dyssynchrony (LV lateral wall activates late). Incomplete LBBB may still produce clinically relevant dyssynchrony.
- **Aortic stenosis**: Watch for progressive QRS widening over serial ECGs as a marker of worsening conduction system involvement.

## 5. STAT Classification

| Category | Classification | Rationale |
|---|---|---|
| Isolated incomplete LBBB, known/stable | NOT STAT | Document; compare to prior ECGs; outpatient workup |
| New incomplete LBBB in chest pain | MODERATE-HIGH ALERT | May represent acute ischemia or acute cardiomyopathy; Sgarbossa principles apply |
| Incomplete LBBB with progressive widening on serial ECGs | MODERATE ALERT | Approaching complete LBBB; evaluate conduction disease |
| Incomplete LBBB + hemodynamic instability | HIGH ALERT | Evaluate for cardiomyopathy, acute MI, or rate-related cause |
| Incomplete LBBB + symptomatic HF with EF <35% | MODERATE ALERT | CRT consideration territory |

**Overall STAT status: NOT STAT** — but incomplete LBBB is NEVER a benign normal variant. Always warrants documentation, comparison to prior ECGs, and clinical correlation.

## 6. Reasoning Complexity Analysis

### 6.1 Pattern Recognition Complexity
Incomplete LBBB is harder to recognize than complete LBBB because the changes are subtler. The key features — broad notched R, absent septal Q, discordant ST-T — may each be individually mild. The algorithm must check all three simultaneously.

### 6.2 Measurement Precision Requirement
QRS duration in the 110–119ms range is the defining boundary. The same precision challenges as incomplete RBBB apply, but LBBB is more complex because terminal forces may be low-amplitude and harder for automated algorithms to detect.

### 6.3 Discordant ST-T Interpretation
The algorithm must recognize secondary ST changes as expected (not pathological) while simultaneously remaining alert for concordant ST changes that would trigger Sgarbossa criteria. This dual-track analysis adds complexity.

### 6.4 Clinical Context Sensitivity
Unlike incomplete RBBB, incomplete LBBB is almost always associated with underlying pathology. The algorithm must flag this condition more aggressively for clinical follow-up than incomplete RBBB.

### 6.5 Comparison to Prior ECGs
The most important clinical question with incomplete LBBB is: is this new or old? New LBBB or incomplete LBBB has different implications than chronic stable finding. The system must support serial ECG comparison.

### 6.6 Difficulty Score

| Dimension | Score (1–5) | Rationale |
|---|---|---|
| Pattern recognition | 2 | Same morphology as complete LBBB but subtler |
| Measurement precision | 2 | Same QRS duration borderline challenge as incomplete RBBB |
| ST-T interpretation (discordance) | 3 | Must distinguish secondary from primary changes; Sgarbossa awareness |
| Differential complexity | 2 | Key distinction is QRS duration and presence of broad R + absent Q |
| Clinical integration | 2 | Less benign than incomplete RBBB; structural disease workup needed |
| Serial comparison | 2 | Progressive widening is key clinical flag |
| Rarity/recognition challenge | 1 | Well-characterized condition |
| **Overall Difficulty** | **2.0/5** | Similar to incomplete RBBB but ST interpretation adds complexity |

**RRC** — incomplete LBBB is defined by the QRS duration bracket (100-119ms); morphology changes are supportive but the duration criterion from RRC is the defining characteristic.

### Cross-Domain Hints
- RRC emits: `cross_domain_hint: "QRS 100-119ms — MR must assess for partial LBBB morphology (broad R in I/V5-V6, reduced septal Q) to confirm incomplete LBBB"`

### CDS Specific Role
CDS integrates RRC QRS duration (100-119ms) and MR morphology (partial LBBB pattern) to confirm incomplete LBBB. Unlike complete LBBB, incomplete LBBB does not fully invalidate standard ST ischemia criteria — CDS applies a cautious approach: if significant ST changes are detected, it notes that incomplete LBBB partially reduces specificity of ST interpretation and recommends clinical correlation rather than applying full Sgarbossa scoring. Standard integration overall.

## 9. Edge Cases and Pitfalls

**Pitfall 1 — ST elevation misread as STEMI**: The secondary ST elevation in V1–V3 in incomplete LBBB is NOT a STEMI. The algorithm must apply Sgarbossa criteria and flag the ST change as "secondary/expected" unless concordant changes are present.

**Pitfall 2 — LVH strain pattern misclassified as incomplete LBBB**: LVH with strain shows ST depression and T inversion in V5–V6 (similar to incomplete LBBB) but typically preserves septal Q waves and has QRS <110ms. Check Q waves and duration carefully.

**Pitfall 3 — Dismissing incomplete LBBB as benign**: Unlike incomplete RBBB, this is almost always pathological. Clinical evaluation for structural heart disease is mandatory, not optional.

**Pitfall 4 — WPW left-sided pathway**: A left lateral accessory pathway preexcites the right side of the heart first, mimicking LBBB morphology (right-to-left initial septal activation, broad lateral R, no septal Q). Distinguishing features: short PR interval (<120ms), delta wave (slurred initial upstroke of QRS), normal QRS duration may be borderline. Clinical presentation (SVT, young patient) and EP study are definitive.

**Pitfall 5 — Rate-related LBBB in AF**: In AF with fast ventricular rates, rate-related LBBB (Ashman phenomenon) produces LBBB morphology at fast rates. This looks like WCT (wide complex tachycardia) and must not trigger a VT alert if AF is identified. Key: irregular rhythm confirms AF; LBBB morphology at fast rates with normalization at slower rates.

**Pitfall 6 — Sgarbossa in incomplete LBBB**: Sgarbossa criteria were validated for complete LBBB. Their sensitivity/specificity in incomplete LBBB (where baseline discordance is less exaggerated) is less well established. Flag for clinical correlation rather than automated diagnosis in borderline cases.
