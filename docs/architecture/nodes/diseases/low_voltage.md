## 1. Pathophysiology Root Cause

### 1.1 Signal Attenuation Mechanism
QRS voltage on the surface ECG reflects the sum of all ventricular myocardial electrical vectors. The amplitude at any surface electrode depends on: (1) the magnitude of the myocardial dipoles; (2) the distance between the heart and the electrode; (3) the electrical conductivity of the intervening tissue. Low voltage arises when any of these factors reduces the effective signal reaching the surface electrodes.

**Interposed insulating medium**: Pericardial fluid, pleural effusion, and subcutaneous fat conduct electricity poorly. They interpose a high-resistance medium between the heart and the recording electrode, attenuating the signal. The effect is inversely proportional to conductivity and proportional to the thickness of the insulating layer.

**Reduced myocardial mass or signal**: In infiltrative cardiomyopathies (amyloid, sarcoid, hemochromatosis), the conducting myocardium is replaced by electrically inert deposits. Fewer viable myocardial cells generate smaller total electrical dipoles. The signal is reduced at its source.

**Hyperinflated lung**: Air is an excellent electrical insulator. COPD/emphysema increases lung volume and displaces the heart inferiorly, moving it further from the precordial electrodes. Additionally, hyperinflated lung surrounds the heart, creating an insulating air medium.

**Obesity**: Increased thickness of subcutaneous tissue attenuates the signal between the myocardium and the skin surface electrodes.

### 1.2 Critical Clinical Distinction
Low voltage is a common, often benign finding (obesity, COPD) but can indicate serious pathology (tamponade, amyloid). The clinical context — acuity, symptoms, other ECG findings — determines the urgency of evaluation.

### 1.3 Amyloidosis Voltage-Mass Mismatch
Cardiac amyloidosis (transthyretin or light-chain types) produces a near-pathognomonic combination: low voltage QRS on ECG despite markedly increased LV wall thickness on echocardiography. Normal LVH produces increased voltage because it is hypertrophied muscle generating more electrical signal. Amyloid deposits between and within myocytes do not conduct electricity — they replace muscle with non-conducting extracellular protein fibrils. The result: thickened walls (echo) + decreased voltage (ECG) = voltage-mass mismatch, which is ~95% specific for cardiac amyloidosis in the appropriate clinical context.

## 2. ECG Presentation — Lead by Lead

### 2.1 Diagnostic Criteria (2025 AHA/ESC Guidelines)

| Criterion | Threshold | Sensitivity | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| Limb lead low voltage | QRS amplitude <5 mm in ALL limb leads (I, II, III, aVR, aVL, aVF) | ~50% for tamponade | Most specific criterion |
| Precordial low voltage | QRS amplitude <10 mm in ALL precordial leads (V1–V6) | Varies | Less commonly used alone |
| Combined criteria | Both limb AND precordial criteria met | Higher specificity | Most clinically significant |
| Limb only, normal precordial | <5 mm limb only | May indicate obese body habitus | Less pathological |

Note: "QRS amplitude" = peak-to-trough measurement of the entire QRS complex including both positive and negative deflections (R + S amplitude).

### 2.2 Lead-by-Lead Manifestation

| Lead | Low Voltage Finding | Amyloid Pattern Note | Tamponade Note |
|---|---|---|---|
| I | <5 mm total QRS amplitude | Pseudo-infarct Q waves may coexist | Electrical alternans may be superimposed |
| II | <5 mm total QRS amplitude | PR prolongation common | Alternans |
| III | <5 mm total QRS amplitude | Variable Q waves | Alternans |
| aVR | <5 mm (inverted QRS) | — | Alternans |
| aVL | <5 mm total QRS amplitude | Pseudo-infarct | Alternans |
| aVF | <5 mm total QRS amplitude | Pseudo-infarct inferior pattern | Alternans |
| V1 | <10 mm; often rS or qR | rS or QS pattern common in amyloid | Alternans |
| V2 | <10 mm | QS or rS; anteroseptal pseudo-infarct | Alternans |
| V3 | <10 mm | QS or rS | Alternans |
| V4 | <10 mm | Transition; R-wave progression loss | Alternans |
| V5 | <10 mm; R wave amplitude reduced | Reduced precordial voltages | Alternans |
| V6 | <10 mm | Reduced | Alternans |

### 2.3 Key Leads
- **Limb leads (I, II, III, aVR, aVL, aVF)**: The primary criterion. All six limb leads must be <5 mm for formal low voltage diagnosis.
- **V1–V4**: Particularly relevant in amyloidosis (pseudo-infarct Q/QS patterns in anterior leads without prior MI).
- **Any lead**: When electrical alternans is superimposed on low voltage, tamponade diagnosis should be triggered.

### 2.4 Beat-by-Beat Considerations
- Low voltage itself is a static finding — amplitude does not change beat to beat unless electrical alternans is present
- In pericardial effusion increasing toward tamponade, low voltage may develop over hours (acute effusion) or days (slowly accumulating effusion)
- Comparing to prior ECG: new low voltage (previously normal QRS amplitude) is a STAT finding — suggests acute pericardial effusion or rapid amyloid progression

## 3. Morphology Details

### 3.1 QRS Appearance in Low Voltage
The QRS complex is morphologically normal but scaled down in amplitude. The R-wave progression, axis, and width are preserved. Low voltage is a quantitative, not morphological, abnormality. In amyloidosis the QRS may additionally show abnormal Q waves or poor R-wave progression that resembles infarction.

### 3.2 Amyloid Pseudo-Infarct Pattern
In cardiac amyloidosis, QS or QR patterns develop in anterior (V1–V4) and/or inferior (II/III/aVF) leads without actual myocardial infarction. The mechanism: amyloid infiltration of the conducting myocardium creates areas of electrical silence mimicking infarct zones. The combination of these pseudo-infarct Q waves + low voltage is highly characteristic of amyloid. Troponin may be mildly elevated chronically but without the acute rise-fall pattern of STEMI.

### 3.3 Pericardial Effusion Pattern
New low voltage in a patient with acute pericarditis, systemic lupus, malignancy, or post-cardiac surgery should trigger immediate echo to assess for hemodynamically significant effusion. The appearance is simply reduced QRS amplitude across all leads with no other specific morphology.

### 3.4 COPD/Emphysema Low Voltage
COPD low voltage is typically accompanied by: right axis deviation, clockwise rotation (poor R-wave progression, S1S2S3 pattern), tall P waves in II (P pulmonale), and an overall "long thin heart" on X-ray. The precordial leads show notoriously poor R-wave progression with persistent S waves laterally.

### 3.5 Obesity-Related Low Voltage
Body habitus-related low voltage typically affects limb leads more than precordial leads and is associated with normal echocardiography and no clinical cardiac disease. The diagnosis of obesity-related low voltage is one of exclusion — pathological causes must first be excluded.

### 3.6 Hypothyroidism
Severe hypothyroidism can produce pericardial effusion → low voltage (the effusion is the mechanism, not the thyroid hormone deficiency itself). Additionally, sinus bradycardia is prominent. TSH level confirms.

### 3.7 Tamponade Triad Completion
Low voltage + electrical alternans + sinus tachycardia = tamponade triad. Each element alone is non-specific. The triad is highly specific for tamponade physiology. When all three are present, bedside echocardiography is mandatory immediately.

## 4. Differential Diagnosis

### 4.1 Mimics

| Condition | Differentiating Feature |
|---|---|
| Obesity | Limb leads primarily affected; precordial often preserved; no symptoms; BMI confirms |
| COPD/emphysema | Right axis, S1S2S3, P pulmonale, poor R-wave progression; history and CXR |
| Pleural effusion | Lateralizing: large left pleural effusion attenuates left-sided leads more; CXR confirms |
| Cardiac amyloid | Pseudo-infarct Q waves + voltage-mass mismatch on echo; echocardiography diagnostic |
| Pericardial effusion (no tamponade) | Low voltage without electrical alternans or tachycardia; echo shows effusion without tamponade physiology |
| Pericardial tamponade | Low voltage + electrical alternans + tachycardia; echo confirms compression |
| Hypothyroidism | Bradycardia; TSH elevated; effusion on echo |
| Myocarditis (acute) | Low voltage may appear in acute phase; troponin elevated; echo shows diffuse dysfunction |
| Normal variant | Some thin individuals or children; no pathology |

### 4.2 Coexisting Conditions
- **Low voltage + amyloid + atrial fibrillation**: Atrial amyloid deposition predisposes to AF; AF + low voltage + voltage-mass mismatch = high probability cardiac amyloidosis
- **Low voltage + COPD + right heart failure**: Cor pulmonale producing both COPD-related low voltage and right heart dilation/failure
- **Low voltage + tamponade + malignancy**: Malignant pericardial effusion (the most common cause of large effusions in cancer patients)

## 5. STAT Classification

| Finding | STAT Level | Action |
|---|---|---|
| New low voltage + electrical alternans + tachycardia | STAT — Critical | Bedside echo immediately; prepare for pericardiocentesis |
| New low voltage in known malignancy patient | STAT — Urgent | Echo within 1 hour; malignant effusion exclusion |
| New low voltage in post-cardiac surgery patient | STAT — Urgent | Echo; rule out hemopericardium/tamponade |
| Low voltage + pseudo-infarct Q waves, no prior MI | Urgent | Cardiology consult; amyloid workup (echo, SPECT, biopsy pathway) |
| Chronic low voltage in obese COPD patient, stable | Non-urgent | Document; baseline comparison; no acute action |
| New low voltage vs prior ECG baseline | Urgent | Same-day echo; identify cause |

## 6. Reasoning Complexity Analysis

### 6.1 Reasoning Domains Required
- Voltage measurement: per-lead amplitude measurement across all 12 leads
- Threshold application: limb leads <5 mm, precordial leads <10 mm
- Distribution assessment: limb only vs precordial only vs both
- Coexisting features: electrical alternans, sinus tachycardia, Q waves, axis changes
- Clinical integration: body habitus, lung disease history, malignancy, cardiac surgery, drugs, thyroid function
- Temporal comparison: new vs chronic (requires prior ECG)

### 6.2 Feature Dependencies
- Low voltage diagnosis requires all leads of a class to meet threshold; a single limb lead with <5 mm is not low voltage
- Precordial amplitude measurement requires accurate baseline identification (TP segment)
- Automated QRS amplitude measurement algorithms are moderately reliable for clear QRS complexes; artifact or poor electrode contact can falsely reduce apparent amplitude
- Amyloid pseudo-infarct Q waves coexist and must be separately flagged

### 6.3 Cross-Condition Interactions
- Tamponade triad: low voltage alone is insufficient; requires alternans and tachycardia for tamponade diagnosis
- LVH masking: in a patient with pre-existing LVH, developing amyloid may "normalize" previously high voltages without falling below the absolute <5 mm threshold — the relative drop is the clue (compare to prior ECG)
- LBBB + low voltage: wide QRS can make amplitude measurement less accurate; each QRS component (R and S) measured separately

### 6.4 Reasoning Chain Sketch
1. Measure peak-to-trough QRS amplitude in all limb leads
2. Apply <5 mm threshold: is it met in ALL limb leads?
3. Measure precordial QRS amplitude; apply <10 mm threshold: is it met in ALL precordial leads?
4. Document which criterion (limb only / precordial only / both) is met
5. Scan for electrical alternans superimposed on low voltage
6. Scan for sinus tachycardia
7. Scan for Q waves / pseudo-infarct pattern (amyloid consideration)
8. Scan for axis changes (COPD pattern) and P-wave changes
9. Compare to prior ECG if available
10. Integrate clinical context: BMI, lung disease, malignancy, prior cardiac history
11. Assign diagnosis probability: tamponade / effusion / amyloid / COPD / obesity / other
12. Assign STAT level

### 6.5 Confidence Anchors
- Low voltage (both limb and precordial) + electrical alternans + tachycardia: high confidence tamponade
- Low voltage + pseudo-infarct Q waves + no prior MI history: high confidence amyloidosis (pending echo)
- Limb low voltage only + obese patient + normal echo: high confidence obesity-related
- New low voltage vs prior ECG: high confidence developing effusion or amyloid until proven otherwise

### 6.6 Difficulty Score

| Dimension | Score (1–5) | Rationale |
|---|---|---|
| Morphology Recognition | 1 | Low voltage is purely quantitative; visual recognition at a glance |
| Interval Measurement | 2 | Amplitude measurement in each lead; straightforward if baseline clean |
| Differential Diagnosis | 3 | Multiple causes; clinical context separates them |
| Clinical Integration | 3 | BMI, lung history, malignancy, prior cardiac history needed |
| Rarity / Prior Probability | 2 | Common finding; most cases are benign |
| Arrhythmia Risk Stratification | 2 | Not directly arrhythmogenic; tamponade triad context required |
| **Overall** | **2.0** | Detection is easy; clinical context determines significance |

## 6A. Agent Assignment

**Architecture:** Option E — 3 Parallel Phase 1 Specialists + 1 Sequential CDS (Node 2.1 Gate Passed 2026-03-27)

| Agent | Role for Low Voltage QRS | Key Features Used |
|-------|--------------------------|-------------------|
| **RRC** (Rhythm/Rate/Conduction) | Not involved | No conduction interval features specific to low voltage; rate and rhythm are contextual only |
| **IT** (Ischemia/Territory) | Not involved | No ischemia-specific features in isolated low voltage |
| **MR** (Morphology/Repolarization) | Primary | QRS amplitude <5mm in ALL six limb leads (I, II, III, aVL, aVF, aVR) — low voltage criterion for limb leads; QRS amplitude <10mm in ALL six precordial leads (V1-V6) — low voltage criterion for precordial leads; full low voltage: both limb AND precordial criteria met; isolated limb-lead low voltage with normal precordial voltage (suggests obesity, emphysema); electrical alternans if co-occurring (MR detects both) |
| **CDS** (Cross-Domain Synthesis) | Standard integration only — generates differential diagnosis | Integrates MR low voltage characterization (limb-only vs precordial-only vs global) with any other contextual findings; generates differential: pericardial effusion (especially if electrical alternans also present), obesity/emphysema (typically isolated limb-lead), hypothyroidism (global low voltage + sinus bradycardia from RRC), cardiac amyloidosis (global low voltage + ECG-echo voltage mismatch context) |

### Primary Agent
**MR** — low voltage is defined entirely by QRS amplitude measurement across all 12 leads: <5mm in all limb leads and/or <10mm in all precordial leads; this is a pure morphology amplitude measurement in the MR domain.

### Cross-Domain Hints
- MR emits (if co-occurring with electrical alternans): `cross_domain_hint: "Low QRS voltage (<5mm limb leads) with electrical alternans detected — CDS must generate pericardial effusion/tamponade flag"`
- MR emits (if global low voltage): `cross_domain_hint: "Global low voltage (limb + precordial) detected — CDS must generate differential: pericardial effusion, cardiac amyloid, hypothyroidism, emphysema"`

### CDS Specific Role
CDS generates the low-voltage differential diagnosis based on pattern and context. For limb-lead-only low voltage with normal precordial voltage: CDS suggests obesity, emphysema, or large pleural effusion as causes (signal attenuation from thoracic distance). For global low voltage (both limb and precordial): CDS generates a broader differential including pericardial effusion (especially if electrical alternans co-detected by MR), cardiac amyloidosis (global low voltage is a hallmark), hypothyroidism (if RRC reports sinus bradycardia), and myocarditis or infiltrative disease. The combination of global low voltage + electrical alternans from MR generates the highest-priority flag: pericardial tamponade — immediate clinical evaluation required.

## 9. Edge Cases and Pitfalls

1. **Technical low voltage**: Incorrect calibration (half-standard, 0.5 mV/cm instead of 1 mV/cm) will halve all amplitudes and mimic pathological low voltage. Always verify the calibration marker (1 mV pulse) is 10 mm tall before diagnosing low voltage.
2. **Limb lead electrode misplacement**: Placing limb electrodes on the torso (as with amputees or immobilized limbs) reduces limb lead amplitude. This is a technical artifact, not pathological low voltage. Technician notes and clinical context confirm.
3. **Voltage-mass mismatch missed**: In patients with known hypertension and expected LVH, the ECG may show "normal" QRS amplitude (~5–8 mm limb leads) while echo shows massively thickened ventricle (wall thickness >15 mm). This "normal but relatively low" voltage in the context of extreme hypertrophy is the amyloid signature. Absolute low voltage threshold alone will miss these cases — voltage-to-mass ratio requires echo data.
4. **COPD masking LVH**: COPD produces low voltage that can mask coexisting LVH. In a hypertensive COPD patient, LVH strain pattern may be absent or attenuated by the insulating lung. The combination of COPD + hypertension requires echo for LVH assessment rather than relying on ECG voltage criteria.
5. **New low voltage after cardiac surgery**: In the ICU post-cardiac surgery, developing pericardial effusion or hemopericardium may manifest as new low voltage before tamponade physiology appears on echo. Serial ECG comparison in the post-surgical period is a critical monitoring tool.
6. **Pleural effusion lateralizing**: A large left-sided pleural effusion may reduce amplitude in left-sided leads (I, aVL, V4–V6) without affecting right-sided leads (V1–V3) symmetrically. This asymmetric low voltage pattern can be distinguished from pericardial effusion (symmetric) and obesity/COPD (symmetric) by comparing lead groups.
