## 1. Pathophysiology Root Cause

### 1.1 LV Strain — Secondary Repolarization Abnormality
LV strain is a secondary repolarization change arising from pressure or volume overload of the left ventricle. The mechanism involves two components:

**Abnormal depolarization**: In LVH, the hypertrophied LV mass takes longer to depolarize and the subendocardial-to-subepicardial depolarization sequence is altered. The delayed and abnormal depolarization forces the repolarization sequence to become discordant with the depolarization vector.

**Subendocardial ischemia**: Chronic pressure overload (hypertension, aortic stenosis) places the subendocardium at risk for supply-demand mismatch. The subendocardium — furthest from the epicardial coronary supply — develops chronic ischemic repolarization changes. This is not plaque-based ischemia but diffuse microvascular and demand-driven subendocardial ischemia.

The combined result: in lateral leads (I, aVL, V4–V6) where the QRS is predominantly positive (reflecting the leftward LV vector), the ST segment is depressed with a characteristic downsloping morphology and the T wave is asymmetrically inverted. The term "LV strain" reflects both the secondary repolarization abnormality and the subendocardial demand ischemia.

### 1.2 RV Strain — Acute vs Chronic
RV strain pattern reflects right ventricular pressure or volume overload causing analogous changes in the right precordial leads (V1–V4):

**Chronic RV strain** (RVH, pulmonary hypertension, chronic cor pulmonale): Prolonged RV pressure overload leads to RV hypertrophy with secondary repolarization changes — T-wave inversions in V1–V4, often with right axis deviation and dominant R in V1.

**Acute RV strain** (massive pulmonary embolism): Sudden afterload increase due to PE causes acute RV dilation and wall stress. The acutely stressed RV generates T-wave inversions in V1–V4, RBBB (acute or incomplete), and the S1Q3T3 pattern. This pattern can develop within hours of a massive PE, making it a time-critical diagnostic sign.

### 1.3 Why Strain Is Not Primary Ischemia
The ST-T changes in strain follow the secondary repolarization principle: they appear in leads where the QRS is predominantly positive (LV strain in lateral leads; RV strain in right precordial leads). Primary ischemic changes also respect coronary territories but arise from different mechanisms. The morphological distinction — asymmetric T inversion in strain vs symmetric T inversion in ischemia — is the key differentiator.

## 2. ECG Presentation — Lead by Lead

### 2.1 Diagnostic Criteria (2025 AHA/ESC Guidelines)

| Pattern | ST Finding | T-Wave Finding | Distribution | Associated QRS |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| LV strain | Downsloping ST depression; concave-down or sagging | Asymmetric inversion: slow descent, rapid ascent | I, aVL, V4–V6 | LVH voltage (Sokolow-Lyon, Cornell criteria) |
| RV strain (chronic) | ST depression or elevation V1–V4 | T-wave inversions V1–V4 | V1–V4 ± II/III/aVF | Right axis deviation; dominant R in V1; RBBB possible |
| RV strain (acute) | ST changes V1–V4; S1Q3T3 | New T inversions V1–V4 | V1–V4 + S1, Q3, T3 | May have new incomplete/complete RBBB; tachycardia |

LVH voltage criteria (Sokolow-Lyon): S in V1 + R in V5 or V6 ≥35 mm. Cornell: R aVL + S V3 ≥28 mm (men) or ≥20 mm (women).

### 2.2 Lead-by-Lead Manifestation

| Lead | LV Strain Finding | RV Strain (Chronic) | RV Strain (Acute PE) |
|---|---|---|---|
| I | ST depression + asymmetric T inversion | Normal | S wave (S1 of S1Q3T3) |
| II | Normal or mild changes | Normal or mild T changes | Sinus tachycardia |
| III | Normal | Right axis Q wave possible | Q wave + T inversion (T3 of S1Q3T3) |
| aVR | ST elevation (reciprocal) | Normal | Possible |
| aVL | ST depression + T inversion | Normal | S wave prominent |
| aVF | Normal or mild | Normal | Variable |
| V1 | Normal or minor | T inversion; dominant R in severe RVH | T inversion; new RBBB may develop |
| V2 | Normal or minor | T inversion | T inversion; RBBB morphology |
| V3 | Transitional | T inversion | T inversion |
| V4 | ST depression + T inversion | T inversion or transition | T inversion; transition |
| V5 | ST depression + asymmetric T inversion | Normal or transition | Usually normal |
| V6 | ST depression + asymmetric T inversion | Normal | Usually normal |

### 2.3 Key Leads
- **LV strain**: I, aVL, V5, V6 — the leads that best demonstrate the high lateral and lateral wall of the LV. V4 often shows transitional changes. aVL is the most sensitive single lead.
- **RV strain**: V1–V4 — the right precordial leads overlying the RV free wall and RVOT. V1 shows the most dramatic changes in RVH; V3–V4 are most sensitive for acute PE changes.
- **S1Q3T3**: Lead I (S wave), Lead III (Q wave + T inversion) — the classic PE pattern. Specific but not sensitive (~20% of PE).

### 2.4 Beat-by-Beat Considerations
- LV strain is a chronic stable finding; it does not change beat to beat
- Acute RV strain in PE can develop within 1–6 hours of occlusion; repeat ECG in a patient with suspected PE who initially had a normal ECG is warranted
- Dynamic changes in V1–V4 T inversions over serial ECGs in a patient with dyspnea: consider evolving PE or worsening RV dysfunction
- New RBBB or S1Q3T3 pattern in a patient who previously had a normal ECG = acute RV overload (PE) until proven otherwise

## 3. Morphology Details

### 3.1 LV Strain ST Depression
The ST depression in LV strain has a characteristic downsloping morphology: the ST segment starts at or slightly below the isoelectric line at the J point and descends further, reaching its nadir in the middle of the ST segment before turning to form the inverted T wave. This downsloping ("sagging") pattern is distinct from horizontal ST depression (ischemia) or upsloping ST depression (early ischemia or digoxin).

### 3.2 Asymmetric T-Wave Inversion in LV Strain
The hallmark of LV strain T inversion: the T wave descends slowly (gradual slope from the ST segment) and ascends rapidly (steep return to baseline). The T wave appears asymmetric — wider on the ascending side. This is opposite to primary ischemic T inversion where both limbs of the T are symmetric (deep V-shape or U-shape). The asymmetry in strain reflects the abnormal repolarization gradient between endocardium and epicardium in the context of abnormal depolarization.

### 3.3 RV Strain T-Wave Inversions
In chronic RV strain, T-wave inversions in V1–V4 are typically symmetric and may extend to V5 in severe cases. The inversion is a secondary repolarization change analogous to LV strain in the opposite territory. In RBBB (which often accompanies RVH), T inversions in V1–V2 are expected secondary changes.

### 3.4 Acute RV Strain T-Wave Inversions
In acute massive PE, the T-wave inversions in V1–V4 appear rapidly. They may be deep (>5 mm) and are associated with ST changes. The pattern: V1 T inversion → V2 T inversion → V3 T inversion progressing over hours represents the wave-front of acute RV ischemia spreading from the most severely overloaded RV free wall anteriorly.

### 3.5 S1Q3T3 Pattern
Three components:
- **S wave in lead I**: Terminal S wave (QRS axis shifts rightward due to acute RV overload)
- **Q wave in lead III**: Small Q wave appears (axis shift and RV dilation vector change)
- **T inversion in lead III**: T inversion in III (ischemia of inferior RV wall or axis-related repolarization change)
Sensitivity ~20–25% for PE; specificity moderate. Present in only a minority of PE cases. Absence does not rule out PE.

### 3.6 LVH Voltage + Strain Pattern
The complete LVH + strain ECG includes:
- High QRS voltage (Sokolow-Lyon ≥35 mm, Cornell ≥28 mm men)
- LV strain ST-T changes in lateral leads
- Possible left axis deviation
- Possible P-mitrale in II (left atrial enlargement from LV diastolic dysfunction)
When all these are present, the diagnosis is unambiguous. When only strain ST-T changes are present without voltage criteria, LVH cannot be confirmed by ECG alone (sensitivity of voltage criteria alone is ~50%).

### 3.7 RBBB vs RV Strain
RBBB produces secondary T-wave inversions in V1–V2 (leads with the terminal R' — the RSR' complex). These are expected secondary changes and not evidence of primary RV ischemia. In RVH, the dominant R wave in V1 (without RSR' morphology) is the indicator, and T inversions extend to V3–V4 as a right axis/voltage effect.

## 4. Differential Diagnosis

### 4.1 Mimics

| Condition | Differentiating Feature |
|---|---|
| Lateral NSTEMI vs LV strain | NSTEMI: symmetric T inversion, troponin positive, new finding; LV strain: asymmetric, chronic, LVH voltage |
| Subacute STEMI evolution vs LV strain | Post-STEMI T inversions are territorial and symmetric; LV strain is diffuse lateral and asymmetric |
| LBBB secondary changes vs LV strain | LBBB: wide QRS; secondary T inversions in lateral leads; QRS width ≥120 ms distinguishes |
| PE (acute RV strain) vs anterior NSTEMI | PE: V1–V4 T inversions with sinus tachycardia, S1Q3T3, dyspnea; NSTEMI: territorial, troponin positive |
| ARVC vs chronic RV strain | ARVC: epsilon wave, LBBB morphology VT, structural RV abnormality; RV strain: no epsilon wave |
| Myocarditis vs acute RV strain | Myocarditis: diffuse ST changes, troponin elevation, echo diffuse dysfunction |
| Cor pulmonale vs PE | Chronic cor pulmonale: stable over time, history of lung disease, no acute change; PE: new acute finding |
| LV strain vs Takotsubo | Takotsubo: global deep T inversions V1–V6 following ST elevation; apical ballooning on echo |

### 4.2 Coexisting Conditions
- **LVH + CAD**: Chronic LV strain can coexist with NSTEMI; new deepening of T inversions beyond the established strain baseline may indicate ischemia superimposed on strain. Serial comparison essential.
- **RV strain + LV strain (biventricular)**: Biventricular failure shows features of both; seen in end-stage cardiomyopathy or acute cardiogenic shock.
- **PE + RBBB**: New RBBB + RV strain pattern in a dyspneic patient = massive PE until proven otherwise.

## 5. STAT Classification

| Finding | STAT Level | Action |
|---|---|---|
| New V1–V4 T inversions + acute dyspnea + tachycardia | STAT — Urgent | PE workup: D-dimer, CTA; anticoagulation pathway |
| S1Q3T3 (new) + acute dyspnea | Urgent | PE evaluation; echo to assess RV |
| New RBBB + V1–V4 T inversions in dyspneic patient | STAT — Urgent | Treat as massive PE; CTA urgently |
| Chronic lateral LV strain + new ST deepening | Urgent | Rule out NSTEMI superimposed; troponin, serial ECG |
| LV strain (chronic), stable | Non-urgent | Document; continue hypertension/AS management |
| RV strain (chronic), stable, known pulmonary HTN | Non-urgent | Document; no new action |
| New lateral T inversions in hypertensive patient | Urgent | Rule out NSTEMI; troponin; compare to prior ECG |

## 6. Reasoning Complexity Analysis

### 6.1 Reasoning Domains Required
- ST-T morphology characterization: asymmetric (strain) vs symmetric (ischemia) T inversion
- Lead distribution: lateral (LV strain) vs right precordial (RV strain)
- Voltage-criteria analysis: confirming LVH as context for LV strain
- Chronicity assessment: chronic vs new (requires prior ECG comparison)
- Acute RV strain recognition: S1Q3T3, new RBBB, V1–V4 T inversions in acute setting
- Clinical integration: blood pressure, valvular disease, lung disease, dyspnea onset, troponin

### 6.2 Feature Dependencies
- Asymmetric T-inversion detection requires accurate waveform shape analysis (slope comparison between ascending and descending T limbs) — currently challenging for automated algorithms
- LVH voltage criteria require accurate amplitude measurement across specific lead combinations
- New vs chronic strain requires prior ECG; without baseline comparison, "new" cannot be confirmed
- Acute PE RV strain requires integration of clinical presentation (dyspnea onset, pleuritic chest pain, risk factors)

### 6.3 Cross-Condition Interactions
- LV strain + NSTEMI: the most clinically challenging interaction; both produce lateral T inversions; troponin and morphology (symmetric vs asymmetric) are the primary differentiators
- Acute RV strain (PE) + chronic RV strain (cor pulmonale): a patient with baseline chronic cor pulmonale who develops PE may show worsening T inversions V1–V4 without meeting "new" criteria because baseline changes already present — comparison to prior ECG is essential
- LBBB + LV strain: LBBB produces ST-T changes identical in distribution to LV strain; the wide QRS differentiates LBBB from LV strain in narrow-QRS hypertensive patients

### 6.4 Reasoning Chain Sketch
**LV Strain:**
1. Detect ST depression + T inversion in lateral leads (I, aVL, V4–V6)
2. Characterize T-wave asymmetry: slow descent vs rapid return (strain) vs symmetric (ischemia)
3. Check QRS width: if <120 ms, LBBB excluded
4. Apply LVH voltage criteria (Sokolow-Lyon, Cornell)
5. Compare to prior ECG: chronic or new
6. Integrate clinical: hypertension history, aortic stenosis, troponin
7. If new + no LVH context + troponin rises → NSTEMI pathway

**RV Strain:**
1. Detect T-wave inversions V1–V4
2. Assess acuity: new vs chronic (prior ECG)
3. Check for S1Q3T3
4. Check for new RBBB
5. Assess QRS width and right axis deviation
6. Integrate clinical: acute dyspnea onset, pleuritic pain, HR, PE risk factors
7. If acute + clinical context → PE pathway

### 6.5 Confidence Anchors
- Asymmetric T inversion lateral leads + LVH voltage + chronic hypertension: high confidence LV strain
- Symmetric T inversion lateral leads + troponin rise + chest pain: high confidence NSTEMI over LV strain
- New T inversions V1–V4 + tachycardia + acute dyspnea: high confidence acute RV strain/PE
- T inversions V1–V4 + right axis deviation + no acute symptoms + COPD history: high confidence chronic RV strain

### 6.6 Difficulty Score

| Dimension | Score (1–5) | Rationale |
|---|---|---|
| Morphology Recognition | 2 | LV strain: visually recognizable pattern with experience. Detection is straightforward |
| Interval Measurement | 2 | ST slope and T-wave asymmetry assessment; no complex intervals |
| Differential Diagnosis | 3 | LV strain vs NSTEMI is clinically critical; RV strain vs ARVC vs other |
| Clinical Integration | 4 | Asymmetric vs symmetric T inversion depends on waveform detail + troponin + BP history |
| Rarity / Prior Probability | 2 | LV strain very common in hypertensive ER population; RV strain common in dyspneic patients |
| Arrhythmia Risk Stratification | 2 | Not directly arrhythmogenic; risk is underlying disease |
| **Overall (detection)** | **2.5** | Detection relatively easy |
| **Overall (ischemia distinction)** | **3.5** | Distinguishing from ischemia requires morphology detail + clinical context |

## 6A. Agent Assignment

**Architecture:** Option E — 3 Parallel Phase 1 Specialists + 1 Sequential CDS (Node 2.1 Gate Passed 2026-03-27)

| Agent | Role for Strain Pattern | Key Features Used |
|-------|--------------------------|-------------------|
| **RRC** (Rhythm/Rate/Conduction) | Not involved | Strain pattern is a repolarization phenomenon; no conduction interval features required |
| **IT** (Ischemia/Territory) | Supporting — must NOT independently classify strain-pattern ST depression as ischemia | ST depression in lateral leads (I, aVL, V5-V6) or right precordial leads (V1-V4) detected as findings; morphology characterization: strain is asymmetric (slow depressed descent, rapid T-wave return — concave ST); ischemia is horizontal or downsloping; IT emits cross_domain_hint when ST depression is detected in leads consistent with strain distribution |
| **MR** (Morphology/Repolarization) | Primary | Asymmetric ST depression with T-wave inversion in leads of high QRS voltage: lateral leads for LVH strain (I, aVL, V5-V6); right precordial leads for RVH strain (V1-V4); ST depression is asymmetric — gradual downslope with rapid T-wave return (opposite of ischemic horizontal/downsloping); high QRS voltage in the same leads (LVH voltage criteria by Sokolow-Lyon/Cornell; RVH criteria by R/S >1 in V1) |
| **CDS** (Cross-Domain Synthesis) | Required — resolves strain vs ischemic ST depression | Receives MR voltage criteria (LVH or RVH confirmed) + IT lateral or right precordial ST depression; if voltage criteria are met AND ST morphology is asymmetric (strain pattern), CDS classifies as LVH/RVH strain and suppresses ischemia flag; if ST morphology is horizontal/downsloping despite hypertrophy, CDS retains and escalates ischemia classification |

### Primary Agent
**MR** — strain pattern is defined by the co-occurrence of high QRS voltage (LVH or RVH criteria) and characteristic asymmetric ST-T morphology (slow descent, rapid T return) in the same leads; both are morphology/repolarization features in the MR domain.

### Cross-Domain Hints
- MR emits: `cross_domain_hint: "Asymmetric ST depression with rapid T-wave return in lateral leads V5-V6/I/aVL co-occurring with LVH voltage criteria (Sokolow-Lyon ≥35mm) — IT must not classify as lateral ischemia; CDS must confirm strain pattern"`
- MR emits (RVH variant): `cross_domain_hint: "Asymmetric ST depression and T-wave inversions in V1-V4 co-occurring with RVH criteria (R/S >1 in V1, R ≥7mm) — IT must not classify as anterior ischemia; CDS must confirm RVH strain pattern"`
- IT emits: `cross_domain_hint: "ST depression detected in lateral or right precordial leads — CDS must determine if co-occurring high voltage criteria (MR) and asymmetric morphology indicate strain vs true ischemia"`

### CDS Specific Role
CDS resolves the most frequent source of false-positive ischemia calls: strain-pattern ST depression in the context of LVH or RVH. It receives MR voltage criteria confirmation (LVH or RVH present) and IT ST depression detection with morphology characterization. Decision logic: if (LVH voltage criteria met AND lateral ST depression with asymmetric morphology) OR (RVH criteria met AND right precordial ST-T changes with asymmetric morphology), CDS classifies as expected strain pattern and suppresses the ischemia flag. If the ST morphology deviates from the expected strain pattern (horizontal ST, downsloping, or ST elevation rather than depression), CDS retains the ischemia classification regardless of the hypertrophy findings.

## 9. Edge Cases and Pitfalls

1. **LV strain masking NSTEMI**: In a hypertensive patient with chronic LV strain, an acute NSTEMI in the same lateral territory may be missed because the baseline already shows T inversions. Key clue: new deepening of T inversions beyond the established strain baseline, symmetric T inversion replacing previously asymmetric strain T waves, troponin rise. Serial ECG comparison and troponin are mandatory in chest pain patients with chronic LV strain.
2. **Asymmetric vs symmetric T inversion — practical limits**: The asymmetric vs symmetric distinction is conceptually important but practically challenging on standard ECGs. The difference can be subtle. Do not rely on morphology alone to exclude ischemia — troponin and clinical context are essential.
3. **New T inversion V1–V4 attributed to RBBB**: In a patient presenting with dyspnea and new RBBB, the T inversions V1–V2 are expected secondary RBBB changes. However, T inversions extending to V3–V4 in the context of new RBBB + dyspnea should not be dismissed — this extension beyond V1–V2 may indicate acute RV strain from PE superimposed on the RBBB pattern.
4. **RV strain in athlete**: Athletes may have mild T inversions in V1–V3 as part of athletic remodeling of the RV (volume overloaded RV from training). This must not be misdiagnosed as chronic cor pulmonale or ARVC. Context: young asymptomatic athlete, normal echo, stable serial ECGs.
5. **LV strain without LVH**: LV strain ST-T changes can occur without meeting voltage criteria for LVH (voltage criteria have ~50% sensitivity). In obese patients, COPD patients, or women (who have lower normal voltage), LVH may exist without voltage criteria. Do not require LVH voltage to diagnose LV strain — strain pattern alone indicates hemodynamic overload.
6. **Overdiagnosis of LV strain in LBBB**: LBBB always produces ST-T changes similar to LV strain in lateral leads. In LBBB, these are expected secondary changes and do NOT represent true strain from hypertrophy. The distinction: LBBB QRS ≥120 ms with concordant ST-T changes is LBBB secondary; narrow QRS with asymmetric lateral T inversions = LV strain. Never diagnose LV strain in the context of LBBB.
7. **Chronic cor pulmonale presenting acutely**: A patient with known COPD and chronic RV strain baseline may present acutely with new dyspnea due to PE. The ECG may show "worsening" of the existing T inversions in V1–V4 rather than entirely new changes. Without baseline ECG for comparison, the acute change may be missed. All COPD patients should have baseline ECG documented.
