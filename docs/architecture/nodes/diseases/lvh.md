## 1. Pathophysiology Root Cause

### 1.1 What Goes Wrong (Anatomy/Physiology)
- Chronic pressure overload (systemic hypertension, aortic stenosis, hypertrophic cardiomyopathy, coarctation of aorta) or volume overload (aortic regurgitation, mitral regurgitation, patent ductus arteriosus) drives compensatory myocardial hypertrophy of the left ventricle
- The LV wall thickens (concentric hypertrophy in pressure overload) or the LV cavity dilates with proportional wall thickening (eccentric hypertrophy in volume overload)
- At the cellular level: increased sarcomere synthesis, myocyte enlargement, and interstitial fibrosis increase total LV mass
- The increased myocardial mass means a larger electrical dipole during depolarization and altered repolarization gradients due to subendocardial ischemia and fibrosis

### 1.2 Electrical Consequence
- **Depolarization**: Greater myocardial mass generates a larger summation vector during ventricular activation, producing higher QRS voltage; the dominant vector is directed leftward and posteriorly (toward the thickened LV free wall)
- **Prolonged depolarization**: Thicker myocardium takes longer to fully depolarize, widening QRS duration (typically still <120ms unless conduction disease coexists)
- **Intrinsicoid deflection delay**: The time from QRS onset to peak R-wave in lateral leads (V5-V6) is prolonged (>0.05s) because the depolarization wavefront takes longer to traverse the thicker LV wall
- **Repolarization**: Subendocardial ischemia from increased oxygen demand and reduced coronary flow reserve reverses the normal repolarization sequence — repolarization now proceeds epicardium-to-endocardium instead of endocardium-to-epicardium, producing ST depression and asymmetric T-wave inversion in leads facing the LV ("strain" pattern)
- **Left atrial abnormality**: Elevated LV end-diastolic pressure transmits backward to the left atrium, causing left atrial enlargement (LAE) and P-mitrale morphology

### 1.3 Why It Appears on ECG
- Leads facing the LV free wall (I, aVL, V5, V6) see a larger approaching depolarization vector → tall R-waves
- Leads facing away from the LV (V1, V2) see the same vector moving away → deep S-waves
- The strain repolarization abnormality produces ST depression and T-wave inversion in leads with tall R-waves (lateral leads), and reciprocal ST elevation with upright T-waves in V1-V3
- The leftward and posterior shift of the mean QRS axis produces left axis deviation in many cases
- LAE appears as broad, notched P-waves in lead II and a deep terminal negative deflection in V1

## 2. ECG Presentation — Lead by Lead

### 2.1 Diagnostic Criteria (2025 AHA/ESC Guidelines)

| Criterion | Threshold | Source |
|-----------|-----------|--------|
| **Sokolow-Lyon voltage** | S(V1) + R(V5 or V6) ≥ 35 mm (3.5 mV) | Sokolow & Lyon 1949; 2022 AHA/ACC/HRS Guideline |
| **Cornell voltage (male)** | R(aVL) + S(V3) > 28 mm | Casale et al. 1985 |
| **Cornell voltage (female)** | R(aVL) + S(V3) > 20 mm | Casale et al. 1985 |
| **Cornell product** | [R(aVL) + S(V3)] × QRS duration > 2440 mm·ms | Molloy et al. 1992 |
| **Romhilt-Estes point score** | ≥5 points = definite LVH; 4 points = probable LVH | Romhilt & Estes 1968 |
| **Peguero-Lo Presti** | Deepest S (any lead) + S(V4) ≥ 28 mm (male), ≥ 23 mm (female) | Peguero et al. 2017 |
| **R(aVL) alone** | > 11 mm | Sokolow-Lyon variant |
| **R(I) + S(III)** | > 25 mm | Gubner-Ungerleider criterion |
| **LV strain pattern** | Asymmetric ST depression + T-wave inversion in leads with tall R-waves | AHA/ACC 2022 |
| **Intrinsicoid deflection** | ≥ 0.05 s in V5 or V6 | Romhilt-Estes component |

**Romhilt-Estes Point Score Breakdown:**

| Feature | Points |
|---------|--------|
| Largest R or S in limb leads ≥ 20 mm, OR S(V1) or S(V2) ≥ 30 mm, OR R(V5) or R(V6) ≥ 30 mm | 3 |
| ST-T strain pattern (without digitalis) | 3 |
| ST-T strain pattern (with digitalis) | 1 |
| Left atrial abnormality (terminal P in V1 ≥ 1 mm deep and ≥ 0.04 s wide) | 3 |
| Left axis deviation ≥ −30° | 2 |
| QRS duration ≥ 0.09 s | 1 |
| Intrinsicoid deflection in V5 or V6 ≥ 0.05 s | 1 |

### 2.2 Lead-by-Lead Manifestation

| Lead | Expected Finding | Why (Vector Explanation) | Sensitivity |
|------|-----------------|------------------------|-------------|
| I | Tall R-wave (often >10 mm); possible ST depression/T inversion if strain present | Lead I faces leftward — LV hypertrophy increases the leftward QRS vector | Moderate |
| II | Normal or mildly tall R-wave; broad notched P-wave (P-mitrale) if LAE present | Lead II has intermediate orientation; LAE prolongs left atrial depolarization | Low for QRS; moderate for P-wave changes |
| III | Normal or small R-wave; deep S-wave possible with LAD | Lead III faces rightward/inferior — the dominant leftward vector projects negatively | Low |
| aVR | Deep QS or rS pattern; ST elevation if lateral strain present (reciprocal) | aVR faces rightward — opposite to LV vector; reciprocal to lateral strain | Low (reciprocal changes only) |
| aVL | Tall R-wave (often >11 mm) — key lead for Cornell criteria; ST depression + asymmetric T inversion with strain | aVL faces the high lateral LV wall directly; strain pattern reflects subendocardial ischemia | High — aVL R-wave alone >11 mm is a diagnostic criterion |
| aVF | Normal or slightly tall R-wave; variable T-wave changes | aVF faces inferiorly — variable LV projection depending on heart position | Low |
| V1 | Deep S-wave (often >25-30 mm); possible ST elevation (reciprocal to lateral strain); terminal negative P-wave if LAE | V1 faces rightward/anterior — the large posterior-leftward LV vector moves directly away from V1 | High — essential for Sokolow-Lyon (S component) |
| V2 | Deep S-wave (often >25 mm); possible early transition with tall R if septal hypertrophy | V2 is anterior — still sees the large LV vector moving away | Moderate-High |
| V3 | Transitional: decreasing S-wave depth, increasing R-wave; used in Cornell (S component) | V3 is the transition zone — S(V3) reflects posterior LV forces | High — essential for Cornell criteria |
| V4 | Increasing R-wave amplitude; S-wave used in Peguero-Lo Presti; possible ST-T changes | V4 begins to face the LV — R-wave grows as the vector approaches; S(V4) captures deep posterior forces | High — key for Peguero-Lo Presti |
| V5 | Tall R-wave (often >25-30 mm); ST depression + asymmetric T-wave inversion with strain | V5 directly faces the LV free wall — maximum R-wave amplitude; strain appears here early | High — essential for Sokolow-Lyon (R component) |
| V6 | Tall R-wave (slightly less than V5); ST depression + asymmetric T-wave inversion with strain; prolonged intrinsicoid deflection | V6 faces the lateral LV wall; strain pattern most classically described here | High — essential for Sokolow-Lyon (R component) |

### 2.3 Key Leads (Most Diagnostic)
- **V1**: Provides the deep S-wave for Sokolow-Lyon criterion
- **V5-V6**: Provide the tall R-wave for Sokolow-Lyon; show classic strain pattern earliest; intrinsicoid deflection measured here
- **aVL**: Single most informative limb lead — R(aVL) >11 mm alone suggests LVH; essential for Cornell criteria
- **V3**: Essential for Cornell voltage criteria (S component)
- **V4**: Essential for Peguero-Lo Presti criteria (S component)
- **V1 (P-wave terminal)**: Shows LAE — a supporting criterion in Romhilt-Estes

### 2.4 Beat-by-Beat Considerations
- LVH is a CONSTANT structural finding — voltage and strain pattern are present on every beat (no beat-to-beat variability expected)
- QRS voltage may vary slightly with respiration (especially in emphysema patients), but the pattern is stable
- If beat-to-beat voltage variation is seen, consider pericardial effusion (electrical alternans) rather than LVH
- Aggregate/average measurement of QRS amplitude across multiple beats provides the most reliable assessment
- PVCs in LVH patients may show exaggerated QRS amplitude and should NOT be used for voltage criteria

## 3. Morphology Details (What the Agent Must See)

### 3.1 P-wave Changes
- **Morphology**: Broad, notched ("M-shaped") P-wave in lead II (P-mitrale) when LAE coexists; deep terminal negative deflection in V1 (≥1 mm deep, ≥40 ms wide)
- **Duration change**: Prolonged >120 ms in lead II (reflects LAE)
- **Axis change**: Normal P-wave axis (0° to +75°); leftward shift if significant LAE

### 3.2 PR Interval Changes
- **Duration**: Usually normal (120-200 ms); may be mildly prolonged if conduction system fibrosis present (LVH with first-degree AV block)
- **Pattern**: Constant

### 3.3 QRS Complex Changes
- **Duration**: Normal to mildly prolonged (80-110 ms); if ≥120 ms, consider coexisting LBBB or IVCD (LVH voltage criteria become unreliable in LBBB)
- **Morphology**: Tall R-waves in V5-V6, I, aVL; deep S-waves in V1-V3; normal activation sequence preserved (unlike bundle branch blocks); may show notching or slurring of QRS in lateral leads with severe hypertrophy
- **Amplitude**: INCREASED — this is the hallmark of LVH; see Section 2.1 for specific thresholds
- **Axis**: Normal to left axis deviation (−30° to 0° common; frank LAD beyond −30° in ~15-20% of LVH)
- **Intrinsicoid deflection**: Delayed ≥0.05 s in V5-V6 (time from QRS onset to peak R-wave)

### 3.4 ST Segment Changes
- **Direction**: Depression in leads with tall R-waves (I, aVL, V5-V6); elevation in leads with deep S-waves (V1-V3, aVR) — the "reciprocal" pattern
- **Morphology**: Downsloping ST depression in lateral leads (characteristic of strain); concave-up morphology in V1-V3 when reciprocal elevation is present
- **Measurement point**: J-point for ST elevation/depression; J+60ms for strain pattern assessment
- **Gender/lead-specific thresholds**: Strain-pattern ST depression is typically 1-3 mm in lateral leads; no specific gender threshold for strain (unlike STEMI criteria)

### 3.5 T-wave Changes
- **Direction**: Inverted in lateral leads (I, aVL, V5-V6) when strain is present; upright and possibly tall in V1-V3 (reciprocal to lateral inversion)
- **Amplitude**: Variable; typically 3-8 mm inverted in lateral leads with significant strain
- **Symmetry**: ASYMMETRIC — this is the defining feature of LVH strain: gradual downsloping ST depression leading into the inverted T-wave, with a sharp rapid return to baseline. Symmetric T-wave inversion suggests ischemia, NOT strain
- **Specific patterns**: "LV strain" pattern = asymmetric ST depression + T-wave inversion convex upward in leads with tall R-waves; the asymmetry distinguishes it from ischemic T-wave inversion (which is symmetric)

### 3.6 QT/QTc Changes
- **QTc**: May be mildly prolonged (440-480 ms) due to prolonged repolarization in hypertrophied myocardium
- **Clinical significance**: QTc prolongation in LVH is associated with increased risk of sudden cardiac death and ventricular arrhythmias
- **Torsades de Pointes risk**: Mildly increased, especially if QTc > 500 ms or if QT-prolonging drugs are added

### 3.7 Other Features
- **U-waves**: May be present in lateral leads; exaggerated U-waves with coexistent hypokalemia
- **Poor R-wave progression**: NOT expected in LVH (this suggests anterior infarction or COPD); LVH should show PROMINENT R-wave progression in V5-V6
- **R-wave regression V5 to V6**: R(V5) > R(V6) is typical; if R(V6) > R(V5), consider normal variant
- **Pseudo-infarct pattern**: Deep S-waves in V1-V3 (from LVH) can mimic poor R-wave progression of anterior MI; however, LVH shows very deep S-waves, not absent R-waves

## 4. Differential Diagnosis

### 4.1 Mimics (What Looks Like This But Isn't)

| Mimic Condition | Shared Features | Distinguishing Features |
|----------------|-----------------|----------------------|
| Normal young thin male | High QRS voltage in precordial leads | No strain pattern; normal ST-T segments; age <35; thin body habitus; no LAE; QRS duration normal |
| Athlete's heart | Increased voltage, sometimes repolarization changes | Early repolarization pattern (not strain); tall symmetric T-waves (not inverted); regression with detraining; no LAE |
| LBBB | Wide QRS, ST-T changes in lateral leads | QRS ≥120 ms with typical LBBB morphology (rS in V1, broad R in V6); ST-T are "appropriately discordant" to QRS, not strain pattern |
| Anterior STEMI (reciprocal) | ST depression in lateral leads | ST depression is dynamic (evolves); associated with ST elevation in V1-V4; troponin elevation; clinical context of acute chest pain |
| Ischemic T-wave inversions | T-wave inversions in lateral leads | SYMMETRIC deep T-wave inversions (unlike asymmetric LVH strain); often dynamic; no increased voltage; clinical context |
| Wellens syndrome | T-wave inversions in precordial leads | Biphasic or deep symmetric T-waves in V2-V3 (not lateral leads); normal voltage; pain-free interval pattern |
| Digitalis effect | ST depression, T-wave changes | "Sagging" ST depression (Salvador Dali mustache); diffuse (not limited to leads with tall R); medication history |
| Hypertrophic cardiomyopathy (HCM) | Increased voltage, repolarization abnormalities | May have deep narrow Q-waves in lateral/inferior leads; extremely high voltages; dagger-shaped septal Q-waves; often younger patients |

### 4.2 Coexisting Conditions
- **LVH + acute ischemia/STEMI**: Strain pattern can mask or mimic ST elevation/depression of ACS. Key: acute changes are DYNAMIC (evolving over minutes-hours), while LVH strain is STATIC. Serial ECGs are critical. New ST elevation discordant to the expected strain pattern suggests superimposed ACS.
- **LVH + LBBB**: Voltage criteria for LVH are unreliable in LBBB because LBBB itself produces increased voltage and altered ST-T. Only Cornell voltage criteria retain some utility in LBBB. Romhilt-Estes and Sokolow-Lyon should NOT be applied in LBBB.
- **LVH + atrial fibrillation**: AFib is common in LVH due to LAE; rate-controlled AFib does not change LVH voltage interpretation, but rapid ventricular response may alter ST-T segments independent of strain
- **LVH + electrolyte abnormalities**: Hypokalemia can exaggerate ST depression and T-wave changes, mimicking worsened strain; hyperkalemia can reduce QRS voltage, masking LVH criteria

## 5. STAT Classification

| Priority | Criteria |
|----------|----------|
| **STAT** | No — LVH alone is NOT a STAT condition. However, LVH with NEW ST-T changes suggesting superimposed ischemia IS STAT |
| **Time-sensitive** | Not acutely; LVH is a chronic structural finding. However, new LVH strain that was not present on prior ECG suggests progressive hypertrophy and warrants cardiology follow-up |
| **Clinical action** | Blood pressure evaluation and management; echocardiography to confirm LV mass and function; evaluation for secondary causes (aortic stenosis, renal artery stenosis, pheochromocytoma); long-term risk stratification for sudden cardiac death |

### 6.1 Reasoning Domains Required to Detect This Condition
- **Voltage measurement**: Precise amplitude measurement of R-waves and S-waves across multiple leads (primary domain)
- **Morphology analysis**: Assessment of ST-T pattern (strain vs ischemia vs normal variant) — requires shape recognition (asymmetric vs symmetric)
- **Axis calculation**: Mean QRS axis determination for Romhilt-Estes score (LAD component)
- **P-wave analysis**: Terminal P-wave morphology in V1 for LAE assessment (Romhilt-Estes component)
- **Interval measurement**: QRS duration (for Cornell product and Romhilt-Estes); intrinsicoid deflection time in V5-V6
- **Lead-group correlation**: Voltage criteria require combining measurements from DIFFERENT leads (V1 + V5 for Sokolow-Lyon; aVL + V3 for Cornell)
- **Sex-specific thresholds**: Cornell voltage and Peguero-Lo Presti have different cutoffs for males and females
- **Cross-domain reasoning**: Required — must integrate voltage + morphology + axis + P-wave + intervals to compute Romhilt-Estes score; must differentiate strain from ischemia using morphology + clinical context

### 6.2 Feature Dependencies
- **ESSENTIAL features**: QRS amplitude in V1, V2, V3, V4, V5, V6, I, aVL (specific leads for specific criteria); ST-T morphology in V5-V6, I, aVL (strain detection); QRS duration (Cornell product)
- **SUPPORTING features**: P-wave terminal force in V1 (LAE); mean QRS axis (LAD); intrinsicoid deflection time; QTc interval
- **EXCLUSION features**: QRS duration ≥120 ms with LBBB morphology → most voltage criteria become unreliable; very low voltage throughout → consider pericardial effusion, COPD, or obesity rather than LVH
- **Per-beat vs aggregate**: Aggregate (multi-beat average amplitude) preferred for voltage; per-beat not required since LVH is a constant structural finding

### 6.3 Cross-Condition Interactions
- **LVH masks STEMI**: LV strain produces baseline ST depression in lateral leads and reciprocal ST elevation in V1-V3, which can mask true anterior STEMI or mimic lateral ischemia. The system must flag when LVH is present alongside new/dynamic ST changes.
- **LVH mimics ischemia**: Strain pattern closely resembles non-ST-elevation ACS. The key differentiator (asymmetric vs symmetric T-wave inversion) must be explicitly evaluated.
- **LBBB invalidates most LVH criteria**: If LBBB is detected, the system must suppress Sokolow-Lyon and Romhilt-Estes and rely only on Cornell criteria with reduced confidence.
- **LVH + WPW**: Delta waves can increase voltage, producing false-positive LVH. The system must detect WPW first and flag LVH voltage criteria as unreliable.
- **LVH + RVH**: Biventricular hypertrophy can cancel out voltage (opposing vectors), producing deceptively normal or low voltage. Look for Katz-Wachtel sign (large biphasic RS complexes in V3-V4).

### 6.4 Reasoning Chain Sketch
**Minimum reasoning chain (single criterion):**
1. Measure S-wave amplitude in V1
2. Measure R-wave amplitude in V5 (or V6, whichever is taller)
3. Sum them → if ≥35 mm → LVH (Sokolow-Lyon)

**Full reasoning chain (comprehensive assessment):**
1. Measure QRS amplitudes in all 12 leads
2. Apply Sokolow-Lyon criterion: S(V1) + R(V5 or V6) ≥ 35 mm
3. Apply Cornell voltage criterion: R(aVL) + S(V3) > 28 mm (male) or > 20 mm (female) — requires sex input
4. Measure QRS duration → compute Cornell product: [R(aVL) + S(V3)] × QRS duration → compare to 2440 mm·ms
5. Apply Peguero-Lo Presti: find deepest S in any lead + S(V4) → ≥28 mm (male) or ≥23 mm (female)
6. Compute Romhilt-Estes score: voltage (3 pts) + strain (3 or 1 pts) + LAE (3 pts) + LAD (2 pts) + QRS duration (1 pt) + intrinsicoid deflection (1 pt)
7. Assess ST-T morphology in V5-V6, I, aVL: strain pattern present? (asymmetric ST depression + T inversion)
8. Check for LAE in V1 and lead II
9. Calculate mean QRS axis → LAD?
10. Check for confounders: LBBB? Obesity? COPD? Thin habitus? Age?
11. Synthesize: how many criteria met? Strain present? → classify as definite/probable/possible LVH with or without strain

### 6.5 Confidence Anchors
- **HIGH confidence**: Multiple voltage criteria met (≥3 systems positive) PLUS strain pattern present PLUS LAE present → definite LVH
- **MODERATE confidence**: Single voltage criterion met with strain or LAE → probable LVH
- **LOW confidence**: Single voltage criterion met without strain in a young, thin patient → possible LVH (may be normal variant)
- **Pathognomonic combination**: Sokolow-Lyon positive + Cornell positive + strain pattern + LAE = near-100% specific for LVH (but still not 100% sensitive for LVH — echo is gold standard)
- **Absent features that LOWER confidence**: Normal voltage in ALL criteria systems (very unlikely to be LVH); symmetric T-wave inversions (suggests ischemia, not strain); QRS ≥120 ms with LBBB (unreliable criteria)

| Dimension | Score (1-5) | Notes |
|-----------|-------------|-------|
| Signal clarity needed | 2 | Voltage measurement is relatively robust to noise; strain morphology needs moderate signal quality |
| Number of leads required | 4 | Requires multiple specific leads (V1, V3, V4, V5, V6, aVL at minimum); cannot be detected from a single lead |
| Cross-domain reasoning | 4 | Voltage + morphology + axis + P-wave + intervals + sex-specific thresholds; Romhilt-Estes requires 6 sub-assessments |

| **IT** (Ischemia/Territory) | Supporting — must NOT independently classify lateral strain ST depression as ischemia | ST depression and T-wave inversion in lateral leads (I, aVL, V5-V6) present in LVH strain pattern; IT flags these but must defer to CDS for ischemia vs strain determination |

| **CDS** (Cross-Domain Synthesis) | Required — resolves LVH strain vs lateral ischemia | Integrates MR voltage criteria with IT lateral ST findings; if MR confirms LVH by any voltage criterion AND ST depression in lateral leads is asymmetric (slow descent, rapid return — strain morphology), CDS suppresses IT lateral ischemia flag and classifies as LVH strain; if ST depression is horizontal or downsloping in lateral leads despite LVH, CDS retains ischemia consideration |

### Primary Agent
**MR** — LVH diagnosis is driven by voltage amplitude measurements across multiple criteria (Sokolow-Lyon, Cornell, Peguero-Lo Presti, Romhilt-Estes) and the identification of repolarization strain pattern; all are morphology/repolarization domain features.

### Cross-Domain Hints
- MR emits: `cross_domain_hint: "LVH voltage criteria met (specify which: Sokolow-Lyon ≥35mm / Cornell >28mm men or >20mm women / Peguero-Lo Presti ≥23mm men) — IT must not classify asymmetric lateral ST depression as ischemia without CDS adjudication; CDS must resolve strain vs ischemia"`
- IT emits: `cross_domain_hint: "ST depression with T-wave inversion in I, aVL, V5-V6 detected — CDS must determine if this represents LVH strain (asymmetric morphology + MR voltage criteria present) or true lateral ischemia"`

### CDS Specific Role
CDS is critical for LVH because lateral strain pattern (ST depression + T-wave inversion in I, aVL, V5-V6) is the most common LVH mimic of lateral ischemia. CDS receives MR voltage criteria findings and IT lateral ST measurements. Decision logic: if MR confirms LVH (any major criterion met) AND IT lateral ST morphology is asymmetric (gradually descending ST with rapid T-wave inversion return — the classic strain morphology), CDS classifies as LVH strain and suppresses lateral ischemia. If ST depression is horizontal, downsloping, or with symmetric T-wave inversion despite LVH, CDS retains and escalates the ischemia flag. CDS also handles the LBBB-in-LVH scenario: if RRC detects LBBB alongside MR LVH, CDS applies full Sgarbossa analysis for ischemia determination.

## 9. Edge Cases and Pitfalls

- **Obesity/large body habitus**: Increased chest wall thickness attenuates QRS voltage → false NEGATIVE for all voltage criteria. ECG sensitivity for LVH drops to <10% in obese patients. The system should flag: "Voltage criteria may underestimate LVH in obese patients."
- **COPD/hyperinflated lungs**: Air trapping increases the distance between heart and electrodes → reduced voltage and rightward axis shift → false negative for LVH and potential false positive for RVH. Low voltage + right axis should prompt consideration of COPD rather than absence of LVH.
- **Thin, young patients (<35 years)**: Normal high voltage in thin chest wall → false POSITIVE. Sokolow-Lyon has a false-positive rate of ~15-20% in healthy young males. The system should increase the specificity threshold or weight Cornell/Peguero-Lo Presti criteria more heavily in this demographic.
- **Women**: Lower normal voltage ranges mean standard Sokolow-Lyon (≥35 mm) is less sensitive for women. Cornell criteria address this with a lower female threshold (>20 mm). The system must use sex-adjusted thresholds.
- **LVH + LBBB**: LBBB itself produces high voltage and ST-T changes that overlap with LVH criteria. Sokolow-Lyon and Romhilt-Estes become unreliable. Only Cornell criteria retain partial validity. The system should report: "LVH assessment limited by LBBB."
- **LVH + RVH (biventricular hypertrophy)**: Opposing vectors may cancel, producing NORMAL voltage despite significant hypertrophy of both ventricles. Look for Katz-Wachtel sign (large equiphasic RS complexes in mid-precordial leads) and mixed criteria (tall R in V5-V6 + tall R in V1).
- **LVH + prior anterior MI**: Loss of anterior forces (Q-waves in V1-V4) may reduce or eliminate the deep S-waves in V1-V2, weakening Sokolow-Lyon. Tall R in V5-V6 with Q-waves in V1-V3 should raise suspicion for LVH + old anterior MI.
- **Electrode misplacement**: Particularly V1-V2 placed too high (intercostal spaces 2-3 instead of 4) → exaggerated S-wave depth → false positive for Sokolow-Lyon. If S(V1) is dramatically deeper than S(V2), consider electrode misplacement.
- **LVH strain vs acute ischemia**: The most dangerous pitfall. Both produce ST depression + T-wave inversion in lateral leads. Key differentiators: (1) Strain is ASYMMETRIC (gradual downslope, rapid upstroke); ischemia is SYMMETRIC. (2) Strain is STATIC on serial ECGs; ischemia is DYNAMIC. (3) Strain occurs in leads with the tallest R-waves; ischemia may occur in any territory. (4) Strain does not produce reciprocal ST elevation in a territorial pattern; STEMI does. The system must flag: "LVH strain present — if clinical suspicion for ACS, serial ECGs and troponin recommended."
- **Digitalis use**: Digitalis produces ST depression ("reverse check mark" or "scooped" ST) that can mimic or exaggerate strain pattern. Medication history is critical. Romhilt-Estes assigns only 1 point (instead of 3) for ST-T changes if patient is on digitalis.
