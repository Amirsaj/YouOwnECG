## 1. Pathophysiology Root Cause

### 1.1 What Goes Wrong (Anatomy/Physiology)
- Acute thrombotic occlusion of the right coronary artery (RCA) in ~85% of cases (right-dominant circulation) or the left circumflex artery (LCx) in ~15% of cases (left-dominant circulation). Approximately 85% of the population is right-dominant (RCA supplies the PDA), 8% is left-dominant (LCx supplies the PDA), and 7% is co-dominant.
- The inferior wall (diaphragmatic surface) of the left ventricle is the territory at risk. This wall rests on the diaphragm and comprises the basal and mid-inferior segments.
- At the cellular level, transmural ischemia occurs within minutes of occlusion. Aerobic metabolism ceases in the subendocardium first, progressing transmurally. ATP depletion opens K_ATP channels, shortening the action potential in ischemic cells. Intracellular K+ leaks out, Na+ and Ca2+ accumulate intracellularly, creating a voltage gradient between ischemic and non-ischemic tissue — the injury current.
- Proximal RCA occlusion carries additional risk: the SA node artery (supplied by RCA in 60% of patients) and the AV node artery (supplied by RCA in 90% of right-dominant patients) may both be compromised, leading to conduction disturbances. The RV marginal branches supply the right ventricle, making RV infarction a hallmark of proximal RCA disease.

### 1.2 Electrical Consequence
- Transmural ischemia creates an injury current that flows from the ischemic zone (relatively negative at rest due to partial depolarization) toward healthy myocardium during diastole, and reverses during the ST segment (systolic phase). This manifests as ST elevation over the injured zone.
- The injury vector points inferiorly and slightly rightward (RCA) or inferiorly and leftward (LCx), directly toward leads II, III, and aVF.
- Repolarization is altered: the ischemic zone repolarizes earlier than normal (shortened action potential), producing the ST-segment shift. Initially, T-waves become hyperacute (tall, broad, symmetric) as a very early sign. As transmural injury evolves, the ST segment elevates with a convex (tombstone) or concave-up morphology.
- Over hours, Q-waves develop as the necrotic tissue becomes electrically silent — the depolarization wavefront is "seen through" the dead tissue as a negative deflection (Q-wave = window theory).

### 1.3 Why It Appears on ECG
- The injury current vector points inferiorly (toward the feet). Leads II, III, and aVF have their positive poles oriented inferiorly, so they record ST elevation.
- Lead III is oriented more rightward than Lead II. In RCA occlusion, the injury vector is inferior AND rightward → ST elevation is greater in III than in II. In LCx occlusion, the vector is inferior AND leftward → ST elevation is greater in II than in III (or equal).
- Leads I and aVL face the lateral and superior-left direction — almost directly opposite to the inferior injury vector — producing reciprocal ST depression. This reciprocal depression in I and aVL is the single most sensitive early marker of inferior STEMI.
- Precordial leads V1-V3 face anteriorly; they may show reciprocal ST depression. If posterior wall extension exists (via posterior descending artery or posterolateral branches), V1-V3 depression deepens and represents true posterior injury seen as a mirror image.

## 2. ECG Presentation — Lead by Lead

### 2.1 Diagnostic Criteria (2025 AHA/ESC Guidelines)

| Criterion | Threshold | Source |
|-----------|-----------|--------|
| ST elevation in inferior leads | >=1 mm (0.1 mV) at J-point in >=2 contiguous leads (II, III, aVF) | 2023 ESC STEMI Guidelines, reaffirmed 2025 |
| New or presumably new LBBB | With clinical suspicion, treat as STEMI equivalent | AHA/ACC 2023 |
| Reciprocal ST depression | Present in I and/or aVL (increases specificity to >95%) | Smith et al., 2024 |
| ST elevation in V4R | >=0.5 mm (>=1 mm in men <30 yr) confirms RV infarction | ESC 2023 |
| Posterior MI extension | ST depression V1-V3 with dominant R-wave, or ST elevation >=0.5 mm in V7-V9 | 2025 AHA universal MI definition update |
| Hyperacute T-waves (earliest sign) | Tall, broad-based, symmetric T-waves in II, III, aVF preceding ST elevation | Nikus et al., 2024 |

### 2.2 Lead-by-Lead Manifestation

| Lead | Expected Finding | Why (Vector Explanation) | Sensitivity |
|------|-----------------|------------------------|-------------|
| I | Reciprocal ST depression (0.5-2 mm), may see terminal T-wave inversion | Lead I faces leftward (0 degrees); inferior injury vector is nearly perpendicular-to-opposite to Lead I. RCA occlusion (rightward vector) produces more depression in I than LCx. | 70-80% for RCA; 40% for LCx |
| II | ST elevation >=1 mm, often with hyperacute T-waves | Lead II positive pole at +60 degrees — directly aligned with inferior injury vector. In LCx occlusion, II elevation >= III. | 90-95% |
| III | ST elevation >=1 mm, typically greatest in RCA occlusion | Lead III at +120 degrees — rightward-inferior orientation best captures RCA injury vector (inferior + rightward). III > II is the hallmark of RCA. | 90-95% (most prominent lead in RCA) |
| aVR | Isoelectric or mild ST depression (0.5-1 mm) | aVR at -150 degrees is roughly opposite to the inferior leads; reciprocal effect is modest. Significant elevation in aVR should prompt consideration of left main or LAD disease. | N/A (non-diagnostic) |
| aVL | Reciprocal ST depression (most sensitive reciprocal sign, often 1-3 mm), may exceed inferior elevation in magnitude | aVL at -30 degrees is the most directly opposite lead to III (+120 degrees). The reciprocal depression in aVL frequently appears BEFORE the inferior ST elevation becomes obvious. | 90-95% (most sensitive reciprocal indicator) |
| aVF | ST elevation >=1 mm, hyperacute T-waves | aVF at +90 degrees — purely inferior orientation, captures both RCA and LCx vectors. Elevation typically intermediate between II and III. | 90-95% |
| V1 | Variable: reciprocal ST depression (common) OR ST elevation if RV involvement | V1 faces the septum/RV. Depression = reciprocal to inferior/posterior injury. Elevation = direct RV injury from proximal RCA occlusion. ST elevation in V1 mandates V4R to confirm RV STEMI. | 50-60% for RV involvement detection |
| V2 | Reciprocal ST depression (0.5-3 mm) | V2 faces anterior-septal region, opposite to the posterior wall. Depression deepens if posterior extension is present (mirror image of posterior injury). | 60-70% for posterior extension |
| V3 | Reciprocal ST depression (0.5-2 mm), may transition to isoelectric | V3 is a transitional lead; depression is common but less pronounced than V1-V2. Persistent deep depression suggests posterior extension. | 50-60% for posterior extension |
| V4 | Usually isoelectric or mild ST depression | V4 faces the anterior wall/apex, largely perpendicular to the inferior vector. Usually minimal changes unless widespread extension. | Low |
| V5 | Mild ST depression or mild elevation depending on lateral extension | V5 faces the low lateral wall. LCx occlusion may produce ST elevation here (lateral extension). RCA occlusion usually shows no change or mild depression. | 30-40% for LCx lateral extension |
| V6 | Similar to V5 — mild depression (RCA) or elevation (LCx with lateral extension) | V6 at the lateral apex; LCx-dominant inferior STEMI may show concurrent lateral involvement with elevation here. | 30-40% for LCx lateral extension |

### 2.3 Key Leads (Most Diagnostic)
- **Primary diagnostic leads**: II, III, aVF (ST elevation — at least 2 of 3 required)
- **Most sensitive early sign**: Reciprocal ST depression in aVL — often appears before inferior ST elevation reaches threshold. An isolated ST depression in aVL in an acute chest pain patient should trigger immediate evaluation for evolving inferior STEMI.
- **Culprit artery differentiation**: III vs II magnitude comparison (III > II = RCA; II >= III = LCx)
- **Extension detection**: V1 (RV involvement), V1-V3 (posterior extension), V5-V6 (lateral extension)
- **Must-obtain supplementary lead**: V4R — any patient with inferior ST elevation must have right-sided leads to evaluate for RV infarction

### 2.4 Beat-by-Beat Considerations
- The ST elevation is typically constant across beats in established STEMI (unlike Prinzmetal angina where it may be transient).
- In the earliest hyperacute phase (first 10-30 minutes), the T-wave changes may fluctuate beat to beat as the ischemic border zone is unstable.
- If atrial fibrillation develops (uncommon but possible with RCA/atrial branch occlusion), beat-to-beat ST variation may occur due to varying diastolic filling times — measure ST in beats with moderate R-R intervals.
- Wenckebach periodicity (Mobitz I) may cause cyclical variation in R-R intervals; the ST elevation persists across all conducted beats regardless of the PR prolongation.
- Post-PVC beats may show exaggerated ST changes (post-ectopic T-wave memory); exclude PVCs from ST measurement.

## 3. Morphology Details (What the Agent Must See)

### 3.1 P-wave Changes
- Morphology: Usually normal early on. If SA node ischemia occurs (proximal RCA), sinus bradycardia or sinus arrest may produce absent P-waves with junctional escape rhythm.
- Duration change: Normal unless atrial infarction coexists (rare — produces PR elevation in aVR, PR depression in inferior leads).
- Axis change: Normal unless junctional rhythm takes over (retrograde P-waves with abnormal axis, typically inverted in II, III, aVF).

### 3.2 PR Interval Changes
- Duration: May be prolonged (first-degree AV block) due to AV node ischemia — occurs in 10-15% of inferior STEMI.
- Pattern: Progressive PR prolongation with dropped beats (Mobitz Type I / Wenckebach) is common and characteristic of inferior MI — occurs in 10-12% of cases. This is usually transient and benign, reflecting AV node ischemia rather than His-Purkinje disease.
- PR depression in inferior leads may suggest concurrent atrial injury or pericarditis (though pericarditis would not show reciprocal ST changes).

### 3.3 QRS Complex Changes
- Duration: Narrow (< 120 ms) in uncomplicated inferior STEMI. Widening suggests bundle branch block (RBBB may develop with extensive septal ischemia from proximal RCA or large inferior MI involving the septum).
- Morphology: Pathologic Q-waves develop in II, III, aVF as necrosis progresses — typically >=30 ms wide and/or >=1/3 the R-wave amplitude. Q-waves may appear within 2-6 hours of onset. Loss of R-wave progression in these leads evolves to QS complexes in completed infarcts.
- Amplitude: R-wave amplitude decreases in inferior leads as myocardium becomes electrically silent. A new dominant R-wave in V1-V2 may appear if posterior extension creates a "mirror Q-wave."
- Axis: May shift leftward (left axis deviation) if there is concurrent left anterior fascicular block from septal ischemia, though this is more characteristic of LAD occlusion.

### 3.4 ST Segment Changes
- Direction: Elevation in II, III, aVF; reciprocal depression in I, aVL, V1-V3.
- Morphology: Initially concave-upward ("smiley face") in early hours, evolving to convex ("tombstone" or "frowny face") as infarction progresses. Tombstone morphology indicates massive transmural injury and portends larger infarct size and worse prognosis.
- Measurement point: J-point measurement is standard. The J-point is where the QRS complex ends and the ST segment begins. Measure ST deviation relative to the TP segment baseline (or PR segment if TP is obscured by tachycardia).
- Gender/lead-specific thresholds: The >=1 mm threshold applies to both sexes for inferior leads (unlike anterior leads where gender-specific thresholds exist for V2-V3). In patients <40 years old, up to 1 mm of ST elevation in III can be a normal variant — clinical correlation and reciprocal changes become critical discriminators.

### 3.5 T-wave Changes
- Direction: Initially hyperacute (tall, upright, broad-based) — this is the earliest ECG sign, preceding ST elevation. As infarction evolves, T-waves invert in the affected leads (II, III, aVF) over 24-72 hours. Terminal T-wave inversion indicates completed infarction with reperfusion.
- Amplitude: Hyperacute T-waves may reach 5-10 mm in height, disproportionately tall relative to the QRS complex. The T-wave/QRS amplitude ratio >0.36 in inferior leads suggests hyperacute phase.
- Symmetry: Hyperacute T-waves are broad-based and symmetric (unlike the normal asymmetric T-wave). Deep, symmetric T-wave inversions in the post-infarct phase indicate completed transmural injury with reperfusion.
- Specific patterns: Wellens-type biphasic T-waves are NOT characteristic of inferior STEMI (they suggest LAD territory). De Winter T-waves are also LAD-specific. The key T-wave pattern here is: hyperacute (earliest) -> ST elevation with upright T (acute) -> ST normalizing with T-wave inversion (evolving) -> Q-waves with isoelectric ST and upright T (chronic).

### 3.6 QT/QTc Changes
- QTc may prolong during acute ischemia (typically 440-500 ms) due to delayed repolarization in the ischemic zone.
- Persistent QTc prolongation >500 ms post-MI increases risk of ventricular arrhythmia, but this is not specific to inferior STEMI.
- Torsades de Pointes risk is elevated in the setting of bradycardia (common in inferior MI) + QT prolongation + hypokalemia — a dangerous triad. Monitor K+ and Mg2+ aggressively.

### 3.7 Other Features
- Osborn waves (J-waves): Not expected unless the patient is hypothermic (confounder to exclude).
- U-waves: Not a feature of inferior STEMI. If present, consider hypokalemia as a comorbidity.
- ST elevation in V4R >=0.5 mm: Pathognomonic for RV infarction in the context of inferior STEMI. This finding has >90% sensitivity and >80% specificity for RV involvement. V4R elevation is transient — may resolve within 10 hours of onset — making early acquisition critical.
- Posterior leads (V7-V9): ST elevation >=0.5 mm confirms posterior wall extension. Lower threshold than standard leads because the electrodes are farther from the injury zone.

## 4. Differential Diagnosis

### 4.1 Mimics (What Looks Like This But Isn't)

| Mimic Condition | Shared Features | Distinguishing Features |
|----------------|-----------------|----------------------|
| Acute pericarditis | ST elevation in inferior leads, chest pain | Diffuse ST elevation (not limited to a coronary territory), PR depression in II/III/aVF with PR elevation in aVR, concave-up morphology in all leads, NO reciprocal ST depression (critical differentiator), friction rub, pleuritic pain worsened by lying flat |
| Pulmonary embolism | ST changes in III and aVF, RV strain | S1Q3T3 pattern, sinus tachycardia (not bradycardia), right axis deviation, RBBB pattern, T-wave inversions in V1-V4 (RV strain), no reciprocal depression in I/aVL (Lead I shows deep S-wave instead), clinical context of DVT risk |
| Benign early repolarization (BER) | ST elevation in inferior leads, young patient | Notched or slurred J-point ("fishhook"), concordant T-waves (ST and T same direction), no reciprocal changes, stable on serial ECGs, normal troponin |
| Normal variant Q-waves in Lead III | Q-waves in III can mimic old inferior MI | Q-wave in III disappears or diminishes with deep inspiration, no Q-waves in II or aVF, no associated ST/T changes |
| Left ventricular hypertrophy (LVH) | ST changes in inferior leads possible (strain pattern) | Voltage criteria for LVH (SV1+RV5 >35mm), asymmetric ST depression/T-wave inversion (strain pattern) rather than elevation, reciprocal pattern not matching coronary territory |
| Myocarditis | ST elevation, troponin elevation, chest pain | Often diffuse or non-territorial ST changes, younger patients, viral prodrome, may mimic any STEMI territory but typically without clean reciprocal changes |
| Takotsubo (stress cardiomyopathy) | ST elevation, chest pain, troponin elevation | Typically apical ballooning pattern (anterior > inferior), emotional/physical stressor, post-menopausal women, wall motion abnormality extends beyond a single coronary territory |

### 4.2 Coexisting Conditions
- **Inferior STEMI + LBBB**: LBBB distorts ST segments and makes STEMI diagnosis extremely difficult. Apply modified Sgarbossa criteria (Smith modification): concordant ST elevation >=1 mm in any lead, concordant ST depression >=1 mm in V1-V3, or discordant ST elevation with ST/S ratio >=0.25 in any lead. Standard inferior STEMI criteria are unreliable in LBBB.
- **Inferior STEMI + Atrial Fibrillation**: AF with rapid ventricular response may cause rate-related ST changes. Focus on the ST segment in beats with moderate R-R intervals. AF in the setting of inferior STEMI may indicate RCA atrial branch involvement.
- **Inferior STEMI + Pre-existing Q-waves (old MI)**: Distinguish acute from chronic by ST-T changes. Acute injury produces ST elevation and hyperacute T-waves overlying old Q-waves. The presence of new ST elevation in leads with pre-existing Q-waves suggests re-infarction.
- **Inferior STEMI + RV pacing**: RV-paced rhythm produces LBBB-like morphology. Apply Sgarbossa criteria. Native beats (if available) should be scrutinized for STEMI changes.
- **Inferior STEMI + Digoxin use**: Digoxin causes "reverse check mark" (downsloping ST depression) that can mask or confuse reciprocal changes in anterior leads. Inferior lead ST elevation remains diagnostic.

## 5. STAT Classification

| Priority | Criteria |
|----------|----------|
| **STAT** | YES — inferior STEMI is a STAT condition. Acute coronary occlusion causes irreversible myocardial necrosis beginning at ~20 minutes and progressing transmurally over 3-6 hours. Every 30-minute delay in reperfusion increases 1-year mortality. |
| **Time-sensitive** | Door-to-balloon time goal: <90 minutes for primary PCI, <120 minutes if transfer required. Door-to-needle time <30 minutes if fibrinolysis is the reperfusion strategy. The system must flag inferior STEMI within seconds of ECG acquisition. |
| **Clinical action** | 1) Activate cardiac catheterization lab immediately. 2) Aspirin 325 mg chewed + P2Y12 inhibitor loading. 3) Heparin bolus. 4) If ST elevation in V4R (RV STEMI): NO nitroglycerin, NO morphine-related volume depletion, give aggressive IV fluids (500-1000 mL NS bolus). 5) If complete heart block: transcutaneous pacer on standby, may need transvenous pacing. 6) Continuous telemetry for high-degree AV block. |

## 6. Reasoning Complexity Analysis (Feeds Into Node 2.1 — Agent Architecture Research)

### 6.1 Reasoning Domains Required to Detect This Condition
- **Lead-group correlation**: Primary domain — comparing ST levels across inferior leads (II, III, aVF) and detecting reciprocal changes in I, aVL, V1-V3. This is fundamentally a spatial pattern recognition task.
- **Morphology analysis**: ST-segment morphology (concave vs convex), T-wave morphology (hyperacute detection), Q-wave formation.
- **Interval measurement**: PR interval monitoring for AV block development, QTc for arrhythmia risk.
- **Rate/rhythm analysis**: Detecting bradycardia, Wenckebach, junctional rhythm, or complete heart block — all common in inferior STEMI.
- **Cross-domain reasoning REQUIRED**: The conduction system assessment agent (or domain) MUST be aware that inferior STEMI predisposes to AV block. The structural assessment domain must evaluate for RV extension. This is not a single-domain diagnosis — it requires at minimum: ischemia detection + conduction analysis + structural (RV) assessment working in concert.
- **Sequential reasoning**: IF inferior ST elevation detected THEN check III vs II ratio (culprit artery) THEN check V1 (RV involvement) THEN check V1-V3 depression depth (posterior extension) THEN check V5-V6 (lateral extension) THEN check PR interval trend (AV block risk).

### 6.2 Feature Dependencies
- **ESSENTIAL features**: ST-segment amplitude at J-point in II, III, aVF (absolute values and relative magnitudes); ST-segment amplitude in I, aVL (reciprocal detection); presence/absence of pathologic Q-waves in II, III, aVF.
- **SUPPORTING features**: T-wave amplitude and morphology in inferior leads (hyperacute detection); T-wave/QRS ratio; ST amplitude in V1-V3 (posterior extension); ST amplitude in V4R (RV infarction — if available); PR interval and its trend across the recording; heart rate and rhythm classification.
- **EXCLUSIONARY features**: Diffuse ST elevation across non-contiguous territories WITHOUT reciprocal changes (suggests pericarditis, not STEMI). PR depression in II, III, aVF with PR elevation in aVR (pericarditis). Sinus tachycardia with S1Q3T3 pattern (PE, not inferior STEMI).
- **Per-beat analysis needed**: PR interval trending (progressive prolongation = Wenckebach), ST amplitude stability (constant = STEMI; intermittent = vasospasm), ectopic beat detection (exclude PVCs from ST measurement).

### 6.3 Cross-Condition Interactions
- **Inferior STEMI affects conduction interpretation**: AV block (1st, 2nd Mobitz I, 3rd degree) in the setting of inferior STEMI is expected and prognostically different from AV block from primary conduction disease. The conduction analysis domain must know the ischemia context.
- **Inferior STEMI affects RV assessment**: RV infarction is a structural consequence of proximal RCA occlusion. The structural analysis domain must be triggered by inferior STEMI detection to assess RV involvement.
- **LBBB masks inferior STEMI**: If LBBB is detected, standard inferior STEMI criteria are invalid — Sgarbossa criteria must be applied instead. This is a hard dependency on QRS morphology assessment.
- **Posterior MI may coexist**: Deep ST depression in V1-V3 in the context of inferior STEMI is not just "reciprocal" — it likely represents true posterior injury. The system must distinguish reciprocal depression from posterior extension (posterior leads V7-V9 or dominant R-wave development in V1-V2 help differentiate).
- **Atrial fibrillation complicates measurement**: If AF is detected, the rhythm domain must flag that ST measurements should use beats with moderate R-R intervals.

### 6.4 Reasoning Chain Sketch

**Minimum reasoning chain (fast path to high confidence):**
1. Detect ST elevation >=1 mm in >=2 of {II, III, aVF}
2. Confirm reciprocal ST depression in aVL and/or I
3. -> INFERIOR STEMI flagged as STAT

**Full reasoning chain (complete evidence assembly):**
1. Detect ST elevation >=1 mm in >=2 of {II, III, aVF}
2. Confirm reciprocal ST depression in aVL and/or I (increases specificity)
3. Confirm reciprocal ST depression in V1-V3 (supports diagnosis)
4. Compare III vs II ST magnitude -> RCA (III > II) vs LCx (II >= III) culprit
5. Assess reciprocal depression magnitude in I and aVL -> deep depression in aVL with III > II further confirms RCA
6. Check V1 for ST elevation -> if present, flag RV involvement, recommend V4R
7. Check V1-V3 depression depth and R-wave amplitude -> if deep depression or new R-wave, flag posterior extension
8. Check V5-V6 -> if elevation, flag lateral extension (suggests large territory or LCx)
9. Assess PR interval -> prolonged or trending upward -> flag AV node ischemia risk
10. Assess heart rate -> bradycardia? -> flag SA node ischemia
11. Check for Q-wave development -> timing of infarct (hyperacute vs acute vs evolving)
12. Assess ST morphology -> convex (tombstone) indicates larger infarct
13. Compile: territory (inferior +/- posterior +/- RV +/- lateral), culprit vessel (RCA vs LCx), conduction status, acuity
14. Generate confidence score and flag STAT

### 6.5 Confidence Anchors
- **HIGH confidence features**: ST elevation >=1 mm in >=2 inferior leads WITH reciprocal depression in aVL (specificity >95%). Convex (tombstone) ST morphology. Pathologic Q-waves developing in real-time. Hyperacute T-waves in inferior leads.
- **LOWER confidence if absent**: No reciprocal changes (could be pericarditis or BER). ST elevation < 1 mm (sub-threshold — may be very early or non-STEMI). ST elevation only in Lead III without II or aVF involvement (could be normal variant, PE, or positional).
- **Pathognomonic combination**: ST elevation in II, III, aVF (III > II) with deep reciprocal depression in aVL + ST elevation in V4R = RCA occlusion with RV infarction. Near-100% specific.
- **Classification thresholds**:
  - *Possible*: ST elevation >=0.5 mm in 2 inferior leads, no clear reciprocal changes, OR isolated aVL depression with borderline inferior changes
  - *Probable*: ST elevation >=1 mm in 2 inferior leads, reciprocal changes present in aVL or V1-V3, consistent clinical context
  - *Definite*: ST elevation >=1 mm in all 3 inferior leads, reciprocal depression in aVL (and usually I), +/- Q-wave development, +/- hyperacute T-waves

### Primary Agent
**IT** — inferior STEMI is a territorial ischemia diagnosis defined by ST elevation in II/III/aVF with coronary territory mapping, which is the IT agent's core domain.

### Cross-Domain Hints
- RRC emits `cross_domain_hint: "Right axis deviation and/or Wenckebach AV block detected in inferior STEMI context — RV infarction possible, correlate with V4R findings from IT"` when these findings are present alongside inferior ST elevation context.

### CDS Specific Role
CDS integrates RRC's conduction findings (AV block pattern, axis) with IT's territorial ST analysis to determine if RV coexistence is present (proximal RCA). When IT reports ST elevation in V4R and RRC flags right axis deviation, CDS upgrades urgency and documents RV involvement. CDS also resolves whether V1–V3 ST depression is reciprocal from the inferior STEMI or represents a separate posterior extension, using IT's posterior lead analysis if available.

## 9. Edge Cases and Pitfalls

- **Inferior STEMI in the setting of LBBB**: Standard ST criteria are unreliable. Apply Smith-modified Sgarbossa criteria. Concordant ST elevation >=1 mm in inferior leads (same direction as QRS) is the most reliable sign. The system should automatically switch to Sgarbossa criteria when LBBB + inferior ST changes are detected.
- **Elderly patients presenting without chest pain**: Up to 30% of patients >75 years present with atypical symptoms (dyspnea, syncope, nausea, confusion) and no chest pain. The ECG may be the only clue. The system must flag STEMI patterns regardless of clinical context input.
- **Inferior + posterior + RV combo (extensive proximal RCA occlusion)**: This triad indicates proximal RCA occlusion before the RV marginal and posterior descending branches. Large territory at risk. ECG shows: inferior ST elevation (III > II) + V1 elevation (RV) + deep V1-V3 depression with tall R (posterior). Hemodynamically devastating — RV failure causes hypotension that does NOT respond to nitroglycerin and may worsen with it. The system must flag all three territories and generate a "proximal RCA — large territory" alert.
- **Wenckebach progressing to complete heart block**: Initial ECG may show only Mobitz I (benign-appearing). The system should flag the combination of inferior STEMI + any AV block as high-risk for progression to complete heart block. If PR interval is progressively prolonging across the recording, this should trigger a "conduction deterioration" warning.
- **Isolated aVL depression as earliest sign**: In the very early minutes of RCA occlusion, the only finding may be ST depression in aVL (before inferior leads meet elevation threshold). The system should have a "subtle inferior STEMI" flag: "Isolated aVL depression in clinical context of acute chest pain — repeat ECG in 15 minutes, consider evolving inferior STEMI."
- **Normal variant Q-waves in Lead III**: A narrow Q-wave in Lead III is common in normal individuals (especially with a more horizontal heart axis). It typically diminishes with deep inspiration. Pathologic inferior MI Q-waves are >=30 ms wide and present in at least 2 inferior leads (not III alone).
- **Right ventricular pacing masking inferior STEMI**: RV pacing produces LBBB-pattern that obscures native ST changes. If the patient has a pacemaker, the system should note that STEMI detection confidence is reduced and recommend comparison with prior ECGs if available.
- **Hyperkalemia confounding inferior ST changes**: Severe hyperkalemia produces peaked T-waves and ST changes that may mimic or mask STEMI. If T-waves are peaked AND narrow (not broad-based as in hyperacute STEMI) in ALL leads (not just inferior), hyperkalemia should be considered as a confounder.
- **Low-voltage ECG (obesity, COPD, pericardial effusion)**: ST elevation may not reach the 1 mm threshold despite transmural ischemia. The system should lower the threshold if diffuse low voltage is detected (all limb leads <5 mm amplitude) — consider flagging at 0.5 mm with a "low voltage, reduced threshold applied" caveat.
- **Female patients**: Women may present with lower ST elevation magnitudes and more atypical symptoms. While the guideline threshold for inferior leads (1 mm) is the same for both sexes, borderline cases in women should be flagged with higher suspicion.
