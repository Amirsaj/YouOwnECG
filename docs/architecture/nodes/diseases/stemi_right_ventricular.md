## 1. Pathophysiology Root Cause

### 1.1 What Goes Wrong (Anatomy/Physiology)
- Acute thrombotic occlusion of the proximal right coronary artery (RCA), specifically PROXIMAL to the acute marginal (RV) branches. The RV marginal branches supply the free wall of the right ventricle. When occlusion occurs distal to these branches, the RV is spared; when proximal, the RV is directly injured.
- RV STEMI almost always accompanies inferior STEMI because the proximal RCA supplies both the RV branches and the inferior wall (posterior descending artery in right-dominant circulation). Isolated RV infarction without inferior wall involvement is exceedingly rare (<1% of RV MIs) and would require a selective occlusion of an RV branch with a patent PDA.
- The RV free wall is thin (3-5 mm vs 10-15 mm for the LV), has lower oxygen demand, and has dual blood supply (RCA branches + Thebesian veins from the RV cavity). Because of these features, the RV is somewhat more resistant to infarction than the LV, and RV function often recovers significantly within days to weeks even after acute RV infarction.
- At the cellular level, transmural ischemia develops in the RV free wall. ATP depletion opens K_ATP channels, shortening the action potential in the ischemic RV cells. The injury current flows from the ischemic RV (relatively negative) toward healthy tissue — the injury vector points anteriorly and rightward because the RV free wall faces the anterior chest wall and sits to the right of the LV.
- Hemodynamic consequence: The RV is a preload-dependent, low-pressure pump. When the RV free wall is infarcted, it becomes akinetic or dyskinetic. RV output drops, which decreases LV preload (the RV is the LV's "priming pump"). This produces the classic clinical triad: hypotension, elevated jugular venous pressure (JVP), and clear lungs. Kussmaul's sign (paradoxical rise in JVP with inspiration) is pathognomonic. This hemodynamic profile is critical because it contrasts sharply with LV failure (which produces pulmonary edema and low JVP).

### 1.2 Electrical Consequence
- The injury current vector from the RV free wall points anteriorly and rightward. Lead V1, positioned at the right sternal border over the RV, is the only standard precordial lead that directly faces the RV injury zone.
- V1 records ST elevation because the RV injury vector projects directly toward V1's positive pole. This is a DIRECT sign of RV injury, not a reciprocal change.
- Right-sided precordial leads (V3R, V4R, V5R, V6R) are positioned on the right chest wall, directly overlying the RV free wall. They record direct ST elevation. V4R (placed at the 5th intercostal space, right midclavicular line — the mirror-image position of V4) is the single most sensitive and specific lead for RV infarction.
- Because the RV free wall contributes relatively little to the overall QRS vector (small muscle mass), the QRS changes from RV infarction are subtle compared to LV infarction. Q-waves in right-sided leads may develop but are less prominent than LV Q-waves.
- The inferior leads (II, III, aVF) show ST elevation from the concomitant inferior wall injury. Characteristically, Lead III shows greater ST elevation than Lead II (III > II) because the proximal RCA injury vector is directed inferiorly and rightward — aligning more with Lead III (+120 degrees) than Lead II (+60 degrees).

### 1.3 Why It Appears on ECG
- **V1 ST elevation**: V1's positive pole faces the RV outflow tract and septal surface. The anteriorly and rightward-directed RV injury vector projects directly onto V1, producing ST elevation. This is the critical finding on standard 12-lead ECG.
- **V4R ST elevation**: V4R sits directly over the RV free wall. The injury vector projects maximally toward V4R, making it the most sensitive lead (~90% sensitivity, ~82% specificity for RV infarction when ST elevation >=1 mm; even higher at >=0.5 mm threshold).
- **III > II pattern**: The combined inferior + rightward injury vector (proximal RCA) aligns more closely with Lead III (+120 degrees, rightward-inferior) than Lead II (+60 degrees, leftward-inferior). This III > II ST elevation ratio is a hallmark of proximal RCA occlusion and should prompt immediate evaluation for RV involvement.
- **V2-V3 transition**: V2-V3 may show ST elevation (if RV injury signal is dominant), ST depression (if posterior extension exists — competing forces), or isoelectric segments (cancellation of anterior RV elevation and posterior reciprocal depression). The V2-V3 pattern helps distinguish which component is dominant.
- **aVL reciprocal depression**: The injury vector is inferior and rightward — directly opposite to aVL (-30 degrees). Deep reciprocal depression in aVL is expected and is often the earliest and most sensitive marker of proximal RCA occlusion.

## 2. ECG Presentation — Lead by Lead

### 2.1 Diagnostic Criteria (2025 AHA/ESC Guidelines)

| Criterion | Threshold | Source |
|-----------|-----------|--------|
| ST elevation in V4R | >=0.5 mm (>=1 mm in men <30 yr) at J-point — most sensitive single criterion | ESC 2023, reaffirmed 2025 |
| ST elevation in V1 | >=1 mm at J-point in the context of inferior STEMI | 2025 AHA Universal MI Definition update |
| Inferior STEMI present | ST elevation >=1 mm in >=2 of II, III, aVF (virtually always accompanies RV STEMI) | 2023 ESC STEMI Guidelines |
| III > II ST elevation | ST elevation in Lead III exceeds Lead II — suggests proximal RCA culprit | Tierala et al., 2009; Nikus et al., 2024 |
| ST elevation V3R-V6R | >=0.5 mm in any right-sided precordial lead — supplementary confirmation | ESC 2023 |
| Kussmaul's sign + hemodynamic triad | Hypotension + elevated JVP + clear lungs — clinical confirmation (not ECG, but integrated into diagnostic algorithm) | Braunwald's Heart Disease, 12th ed. |

### 2.3 Key Leads (Most Diagnostic)
- **Primary diagnostic lead (supplementary)**: V4R — the single most sensitive and specific lead for RV STEMI. ST elevation >=0.5 mm in V4R has ~90% sensitivity and ~82% specificity for RV infarction. ST elevation >=1 mm increases specificity to >90%. V4R should be obtained on EVERY patient with inferior STEMI.
- **Primary diagnostic lead (standard 12-lead)**: V1 — ST elevation in V1 during inferior STEMI is the strongest standard 12-lead indicator of RV involvement. If V1 shows ST elevation AND inferior leads show ST elevation, suspect RV STEMI until proven otherwise.
- **Most sensitive early sign**: Reciprocal ST depression in aVL — often precedes threshold-meeting inferior ST elevation. Deep aVL depression with III > II pattern should immediately trigger suspicion for proximal RCA occlusion with RV involvement.
- **Culprit localization**: III > II confirms proximal RCA (rightward vector). If II >= III, the occlusion is more likely distal RCA or LCx — RV involvement is less likely.
- **Mandatory supplementary leads**: V3R, V4R, V5R, V6R — should be obtained in ALL patients with inferior STEMI. V4R is the highest-yield single lead. ST elevation in V4R is TRANSIENT — it may resolve within 10-12 hours of symptom onset, making early acquisition critical.

### 2.4 Beat-by-Beat Considerations
- The ST elevation in V1 and right-sided leads is typically constant across beats in established RV STEMI, not intermittent.
- RV STEMI frequently coexists with AV node ischemia (AV nodal artery arises from the RCA in ~90% of right-dominant patients). Wenckebach periodicity (Mobitz Type I) is common and may progress to complete heart block. Beat-to-beat PR interval trending is important for early detection of conduction deterioration.
- Sinus bradycardia is common (SA node artery from RCA in ~60% of patients). If atropine-resistant bradycardia develops, temporary pacing may be needed — but avoid RV pacing if possible (may worsen RV function in acute RV MI). Atrial-only pacing or AV sequential pacing is preferred.
- If atrial fibrillation develops (RCA atrial branch involvement), the loss of atrial kick further compromises the already preload-dependent RV, potentially causing acute hemodynamic deterioration. Beat-to-beat ST measurement should use moderate R-R interval beats.
- Post-PVC beats may show exaggerated ST changes; exclude PVCs from ST measurement.

## 3. Morphology Details (What the Agent Must See)

### 3.1 P-wave Changes
- Morphology: Usually normal early on. SA node ischemia (SA nodal artery from proximal RCA in 60% of patients) may cause sinus bradycardia, sinus pauses, or sinus arrest with junctional escape rhythm (absent P-waves or retrograde P-waves inverted in II, III, aVF).
- Duration change: Normal unless right atrial infarction occurs (rare but recognized in proximal RCA occlusion — produces PR elevation in V1 and right-sided leads, PR depression in II).
- Axis change: Normal unless junctional escape develops (retrograde P-wave axis). Right atrial ischemia may subtly change P-wave morphology (loss of the terminal component in V1 that normally represents left atrial depolarization, as right atrial timing is altered).

### 3.2 PR Interval Changes
- Duration: Frequently prolonged (first-degree AV block) due to AV node ischemia — occurs in 15-20% of RV STEMI (higher incidence than inferior STEMI without RV involvement because the proximal RCA location means AV nodal artery is almost always compromised).
- Pattern: Wenckebach (Mobitz Type I) is the most common conduction disturbance, occurring in 12-15% of cases. Progression to complete heart block occurs in 5-8% of proximal RCA occlusions. The AV block in RV STEMI is typically at the AV node level (not infranodal) — escape rhythm is usually junctional at 40-60 bpm, which is narrow-complex and relatively reliable.
- CRITICAL DISTINCTION: AV block from RCA/AV node ischemia is usually transient (resolves with reperfusion) and responds to atropine. This is fundamentally different from infranodal AV block from LAD occlusion (which has an unreliable escape and requires pacing).

### 3.3 QRS Complex Changes
- Duration: Narrow (<120 ms) in uncomplicated RV STEMI. The RV contributes relatively little to the overall QRS because of its thin wall. QRS widening suggests either concurrent septal ischemia, pre-existing bundle branch disease, or hyperkalemia.
- Morphology: Q-waves may develop in V1 and right-sided leads (V3R, V4R) as RV necrosis progresses, but these are less prominent than LV Q-waves because of the RV's smaller muscle mass. Pathologic Q-waves in II, III, aVF develop from the inferior component. Loss of the normal small R-wave in V1 (progression to QS pattern) can occur but must be distinguished from normal variant or LBBB.
- Amplitude: Minimal change in standard leads because RV contribution to overall QRS voltage is small. R-wave amplitude may decrease slightly in right-sided leads as RV tissue becomes electrically silent.
- Axis: Usually no significant change. If concurrent LAFB develops (uncommon with RCA — more typical of LAD), left axis deviation may appear.

### 3.4 ST Segment Changes
- Direction: Elevation in V1 (direct RV injury), elevation in II, III, aVF (inferior component), elevation in right-sided leads V3R-V6R (direct RV injury). Reciprocal depression in I, aVL.
- Morphology: The ST elevation in V1 from RV injury is typically concave-up or gently convex. In the inferior leads, morphology follows the same pattern as inferior STEMI — initially concave-up evolving to convex (tombstone) in large infarcts.
- Measurement point: J-point is standard. For V4R, measure at J-point relative to the PR segment. The >=0.5 mm threshold for right-sided leads accounts for the attenuation of signal from the thin-walled RV.
- Gender/lead-specific thresholds: In men <30 years, up to 1 mm of ST elevation in V4R may be a normal variant (early repolarization). Use the >=1 mm threshold for this demographic. For all others, >=0.5 mm in V4R is diagnostic. V1 elevation >=1 mm in the context of inferior STEMI is diagnostic regardless of age/sex.
- TRANSIENCE: The ST elevation in V4R is the earliest ECG sign to resolve — it may normalize within 10-12 hours of symptom onset (even without reperfusion), while inferior ST elevation persists. This makes EARLY acquisition of right-sided leads critical. A negative V4R does not rule out RV MI if the ECG is obtained >12 hours after symptom onset.

### 3.5 T-wave Changes
- Direction: Initially hyperacute (tall, upright, broad-based) in V1 and right-sided leads — reflecting the earliest phase of RV injury. As infarction evolves, T-waves may invert in V1 and right-sided leads. In inferior leads, the T-wave evolution follows the standard pattern: hyperacute → upright with ST elevation → inversion with resolving ST.
- Amplitude: Hyperacute T-waves in V1 may reach 3-6 mm, disproportionately tall for V1 (where T-waves are normally small or inverted). In V4R, hyperacute T-waves may be the earliest sign, even before frank ST elevation.
- Symmetry: Hyperacute T-waves are broad-based and symmetric, in contrast to the normal asymmetric T-wave.
- Specific patterns: T-wave inversion in V1-V3 in the recovery phase may mimic RV strain pattern from PE or pulmonary hypertension. Clinical context is essential for differentiation. Wellens-type patterns do not apply to RV territory.

### 3.6 QT/QTc Changes
- QTc may prolong during acute ischemia (440-500 ms range), similar to other STEMI territories.
- The combination of bradycardia (common in RV STEMI from SA node ischemia) + QT prolongation + potential hypokalemia (from diuresis or poor intake) creates a particularly dangerous substrate for Torsades de Pointes. This triad should be monitored aggressively.
- Reperfusion arrhythmias (AIVR, VT) are common after PCI for proximal RCA — QTc monitoring is part of the post-reperfusion protocol.

### 3.7 Other Features
- **V4R ST elevation is the hallmark**: >=0.5 mm ST elevation in V4R is the single most important diagnostic criterion. It has ~90% sensitivity and >80% specificity for RV infarction. It is TRANSIENT and must be obtained early.
- **ST elevation in V1 with inferior STEMI**: This combination on standard 12-lead ECG should trigger immediate acquisition of right-sided leads. V1 is the "sentinel" lead for RV involvement on the standard ECG.
- **Loss of V1 ST elevation progressing from V1 to V2-V6**: A pattern of V1 elevation with progressive loss of elevation (or transition to depression) moving from V1 → V2 → V3 → V4 is characteristic of RV injury. If V2 already shows depression while V1 shows elevation, this may indicate concurrent posterior extension (competing signals).
- **Osborn waves**: Not a feature of RV STEMI. If present, evaluate for hypothermia.
- **U-waves**: Not characteristic. If present, evaluate for hypokalemia.
- **Right atrial infarction**: Rare but possible with proximal RCA occlusion. May cause atrial arrhythmias (AF, atrial flutter) and PR-segment changes (elevation in V1/right-sided leads, depression in II).

## 4. Differential Diagnosis

### 4.1 Mimics (What Looks Like This But Isn't)

| Mimic Condition | Shared Features | Distinguishing Features |
|----------------|-----------------|----------------------|
| Acute pulmonary embolism (PE) | ST elevation in V1, RV strain, tachycardia, hypotension, elevated JVP | PE produces sinus tachycardia (not bradycardia as in RV STEMI), S1Q3T3 pattern, T-wave inversions V1-V4 (RV strain, NOT ST elevation), RBBB pattern, right axis deviation. NO inferior ST elevation. No hyperacute T-waves. Tachycardia + clear lungs + elevated JVP overlaps with RV STEMI hemodynamics, but the ECG pattern is fundamentally different. |
| Acute cor pulmonale (any cause) | RV strain, elevated JVP, hypotension | RV strain produces T-wave inversions in V1-V3 (not ST elevation), RAD, RBBB, P-pulmonale. Chronic process vs acute MI. Clinical context: COPD exacerbation, massive PE, tension pneumothorax. |
| Brugada syndrome (Type 1 — coved pattern) | ST elevation in V1, may have V2 involvement | Brugada has characteristic coved ST elevation with negative T-wave in V1-V3 (not upright as in hyperacute RV STEMI). No inferior ST elevation. No reciprocal aVL depression. Pattern may be unmasked by fever or sodium channel blockers. Young patient without ischemic risk factors. |
| Anterior STEMI (proximal LAD) | ST elevation in V1 | Anterior STEMI shows progressive ST elevation across V1-V4 (or V1-V6), NOT limited to V1 alone. Reciprocal depression in inferior leads (II, III, aVF) — this is the OPPOSITE of RV STEMI which has inferior elevation. No III > II pattern. |
| Early repolarization in V1 | ST elevation in V1, young patient | BER shows notched/slurred J-point, concordant T-wave (upright), no reciprocal changes, stable on serial ECGs, no inferior ST elevation. More common in young men. |
| Right ventricular outflow tract tachycardia (RVOT VT) | May show ST changes in V1, inferior axis QRS | RVOT VT produces wide-QRS tachycardia with LBBB morphology and inferior axis. Not a STEMI pattern — the wide QRS and tachycardia distinguish it from RV STEMI with narrow QRS. |
| Pericarditis | ST elevation that may include V1 | Diffuse ST elevation (not limited to V1 + inferior leads), PR depression in II with PR elevation in aVR, concave-up morphology everywhere, NO reciprocal depression (critical), pleuritic pain. |

### 4.2 Coexisting Conditions
- **RV STEMI + Inferior STEMI (nearly always coexist)**: RV STEMI essentially NEVER occurs without inferior STEMI in clinical practice. The inferior STEMI component is diagnosed first on the standard 12-lead; RV involvement is then assessed with V4R. The system should automatically flag "inferior STEMI detected — obtain V4R" as a standing recommendation.
- **RV STEMI + Posterior STEMI (proximal RCA triad)**: Proximal RCA occlusion often produces the devastating triad: inferior + RV + posterior MI. On the standard ECG, V1 receives competing signals (RV elevation vs posterior depression). If V1 appears isoelectric during inferior STEMI, suspect competing forces and obtain both V4R (RV) and V7-V9 (posterior). The hemodynamic impact of this triad is severe — both RV failure (hypotension from preload loss) and extensive LV injury.
- **RV STEMI + AV Block**: AV nodal ischemia from proximal RCA occlusion produces AV block in 15-20% of cases. The combination of RV failure + bradycardia from AV block is hemodynamically devastating. The AV block is typically at the nodal level (narrow escape, atropine responsive) and often resolves with reperfusion. Temporary pacing may be needed — preferring atrial or AV sequential pacing over RV-only pacing (which may worsen RV function).
- **RV STEMI + Atrial Fibrillation**: Loss of atrial kick in the setting of a preload-dependent RV is particularly detrimental. AF may result from right atrial ischemia or elevated right atrial pressure. Rate control and restoration of sinus rhythm become higher priority than in LV STEMI + AF.
- **RV STEMI + Cardiogenic Shock**: RV STEMI is a recognized cause of cardiogenic shock DESPITE clear lungs. The mechanism is RV failure → decreased LV preload → decreased cardiac output. Treatment is IV fluids (the OPPOSITE of LV failure treatment). Pressors (norepinephrine) may be needed. Mechanical support (Impella RP or ECMO) in refractory cases.

## 5. STAT Classification

| Priority | Criteria |
|----------|----------|
| **STAT** | YES — RV STEMI is a STAT condition. Proximal RCA occlusion threatens a large territory (inferior wall + RV + potentially posterior wall). RV infarction causes a unique hemodynamic catastrophe (preload-dependent failure) that requires OPPOSITE management to LV failure. Mismanagement (nitroglycerin, morphine, diuretics) can cause fatal hypotension. |
| **Time-sensitive** | Door-to-balloon <90 minutes. ADDITIONAL time sensitivity: V4R must be obtained within 10-12 hours of symptom onset (ST elevation in V4R is transient). The system must flag inferior STEMI immediately and recommend V4R acquisition in the first clinical message. |
| **Clinical action** | 1) Recognize inferior STEMI with V1 elevation — obtain V4R IMMEDIATELY. 2) If V4R confirms RV STEMI: **NO NITROGLYCERIN** (causes severe hypotension by reducing preload to an already preload-starved RV), **NO MORPHINE** (vasodilatory, further reduces preload), **NO DIURETICS** (reduce preload). 3) TREAT with aggressive IV fluid resuscitation (500-1000 mL NS bolus, repeat as needed to maintain systolic BP >90). 4) If hypotensive despite fluids: norepinephrine (not dopamine — less chronotropic effect). 5) Activate cath lab — primary PCI for proximal RCA occlusion. 6) If AV block develops: atropine first; if refractory, temporary AV sequential pacing (avoid isolated RV pacing). 7) Monitor for AF — cardiovert promptly if hemodynamically significant (atrial kick is critical). 8) Continuous telemetry — risk of VT/VF, progression of AV block, and reperfusion arrhythmias. |

### 6.1 Reasoning Domains Required to Detect This Condition
- **Lead-group correlation (PRIMARY)**: The core detection relies on recognizing the combination of inferior ST elevation (II, III, aVF) with V1 ST elevation — a spatial pattern that distinguishes proximal from distal RCA occlusion. The III > II pattern provides the rightward vector clue.
- **Morphology analysis**: ST elevation morphology in V1 (distinguishing acute RV injury from Brugada, BER, or RBBB-associated changes), T-wave assessment in V1 (hyperacute vs strain vs baseline), R-wave changes.
- **Conduction analysis**: PR interval monitoring for AV block development is critically important because AV block occurs in 15-20% of cases and can cause acute hemodynamic deterioration. The conduction domain must be tightly coupled to the ischemia domain.
- **Rhythm analysis**: Rate assessment (bradycardia detection from SA node ischemia), AF detection (right atrial ischemia), and arrhythmia monitoring (reperfusion VT/VF).
- **Cross-domain reasoning REQUIRED**: Multiple domains must work together: (1) ischemia detection (inferior ST elevation + V1 elevation), (2) conduction assessment (AV block risk), (3) rhythm analysis (bradycardia/AF), (4) structural assessment (RV function — though this is more echocardiographic than ECG). The clinical management implications (NO nitroglycerin, IV fluids) require the system to integrate the ECG diagnosis with treatment recommendations.
- **Sequential reasoning**: IF inferior ST elevation detected THEN check III > II (proximal RCA?) THEN check V1 for ST elevation (RV injury?) THEN recommend V4R THEN IF V4R positive → flag RV STEMI with treatment modifications THEN assess PR interval (AV block risk) THEN assess for posterior extension (V1-V3 ST segment trend).

### 6.2 Feature Dependencies
- **ESSENTIAL features**: ST-segment amplitude at J-point in II, III, aVF (inferior elevation); ST-segment amplitude in V1 (elevation = RV injury sign); III vs II ST elevation comparison (III > II = proximal RCA); ST-segment amplitude in V4R if available (>=0.5 mm = diagnostic).
- **SUPPORTING features**: aVL ST depression magnitude (deep depression supports proximal RCA); V2-V3 ST segment trend (elevation → RV dominant, depression → posterior dominant, isoelectric → competing forces); PR interval and trending (AV block risk); heart rate (bradycardia from SA node ischemia); P-wave morphology (atrial ischemia/AF risk); V1 T-wave morphology (hyperacute = early RV injury).
- **EXCLUSIONARY features**: Sinus tachycardia with S1Q3T3 and V1-V4 T-wave inversions (PE, not RV STEMI). Diffuse ST elevation without reciprocal changes (pericarditis). V1-V3 coved ST elevation with negative T-wave (Brugada). Progressive V1-V4 ST elevation with reciprocal inferior depression (anterior STEMI — the opposite pattern).
- **Per-beat analysis needed**: PR interval trending (progressive prolongation = Wenckebach developing), beat-to-beat R-R regularity (detecting AF onset), ST amplitude stability (constant = STEMI), and PVC detection (exclude from ST measurement; frequent PVCs may herald reperfusion arrhythmia).

### 6.3 Cross-Condition Interactions
- **RV STEMI is ALWAYS in the context of inferior STEMI**: The system's inferior STEMI detection must trigger automatic assessment for RV extension. RV STEMI should never be a "standalone" detection pathway — it is an extension analysis of inferior STEMI detection.
- **RV STEMI + posterior STEMI creates competing V1 signals**: The RV injury pushes V1 ST up, while posterior reciprocal changes push V1 ST down. The system must recognize that an isoelectric V1 during inferior STEMI may not mean "no RV or posterior involvement" — it may mean "both present, signals canceling." This requires recommending BOTH V4R and V7-V9.
- **RV STEMI changes management of inferior STEMI**: The treatment modifications (no nitroglycerin, IV fluids) are OPPOSITE to standard LV STEMI management. The system's treatment recommendation module must be aware of the RV STEMI flag.
- **AV block in RV STEMI is prognostically different**: AV block from AV node ischemia (proximal RCA) is typically transient and at the nodal level (narrow escape). This is distinct from infranodal AV block from LAD occlusion (wide escape, permanent). The conduction domain must integrate the ischemia territory context.
- **PE and RV STEMI share hemodynamic profiles**: Both produce hypotension + elevated JVP + clear lungs. The ECG is the key differentiator. The system must clearly distinguish the ECG patterns: RV STEMI = inferior ST elevation + V1 elevation + III > II vs PE = sinus tachycardia + S1Q3T3 + V1-V4 T-wave inversions + RBBB.

### 6.4 Reasoning Chain Sketch

**Minimum reasoning chain (fast path to high confidence):**
1. Detect inferior STEMI (ST elevation >=1 mm in >=2 of II, III, aVF)
2. Detect ST elevation in V1 (>=1 mm)
3. Confirm III > II
4. -> Flag RV STEMI suspected, recommend V4R, flag STAT with treatment modifications (NO nitroglycerin)

**Full reasoning chain (complete evidence assembly):**
1. Detect inferior ST elevation >=1 mm in >=2 of {II, III, aVF}
2. Confirm reciprocal ST depression in aVL and/or I (validates inferior STEMI)
3. Compare III vs II → III > II confirms rightward vector (proximal RCA)
4. Assess V1 → ST elevation present? → direct RV injury sign
5. Assess V2-V3 → elevation (RV dominant), depression (posterior dominant), isoelectric (competing)
6. Recommend V4R → if available and >=0.5 mm elevation → CONFIRMED RV STEMI
7. Assess PR interval → prolonged? trending upward? → flag AV block risk
8. Assess heart rate → bradycardic? → flag SA node ischemia
9. Check for V1-V3 deep depression → if present despite V1 elevation, posterior extension likely
10. Assess P-wave → irregular/absent? → AF risk from right atrial ischemia
11. Compile: territory (inferior + RV +/- posterior), culprit vessel (proximal RCA), conduction status, hemodynamic risk level
12. Generate confidence score, flag STAT, output treatment modifications (NO NTG, IV fluids, AV block monitoring)

### 6.5 Confidence Anchors
- **HIGH confidence features**: Inferior ST elevation (III > II) + V1 ST elevation + V4R ST elevation >=0.5 mm. This combination is near-pathognomonic for proximal RCA occlusion with RV infarction.
- **LOWER confidence if absent**: No V1 elevation (may occur if posterior reciprocal depression cancels RV elevation — does not exclude RV STEMI). V4R not obtained (cannot confirm). II > III (suggests distal RCA or LCx — RV involvement less likely but not excluded).
- **Pathognomonic combination**: Inferior ST elevation (III > II) + V1 ST elevation + V4R ST elevation >=1 mm + reciprocal aVL depression + sinus bradycardia = proximal RCA occlusion with RV STEMI. Near-100% specific.
- **Classification thresholds**:
  - *Possible*: Inferior STEMI with III > II but V1 isoelectric; or V4R showing 0.5 mm elevation with borderline inferior changes
  - *Probable*: Inferior STEMI (III > II) + V1 ST elevation >=0.5 mm; or V4R >=0.5 mm with clear inferior STEMI
  - *Definite*: Inferior STEMI (III > II) + V1 ST elevation >=1 mm + V4R >=0.5 mm + reciprocal aVL depression

| Signal clarity needed | 3 | V1 ST elevation from RV is a modest signal (thin RV wall produces less injury current than thick LV). Baseline wander or V1 electrode misplacement can mask or simulate the finding. V4R signal is also modest. |
| Number of leads required | 4 | Core suspicion from standard 12-lead (V1 + II, III, aVF), but CONFIRMATION requires supplementary V4R (and ideally V3R-V6R). Without right-sided leads, diagnosis remains "suspected." |
| Cross-domain reasoning | 4 | Requires ischemia detection (inferior STEMI + V1 elevation), conduction assessment (AV block risk), rhythm analysis (bradycardia/AF), and integration with treatment recommendations. Similar complexity to inferior STEMI. |
| Temporal pattern complexity | 3 | V4R ST elevation is TRANSIENT (resolves within 10-12 hours). This time-dependent feature adds urgency but also means the window for detection is narrow. PR interval trending adds per-beat temporal analysis. |
| Differential complexity | 3 | Must distinguish from PE (most important differential — overlapping hemodynamic presentation), Brugada (V1 elevation), anterior STEMI (V1 elevation but with V2-V4 progression), and pericarditis. Less mimics than posterior STEMI but PE overlap is clinically dangerous. |
| Rarity in PTB-XL | 4 | RV STEMI is typically labeled under "inferior MI" in PTB-XL without explicit RV annotation. V4R data is almost never available in standard databases. The system may need to infer RV involvement from V1 patterns + III > II ratio rather than from V4R data. |
| **Overall difficulty** | **3.5** | **Moderate-high — detection on standard 12-lead is achievable (V1 elevation + III > II + inferior STEMI), but CONFIRMATION requires supplementary leads (V4R) that are rarely in standard databases. The transient nature of V4R changes and the critical treatment modifications make this a high-stakes detection task.** |

## 6A. Agent Assignment

**Architecture:** Option E — 3 Parallel Phase 1 Specialists + 1 Sequential CDS (Node 2.1 Gate Passed 2026-03-27)

| Agent | Role for RV STEMI | Key Features Used |
|-------|--------------------------|-------------------|
| **RRC** (Rhythm/Rate/Conduction) | Supporting | Heart rate (bradycardia from SA/AV node ischemia), PR interval and AV block type (Wenckebach from RCA), right axis deviation, junctional escape rhythm detection |
| **IT** (Ischemia/Territory) | Primary | ST elevation ≥1 mm in II/III/aVF (obligate inferior component), ST elevation ≥0.5 mm in V1 (RV direct injury), ST elevation ≥0.5–1 mm in V4R (confirmatory), III > II magnitude (RCA signature), reciprocal depression in I/aVL, coronary territory assignment (proximal RCA) |
| **MR** (Morphology/Repolarization) | Supporting | RV hypertrophy morphology pattern (to exclude as competing finding), QRS morphology in V1 (tall R from RVH vs RV infarct pattern) |
| **CDS** (Cross-Domain Synthesis) | Required — resolves RV + inferior STEMI coexistence | Integrates RRC's axis/AV block with IT's V1 and V4R elevation alongside inferior ST findings; confirms proximal RCA occlusion pattern; flags treatment implication (no nitrates for RV infarct); resolves MR's RVH morphology against acute RV injury |

### Primary Agent
**IT** — RV STEMI is detected via ST elevation in right-sided leads (V1, V4R) in the context of inferior STEMI, which is the IT agent's ischemia/territory domain.

### Cross-Domain Hints
- RRC emits `cross_domain_hint: "Right axis deviation and/or Wenckebach AV block in inferior STEMI context — proximal RCA occlusion with RV involvement suspected; correlate IT V4R findings"` when these are detected alongside inferior context.
- IT emits `cross_domain_hint: "ST elevation in V1 alongside inferior STEMI — RV infarction possible; MR must assess RVH morphology to exclude competing diagnosis"` when V1 elevation accompanies inferior STEMI pattern.

### CDS Specific Role
CDS integrates the three-component RV STEMI signature: RRC's conduction findings (Wenckebach, right axis), IT's right-sided ST elevation (V1, V4R) alongside inferior STEMI, and MR's RV morphology assessment. CDS confirms proximal RCA occlusion pattern and explicitly documents the RV infarction coexistence diagnosis with clinical urgency flag. CDS resolves the MR vs IT conflict if RVH morphology is present by weighing acute ST elevation against chronic RVH criteria.

## 9. Edge Cases and Pitfalls

- **Nitroglycerin-induced cardiovascular collapse**: The most dangerous clinical pitfall. RV STEMI patients given sublingual or IV nitroglycerin can develop profound hypotension (systolic <60 mmHg) because the already preload-starved RV cannot compensate for venodilation. This can precipitate cardiogenic shock or cardiac arrest. The system MUST generate a prominent "NO NITROGLYCERIN" alert whenever RV STEMI is suspected — even before V4R confirmation.
- **V4R obtained too late**: ST elevation in V4R resolves within 10-12 hours of symptom onset (unlike inferior ST elevation which persists 24-48 hours). If the patient presents >12 hours after onset, V4R may be falsely negative. The system should note: "If V4R is negative but presentation is late (>12 hours), RV STEMI cannot be excluded by V4R alone — use clinical assessment (JVP, Kussmaul's sign) and echocardiography."
- **Competing V1 signals (RV elevation + posterior depression)**: Proximal RCA occlusion may produce simultaneous RV injury (V1 elevation) and posterior reciprocal changes (V1 depression). The net V1 appearance may be isoelectric, mildly elevated, or mildly depressed — depending on relative infarct sizes. The system should flag: "Inferior STEMI with near-isoelectric V1 — consider competing RV and posterior forces; obtain BOTH V4R AND V7-V9."
- **RV STEMI misdiagnosed as pulmonary embolism**: Both present with hypotension, elevated JVP, clear lungs, and tachycardia/tachypnea. The ECG is the critical differentiator: inferior STEMI + V1 elevation + III > II = RV STEMI; sinus tachycardia + S1Q3T3 + V1-V3 T-wave inversions + RBBB = PE. The system must explicitly compare these patterns when RV STEMI is detected, as the treatments are completely different (anticoagulation for PE vs PCI + fluids for RV STEMI).
- **AV block progressing to complete heart block**: Initial ECG may show only Mobitz I (Wenckebach), which appears relatively benign. However, in proximal RCA occlusion, progression to complete heart block can occur suddenly. The system should flag: "Inferior STEMI + any degree of AV block = HIGH RISK for complete heart block. Transcutaneous pacer pads should be applied. If temporary pacing needed, prefer AV sequential over RV-only pacing (avoid worsening RV function)."
- **Atrial fibrillation causing hemodynamic deterioration**: Loss of atrial kick in a preload-dependent RV can cause acute hemodynamic collapse. AF in the setting of RV STEMI is more hemodynamically significant than in LV STEMI. Prompt cardioversion should be considered if AF causes hypotension.
- **Low-voltage ECG obscuring V1 elevation**: In patients with obesity, COPD, or large body habitus, the already-modest V1 elevation from RV injury may fall below detection threshold. The system should lower the V1 threshold if diffuse low voltage is detected and flag "low voltage — RV STEMI detection confidence reduced."
- **Right bundle branch block confounding V1 interpretation**: RBBB produces characteristic changes in V1 (rSR' pattern, wide QRS). Superimposed RV STEMI may be hard to distinguish. Look for: new ST elevation CONCORDANT with the terminal R' wave (normally, RBBB produces discordant ST depression after the R'). Concordant V1 elevation in RBBB is suspicious for acute RV injury.
- **Pacemaker rhythm**: RV-paced rhythm produces LBBB-like QRS that obscures native V1 morphology. STEMI detection relies on Sgarbossa criteria. If paced rhythm + inferior STEMI is detected, the system should note that V4R may still be diagnostic for RV involvement even in paced rhythm (the ST segment in V4R reflects the injury current independent of the QRS origin).
- **Inferior STEMI without RV involvement (distal RCA occlusion)**: Not every inferior STEMI has RV involvement. Distal RCA occlusion (past the RV branches) produces inferior STEMI without RV injury. The system should differentiate: III > II with V1 elevation = proximal (RV likely); II >= III without V1 elevation = distal or LCx (RV unlikely). However, V4R should still be obtained to confirm, as the pattern is not 100% sensitive.
