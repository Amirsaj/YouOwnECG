## 1. Pathophysiology Root Cause

### 1.1 What Goes Wrong (Anatomy/Physiology)
- Thrombus (most commonly from deep venous thrombosis of lower extremities) embolizes to the pulmonary arterial system, lodging in main, lobar, or segmental pulmonary arteries
- Acute obstruction of the pulmonary vascular bed increases pulmonary vascular resistance (PVR) dramatically
- The right ventricle (a thin-walled, low-pressure chamber) is suddenly forced to generate much higher pressures to push blood through the obstructed pulmonary circulation → acute RV pressure overload
- RV dilation occurs as the RV fails to cope with the increased afterload → interventricular septum shifts leftward → impaired LV filling → decreased cardiac output → hypotension and shock in massive PE
- RV myocardial oxygen demand increases (higher wall stress) while RV coronary perfusion decreases (RV distension compresses RV coronary arteries) → RV ischemia and strain

### 1.2 Electrical Consequence
- **Acute RV dilation and strain** shifts the electrical axis rightward and anteriorly:
  - The RV depolarization vector becomes larger and more dominant → rightward axis deviation
  - S1Q3T3 pattern: the rightward shift produces a deep S-wave in lead I (terminal vector moving away from lead I) and a Q-wave + inverted T-wave in lead III (dominant vector moving toward III with RV strain repolarization)
- **RV conduction delay**: Acute RV dilation stretches the right bundle branch → new RBBB or incomplete RBBB (rSR' in V1, wide S in I and V6)
- **RV strain repolarization**: T-wave inversions in V1-V4 (anterior leads overlying the strained RV) — the repolarization sequence is reversed in the acutely overloaded RV
- **Right atrial overload**: Elevated RV end-diastolic pressure transmits backward to the RA → peaked P-waves in lead II (P-pulmonale)
- **Sinus tachycardia**: Sympathetic activation from hypoxemia, pain, decreased cardiac output, and catecholamine release → most common ECG finding
- **Atrial arrhythmias**: RA dilation and stretch can trigger atrial fibrillation or flutter

### 1.3 Why It Appears on ECG
- The acute rightward shift of the mean QRS vector projects a larger force toward right-sided leads (V1, III, aVR) and away from left-sided leads (I, aVL, V5-V6)
- V1-V4 overlay the acutely strained RV → T-wave inversions appear here as the RV repolarization sequence is disrupted
- Lead I sees the rightward terminal vector moving away → deep S-wave (the "S1" of S1Q3T3)
- Lead III sees the rightward vector approaching → Q-wave formation; RV strain causes T-wave inversion (the "Q3T3")
- The pattern is ACUTE and DYNAMIC — it appears suddenly (unlike chronic RVH which develops gradually) and resolves as the PE resolves or the RV adapts
- CRITICAL: ECG is INSENSITIVE for PE — up to 25% of PE patients have a completely normal ECG, and isolated sinus tachycardia (another 25-40%) is nonspecific. The ECG findings of PE represent the severe end of the hemodynamic spectrum.

## 2. ECG Presentation — Lead by Lead

### 2.1 Diagnostic Criteria (2025 AHA/ESC Guidelines)

| Criterion | Threshold | Source |
|-----------|-----------|--------|
| **Sinus tachycardia** | Heart rate >100 bpm — most COMMON finding (~40-50% of PE) | 2019 ESC PE Guidelines |
| **S1Q3T3 (McGinn-White)** | S-wave ≥1.5 mm in I + Q-wave in III + inverted T in III — present in ~20% | McGinn & White 1935; 2019 ESC |
| **New RBBB** | rSR' in V1 with QRS ≥120 ms + wide S in I, V6 — present in ~15-25% of massive PE | 2019 ESC PE Guidelines |
| **New incomplete RBBB** | rSR' in V1 with QRS 100-119 ms | 2019 ESC PE Guidelines |
| **T-wave inversions V1-V4** | Inverted T-waves in ≥2 consecutive anterior leads — RV strain; present in ~35% of submassive/massive PE | 2019 ESC PE Guidelines |
| **Right axis deviation** | QRS axis > +90° (or acute rightward shift from prior ECG) | 2019 ESC PE Guidelines |
| **P-pulmonale** | Peaked P-wave >2.5 mm in lead II — right atrial overload | Classic criterion |
| **Clockwise rotation** | R/S transition point shifted to V5-V6 (late precordial transition) | Classic criterion |
| **ST elevation V1** | ST elevation in V1 (±V2) — acute RV injury | Associated with massive PE |
| **New atrial fibrillation** | New-onset AFib in clinical context of PE | 2019 ESC PE Guidelines |
| **Low voltage** | QRS <5 mm in all limb leads | Nonspecific; may occur with RV failure |
| **Normal ECG** | Completely normal — does NOT exclude PE | Up to 25% of confirmed PE |

**Daniel Score (ECG Severity Score for PE — correlates with RV dysfunction):**

| Finding | Points |
|---------|--------|

### 2.2 Lead-by-Lead Manifestation

| Lead | Expected Finding | Why (Vector Explanation) | Sensitivity |
|------|-----------------|------------------------|-------------|
| I | Deep S-wave (≥1.5 mm) — the "S1" of S1Q3T3; may also show low R-wave amplitude | Acute rightward axis shift moves the terminal QRS vector AWAY from lead I (which faces leftward) → prominent S-wave | Moderate (~20-25% for S1Q3T3 complete pattern) |
| II | Sinus tachycardia; peaked P-wave (P-pulmonale >2.5 mm) if RA overload present; ST usually normal | Lead II faces inferiorly — tachycardia is the most visible finding here; RA strain produces peaked P-waves | High for tachycardia; Low for P-pulmonale |
| III | Q-wave + T-wave inversion — the "Q3T3" of S1Q3T3; possible mild ST elevation | Acute rightward shift directs the initial QRS vector away from lead III (Q-wave) then toward it; RV strain reverses repolarization → T inversion in III | Moderate (~20-25% for Q3T3) |
| aVR | ST elevation (reciprocal to diffuse ST-T changes); possible tall R-wave with rightward axis | aVR faces rightward-superior — with extreme RAD, the QRS vector may project toward aVR; ST elevation reflects reciprocal changes | Low |
| aVL | Deep S-wave; low R-wave amplitude; possible T-wave inversion | aVL faces leftward-superior — the rightward axis shift moves QRS vector away from aVL | Low |
| aVF | Possible Q-wave (if III has Q-wave, aVF may too); T-wave may be inverted; P-pulmonale | aVF faces inferiorly — partial reflection of the rightward shift and RV strain | Low-Moderate |
| V1 | rSR' pattern (RBBB or incomplete RBBB); possible ST elevation (RV injury); T-wave inversion (RV strain); peaked P-wave | V1 overlies the RV outflow tract — acute RV dilation and conduction delay produce RBBB pattern; RV strain directly beneath V1 produces T inversion; ST elevation indicates RV injury | High — V1 is the most informative single lead for PE |
| V2 | Possible rSR' continuation; T-wave inversion (RV strain); ST changes variable | V2 overlies the RV/septum — RV strain pattern continues here; T inversion reflects anterior RV involvement | Moderate-High |
| V3 | T-wave inversion (RV strain); transition to normal precordial pattern may be delayed (clockwise rotation) | V3 is the typical transition zone — clockwise rotation from RV dilation shifts transition rightward, delaying R-wave progression | Moderate |
| V4 | T-wave inversion may extend here in massive PE; delayed R-wave progression (clockwise rotation) | V4 begins to face the LV — T inversions extending to V4 indicate more extensive RV strain; poor R progression reflects clockwise rotation | Moderate (indicates larger PE if T inversion extends here) |
| V5 | Usually normal; may show late R-wave transition (clockwise rotation); possible small S-wave | V5 faces the LV — mostly spared unless massive PE causes global hemodynamic compromise | Low |
| V6 | Usually normal; wide S-wave if RBBB present | V6 faces the lateral LV — wide terminal S from RBBB; otherwise mostly unaffected | Low (S-wave in RBBB only) |

### 2.3 Key Leads (Most Diagnostic)
- **V1**: Single most informative lead — shows RBBB pattern (rSR'), T-wave inversion, possible ST elevation from RV injury, and peaked P-wave (RA overload)
- **V1-V4 (as a group)**: Anterior T-wave inversions (RV strain pattern) — the MORE leads that show T inversion, the MORE severe the RV overload
- **Lead I**: Deep S-wave (S1 component) — watch for acute appearance on serial ECG
- **Lead III**: Q-wave + T-wave inversion (Q3T3 component) — combined with S1, completes the McGinn-White pattern
- **Lead II**: Sinus tachycardia (most common finding) and P-pulmonale
- The COMBINATION of findings across leads is more important than any single lead — isolated S1Q3T3 has low sensitivity but combined with tachycardia + RBBB + anterior T inversions, specificity for acute RV overload increases substantially

### 2.4 Beat-by-Beat Considerations
- ECG findings in PE are generally CONSTANT across beats (no beat-to-beat variability in the RV strain pattern)
- Sinus tachycardia may show rate variability with respiration or sympathetic fluctuations
- If new-onset atrial fibrillation is triggered by PE, the ventricular response will be irregularly irregular — the RV strain pattern (T inversions V1-V4) persists across beats despite the irregular rhythm
- In massive PE with hemodynamic collapse, sinus bradycardia may appear (ominous — pulseless electrical activity and electromechanical dissociation are pre-terminal)
- Acute changes evolve over hours — serial ECGs may show progression from sinus tachycardia alone to the full S1Q3T3 + RBBB + anterior T inversion pattern as RV failure worsens

## 3. Morphology Details (What the Agent Must See)

### 3.1 P-wave Changes
- **Morphology**: Peaked, tall P-waves in lead II (P-pulmonale) — reflecting acute right atrial dilation and pressure overload; peaked P-waves may also be visible in III and aVF
- **Duration change**: Normal or mildly shortened (right atrial activation is faster with higher pressure, unlike left atrial enlargement which prolongs P-wave)
- **Axis change**: P-wave axis may shift rightward (>+75°) reflecting right atrial dominance
- **Threshold**: P-wave >2.5 mm in lead II = P-pulmonale; in V1, the initial positive P-wave deflection may be taller than normal

### 3.2 PR Interval Changes
- **Duration**: Normal (120-200 ms); no direct effect on AV conduction from PE
- **Pattern**: Constant; may appear shortened relative to baseline due to sinus tachycardia (shorter PR at faster rates)

### 3.3 QRS Complex Changes
- **Duration**: Normal to wide. If new RBBB: ≥120 ms with rSR' in V1. If incomplete RBBB: 100-119 ms with rSR' in V1. Normal QRS duration does not exclude PE.
- **Morphology**: rSR' (or rsR') in V1 with RBBB; deep S-wave in leads I and V6 (RBBB); Q-wave in lead III (and sometimes aVF) from acute rightward axis shift — this Q3 is typically narrow (<40 ms) and shallow, NOT the deep wide Q of prior MI
- **Amplitude**: May be low voltage globally if RV failure causes reduced cardiac output or if large PE reduces effective cardiac electrical generation; not a primary finding
- **Axis**: Right axis deviation (>+90°); in some cases extreme RAD (>+120°). Acute axis shift from prior ECG is more diagnostic than absolute axis value. The term "SI-QIII pattern" (prominent S in I, Q in III) reflects the rightward axis shift.
- **Clockwise rotation**: Late precordial transition — R/S ratio does not reach 1:1 until V5 or V6 (normally occurs at V3-V4). This reflects RV dilation rotating the heart clockwise when viewed from below.

### 3.4 ST Segment Changes
- **Direction**: Elevation possible in V1 (±V2) — reflects acute RV injury; depression possible in lateral leads (I, aVL, V5-V6) from reciprocal changes
- **Morphology**: ST elevation in V1 is typically concave or straight; not convex (convex would suggest STEMI involving the right coronary artery)
- **Measurement point**: J-point
- **Clinical significance**: ST elevation in V1 in the setting of sinus tachycardia + RBBB + T inversions V1-V4 strongly suggests massive PE with RV injury. Isolated ST elevation in V1 is nonspecific.

### 3.5 T-wave Changes
- **Direction**: Inverted in V1-V4 (RV strain pattern) — the hallmark repolarization change of PE. May extend to V1-V3 in submassive PE or V1-V6 in massive PE. Also inverted in III (part of S1Q3T3) and sometimes aVF.
- **Amplitude**: Variable depth; typically 1-5 mm inverted; deeper inversions correlate with more severe RV strain
- **Symmetry**: Typically symmetric and deep (similar to ischemic T-wave inversions, NOT asymmetric like LVH strain)
- **Specific patterns**: The "RV strain pattern" (T inversions V1-V3 or V1-V4) is the single most common ECG abnormality after sinus tachycardia in confirmed PE. It can mimic Wellens syndrome (anterior ischemia) or anterior non-ST-elevation MI — clinical context is essential.

### 3.6 QT/QTc Changes
- **QTc**: May be prolonged due to RV strain and myocardial ischemia; not a primary diagnostic feature
- **Clinical significance**: QTc prolongation in PE correlates with severity of RV dysfunction
- **Torsades de Pointes risk**: Not a primary concern in PE

### 3.7 Other Features
- **Sinus tachycardia**: The MOST common and often the ONLY ECG finding. Present in ~40-50% of all PE cases. Nonspecific but in the right clinical context (dyspnea, chest pain, hypoxemia, DVT risk factors), unexplained sinus tachycardia should raise PE suspicion.
- **New atrial fibrillation or flutter**: RA dilation can trigger atrial arrhythmias. New-onset AFib in a patient with dyspnea and hypoxemia should prompt PE workup.
- **Sinus bradycardia in massive PE**: Ominous sign — suggests severe hemodynamic compromise, impending PEA arrest. Bradycardia in the setting of PE is a pre-terminal finding.
- **Right ventricular injury pattern**: ST elevation in V1 with T-wave inversion — mimics anterior STEMI but the distribution (V1 only, not V1-V4) and context differ. If V1 ST elevation + III ST elevation + I ST depression → consider RV MI (from right coronary occlusion) vs massive PE — both cause RV injury.

## 4. Differential Diagnosis

### 4.1 Mimics (What Looks Like This But Isn't)

| Mimic Condition | Shared Features | Distinguishing Features |
|----------------|-----------------|----------------------|
| **Anterior STEMI / Wellens** | T-wave inversions V1-V4; ST changes | STEMI: ST elevation in V1-V4 with reciprocal inferior depression; troponin rise with typical curve; no RBBB or S1Q3T3; coronary territory distribution. Wellens: biphasic (Type A) or deep symmetric (Type B) T-waves V2-V3; pain-free when ECG is recorded; no tachycardia; no RBBB |
| **Right ventricular MI** | ST elevation V1; RV strain; possible RBBB | RV MI: accompanies inferior STEMI (ST elevation II, III, aVF); ST elevation in right-sided leads (V4R); no S1Q3T3; no anterior T inversions; acute coronary syndrome presentation |
| **Chronic RVH (from pulmonary hypertension, COPD)** | RAD; RV strain T inversions; P-pulmonale; RBBB | Chronic: findings are STABLE on serial ECGs; tall R-wave in V1 (R/S >1) from longstanding RVH; no acute onset; no sinus tachycardia from acute event |
| **Acute exacerbation of COPD** | Sinus tachycardia; RAD; P-pulmonale; low voltage; poor R-wave progression | COPD: typically has pre-existing low voltage and RAD; no new RBBB; no S1Q3T3; no acute T inversions; clinical context of bronchospasm, not embolic event |
| **Pneumothorax (tension)** | Sinus tachycardia; RAD; low voltage; acute dyspnea | Pneumothorax: low voltage predominantly on the AFFECTED side; no RBBB; no S1Q3T3; absent breath sounds on exam; chest X-ray diagnostic |
| **Hyperventilation/anxiety** | Sinus tachycardia; nonspecific ST-T changes | No RBBB; no RAD; no S1Q3T3; no anterior T inversions; ECG normalizes when tachycardia resolves |
| **Brugada syndrome** | rSR' in V1; ST changes in V1-V3 | Brugada: coved ST elevation in V1-V3 (Type 1) or saddle-back pattern (Type 2); no sinus tachycardia; no S1Q3T3; no RAD; no clinical PE symptoms; may have family history of sudden death |

### 4.2 Coexisting Conditions
- **PE + pre-existing RBBB**: If RBBB is chronic and known, loss of this as a "new" finding reduces its diagnostic value. The system should compare to prior ECG if available. New T-wave inversions V1-V4 SUPERIMPOSED on known RBBB are still valuable for PE detection.
- **PE + atrial fibrillation**: AFib may be triggered by PE (new-onset) or pre-existing. The RV strain pattern (T inversions V1-V4, RBBB, RAD) is still assessable despite irregular rhythm. Loss of P-waves eliminates P-pulmonale as a criterion.
- **PE + COPD**: Both produce RAD, P-pulmonale, and low voltage. The ACUTE appearance of S1Q3T3 or new RBBB in a COPD patient suggests superimposed PE. Serial ECGs are invaluable.
- **PE + concurrent MI**: Both can occur simultaneously (especially in critically ill patients). Territorial ST elevation (coronary distribution) + S1Q3T3 and RBBB → suspect both MI and PE. Troponin is elevated in both. CTPA and coronary angiography may both be needed.

## 5. STAT Classification

| Priority | Criteria |
|----------|----------|
| **STAT** | Yes — massive PE is a STAT emergency (hemodynamic instability, RV failure, cardiac arrest risk). Submassive PE is time-sensitive (RV dysfunction without hemodynamic collapse). However, the ECG CANNOT diagnose PE — it can only RAISE SUSPICION. The system must clearly communicate: "ECG findings suggestive of acute RV strain — clinical correlation for PE recommended." |
| **Time-sensitive** | Critical — massive PE has ~25-65% mortality if untreated; systemic thrombolysis or catheter-directed therapy must be initiated within minutes to hours. Even submassive PE benefits from early anticoagulation and close monitoring. |
| **Clinical action** | If ECG raises PE suspicion: immediate CTPA (CT pulmonary angiography) or bedside echocardiography (RV dilation, McConnell's sign) if too unstable for CT; initiate anticoagulation (heparin) if clinical probability is high; for massive PE with hemodynamic instability: systemic thrombolysis (alteplase), catheter-directed therapy, or surgical embolectomy; supportive care (fluids, vasopressors, supplemental oxygen). |

## 6. Reasoning Complexity Analysis (Feeds Into Node 2.1 — Agent Architecture Research)

> **NOTE**: This section does NOT pre-assign agents. It documents the reasoning

> architecture to handle ALL conditions. The actual agent assignment is filled
> in AFTER Node 2.1 research completes.

### 6.1 Reasoning Domains Required to Detect This Condition
- **Rate analysis**: Sinus tachycardia detection — fundamental and straightforward
- **Rhythm analysis**: Detection of new atrial fibrillation/flutter
- **Morphology analysis**: Recognition of rSR' pattern in V1 (RBBB); Q-wave in III; S-wave depth in I; T-wave inversion pattern across V1-V4
- **Axis calculation**: Rightward axis deviation; acute axis shift from prior ECG
- **Lead-group correlation**: Must integrate findings across MULTIPLE lead groups — S in I (lateral) + Q and T inversion in III (inferior) + RBBB in V1 + T inversions V1-V4 (anterior) — this is inherently a MULTI-TERRITORY assessment
- **P-wave analysis**: P-pulmonale detection (peaked P >2.5 mm in II)
- **Pattern recognition**: S1Q3T3 as a composite pattern; "RV strain" as a multi-lead pattern
- **Probabilistic reasoning**: Required — no single ECG finding is diagnostic of PE. The system must assess the COMBINATION of findings and their cumulative probability. More findings = higher suspicion. The system should output a PROBABILITY assessment, not a binary diagnosis.
- **Clinical context integration**: ECG findings alone are insufficient — the system must clearly communicate that PE is a clinical diagnosis aided by ECG, not an ECG diagnosis.

### 6.2 Feature Dependencies
- **ESSENTIAL features**: Heart rate (tachycardia); QRS morphology in V1 (RBBB detection); T-wave direction in V1-V4 (RV strain); S-wave amplitude in lead I; Q-wave and T-wave in lead III; QRS axis
- **SUPPORTING features**: P-wave amplitude in lead II (P-pulmonale); precordial R-wave transition point (clockwise rotation); ST segment in V1 (RV injury); QRS voltage (low voltage); rhythm (new AFib)
- **EXCLUSION features**: None that definitively exclude PE — a completely NORMAL ECG does NOT exclude PE. However, if the ECG shows clear STEMI pattern (territorial ST elevation with reciprocal depression and evolving Q-waves), the diagnosis shifts from PE to MI (though both can coexist).
- **Per-beat vs aggregate**: Aggregate for most features; per-beat only if assessing for electrical alternans (suggests effusion/tamponade, not PE specifically) or irregular rhythm (new AFib)

### 6.3 Cross-Condition Interactions
- **PE vs anterior STEMI**: T-wave inversions V1-V4 can be identical in appearance. Differentiators: PE has S1Q3T3, sinus tachycardia, RBBB, RAD; STEMI has territorial ST elevation, reciprocal depression, evolving Q-waves, no S1Q3T3. The system MUST consider both diagnoses when anterior T inversions are present.
- **PE vs Wellens syndrome**: Both show anterior T-wave inversions. Wellens: biphasic or deep symmetric T-waves specifically in V2-V3; occurs during pain-free intervals; no tachycardia, RBBB, or S1Q3T3.
- **PE findings obscured by pre-existing LBBB**: LBBB alters the entire QRS-ST-T morphology, making S1Q3T3, RBBB, and anterior T inversions uninterpretable. In the setting of LBBB, ECG has essentially no value for PE detection.
- **PE + WPW**: Delta waves can produce pseudo-Q waves and alter the QRS axis, potentially mimicking or masking S1Q3T3. WPW should be identified first; if present, PE ECG criteria are unreliable.

### 6.4 Reasoning Chain Sketch
**Minimum reasoning chain (sinus tachycardia + suspicion):**
1. Detect sinus tachycardia (>100 bpm)
2. In clinical context of dyspnea/chest pain/hypoxemia → flag: "Unexplained sinus tachycardia — consider PE among differentials"

**Full reasoning chain (comprehensive RV strain pattern):**
1. Measure heart rate → tachycardia present?
2. Assess QRS in V1 → rSR' pattern? → new RBBB or incomplete RBBB?
3. Assess T-waves V1-V4 → inversions present? → how many consecutive leads? (more = worse)
4. Assess lead I → deep S-wave present?
5. Assess lead III → Q-wave present? T-wave inverted?
6. S1 + Q3 + T3 → S1Q3T3 pattern?
7. Calculate QRS axis → RAD present? (>+90°)
8. Assess P-wave in lead II → peaked >2.5 mm (P-pulmonale)?
9. Assess precordial transition → delayed (clockwise rotation)?
10. Assess V1 ST segment → ST elevation (RV injury)?
11. Check for new AFib
12. Count the number of positive findings → compute Daniel score
13. Synthesize → "ECG shows [N] findings suggestive of acute RV strain (Daniel score [X]/21). In the appropriate clinical context, this pattern raises suspicion for pulmonary embolism. ECG cannot confirm or exclude PE — recommend CTPA or bedside echocardiography."

### 6.5 Confidence Anchors
- **HIGH confidence (for RV strain, not PE itself)**: S1Q3T3 + new RBBB + T inversions V1-V4 + sinus tachycardia + RAD → classic acute RV strain pattern highly suggestive of PE (or other acute RV overload)
- **MODERATE confidence**: Sinus tachycardia + T inversions V1-V3 without S1Q3T3 or RBBB → nonspecific but concerning for RV strain
- **LOW confidence**: Isolated sinus tachycardia → present in many conditions; very low specificity for PE
- **Pathognomonic**: NONE — no ECG pattern is pathognomonic for PE. Even the full S1Q3T3 + RBBB + anterior T inversions pattern can occur in any cause of acute RV overload (acute pulmonary hypertension, ARDS, massive pneumothorax). The system must ALWAYS communicate this uncertainty.
- **Absent features that LOWER confidence**: Normal heart rate + normal axis + no RBBB + normal T-waves in V1-V4 + no S1Q3T3 → PE is still possible (ECG is normal in ~25% of PE), but the ECG does NOT raise PE suspicion
- **The more findings, the more severe**: Number of PE ECG findings correlates with PE severity — isolated sinus tach = possibly small PE; full pattern = likely massive PE with significant RV dysfunction

| Dimension | Score (1-5) | Notes |

| Signal clarity needed | 2 | Most features (RBBB, T inversions, tachycardia) are robust to moderate noise; S1Q3T3 requires clear S-wave measurement |

## 6A. Agent Assignment

**Architecture:** Option E — 3 Parallel Phase 1 Specialists + 1 Sequential CDS (Node 2.1 Gate Passed 2026-03-27)

| Agent | Role for Pulmonary Embolism | Key Features Used |
|-------|--------------------------|-------------------|
| **RRC** (Rhythm/Rate/Conduction) | Primary (co-primary) | Sinus tachycardia (most common finding, rate typically 100–130 bpm), new right axis deviation, S1 detection in lead I (deep S-wave ≥1.5 mm), Q3 detection in lead III, new RBBB or incomplete RBBB (acute right heart strain), atrial arrhythmias (atrial flutter/fibrillation from right atrial dilation) |
| **IT** (Ischemia/Territory) | Supporting | ST depression in V1–V4 (must NOT be classified as primary anterior ischemia — represents RV strain, not LAD territory ischemia); flags for CDS when ST changes are detected in V1–V4 without anterior territory elevation |
| **MR** (Morphology/Repolarization) | Primary (co-primary) | S1Q3T3 pattern morphology (S-wave in I, Q-wave in III, T-wave inversion in III), T-wave inversions V1–V4 (right precordial strain pattern), incomplete or complete RBBB morphology, P pulmonale (tall P-wave in II ≥2.5 mm), right axis deviation QRS morphology |
| **CDS** (Cross-Domain Synthesis) | Required — integrates RRC + MR + IT to generate RV strain composite score | Combines RRC's sinus tachycardia + right axis + S1 + RBBB findings with MR's S1Q3T3 + T-wave inversion pattern + IT's non-territorial ST depression to generate a probabilistic RV strain composite score; flags that ECG cannot definitively diagnose PE |

### Primary Agent
Both **RRC** and **MR** are co-primary: RRC owns the rhythm and axis components (sinus tachycardia, right axis, S1 component), while MR owns the morphology components (S1Q3T3 pattern, T-wave inversions V1–V4, RBBB morphology). Neither alone is sufficient for PE pattern recognition.

### Cross-Domain Hints
- IT emits `cross_domain_hint: "ST depression V1–V4 detected without anterior ST elevation — RV strain pattern possible; do not classify as primary anterior ischemia; forward to CDS for PE composite scoring"` when non-territorial anterior ST depression is identified.
- RRC emits `cross_domain_hint: "Sinus tachycardia with new right axis deviation and/or RBBB — acute RV strain possible; MR S1Q3T3 morphology required for PE pattern assessment"` when these rhythm findings coexist.

### CDS Specific Role
CDS integrates the multi-domain PE pattern: sinus tachycardia (RRC) + right axis deviation (RRC) + S1Q3T3 (MR) + T-wave inversions V1–V4 (MR) + non-territorial ST depression V1–V4 (IT) + RBBB (RRC/MR). CDS generates a PE probability composite score based on the number and specificity of findings present, while explicitly noting that ECG cannot diagnose PE and that CT pulmonary angiography is required for confirmation. CDS also differentiates chronic RVH pattern (pre-existing right axis + RVH voltage) from acute RV strain (new axis deviation + tachycardia) using the acuity of the findings.

### 8.3 Clinician Explanation (Plain Language)
- **ER nurse**: "This ECG shows signs that the right side of the heart is under stress (RV strain pattern). This can be caused by a blood clot in the lungs (pulmonary embolism) but the ECG alone cannot confirm it. A CT scan of the lungs (CTPA) is needed to check for a blood clot. Please flag this to the physician immediately."
- **Cardiologist**: "ECG demonstrates an acute RV strain pattern: [list specific findings — e.g., sinus tachycardia at [X] bpm, S1Q3T3, new incomplete RBBB, T-wave inversions V1-V[N], rightward axis at [X]°]. Daniel score: [X]/21. These findings are consistent with acute right ventricular pressure overload. Differential includes pulmonary embolism, acute pulmonary hypertension, or other cause of acute cor pulmonale. ECG sensitivity for PE is limited (~50% for submassive/massive PE, much lower for subsegmental PE). Recommend CTPA if clinical suspicion warrants; bedside echocardiography for RV dilation/McConnell's sign if hemodynamically unstable."

## 9. Edge Cases and Pitfalls

- **Normal ECG does NOT exclude PE**: This is the single most important point. Up to 25% of confirmed PE patients have a completely normal ECG. The system must NEVER report "PE excluded based on ECG." If clinical suspicion exists, imaging is required regardless of ECG.
- **Isolated sinus tachycardia**: The most common ECG finding in PE is also the most nonspecific. Sinus tachycardia alone is present in dozens of conditions (fever, pain, anxiety, dehydration, anemia, hyperthyroidism, etc.). The system should flag it as a PE consideration only when combined with clinical context or other ECG findings.
- **S1Q3T3 without PE**: S1Q3T3 can be a normal variant in some individuals, especially with a vertical heart axis. It can also occur in acute RV overload from ANY cause (acute pulmonary hypertension, massive pneumothorax, ARDS). The finding is suggestive but not diagnostic.
- **Anterior T-wave inversions mimicking ACS**: T inversions V1-V4 from PE can be indistinguishable from anterior ischemia (Wellens, NSTEMI). The presence of sinus tachycardia + RBBB + S1Q3T3 + RAD shifts the differential toward PE. The absence of these supporting findings makes anterior ischemia more likely. When in doubt, both CTPA and cardiology consultation may be needed.
- **Chronic RVH vs acute PE**: Chronic RVH shows a stable R-wave dominance in V1 (R/S >1), RAD, and RV strain pattern that has been present for months to years. Acute PE shows NEW findings that were not on prior ECGs. Comparison to a prior ECG is invaluable.
- **PE in the setting of LBBB**: LBBB fundamentally alters the QRS and ST-T pattern, making S1Q3T3, RBBB, and anterior T inversions uninterpretable. In LBBB, ECG cannot contribute to PE assessment. The system should report: "Pre-existing LBBB — ECG findings for PE are unreliable."
- **Submassive vs massive PE**: Massive PE (with hemodynamic instability) typically shows more ECG findings (full S1Q3T3 + RBBB + diffuse anterior T inversions + marked tachycardia). Submassive PE may show only T inversions V1-V3. Small/segmental PE may have a completely normal ECG.
- **PE in young patients**: Younger patients may have higher compensatory capacity — ECG may remain normal despite hemodynamically significant PE because the RV compensates better initially. Normal ECG in a young patient with PE risk factors should not provide false reassurance.
- **Serial ECG evolution**: PE ECG changes can evolve over hours. Initial ECG may show only sinus tachycardia; repeat ECG 2-4 hours later may show emerging S1Q3T3 and T inversions as RV failure progresses. Serial ECGs increase diagnostic yield.
- **Post-thrombolysis or post-anticoagulation**: As PE resolves, ECG findings reverse — tachycardia resolves first, then RBBB, then S1Q3T3, and T inversions resolve last (may persist for weeks). Persistent T inversions V1-V4 after PE treatment are common and do not necessarily indicate ongoing PE.
