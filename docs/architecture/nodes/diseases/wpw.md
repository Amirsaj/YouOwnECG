## 1. Pathophysiology Root Cause

### 1.1 Anatomic Substrate

WPW results from an accessory pathway (Bundle of Kent) — an anomalous muscular bridge connecting atrial and ventricular myocardium outside the normal AV node/His-Purkinje axis. The pathway bypasses the AV node, which normally imposes a 120–200ms conduction delay protecting the ventricles from excessively rapid atrial rates. Pathways can be located anywhere around the AV ring: right free wall, left free wall, posteroseptal, anteroseptal. Location determines the ECG appearance of the delta wave in each lead.

### 1.2 Cellular Mechanism

In sinus rhythm, each impulse conducts simultaneously via the normal AV node–His-Purkinje system AND via the accessory pathway. Because the accessory pathway lacks the AV node's decremental conduction properties, it depolarizes adjacent ventricular myocardium before the normal Purkinje wavefront arrives — creating early (pre-excited) ventricular activation. This produces the delta wave: a slow, slurred initial QRS upstroke reflecting cell-to-cell myocardial conduction rather than fast Purkinje propagation. The terminal portion of QRS is completed normally via the His-Purkinje system as the two wavefronts merge (fusion complex).

### 1.3 The Lethal Mechanism — Pre-Excited AFib

The AV node's decremental conduction acts as a physiological rate limiter. During atrial fibrillation, the AV node typically limits ventricular rate to 100–180 bpm. The accessory pathway has NO such rate-limiting property and can conduct impulses at 200–300+ bpm. AFib in a WPW patient conducting primarily via the accessory pathway produces very rapid, irregular, wide-complex QRS complexes. If the shortest RR interval is <250ms, the pathway is capable of conducting fast enough to degenerate into ventricular fibrillation. This is the mechanism of sudden cardiac death in WPW.

## 2. ECG Presentation — Lead by Lead

### 2.1 Diagnostic Criteria (2025 AHA/ESC Guidelines)

Classic WPW (manifest pre-excitation):
1. PR interval <120ms (AV node bypassed — conduction reaches ventricle early)
2. Delta wave: slurred, slowly rising initial QRS upstroke in one or more leads
3. Wide QRS: total duration >120ms (fusion of pre-excited and normally conducted activation)
4. Secondary ST-T changes: discordant to the delta wave direction (repolarization abnormality from abnormal depolarization)

WPW pattern = ECG findings above without documented tachyarrhythmia.
WPW syndrome = WPW pattern PLUS documented symptomatic tachyarrhythmia.

Intermittent pre-excitation: delta wave present in some beats, absent in others (pathway has longer refractory period at some rates).

### 2.2 Lead-by-Lead Manifestation

| Lead | Expected Finding | Why | Sensitivity |
|------|-----------------|-----|-------------|
| I | Delta wave polarity depends on pathway location; wide QRS | Left lateral pathway: positive delta; right lateral: negative delta | Moderate (location-dependent) |
| II | Delta wave and wide QRS; ST-T discordant | Inferior lead; reflects inferior pathway component | Moderate |
| III | Delta wave; most sensitive for inferolateral pathway location | Negative delta III = inferolateral pathway | High for inferolateral |
| aVR | Positive delta aVR = left lateral pathway | aVR points rightward; left lateral pre-excitation creates rightward initial vector | High for left lateral |
| aVL | Variable; positive in right-sided pathways | Superior lead — right-sided pre-excitation aims early vector toward aVL | Moderate |
| aVF | Delta wave; positive or negative depending on pathway | Inferior lead; inferior pathway location: positive; superior: negative | Moderate |
| V1 | KEY LEAD: negative delta V1 = right-sided pathway; positive delta V1 = left-sided pathway | Right-sided pathways activate right ventricle first, generating leftward initial vector (negative in V1); left-sided pathways do opposite | Very high |
| V2 | Negative or positive delta; confirms V1 finding | Same principle as V1 | High |
| V3 | Transitional; delta wave present | Transition from negative to positive with left-to-right pathway spectrum | Moderate |
| V4 | Usually positive delta in left-sided pathways | Precordial transition zone | Moderate |
| V5 | Usually positive delta | Left lateral lead; left-sided pre-excitation strongly positive | High |
| V6 | Positive delta in most configurations | Most lateral precordial lead | High |
| All leads | Short PR <120ms | AV node bypass — impulse reaches ventricle early via accessory pathway | Very high |

### 2.3 Key Leads

- **V1**: Primary lead for right vs. left pathway lateralization — negative delta = right-sided; positive delta (or dominant R) = left-sided
- **aVR**: Positive delta in aVR points to left lateral pathway
- **III**: Negative delta in III = inferolateral pathway location
- **I and aVL**: Negative delta = left lateral pathway
- **II, III, aVF**: Negative delta in all three inferior leads = superior pathway location (anteroseptal)

### 2.4 Beat-by-Beat Considerations

In patients with intermittent pre-excitation, delta waves may appear and disappear across the rhythm strip — particularly rate-dependent. The disappearance of delta waves at faster rates is often paradoxically reassuring (pathway has a long refractory period and cannot conduct at fast rates). The appearance of pre-excitation only at slow rates may indicate enhanced AV nodal conduction masking the pre-excitation at faster rates.

During pre-excited AFib: QRS complexes are irregular, wide, and morphologically vary beat-to-beat due to changing degrees of fusion between accessory pathway and AV node conduction. The combination of IRREGULAR + WIDE + VERY FAST (>200 bpm) = pre-excited AFib until proven otherwise.

## 3. Morphology Details

### 3.1 PR Interval

<120ms. In some patients with slow accessory pathways (decremental conduction), PR may be near-normal — these pathways are less dangerous but harder to detect. The true PR measurement is from P onset to delta wave onset, not to the first sharp QRS deflection.

### 3.2 Delta Wave Characteristics

- Gradual, slurring upstroke lasting 20–70ms
- Polarity varies by lead (see pathway localization tables)
- May be isoelectric in some leads (appears absent) — do not conclude no pre-excitation from one lead
- Pseudo-Q waves: a negative delta wave in a lead appears as a Q wave — mimics myocardial infarction
- Pseudo-R' wave: a positive delta wave in V1 may mimic RBBB

### 3.3 QRS Width

120–180ms typical. Width reflects degree of pre-excitation — greater accessory pathway conduction proportion → wider QRS. If AV node conduction speeds up (sympathetic activation), QRS may narrow as more activation proceeds via normal pathway (less delta contribution).

### 3.4 QRS Axis

Determined by pathway location. Left lateral pathway: right-axis or normal axis. Right lateral pathway: left-axis deviation. Inferior pathway: superior axis.

### 3.5 ST-T Wave Changes

Always present as secondary changes — discordant to the terminal QRS direction. If delta wave is positive in a lead, expect ST depression and T-wave inversion in that lead. These are NOT ischemic changes; they reflect abnormal repolarization from abnormal depolarization. Do NOT interpret as ischemia in a known WPW patient.

### 3.6 Pathway Localization Summary (Delta Wave Polarity Rules)

| Pathway Location | V1 | I | aVF | III | aVR |
|-----------------|-----|---|-----|-----|-----|
| Left lateral | Positive | Negative | Positive | Variable | Positive |
| Left posterolateral | Positive | Negative | Negative | Negative | Positive |
| Posteroseptal (left) | Positive | Positive | Negative | Negative | Negative |
| Right free wall | Negative | Positive | Positive | Variable | Negative |
| Right anteroseptal | Negative/isoelectric | Positive | Positive | Positive | Negative |
| Right posteroseptal | Negative | Positive | Negative | Negative | Variable |

### 3.7 Pre-Excited AFib — Morphology

- Irregular rhythm (critical distinction from antidromic AVRT which is regular)
- Wide QRS complexes with delta waves
- QRS morphology varies beat-to-beat (fusion ratios change)
- Shortest pre-excited RR interval <250ms = HIGH RISK for degeneration to VF
- Shortest RR interval >350ms = likely safer but still requires urgent management

## 4. Differential Diagnosis

### 4.1 Mimics

| Mimic | How to Distinguish |
|-------|--------------------|
| Inferior MI (Q-waves in II/III/aVF) | WPW pseudo-Q: short PR, delta wave present; STEMI: normal PR, no delta, ST elevation |
| Anterior MI (Q-waves V1–V4) | Negative delta V1–V4 mimics septal Q-waves; short PR differentiates |
| RBBB | RBBB: normal PR, rSR' in V1, no delta; WPW right pathway: short PR, delta, pseudo-rSR' |
| LBBB | LBBB: normal or prolonged PR, no delta; WPW left pathway: short PR, delta |
| LVH (tall R in lateral leads) | LVH: no short PR, no delta, normal QRS duration |
| Antidromic AVRT | Wide regular tachycardia — same as WPW antidromic; requires history and electrophysiology |
| VT | VT: irregular in polymorphic form or regular in monomorphic; no delta; dissociated P-waves |
| Pre-excited AFib vs. VF | Pre-excited AFib: irregular wide QRS with delta; VF: chaotic, no organized complexes |

### 4.2 Coexisting Conditions

- Ebstein's anomaly: strong association with right-sided accessory pathways (multiple pathways common)
- HCM: accessory pathways may coexist; WPW + HCM increases sudden death risk significantly
- Congenital heart disease (corrected transposition, ASD): higher prevalence of accessory pathways
- AFib: in the general WPW population, AFib prevalence is increased; mechanism includes pathway-mediated AVRT triggering AFib

## 5. STAT Classification

| Priority | Criteria |
|----------|---------|
| CRITICAL — Immediate Cardioversion | Pre-excited AFib + hemodynamic instability (hypotension, altered consciousness, chest pain) |
| CRITICAL — Immediate Cardiology/EP Consult | Pre-excited AFib + shortest RR interval <250ms (high VF risk) even if stable |
| STAT — Urgent Management | Pre-excited AFib, stable, any rate — do NOT use adenosine, verapamil, digoxin |
| STAT | Antidromic AVRT (wide complex regular tachycardia in known WPW) |
| HIGH — Urgent Cardiology | WPW pattern + syncope (risk stratification required — EP study) |
| HIGH — Urgent Cardiology | Newly discovered WPW in high-risk occupation (pilot, athlete, driver) |
| ROUTINE — Cardiology Referral | Incidentally discovered WPW pattern, asymptomatic, low-risk features |

**Absolute contraindications in WPW + AFib/flutter:**
- Adenosine: blocks AV node → all conduction forced through accessory pathway → VF
- Verapamil/diltiazem: same mechanism — AV node blockade accelerates accessory pathway conduction
- Digoxin: shortens accessory pathway refractory period — paradoxically increases VF risk

**Safe drugs in pre-excited AFib:** Procainamide (IV) or ibutilide. DC cardioversion if unstable.

## 6. Reasoning Complexity Analysis

### 6.1 Reasoning Domains Required

- Delta wave detection: identify slurring of initial QRS upstroke
- PR interval measurement: confirm <120ms
- QRS width measurement: confirm >120ms
- Pathway localization: multi-lead delta wave polarity analysis
- Tachyarrhythmia recognition: identify AFib, AVRT variants in WPW
- Safety reasoning: identify contraindicated medications in specific WPW scenarios
- Rhythm characterization: regular vs. irregular, narrow vs. wide in tachycardia context

### 6.2 Feature Dependencies

```
Short PR (<120ms)?
    → Yes: look for delta wave
Delta wave present in ≥1 lead?
    → Yes: WPW pattern confirmed
    → Assess QRS width: >120ms? → Manifest pre-excitation
    → Determine rhythm: sinus? AFib? Regular tachycardia?
        → AFib + wide QRS → Pre-excited AFib → CRITICAL DANGER
        → Regular wide QRS tachycardia → Antidromic AVRT vs. VT
        → Regular narrow QRS tachycardia → Orthodromic AVRT (looks like SVT)
Pathway localization:
    → V1 polarity: negative = right-sided; positive = left-sided
    → aVR, I, III: refine to subregion
```

### 6.3 Cross-Condition Interactions

- AFib detection in WPW: standard AFib criteria (irregular, no clear P-waves) apply BUT the QRS is wide — must not be mistaken for VT or polymorphic VT
- STEMI + WPW: ST changes in WPW are routinely abnormal (secondary T-wave inversion). New ST elevation in WPW requires extreme caution — can be masked or exaggerated. ACS workup based on symptoms and troponin, not ECG alone
- Hyperkalemia: slows conduction and widens QRS — can superficially mimic WPW if combined with short PR (rare), but no delta wave morphology
- Rate-dependent BBB: wide QRS in SVT with aberrancy mimics antidromic AVRT — context and full history required

### 6.4 Reasoning Chain Sketch

```
1. Measure PR interval → <120ms? → Pre-excitation candidate
2. Inspect initial QRS upstroke all 12 leads → slurred delta wave?
3. Measure QRS duration → >120ms with delta → manifest WPW
4. Determine rhythm: sinus, AFib, regular tachycardia
5. If AFib: measure shortest RR interval in any 10-beat segment
   → <250ms → CRITICAL alert; measure ALL RR intervals
6. If regular tachycardia + wide QRS: antidromic AVRT vs. VT
7. If regular tachycardia + narrow QRS: orthodromic AVRT (looks like SVT)
8. Localize pathway using delta wave polarity (V1 first, then I, aVF, III)
9. Flag contraindicated medications if AFib/flutter present
10. Assign STAT level
```

### 6.5 Confidence Anchors

- High confidence: Short PR + delta wave + wide QRS in sinus rhythm = manifest WPW
- High confidence: Irregular + wide + very fast tachycardia = pre-excited AFib until proven otherwise
- Moderate confidence: Pathway localization (algorithm-based localization has ~80–85% accuracy vs. EP mapping)
- Low confidence: Distinguishing antidromic AVRT from VT on ECG alone — EP study required
- Note: intermittent pre-excitation in a single resting ECG may show no delta — normal ECG does not exclude WPW

## 6A. Agent Assignment

**Architecture:** Option E — 3 Parallel Phase 1 Specialists + 1 Sequential CDS (Node 2.1 Gate Passed 2026-03-27)

| Agent | Role for WPW | Key Features Used |
|-------|--------------------------|-------------------|
| **RRC** (Rhythm/Rate/Conduction) | Primary (co-primary) | Short PR interval (<120 ms), delta wave at QRS onset (slurred initial upstroke), total QRS duration (prolonged due to delta), AV preexcitation confirmation; AFib detection (irregularly irregular rhythm) triggering emergent WPW+AFib escalation |
| **IT** (Ischemia/Territory) | Supporting | Pseudo-infarction Q-waves from negative delta waves in inferior (Type B: right-sided pathway) or lateral leads must NOT be classified as pathological Q-wave infarction; IT suppresses Q-wave-based territorial MI calls; flags for CDS |
| **MR** (Morphology/Repolarization) | Primary (co-primary) | Delta wave morphology and polarity across all 12 leads (pathway localization: positive delta in V1 = left-sided/Type A; negative delta in II/III/aVF = right-sided/Type B), pseudo-LBBB or RBBB QRS pattern, secondary ST-T changes (discordant repolarization), QRS axis during pre-excitation |
| **CDS** (Cross-Domain Synthesis) | Required — CRITICAL: WPW+AFib escalation; suppresses IT false-positive Q-waves | Receives RRC's AFib detection + MR's delta wave confirmation → generates emergent WPW+AFib diagnosis; suppresses IT's pseudo-infarction Q-wave calls by confirming delta wave morphology; generates pathway localization from MR delta wave polarity analysis |

### Primary Agent
Both **RRC** and **MR** are co-primary: RRC owns the PR interval shortening and preexcitation confirmation, while MR owns the delta wave morphology analysis and pathway localization. Both are required for the complete WPW diagnosis.

### Cross-Domain Hints
- RRC emits `cross_domain_hint: "Short PR (<120 ms) with delta wave and irregularly irregular rhythm — WPW + AFib detected; emergent escalation required; MR delta wave analysis required to confirm accessory pathway"` when AFib accompanies preexcitation.
- IT emits `cross_domain_hint: "Negative delta waves mimicking Q-waves in inferior or lateral leads — pseudo-infarction pattern; MR must confirm delta wave morphology before any territorial MI call is made"` when negative delta waves are identified in infarction-suspicious leads.

### CDS Specific Role
CDS performs three critical functions for WPW: (1) WPW+AFib escalation — when RRC identifies AFib alongside MR's delta wave confirmation, CDS generates the highest-urgency diagnosis with explicit contraindication note (no AV-blocking agents — digoxin, verapamil, diltiazem, adenosine are dangerous); (2) IT false-positive suppression — CDS overrides IT's pseudo-Q-wave territorial MI calls by confirming the delta-wave origin; (3) pathway localization — CDS synthesizes MR's lead-by-lead delta polarity analysis to generate accessory pathway location (left free wall, right free wall, posteroseptal, anteroseptal).

## 9. Edge Cases and Pitfalls

**Intermittent pre-excitation may not be present on a single resting ECG.** A normal ECG does not exclude WPW. If clinical suspicion exists (young patient, syncope, palpitations), exercise stress test or event monitor may unmask pre-excitation.

**Isoelectric delta waves in some leads create "concealed" Q-waves.** The algorithm must check all 12 leads — a negative delta in lead III alone can produce a convincing Q-wave mimicking inferior MI. Short PR is the key differentiator.

**Orthodromic AVRT looks identical to AVNRT or AT on ECG.** Both are narrow-complex regular tachycardias. Retrograde P-waves after QRS (short RP interval) in orthodromic AVRT. RP interval <70ms favors AVNRT; RP 70–100ms favors orthodromic AVRT but unreliable without EP study. Adenosine is SAFE in orthodromic AVRT (AV node is not a critical limb for dangerous conduction).

**Antidromic AVRT vs. VT is a high-stakes distinction.** Both are wide regular tachycardias. Antidromic AVRT: AV dissociation absent, delta-like morphology, known WPW history; VT: AV dissociation, fusion beats, capture beats. When in doubt, treat as VT.

**WPW + AFib starting as apparently narrow QRS.** If AFib begins with primarily AV nodal conduction, QRS may be narrow initially. As the accessory pathway engages and the rate increases, QRS widens. Serial beats must be monitored — progressive widening in AFib is a red flag.

**Concealed accessory pathways.** Some pathways conduct only retrogradely (from ventricle to atrium) — they do NOT produce delta waves or short PR on resting ECG. These pathways still support orthodromic AVRT but cannot cause pre-excited AFib. Not detectable on standard ECG; this node covers manifest (antegrade-conducting) pathways only.
