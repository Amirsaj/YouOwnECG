## 1. Pathophysiology Root Cause

### 1.1 What Goes Wrong

Junctional rhythm occurs when the AV node or the proximal His bundle assumes the role of cardiac pacemaker. This happens by one of two mechanisms:

**Passive (escape) mechanism — sinus node suppression:**
The sinus node slows below the intrinsic rate of the junctional pacemaker (40–60 bpm), allowing the AV junctional tissue to emerge as an escape rhythm. Causes include:
- Inferior myocardial infarction: ischemia of the AV node (supplied by the AV nodal artery, a branch of the RCA in 85–90% of individuals) directly depresses sinus node function and/or enhances vagal tone via Bezold-Jarisch reflex
- Digitalis toxicity: enhances automaticity of junctional tissue; suppresses sinus node
- Beta-blockers, calcium channel blockers (non-dihydropyridine): sinus node depression
- Sick sinus syndrome: sinus node failure → junctional escape
- Post-cardiac surgery: direct surgical trauma to sinus node or AV node
- Increased vagal tone: vasovagal episode, athletes at rest (normal variant)
- Hypoxia, hyperkalaemia

**Active (accelerated / tachycardia) mechanism — enhanced junctional automaticity:**
The junctional pacemaker fires faster than its intrinsic rate due to:
- Digitalis toxicity (most common cause of accelerated junctional rhythm)
- Acute inferior MI (AV nodal ischemia, catecholamines)
- Catecholamine excess, sympathomimetic drugs
- Post-operative state (cardiac surgery, congenital heart disease repair)
- Myocarditis, electrolyte abnormalities (hypokalaemia)
- Junctional tachycardia in congenital heart disease (post-Fontan, post-Tetralogy repair) — a distinct entity

### 1.2 Electrical Consequence

The AV node/His junction activates both the atria and the ventricles from a mid-cardiac location:
- **Ventricular activation**: proceeds normally through the His-Purkinje system → narrow QRS (normal width and morphology)
- **Atrial activation**: conducted retrogradely up to the atria from the AV node → atrial depolarization travels in the OPPOSITE direction from normal sinus activation

Normal sinus P-wave: atria depolarize superior-to-inferior and right-to-left → positive P in II/III/aVF, negative in aVR.
Retrograde junctional P-wave: atria depolarize inferior-to-superior and left-to-right → NEGATIVE P in II/III/aVF, POSITIVE in aVR.

### 1.3 Why It Appears on ECG

Three patterns of P-wave relationship to QRS, depending on conduction velocity in both directions from the junctional focus:

1. **Retrograde P BEFORE QRS** (PR < 120 ms): junctional impulse reaches atria first, then ventricles — retrograde atrial activation completes just before ventricular depolarization
2. **Retrograde P BURIED in QRS**: simultaneous atrial and ventricular activation — P not separately visible, or barely alters QRS terminal portion
3. **Retrograde P AFTER QRS** (RP' < 200 ms): junctional impulse reaches ventricles first, then atria — ventricular depolarization completes before atrial

In all three patterns, QRS is narrow (normal His-Purkinje conduction) and P-wave polarity in inferior leads is inverted.

## 2. ECG Presentation — Lead by Lead

### 2.1 Diagnostic Criteria (2025 AHA/ESC Guidelines)

1. Regular narrow QRS rhythm
2. Rate classification:
   - Junctional escape: 40–60 bpm
   - Accelerated junctional rhythm: 60–100 bpm
   - Junctional tachycardia: >100 bpm (typically 100–130 bpm)
3. Retrograde P-waves: negative in II/III/aVF, positive in aVR
4. P-wave relationship to QRS: before (PR <120 ms), buried, or immediately after QRS
5. QRS morphology: narrow (≤120 ms) unless coexisting bundle branch block or aberrant conduction
6. If AV dissociation present: sinus P-waves march through at slower rate, independent of junctional QRS

### 2.2 Lead-by-Lead Manifestation

| Lead | Finding |
|------|---------|
| I | Narrow QRS; P-wave may be isoelectric, slightly negative, or absent depending on pattern |
| II | KEY: retrograde P-wave — deeply inverted (negative) if visible before or after QRS |
| III | Retrograde P-wave — negative (inverted) |
| aVR | Retrograde P-wave — POSITIVE (upright) — diagnostic of retrograde atrial activation |
| aVL | P-wave variable; often isoelectric or weakly positive |
| aVF | Retrograde P-wave — negative (inverted) |
| V1 | Narrow QRS; retrograde P may be positive or biphasic if visible (atrial activation from inferior-to-superior in frontal plane, variable in horizontal plane) |
| V2 | Narrow QRS; retrograde P variable |
| V3 | Narrow QRS |
| V4 | Narrow QRS |
| V5 | Narrow QRS |
| V6 | Narrow QRS |

### 2.3 Key Leads (Most Diagnostic)

- **Lead II**: Primary — retrograde P-wave morphology (negative) vs sinus P (positive); most visible P-wave in the body
- **aVR**: Retrograde P-wave is POSITIVE in aVR — confirms retrograde direction (sinus P is always negative in aVR)
- **aVF**: Negative retrograde P confirms inferior-to-superior retrograde atrial activation
- **Long rhythm strip (Lead II or V1)**: Rate measurement, P-QRS relationship, AV dissociation detection

### 2.4 Beat-by-Beat Considerations

- Regular rhythm (junctional pacemaker fires rhythmically)
- AV dissociation: sinus P-waves at a slower rate may be visible "marching through" the junctional rhythm, occasionally capturing the ventricles (capture beats — narrow, preceded by sinus P at longer PR)
- Capture beats in the setting of AV dissociation confirm that the junctional rate exceeds the sinus rate (not complete AV block — the ventricle responds to the sinus P when timing allows)
- Accelerated junctional rhythm (isorhythmic AV dissociation): junctional rate ≈ sinus rate — P-waves may appear to "float" in and out of the QRS as the two rates are nearly equal

## 3. Morphology Details

### 3.1 P-wave Changes

The retrograde P-wave is the pathognomonic feature. Characteristics:

- **Polarity**: inverted (negative) in II, III, aVF; upright (positive) in aVR; variable in I, aVL, V1
- **Amplitude**: smaller than normal sinus P (retrograde conduction may be incomplete, producing low-amplitude or absent P)
- **Duration**: shorter than sinus P (retrograde conduction is direct, not using the slower inter-atrial pathways)
- **Timing relative to QRS**:
  - Before QRS (PR 80–120 ms): junctional beat with antegrade-first conduction — P is short and inverted
  - Within QRS: P completely masked — no separate P visible; QRS may have subtle terminal deformity
  - After QRS (RP' < 200 ms): most common pattern — retrograde P appears as inverted P in inferior leads after the narrow QRS

If retrograde VA conduction is absent (blocked): no P-wave at all, or sinus P marches independently → AV dissociation

### 3.2 PR Interval Changes

- If retrograde P BEFORE QRS: PR interval < 120 ms (typically 80–110 ms) — very short, not a normal PR
- If retrograde P AFTER QRS: RP' interval (not PR) measured instead — typically 80–160 ms
- Normal sinus PR intervals are absent (there is no sinus-conducted P-QRS relationship)
- When AV dissociation is present: sinus P occasionally precedes a junctional QRS — the PR will be variable and shorter than usual (not a true conducted PR)

### 3.3 QRS Complex Changes

- Narrow (≤120 ms): ventricular activation via normal His-Purkinje — this is the key feature that distinguishes junctional from ventricular rhythms
- Morphology: identical to the patient's sinus-conducted QRS (same axis, same morphology — because conduction path is the same)
- Wide QRS may occur if: pre-existing BBB, aberrant conduction, or acceleration-dependent bundle branch block at faster junctional rates
- Junctional rhythm with wide QRS still classified as junctional if P-wave pattern confirms (retrograde inverted P, rate 40–130 bpm) — bundle branch block does not change the origin

### 3.4 ST Segment Changes

- ST segment of junctional beats is identical to the patient's sinus beats (same ventricular depolarization path)
- ST changes on junctional beats indicate: (a) underlying ischemia (inferior MI — the same ischemia causing junctional rhythm); (b) digitalis effect (sagging ST-T pattern)
- In inferior MI with junctional escape: ST elevation in inferior leads represents the STEMI, not the junctional rhythm — critical to recognize both simultaneously

### 3.5 T-wave Changes

- T-wave morphology: same as sinus beats (normal His-Purkinje repolarization)
- Digitalis effect: "reverse tick" or sagging ST-T in multiple leads — coexists with accelerated junctional rhythm in digitalis toxicity
- Retrograde P superimposed on T-wave: if retrograde P is after QRS and falls on the T-wave, T-wave morphology is deformed

### 3.6 QT/QTc Changes

- QT interval: same as sinus-conducted beats (identical ventricular conduction)
- QTc should be measured during junctional rhythm using standard Bazett or Fridericia formula — clinically valid since ventricular conduction is normal
- Prolonged QTc during junctional rhythm is a warning if causing bradycardia + long QT → TdP risk

### 3.7 Other Features

- **Isorhythmic AV dissociation**: Junctional rate ≈ sinus rate — the two pacemakers operate near the same rate, causing the sinus P to "wander" in and out of the QRS complex on successive beats. The P-QRS relationship varies cyclically. This is seen classically in accelerated junctional rhythm.
- **Warm-up phenomenon**: Junctional tachycardia may show gradual acceleration at onset (warm-up) — unlike the abrupt onset of reentrant SVT. This gradual acceleration distinguishes automatic junctional tachycardia from AVNRT.
- **Junctional escape after sinus pause**: The junctional beat appears exactly at the intrinsic junctional rate after the sinus pause — the escape interval is the reciprocal of the junctional rate (e.g., 1500 ms escape = 40 bpm junctional rate).

## 4. Differential Diagnosis

### 4.1 Mimics

| Condition | Key Differentiator |
|-----------|-------------------|
| AVNRT (typical) | Rate 150–250 bpm; reentrant mechanism; abrupt onset; pseudo-R' in V1; pseudo-S in inferior leads — RP' typically <70 ms |
| Sinus bradycardia | Upright P-waves in II/III/aVF; normal PR interval (>120 ms); no retrograde P |
| Complete (3rd degree) AV block | Complete AV dissociation with no capture beats; ventricular escape often wide (idioventricular rate 20–40 bpm) unless His bundle escape (narrow QRS at 40–60 bpm — identical appearance to junctional rhythm) |
| Atrial tachycardia with short PR | P-wave morphology not retrograde — may be positive in inferior leads; RP' > PR; warm-up not typical of reentrant AT |
| Accelerated idioventricular rhythm (AIVR) | Wide QRS (>120 ms); rate 40–100 bpm; occurs in acute MI reperfusion; ventricular origin |
| Sinus rhythm with first-degree AV block and inverted P | Rare low atrial rhythm; P-wave inverted in II/III/aVF but PR > 120 ms; rate same as preceding sinus |

### 4.2 Coexisting Conditions

- **Inferior STEMI**: Junctional escape is a direct consequence of AV nodal ischemia in inferior MI. ST elevation in II/III/aVF simultaneously with junctional rhythm = STEMI + AV node ischemia. Both diagnoses must be captured.
- **Digitalis toxicity**: Accelerated junctional rhythm (60–100 bpm) with regularization of previously irregular atrial fibrillation is a classic digitalis toxicity pattern. Do not mistake this regularization for spontaneous conversion to sinus rhythm.
- **Post-cardiac surgery**: Common in the first 24–48 hours after open-heart surgery. Rate may be fast (junctional tachycardia 100–200 bpm) — treat to avoid hemodynamic compromise.
- **Complete AV block with junctional escape**: His bundle pacemaker produces narrow QRS at 40–60 bpm — indistinguishable from junctional escape by QRS alone. AV dissociation (sinus P marching through at faster rate with no capture) confirms complete block.

## 5. STAT Classification

**Depends on rate, cause, and hemodynamic status:**

**NOT STAT — junctional escape in asymptomatic patient:**
- Resting bradycardia in athlete (40–50 bpm junctional escape, asymptomatic)
- Mild vagally mediated junctional escape during Valsalva or sleep
- No hemodynamic compromise

**STAT-2 (Urgent) when:**
- Symptomatic bradycardia from junctional escape: dizziness, pre-syncope, hypotension — consider atropine, assess underlying cause
- Accelerated junctional rhythm (60–100 bpm): evaluate for digitalis toxicity, inferior MI, post-surgical state
- Junctional rhythm with concurrent inferior ST changes: AV nodal ischemia in the context of inferior MI

**STAT-1 (Immediate) when:**
- Junctional escape + hemodynamic compromise (hypotension, syncope, altered consciousness) → atropine, transcutaneous pacing
- Junctional rhythm + confirmed inferior STEMI → primary PCI pathway; AV nodal ischemia usually resolves with reperfusion
- Digitalis toxicity with hemodynamically significant accelerated junctional tachycardia: hold digoxin, correct electrolytes, consider digoxin-specific antibody fragments (Digibind) if severe
- Junctional tachycardia post-cardiac surgery with rate >150 bpm and hemodynamic compromise: amiodarone, electrolyte correction, overdrive pacing

## 6. Reasoning Complexity Analysis

### 6.1 Reasoning Domains Required

1. Rate measurement and classification (escape vs accelerated vs tachycardia)
2. P-wave polarity analysis in inferior leads and aVR (retrograde vs sinus)
3. P-wave timing relative to QRS (before/during/after)
4. QRS morphology assessment (narrow = junctional, wide = ventricular or BBB)
5. AV dissociation detection (sinus P marching through at different rate)
6. Isorhythmic AV dissociation recognition (P-wave "wandering" into QRS)
7. Context integration: inferior MI, digitalis exposure, post-surgical state, electrolytes

### 6.2 Feature Dependencies

- P-wave polarity requires clean signal in inferior leads (II, III, aVF) and aVR — baseline noise or artifact can obscure low-amplitude retrograde P
- P-wave buried in QRS: no visible P — diagnosis inferred from rate and QRS morphology alone
- AV dissociation requires monitoring of P-P intervals independent of RR intervals — requires rhythm strip analysis, not just single 12-lead snapshot
- Warm-up phenomenon detection requires rhythm strip showing onset

### 6.3 Cross-Condition Interactions

- Inferior STEMI + junctional rhythm: two simultaneous diagnoses — the agent must recognize and report both independently
- Digitalis toxicity: AF regularization due to accelerated junctional rhythm is a critical pattern — the rhythm changes from irregularly irregular (AF) to regularly irregular or regular. This is a toxicity marker.
- Junctional escape after pause: if the pause was caused by a blocked PAC, the junctional escape appears after the blocked PAC pause — two layered findings
- Junctional rhythm in complete AV block: narrow QRS junctional escape + complete AV dissociation = complete AV block at the AV nodal level (not infranodal) — distinct management implication (atropine may work vs may not in infranodal block)

### 6.4 Reasoning Chain Sketch

```
Regular narrow QRS rhythm, rate < 150 bpm?
  └── Yes → P-wave visible?
        ├── No P visible → QRS at junctional rate? → junctional rhythm (buried P)
        └── P present → P polarity in lead II?
              ├── Positive P before QRS with normal PR → sinus rhythm (not junctional)
              └── Negative P in inferior leads / positive in aVR → retrograde P
                    ├── P before QRS (PR < 120 ms) → junctional with antegrade P first
                    ├── P after QRS (RP' < 200 ms) → junctional with retrograde P after
                    └── P-P rate ≠ R-R rate → AV dissociation
                          ├── Sinus faster, junctional QRS: complete AV block
                          └── Junctional faster, sinus P marching through: accelerated junctional / isorhythmic
  └── Rate classification:
        ├── 40–60 bpm → junctional escape
        ├── 60–100 bpm → accelerated junctional (evaluate for inferior MI, digitalis)
        └── >100 bpm → junctional tachycardia (post-surgical, congenital, digitalis)
```

### 6.5 Confidence Anchors

- High confidence junctional rhythm: narrow QRS + inverted P in II/III/aVF + upright P in aVR + rate 40–100 bpm
- High confidence junctional escape: rate 40–60 bpm + narrow QRS + preceded by sinus pause or bradycardia
- Uncertainty trigger: no visible P-wave with narrow QRS at 60–100 bpm — junctional vs sinus rhythm with isoelectric P; increase sensitivity in aVR for subtle positive P
- Uncertainty trigger: narrow QRS AV dissociation — junctional escape vs 3rd degree AV block (His bundle escape) — look for capture beats to distinguish
- High-risk flag: junctional rhythm + inferior ST elevation = both diagnoses simultaneously

### 6.6 Difficulty Score

| Dimension | Score |
|-----------|-------|
| Signal clarity needed | 3/5 — retrograde P-waves are often small or buried; aVR P-wave polarity requires clean baseline |
| Number of leads required | 3/5 — II, III, aVF, aVR essential; long rhythm strip needed for AV dissociation |
| Cross-domain reasoning | 3/5 — rate classification, P polarity, AV dissociation, and clinical context all required |
| Temporal pattern complexity | 3/5 — isorhythmic AV dissociation and AV dissociation detection require multi-beat analysis |
| Differential complexity | 2/5 — differential is manageable; AVNRT exclusion (rate/mechanism) is main challenge |
| Rarity in PTB-XL | 3/5 — junctional rhythms are present but less common than sinus rhythms in dataset |
| **Overall difficulty** | **2.5/5** |

## 6A. Agent Assignment

**Architecture:** Option E — 3 Parallel Phase 1 Specialists + 1 Sequential CDS (Node 2.1 Gate Passed 2026-03-27)

| Agent | Role for Junctional Rhythm | Key Features Used |
|-------|--------------------------|-------------------|
| **RRC** (Rhythm/Rate/Conduction) | Primary | Rate 40–60 bpm (junctional escape) or 60–100 bpm (accelerated junctional), absent or retrograde P-waves (inverted in II/III/aVF), retrograde P before or after QRS or buried within QRS, 1:1 retrograde AV relationship |
| **IT** (Ischemia/Territory) | Not involved | Ischemic ST assessment not part of junctional rhythm classification |
| **MR** (Morphology/Repolarization) | Supporting | QRS morphology (narrow = typical junctional; wide = aberrant or pre-existing BBB); confirms no bundle branch pattern from supra-junctional origin |
| **CDS** (Cross-Domain Synthesis) | Standard integration only | Classifies junctional rhythm as escape (in bradycardia context) vs accelerated junctional (digitalis toxicity, inferior MI, post-cardiac surgery); integrates rate with clinical context |

### Primary Agent
**RRC** — Junctional rhythm is identified by rate (40–100 bpm), absent or retrograde P-waves, and narrow QRS; all defining features are in the RRC domain.

### Cross-Domain Hints
- RRC → MR: `cross_domain_hint: "junctional_rhythm_with_wide_QRS — confirm BBB morphology vs aberrant conduction"`

### CDS Specific Role
CDS uses the junctional rate to distinguish escape rhythm (rate 40–60, suppressed by a higher pacemaker failure) from accelerated junctional tachycardia (rate 60–100, triggered by enhanced automaticity from digitalis toxicity, inferior MI, or post-surgical states). This rate-context integration is the primary CDS task for this condition.

## 9. Edge Cases and Pitfalls

1. **Junctional escape vs complete AV block (His bundle escape)**: Both produce narrow QRS at 40–60 bpm. The key distinction is AV dissociation: in complete AV block, sinus P-waves march through at a faster rate with no capture beats. In junctional escape due to sinus node failure or suppression, P-waves are absent (sinus suppressed), retrograde, or present at a slower rate. Capture beats (junctional rhythm with occasional sinus-conducted beat) confirm the junction is capable of conducting and exclude complete block.

2. **Isorhythmic AV dissociation misread as normal sinus rhythm**: When junctional and sinus rates are nearly equal, the sinus P-wave appears to "merge with" the QRS cyclically. Strip may superficially look normal. Careful PP vs RR interval measurement reveals the slight discordance. aVR P-wave polarity cycle reveals when the retrograde P is visible.

3. **Digitalis toxicity: AF regularization**: A patient with known AF who suddenly appears to have a regular rhythm is NOT in sinus rhythm until proven otherwise. Accelerated junctional rhythm (60–80 bpm) causes a regular ventricular response in AF — P-waves are still chaotic (AF baseline) but QRS is regular. This is a toxicity sign requiring immediate digoxin cessation.

4. **Junctional escape buried P mistaken for no P**: Absence of visible P-waves in a narrow complex bradycardia should prompt systematic examination of all 12 leads — particularly V1 (where a retrograde P may produce a positive deflection) and aVR (where it is upright). The P is often visible in at least one lead.

5. **Inferior STEMI missed because attention focuses on rhythm**: In the setting of junctional escape, the rhythm abnormality captures attention. The ST segments in the sinus beats (if any) or the junctional beats must also be assessed. Inferior ST elevation can be present simultaneously.

6. **Junctional tachycardia post-cardiac surgery**: Rates can reach 150–200 bpm in the immediate post-operative period, particularly in pediatric congenital heart disease repair. This is not AVNRT — it is automatic junctional tachycardia. Mechanism is catecholamine excess + surgical trauma. Adenosine will not terminate it. Treatment is amiodarone, cooling, electrolyte correction.

7. **Retrograde P in V1 may be positive — not a sinus P**: In junctional rhythm, the retrograde P may be positive or biphasic in V1 (atrial activation direction in the horizontal plane may travel anteriorly). Do not confuse with normal sinus P in V1. The inferior lead negativity is the confirmatory key.

8. **Atropine response**: In junctional escape due to vagal tone or inferior MI, atropine (0.5 mg IV) increases sinus rate and may restore sinus dominance — temporary diagnostic and therapeutic test. If atropine fails, consider temporary pacing. In digitalis toxicity, atropine is ineffective for junctional tachycardia.
