## 1. Pathophysiology Root Cause

### 1.1 Normal R-Wave Progression — First Principles
In healthy individuals, the R-wave (the first positive deflection of the QRS complex) increases progressively in amplitude from V1 to V4 (or V5), then may decrease slightly in V5-V6. This normal pattern reflects the sequential activation of the interventricular septum and the ventricular free walls.

The R-wave amplitude in any given precordial lead reflects the net electrical vector directed TOWARD that electrode at the moment of ventricular activation:

- **V1** (right sternal border, 4th ICS): Records the earliest activation of the septum (right-to-left and anterior) — produces a small initial R wave before the dominant S wave (as the left ventricle mass moves the dominant vector leftward and away from V1)
- **V2–V3**: Transition zone — R-wave amplitude increases as more lateral anterior LV is activated
- **V4**: Typically R = S (transition zone); maximum anterior wall activation forces
- **V5–V6**: Dominant R-wave as lateral and posterior LV wall forces dominate; S-wave diminishes

**Normal R-wave amplitude reference ranges** (approximate, significant individual variation):
- V1: 1–5mm
- V2: 3–8mm
- V3: 5–15mm
- V4: 7–20mm (often maximum)
- V5: 5–18mm
- V6: 3–15mm

### 1.2 Why PRWP Occurs — Unified Mechanism
Poor R-wave progression (PRWP) results from any process that reduces the anterior directed electrical forces generated during ventricular depolarization in the precordial leads V1-V4. The causes are broadly:

1. **Loss of anterior myocardium (scar)**: Prior anterior MI destroys anterior wall myocardium → those cells no longer contribute to anterior-directed depolarization forces → R-wave in corresponding leads fails to appear or remains small
2. **Conduction abnormality (LBBB)**: In LBBB, normal septal activation (left-to-right) is reversed → the initial QRS vector is directed rightward not leftward → small or absent R in V1-V2 and dominant QS pattern across anterior leads
3. **Anatomical repositioning of the heart**: COPD/emphysema causes a vertically positioned, "drop-down" heart (low diaphragm, clockwise rotation) → the precordial leads no longer face the anterior LV wall in their normal orientation → R-wave forces do not point toward V1-V4
4. **Electrode misplacement**: Electrodes placed too high on the chest record from a position more superior than standard → apparent loss of R-wave amplitude
5. **LVH**: Left ventricular hypertrophy increases leftward and posterior forces, shifting the dominant QRS vector away from V1-V3 → relative PRWP

### 1.3 The Transition Zone
The **transition zone** is defined as the lead where the R-wave amplitude equals the S-wave amplitude (R = S), transitioning from S>R (V1-V3) to R>S (V4-V6).

- **Normal transition**: V3-V4 (R = S at V3 or V4)
- **Clockwise rotation (delayed transition / rightward shift)**: Transition occurs at V5 or V6 (R<S persists through V4) → seen in COPD, posterior MI, clockwise rotated hearts
- **Counterclockwise rotation (early transition / leftward shift)**: Transition occurs at V1-V2 (R already >S by V2) → seen in posterior MI (tall R in V1), posterior wall hypertrophy, WPW with posterior pathway

PRWP specifically refers to failure of R-wave to grow in amplitude from V1→V4, OR to actual R-wave regression (R-wave amplitude decreasing across V1→V4).

## 2. ECG Presentation — Lead by Lead

### 2.1 Diagnostic Criteria (2025 AHA/ESC Guidelines)

There is no single universally adopted criterion for PRWP, but the most commonly used definitions include:

**Definition 1 (most widely used):**
- R-wave amplitude in V3 ≤3mm (with no LBBB, WPW, or other confounders)

**Definition 2 (more sensitive):**
- Failure of R-wave to increase by ≥1mm from V1 to V4
- Or: R in V4 < R in V3 < R in V2 (reverse progression)

**Definition 3 (for PRWP suggesting prior MI specifically):**
- R in V4 < R in V3 and/or R in V3 < R in V2, with no alternative explanation (LBBB, COPD, misplacement)

**Transition zone shift as a supplementary criterion:**
- Transition zone at V5 or V6 (delayed) is concordant with PRWP from anterior wall causes
- Transition zone at V1-V2 (early) suggests posterior MI (not PRWP in the classical sense)

None of these definitions are perfectly sensitive or specific, which contributes to PRWP's status as a soft sign requiring clinical correlation.

### 2.2 Lead-by-Lead Manifestation

| Lead | Expected Finding in PRWP | Notes |
|------|--------------------------|-------|
| I | Normal | Limb lead unaffected by anterior PRWP |
| II | Normal | Limb lead unaffected |
| III | Normal | Limb lead unaffected |
| aVR | Normal | Limb lead unaffected |
| aVL | May show Q wave if prior anterior/lateral MI | Only if lateral wall involved |
| aVF | Normal | Not anterior territory |
| V1 | Small r or QS | Baseline may be normal; QS in V1 alone is often normal variant |
| V2 | Small r, similar to or smaller than V1 | R-wave fails to grow |
| V3 | Small r or QS — key lead | R ≤3mm in V3 is the most common PRWP criterion |
| V4 | R-wave should be largest here; in PRWP: small or absent | Critical lead — expected peak R-wave not present |
| V5 | May finally show transition here | Delayed transition pattern |
| V6 | R-wave may be normal by V5-V6 despite V1-V4 PRWP | R-wave amplitude may recover in lateral leads |

### 2.3 Key Leads
- **V3**: The single most useful lead — R ≤3mm in V3 is the most practical PRWP threshold
- **V4**: Should have the largest R-wave in normal progression; absent or small R in V4 with prior V3 PRWP confirms the pattern
- **Transition zone leads**: V3-V5 used to determine if transition is delayed or absent

### 2.4 Beat-by-Beat Considerations
- PRWP assessment should be performed in sinus rhythm when possible; VPBs have aberrant QRS morphology
- Consistent PRWP across all beats is a true finding; PRWP seen only in PVCs or aberrant beats is not meaningful
- In AF: assess PRWP across multiple sinus-equivalent beats; irregular rhythm with AF makes all precordial amplitude measurements less reliable

## 3. Morphology Details

### 3.1 R-Wave Amplitude Measurement
- Measured from the isoelectric baseline to the peak of the R wave in each precordial lead
- Units: mm (1mm = 0.1mV at standard calibration 10mm/mV)
- Measured at the QRS complex level, not from the P-wave baseline if the P-wave distorts the baseline

### 3.2 QS Complex versus Small r Wave
- A **QS complex** = entirely negative QRS deflection; no R wave at all
- A **small r** = R wave present but small amplitude; technically not a QS
- QS in V1-V2: Can be a normal finding (V1 QS alone is common; V1-V2 QS is more concerning but not definitive)
- QS in V1-V3: More significantly abnormal; consider prior anterior septal MI
- QS in V1-V4: Highly suggestive of significant prior anterior MI when LBBB excluded

### 3.3 Reverse R-Wave Progression
Reverse R-wave progression (R-wave amplitude DECREASES from V1→V3) is a stronger indicator of pathology than simply slow progression. If R in V3 < R in V2 and R in V2 < R in V1 (or R3 < R2 < R1), this is reverse progression — more specific for prior anterior MI or LBBB than plain PRWP.

### 3.4 PRWP in LBBB
In LBBB, the normal septal q waves in V5-V6 are absent, and the precordial leads V1-V4 typically show QS or rS complexes as part of the LBBB conduction pattern. PRWP in LBBB is an EXPECTED FINDING — it does not add diagnostic information about prior MI and should not be reported as PRWP independently of the LBBB. The system must flag "PRWP cannot be assessed in LBBB — LBBB creates PRWP as expected pattern."

### 3.5 PRWP in COPD
COPD/pulmonary hyperinflation drops the diaphragm and rotates the heart clockwise (viewed from below). This moves the LV away from the standard V1-V4 precordial positions, causing:
- Delayed transition (V5 or V6)
- Low voltage across the precordial leads
- PRWP without pathological Q waves
- Low-amplitude QRS generally
- Prominent S in V5-V6 ("clockwise rotation pattern")
Key distinction from anterior MI PRWP: No pathological Q waves; low voltage throughout; clinical COPD history.

### 3.6 PRWP and Electrode Misplacement
Electrodes placed 1-2 intercostal spaces too high on the chest produce apparent PRWP. This is a technical artifact and the most correctable cause. Suspected if: PRWP on one ECG but not prior ECGs; patient repositioning normalizes R waves; no clinical history of MI.

### 3.7 Combined PRWP with Other Findings
- **PRWP + pathological Q waves in V1-V4**: Consistent with prior anterior MI (strongest evidence)
- **PRWP + LBBB**: PRWP expected — no additional diagnostic implication
- **PRWP + low voltage**: Suggests COPD, pericardial effusion, amyloid — not primarily anterior MI
- **PRWP + left axis deviation**: Consider anterior MI with LAFB (LAD territory affecting fascicle)
- **PRWP + early transition (tall R in V1)**: Consider posterior MI — opposite of PRWP; tall R in V1 is the reciprocal of a posterior Q wave

## 4. Differential Diagnosis

### 4.1 Mimics
| Cause of PRWP | Key Distinguishing Features |
|---------------|------------------------------|
| Prior anterior MI | Pathological Q waves V1-V3; history of MI; normal voltage elsewhere |
| LBBB | Broad notched QRS; QS in V1-V3 expected as part of LBBB pattern; do not report separately |
| LVH | High voltage lateral leads; slow R progression anteriorly; strain pattern lateral |
| COPD/Emphysema | Low voltage; clockwise rotation; delayed transition; S waves in V5-V6; hyperinflation on CXR |
| Electrode misplacement | PRWP absent on prior or repeat ECG; normalizes with electrode repositioning |
| WPW | Short PR; delta waves; PRWP from pre-excitation altering initial QRS vector |
| Cardiomyopathy (dilated) | Global hypokinesia; diffuse PRWP; no specific coronary territory |
| Normal variant | Mild PRWP in young adults with slim chest; no pathological Q; full clinical correlation |

### 4.2 Coexisting Conditions
- **PRWP + active ischemia**: PRWP from prior anterior MI may coexist with new ST changes from re-ischemia. Do not dismiss ST changes as "old changes" because PRWP is present.
- **PRWP + LAFB**: LAD territory MI can cause both PRWP (from anterior wall loss) and LAFB (from anterior fascicle damage). Combined findings strengthen the conclusion of prior proximal LAD MI.
- **PRWP in paced rhythm**: Ventricular pacing from RV apex produces LBBB-like morphology with expected PRWP. Cannot interpret PRWP in paced rhythm.

## 6. Reasoning Complexity Analysis

### 6.1 Cause Determination
PRWP is fundamentally a non-specific finding. The same R-wave pattern can result from prior anterior MI, LBBB, COPD, lead misplacement, or normal variation. The system cannot definitively assign a cause from the ECG alone. The output should therefore list the differential in order of probability and flag the most important clinical distinctions.

### 6.2 LBBB Gating
Detection of LBBB must precede PRWP interpretation. If LBBB is present, PRWP analysis is suspended and the output reports "PRWP assessment not applicable — LBBB pattern." Failure to gate on LBBB will produce false-positive PRWP diagnoses.

### 6.3 Q-Wave Co-Detection
PRWP occurring alongside pathological Q waves in V1-V4 substantially increases the probability of prior anterior MI. The system should combine PRWP and Q-wave findings to produce a higher-confidence prior MI interpretation rather than reporting them independently.

### 6.4 Voltage Context
Low voltage throughout the ECG (limb leads <5mm, precordial <10mm) shifts the PRWP differential toward COPD, pericardial effusion, or infiltrative cardiomyopathy rather than anterior MI. High voltage in lateral leads (LVH) also shifts the interpretation.

### 6.5 New vs. Established PRWP
New PRWP appearing acutely in the context of chest pain and without prior LBBB or COPD history should be treated as possible evolving anterior MI. PRWP with stable, unchanged appearance on serial ECGs is a chronic finding. Comparison with prior ECGs is essential to determine acuity.

### 6.6 Difficulty Score

| Dimension | Score (1–5) | Rationale |
|-----------|-------------|-----------|
| PRWP Detection | 2 | R-wave amplitude measurement is straightforward |
| Cause Attribution | 3 | Multiple causes; no single ECG discriminator |
| LBBB Gating | 2 | LBBB detection gate must fire first; manageable |
| Q-Wave Integration | 2 | Combining PRWP + Q waves raises confidence; straightforward |
| New vs. Chronic Distinction | 3 | Requires prior ECG comparison |
| Low Voltage / COPD Discrimination | 3 | Voltage context requires multi-lead analysis |
| **Overall Difficulty** | **2.2** | Soft sign with broad differential; moderate reasoning required |

## 6A. Agent Assignment

**Architecture:** Option E — 3 Parallel Phase 1 Specialists + 1 Sequential CDS (Node 2.1 Gate Passed 2026-03-27)

| Agent | Role for Poor R-Wave Progression | Key Features Used |
|-------|--------------------------|-------------------|
| **RRC** (Rhythm/Rate/Conduction) | Not involved | No rhythm or conduction features specific to PRWP |
| **IT** (Ischemia/Territory) | Supporting | Pathological Q-waves in V1–V4 when present alongside PRWP (raises old anterior MI probability); absence of Q-waves favors non-MI causes of PRWP |
| **MR** (Morphology/Repolarization) | Primary | R-wave amplitude in V1–V6: R in V1 <1 mm or R/S ratio <1 in V3; transition zone: lead where R = S (normal = V3–V4); reversal of progression; LBBB detection (gate — suspends PRWP interpretation if LBBB present); LVH voltage criteria (alternative cause) |
| **CDS** (Cross-Domain Synthesis) | Required — multi-cause differential | Integrates MR's R-wave progression pattern with IT's Q-wave findings; classifies PRWP cause as old anterior MI (Q-waves present) vs LBBB (MR gate) vs LVH (voltage criteria) vs COPD (low voltage + rightward axis) vs lead placement artifact |

### Primary Agent
**MR** — PRWP is defined by inadequate R-wave amplitude growth from V1 to V4; per-lead R-wave amplitude measurement and transition zone analysis are core MR morphology functions.

### Cross-Domain Hints
- MR → IT: `cross_domain_hint: "poor_R_wave_progression_detected — evaluate V1_V4 for pathological Q waves (old anterior MI cause)"`
- MR → CDS: `cross_domain_hint: "PRWP_with_or_without_Q_waves — integrate with QRS voltage and axis for cause classification"`

### CDS Specific Role
CDS applies the differential diagnosis logic for PRWP by integrating MR's R-wave progression measurements with IT's Q-wave findings and additional contextual features. The decision tree: (1) if LBBB detected (MR gate), PRWP is expected and not independently interpreted; (2) if Q-waves present in V1–V4 (IT), old anterior MI is the primary cause; (3) if LVH voltage criteria met (MR), LVH is a contributing cause; (4) if low voltage throughout with rightward axis (MR + RRC), COPD/emphysema is the likely cause; (5) if none of the above, technical lead placement error is flagged.

## 9. Edge Cases and Pitfalls

**1. LBBB produces PRWP as an expected normal consequence.** In LBBB, septal activation is reversed (right bundle depolarizes the septum from right to left instead of left to right). This reversal eliminates the normal initial anterior forces, producing QS or rS complexes in V1-V3 as part of the LBBB pattern. Do NOT report PRWP when LBBB is already identified — it adds no information and creates confusion. The system must detect LBBB first and suppress PRWP as a separate finding.

**2. Lead electrode misplacement is a correctable cause of PRWP.** Electrodes placed 1-2 intercostal spaces too high on the chest (common in clinical practice, estimated 1-4% of ECGs) produce QS complexes or poor R-wave progression in V1-V3 that normalizes when electrodes are placed correctly. This is a technical artifact, not a diagnostic finding. Suspected when: (a) PRWP is not present on prior ECGs, (b) no clinical history of MI, COPD, or LVH, (c) the pattern is limited to V1-V3 only without any Q-wave duration abnormality.

**3. PRWP is not diagnostic of prior anterior MI without supporting evidence.** PRWP alone is a soft, nonspecific sign. Its positive predictive value for prior anterior MI increases substantially when: (a) pathological Q waves are also present in V1-V4, (b) history of prior anterior MI is documented, (c) other causes (LBBB, COPD, misplacement) are excluded. The system should report PRWP with a differential and not definitively state "prior anterior MI" based on PRWP alone.

**4. COPD PRWP mimicking anterior MI.** Chronic obstructive pulmonary disease produces PRWP through mechanical rotation of the heart, not through myocardial loss. Key differentiators: (a) low voltage globally (limb leads often <5mm QRS amplitude); (b) delayed clockwise rotation with large S waves in V5-V6; (c) right axis deviation possible; (d) no pathological Q-wave duration criterion met; (e) clinical and radiographic evidence of COPD. If low voltage and PRWP coexist, COPD/hyperinflation should be the primary consideration.

**5. Early transition (tall R in V1-V2) is the OPPOSITE of PRWP.** A tall, broad R wave in V1 (R ≥ S in V1, especially with upright T-wave and no RBBB) represents early counterclockwise rotation and is a sign of posterior MI (or posterior wall hypertrophy, WPW with posterior pathway, or RBBB). This is NOT PRWP. The system must distinguish PRWP (delayed/absent R-wave growth) from early R-wave transition (tall R in V1-V2).

**6. PRWP in the context of new chest pain is an acute red flag.** If a patient presents with new chest pain and the ECG shows new PRWP (not present on prior ECG) without LBBB or COPD, this suggests evolving anterior MI with new anterior wall loss. This is a time-critical finding requiring immediate assessment — not a chronic annotation.

**7. Very slim young patients with small precordial QRS amplitudes.** In young, thin-chested individuals with a small heart, precordial QRS amplitudes may be normally small, producing apparent PRWP without pathology. Global small QRS amplitude with a narrow QRS and no pathological Q waves in a young patient with no cardiac history should be annotated as "possible normal variant — PRWP criteria met but low prior probability of MI given demographics."
