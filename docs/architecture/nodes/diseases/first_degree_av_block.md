## 1. Pathophysiology Root Cause

### 1.1 What Goes Wrong

The AV node is the physiological gatekeeper between atrial and ventricular conduction. Under normal conditions, the AV node imposes a deliberate conduction delay of 120–200ms (the PR interval), allowing atrial systole to complete before ventricular depolarization begins. In first degree AV block, this delay is pathologically prolonged beyond 200ms, but every single P-wave still conducts to the ventricles — hence "block" is technically a misnomer. It is a conduction delay, not a true block.

The AV node is structurally unique: it is composed of slow-response action potential cells (calcium-dependent, not sodium-dependent), which makes it exquisitely sensitive to:
- Increased vagal tone (acetylcholine slows calcium channel opening)
- Ischemia (RCA supplies the AV node in ~90% of patients via the AV nodal artery)
- Drug effects (beta-blockers, calcium channel blockers, digoxin)
- Inflammation (myocarditis, Lyme disease, sarcoidosis)
- Fibrosis (age-related degeneration — Lev disease, Lenègre disease)

Site of delay is almost always the AV node itself (supranodal or nodal). Infranodal first degree block (delay in His bundle) is rare and carries different implications.

### 1.2 Electrical Consequence

Each sinus P-wave still reaches the AV node, traverses it, and eventually propagates through the His-Purkinje system normally. The ventricular conduction pathway is unaffected — His-Purkinje conduction proceeds normally once the depolarization front exits the AV node. Therefore:
- Every P conducts → every P has a following QRS (1:1 P:QRS ratio)
- PR interval is prolonged and CONSTANT beat-to-beat (unlike Wenckebach)
- QRS morphology is normal (narrow) because infranodal conduction is intact
- PR prolongation reflects prolonged nodal transit time, not failure

At very long PR intervals (>300ms), the atria may contract while the mitral and tricuspid valves are partially or fully closed, producing the hemodynamic equivalent of pseudo-pacemaker syndrome — elevated atrial pressure, reduced preload, and occasionally symptomatic hypotension.

### 1.3 Why It Appears on ECG

The PR interval represents the time from first atrial depolarization (P-wave onset) to first ventricular depolarization (Q-wave onset, or R-wave onset if no Q). Any prolongation of transit through the AV node directly prolongs this interval. Because the delay is uniform and passive (not beat-dependent), the PR interval is constant throughout the rhythm strip. The surface ECG therefore shows a stable, uniformly prolonged PR on every beat.

## 2. ECG Presentation — Lead by Lead

### 2.1 Diagnostic Criteria (2025 AHA/ESC Guidelines)

- **PR interval > 200ms** (> 1 large box at 25mm/s) on every beat
- **Every P-wave is followed by a QRS** (1:1 AV conduction — no dropped beats)
- **PR interval is constant** beat-to-beat (variation < 20ms is acceptable in sinus arrhythmia)
- **QRS duration normal** (< 120ms) unless concurrent bundle branch block
- Measured in lead II or the lead with the clearest P-wave onset

Graded severity: mild (200–250ms), moderate (250–300ms), severe (>300ms — hemodynamically relevant).

### 2.2 Lead-by-Lead Manifestation

| Lead | Expected Finding | Why | Sensitivity |
|------|-----------------|-----|-------------|
| I | Normal P, prolonged PR, narrow QRS | Lateral vector — shows P and QRS well | Moderate |
| II | Best lead: upright P, visibly long PR gap, narrow QRS | Parallel to inferior axis — tallest P, clearest PR measurement | High |
| III | Upright or biphasic P, prolonged PR | Inferior lead — useful for inferior MI context | Moderate |
| aVR | Inverted P (normal), prolonged PR, inverted QRS | Opposite to II — confirms sinus P-wave | Moderate |
| aVL | Flat or biphasic P, prolonged PR | Lateral lead — variable P morphology | Low–Moderate |
| aVF | Upright P, prolonged PR, narrow QRS | Inferior lead — complements II and III | Moderate |
| V1 | Biphasic P (normal), prolonged PR, rS pattern | Rightward lead — good for PR onset marking if biphasic P clear | High |
| V2 | Biphasic or upright P, prolonged PR | Anterior — transitional | Moderate |
| V3 | Upright P, prolonged PR | Anterior | Low–Moderate |
| V4 | Upright P, prolonged PR | Anterior-lateral transition | Low |
| V5 | Upright P, prolonged PR | Lateral | Low |
| V6 | Upright P, prolonged PR, narrow R | Lateral — similar to lead I | Low |

**Best leads for PR measurement:** II and V1 (P-wave onset clearest; use whichever shows earliest P-wave onset for accurate PR).

### 2.3 Key Leads

- **Lead II:** Primary diagnostic lead. Upright P-wave with visibly elongated isoelectric segment before QRS. PR gap should be unmistakably wider than one large box.
- **V1:** Secondary confirmation. Biphasic P-wave onset visible; often gives the earliest P onset in the precordial leads. Useful when II is ambiguous.
- **aVR:** Confirms sinus P-wave morphology (inverted P in aVR = normal sinus P-wave direction). Rules out ectopic atrial rhythm as cause.

### 2.4 Beat-by-Beat Considerations

First degree AV block is entirely stable beat-to-beat. There is no grouped beating, no dropped beats, no change in the PR interval. The rhythm is regular (assuming underlying sinus rhythm). The only temporal feature to monitor is whether PR is trending longer over time (could indicate progression to Wenckebach) or shortening (improving AV node function, e.g., resolving ischemia, drug clearance).

In bradycardia with first degree AV block, the P-wave may encroach on the preceding T-wave at slow heart rates — this can make the PR appear artificially short or make P-waves hard to identify. Always measure PR from clear P-wave onset to QRS onset in a run of at least 3 beats.

## 3. Morphology Details

### 3.1 P-Wave Morphology
Normal sinus P-wave: upright in I, II, aVF; inverted in aVR; duration < 120ms; amplitude < 2.5mm. First degree AV block does not alter P-wave morphology unless the underlying etiology also causes atrial enlargement (e.g., inferior MI with right atrial involvement).

### 3.2 PR Interval
The defining feature. Measured from P-wave onset to QRS onset. Use the lead where both onsets are most visible. At severe prolongation (>300ms), visually the P-wave appears to be "floating" far from the QRS. At exactly 200ms (borderline), measure carefully — 5 small boxes precisely.

### 3.3 QRS Complex
Expected to be narrow (< 120ms) in isolated first degree AV block. Wide QRS (≥ 120ms) in the setting of first degree AV block suggests concurrent bundle branch block or aberrant conduction — does NOT mean the AV block is infranodal by itself, but the combination raises clinical concern.

### 3.4 ST Segment and T-Wave
Not altered by first degree AV block itself. Any ST changes are due to the underlying etiology (e.g., inferior STEMI causing both first degree block and ST elevation in II, III, aVF). The algorithm must evaluate ST changes independently of the PR prolongation.

### 3.5 QT Interval
Not directly affected by first degree AV block. However, drugs that cause first degree block (e.g., amiodarone, sotalol, certain antiarrhythmics) may also prolong the QT interval independently — check QTc.

### 3.6 Rhythm Regularity
Regular in first degree AV block with underlying sinus rhythm. Irregular if underlying atrial fibrillation with prolonged AV conduction — note that PR cannot be measured in AFib (no discrete P-waves), so first degree block is not diagnosed during AFib.

### 3.7 Rate
Any heart rate is possible. First degree AV block does not alter heart rate (every P still conducts). Rate is determined by the sinus node, not the AV node delay.

## 4. Differential Diagnosis

### 4.1 Mimics

| Condition | How it Mimics First Degree AV Block | Distinguishing Feature |
|-----------|-------------------------------------|----------------------|
| Wenckebach (Mobitz Type I) | First beat of a Wenckebach cycle has the shortest PR (may still be >200ms) | PR progressively lengthens; eventually a P is not conducted |
| Ectopic atrial rhythm | Long PR from abnormal atrial focus near AV node | P-wave morphology abnormal; inverted P in II, III, aVF |
| Junctional rhythm with retrograde P-wave | P-wave after QRS or hidden in QRS — may look like long PR | P-wave inverted in inferior leads; RP relationship, not PR |
| Atrial flutter with 2:1 conduction | Flutter waves may look like P+QRS with long "PR" | Regular 300bpm atrial rate; sawtooth pattern; no isoelectric baseline between flutter waves |
| PR segment depression (pericarditis) | Visually appears to lengthen distance to QRS onset | PR depression is a downsloping segment, not isoelectric; diffuse ST elevation pattern |
| Pre-excitation (WPW) | Short PR — opposite, not a true mimic; but if delta wave is subtle, QRS onset ambiguous | Delta wave slurs QRS upstroke; PR is SHORT in WPW (< 120ms typically) |

### 4.2 Coexisting Conditions

- **Inferior STEMI:** RCA ischemia simultaneously causes first degree block AND ST elevation in II, III, aVF. First degree block in this context is a direct marker of AV nodal ischemia and predicts risk of progression to higher-degree block.
- **Digoxin toxicity:** First degree block + regularized AFib + PAT with block = digoxin toxicity pattern.
- **Lyme carditis:** First degree block (often severe, PR > 300ms) is the most common ECG manifestation. May fluctuate dramatically. In young patients with first degree block and no other cause, consider Lyme.
- **Myocarditis:** Any degree of AV block with chest pain, fever, and elevated troponin in a young patient — myocarditis until proven otherwise.

## 5. STAT Classification

| Parameter | Assessment |
|-----------|------------|
| Isolated first degree AV block | NOT STAT — incidental finding, no immediate action |
| First degree block + inferior STEMI | STAT — RCA ischemia; AV nodal involvement marker |
| First degree block + symptoms (syncope, presyncope) | URGENT — rule out higher-degree block, structural heart disease |
| First degree block + new wide QRS | URGENT — bifascicular block pattern; pacemaker risk assessment |
| Severe first degree block (PR > 300ms) | MONITOR — hemodynamic risk from atrial-ventricular dyssynchrony |
| First degree block + prior complete heart block history | URGENT — known conduction system disease, progression risk |
| First degree block in known digoxin patient | URGENT — assess for toxicity; check digoxin level |

**Default STAT level for isolated finding: GREEN (routine monitoring).**
**Escalate to ORANGE/RED based on clinical context (STEMI, symptoms, drugs).**

## 6. Reasoning Complexity Analysis

### 6.1 Signal Clarity Needed
PR interval measurement requires clear identification of P-wave onset AND QRS onset. The challenge is locating the exact start of the P-wave, which may have a slow initial deflection. ECGdeli fiducial points (P onset, Q onset) must be accurate to within ±10ms for reliable first degree AV block diagnosis. Baseline wander or noise degrades this.

### 6.2 Number of Leads Required
Diagnosis can technically be made in a single lead (II or V1). Practical minimum: 2 leads to confirm P-wave morphology (sinus vs. ectopic). Full 12-lead required for contextual assessment (inferior ST changes, QRS width, QTc).

### 6.3 Cross-Domain Reasoning
Modest cross-domain reasoning required. Must integrate:
- Rate and rhythm analysis (rule out 2:1 block masquerading as long PR)
- Drug context (beta-blockers, digoxin — changes STAT level)
- Clinical context (inferior STEMI → escalate; isolated finding → routine)
- QRS width assessment (bundle branch disease?)

### 6.4 Temporal Pattern Complexity
Low temporal complexity. No beat-to-beat variation expected. Stability of PR over time is a key feature (distinguishes from Wenckebach). Requires minimum 3–5 beats for reliable PR measurement, not prolonged strips.

### 6.5 Differential Complexity
Low-to-moderate. The main trap is Wenckebach presenting with a short strip showing only the first beats of the cycle (all with prolonged but apparently constant PR). Longer strip analysis resolves this. Ectopic atrial rhythm is differentiated by P-wave morphology.

### 6.6 Difficulty Score

| Dimension | Score | Rationale |
|-----------|-------|-----------|
| Signal clarity needed | 2/5 | Precise P onset and QRS onset required; standard fiducial detection usually adequate |
| Number of leads required | 1/5 | Single lead (II) sufficient for diagnosis; context uses more |
| Cross-domain reasoning | 2/5 | Drug and clinical context needed; straightforward once identified |
| Temporal pattern complexity | 1/5 | No beat-to-beat variation; static measurement |
| Differential complexity | 2/5 | Wenckebach is main trap; requires longer strip to exclude |
| Rarity in PTB-XL | 2/5 | Common finding in PTB-XL; well-represented training data |
| **Overall difficulty** | **1.2/5** | **Interval measurement task; lowest complexity in AV block spectrum** |

### Primary Agent
**RRC** — First-degree AV block is defined entirely by PR interval >200 ms with 1:1 conduction; this is a pure interval measurement task in the RRC domain.

### Cross-Domain Hints
No cross-domain hints required — single-domain condition.

### CDS Specific Role
CDS performs standard integration only. First-degree AVB is most commonly a secondary finding accompanying other diagnoses. CDS notes its presence in the integrated report and flags it if concurrent findings (e.g., inferior STEMI, digitalis effect) provide a clinically relevant cause for the PR prolongation.

## 7. RAG Knowledge Requirements

### 7.1 Core Knowledge Chunks Required
- AV node anatomy and blood supply (RCA → AV nodal artery in 90%; LCx in 10%)
- PR interval normal range and measurement technique
- Drug effects on AV conduction: beta-blockers, non-dihydropyridine CCBs, digoxin, amiodarone
- Lyme carditis ECG presentation and PR behavior
- Inferior STEMI + AV block clinical significance and monitoring protocol
- Pseudo-pacemaker syndrome: hemodynamics of very long PR interval
- AV block grading schema (first, second Type I/II, high-grade, complete)

### 7.2 Key Clinical Thresholds for RAG Retrieval
- PR > 200ms → first degree AV block diagnosis trigger
- PR > 300ms → flag for hemodynamic assessment (pseudo-pacemaker syndrome risk)
- PR > 200ms + inferior ST elevation → RCA ischemia protocol trigger
- PR > 200ms + new wide QRS → bifascicular block assessment protocol
- PR > 200ms + known digoxin → digoxin toxicity workup trigger

## 9. Edge Cases and Pitfalls

**Pitfall 1 — Wenckebach with a short strip:** If only the first 3 beats of a Wenckebach cycle are captured, all show progressively (but slightly) longer PR. May be misread as stable first degree block. Mitigation: always request rhythm strip ≥ 10 seconds; measure PR on every visible beat.

**Pitfall 2 — P-wave buried in T-wave:** At slow heart rates with severe first degree block, the P-wave may be partially or fully merged with the preceding T-wave. The T-wave appears notched or the PR appears short. Mitigation: increase paper speed to 50mm/s on display; use V1 where P-wave deflection is often distinct from T-wave.

**Pitfall 3 — Ectopic atrial tachycardia with long PR:** An atrial focus near the AV node may produce a long PR (slow antegrade conduction through the node from an unusual entry point). P-wave morphology will be abnormal (inverted in inferior leads). Mitigation: always check P-wave axis/morphology; sinus P-wave is upright in I, II and inverted in aVR.

**Pitfall 4 — 2:1 AV block at high atrial rate:** At 150bpm sinus rate with 2:1 conduction, ventricular rate is 75bpm — the "normal" rate masks the pathology. The conducted P-waves may have a long PR (first degree + 2:1 = high-grade block). Mitigation: always count P-waves independently of QRS; verify PP interval and P:QRS ratio.

**Pitfall 5 — Pseudo-normalization after inferior STEMI treatment:** PR interval may dramatically shorten after reperfusion of RCA territory. Residual mild PR prolongation may persist. Trend the PR over time during the admission — normalization is a marker of AV nodal reperfusion.

**Pitfall 6 — Rate-dependent PR changes:** PR normally shortens with tachycardia (faster AV nodal recovery). A PR that is borderline long (200–210ms) at rest may normalize with mild tachycardia. This is physiological AV nodal behavior, not true first degree block in the classic sense. Measure PR at resting heart rate.
