## 1. Pathophysiology Root Cause

### 1.1 What Goes Wrong (Anatomy/Physiology)

Nothing goes wrong — this is the normal state. The sinoatrial (SA) node, located in the high right atrium at the junction of the superior vena cava, functions as the dominant pacemaker. SA node cells exhibit automaticity via phase 4 spontaneous depolarization driven by the "funny current" (I_f, HCN channels), inward calcium current, and decay of outward potassium current. The intrinsic rate is 60–100 bpm, modified continuously by the autonomic nervous system: sympathetic input (norepinephrine, beta-1 receptors) increases rate; parasympathetic input (acetylcholine, muscarinic receptors) decreases rate.

Impulse propagation follows the normal sequence: SA node → internodal tracts (Bachmann's bundle to left atrium) → AV node (physiological delay) → Bundle of His → right and left bundle branches → Purkinje network → ventricular myocardium.

### 1.2 Electrical Consequence

Each cycle produces a predictable sequence of electrical events:
- **P-wave**: Atrial depolarization, originating high in the right atrium, spreading inferiorly and leftward
- **PR interval**: AV nodal conduction delay (0.12–0.20 s at normal rates), allowing ventricular filling
- **QRS complex**: Rapid ventricular depolarization via His-Purkinje system (<0.12 s), predominantly leftward and inferior
- **ST segment**: Isoelectric ventricular plateau (phase 2 of action potential)
- **T-wave**: Ventricular repolarization, normally concordant with QRS (same direction)

### 1.3 Why It Appears on ECG

The P-wave vector originates high right → low left, projecting positively onto leads I, II, aVF, aVL and negatively onto aVR. The mean P-wave axis is 0–75°. The QRS axis is 0–90° in most adults (acceptable −30° to +90°). The T-wave axis parallels the QRS axis within 45°. Because all conduction follows the normal pathway, no aberrant vectors are present and all intervals fall within established normal ranges.

## 2. ECG Presentation — Lead by Lead

### 2.1 Diagnostic Criteria (2025 AHA/ESC Guidelines)

1. Heart rate 60–100 bpm
2. Upright P-wave in lead II (P-wave axis 0–75°)
3. Negative P-wave in aVR
4. Every P-wave followed by a QRS complex
5. Constant PR interval 120–200 ms (measured lead II or V1)
6. QRS duration <120 ms
7. Regular rhythm (PP and RR intervals constant, variation <10%)

All 7 criteria must be met for the designation "Normal Sinus Rhythm."

### 2.2 Lead-by-Lead Manifestation

| Lead | Expected Finding | Why | Sensitivity for NSR |
|------|-----------------|-----|----------------------|
| I | Upright P, positive or biphasic QRS, upright T | Leftward depolarization vectors project positively | High — abnormal P here suggests non-sinus origin |
| II | Upright P (tallest, most diagnostic), positive QRS, upright T | Most aligned with P-wave axis (0–75°); standard diagnostic lead | Highest — primary lead for rhythm identification |
| III | Upright P (may be small or biphasic), variable QRS, upright T | Inferior lead, rightward component may reduce P amplitude | Moderate — can have small or notched P normally |
| aVR | Inverted P, predominantly negative QRS, inverted T | Rightward lead — all vectors project negatively | High — inverted P in aVR is required for sinus rhythm |
| aVL | Upright P (may be small), biphasic or positive QRS, upright or biphasic T | High lateral lead, less aligned with P-vector | Moderate — small P normal; negative P suggests non-sinus |
| aVF | Upright P, positive QRS, upright T | Inferior lead, well-aligned with normal P-axis | High — confirms inferior P-wave axis |
| V1 | Biphasic P (positive then negative), rS pattern QRS, upright T | Right precordial: initial right atrial → then left atrial component | High — biphasic P with terminal negative deflection is normal |
| V2 | Small biphasic or upright P, rS or RS, upright T | Transition zone precordial | Moderate |
| V3 | Upright P, RS or qRS, upright T | Transition zone (R=S often here) | Low — variable at transition |
| V4 | Upright P, qRS or Rs, upright T | Leftward transition | Moderate |
| V5 | Upright P, qRs or Rs, upright T | Lateral precordial, left ventricle dominant | High — confirms leftward repolarization |
| V6 | Upright P, qRs, upright T | Most lateral, mirrors lead I and aVL | High — R>S, small q normal (septal) |

### 2.3 Key Leads (Most Diagnostic)

- **Lead II**: Primary rhythm strip lead. Tallest P-waves, most aligned with sinus vector. Upright P in II is the single most important feature.
- **aVR**: Inverted P mandatory. If P is upright in aVR, rhythm is NOT sinus.
- **V1**: Biphasic P (+ then −) is pathognomonic for sinus origin with normal atrial activation.

### 2.4 Beat-by-Beat Considerations

- PP interval should vary <10% (or <120 ms) between consecutive cycles. Greater variability suggests sinus arrhythmia.
- PR interval is constant beat-to-beat in NSR. Any progressive lengthening suggests Wenckebach; sudden dropped beats suggest Mobitz II.
- Every P must be followed by a QRS. One-to-one P:QRS ratio is definitional.
- QRS morphology is constant — any beat-to-beat variation in QRS shape suggests aberrant conduction, PVCs, or fusion beats.

## 3. Morphology Details (What the Agent Must See)

### 3.1 P-wave Changes

- **Duration**: 80–120 ms (normal); <80 ms is acceptable
- **Amplitude**: 0.5–2.5 mm in limb leads; <1.5 mm in V1
- **Axis**: 0–75° (upright II, aVF, aVL; inverted aVR)
- **Morphology**: Smooth, dome-shaped. May be slightly notched in limb leads (normal septal notch <40 ms between peaks). Biphasic in V1 with terminal negative component <1 mm deep and <40 ms duration.
- **Constancy**: All P-waves look identical. Any P-wave morphology change suggests ectopic or wandering pacemaker.

### 3.2 PR Interval Changes

- **Normal range**: 120–200 ms (3–5 small squares at 25 mm/s)
- **Measured**: Onset of P to onset of QRS, in the lead where both are most clearly visible (usually II or V5)
- **Constancy**: Fixed PR interval is a hallmark of NSR. Rate-related shortening at higher rates is physiological (AV node conducts faster at higher rates).

### 3.3 QRS Complex Changes

- **Duration**: <120 ms (normal), ideally <100 ms
- **Axis**: −30° to +90° (strictly 0–90° for most adults)
- **Amplitude**: Normal ranges vary by lead (Sokolow-Lyon criteria used for hypertrophy, not applicable here)
- **Morphology**: Small septal q in I, aVL, V5, V6 (left-to-right initial septal depolarization). rS in V1, transitioning to Rs or qR by V5–V6.
- **Progression**: R-wave amplitude increases V1→V5, with R>S by V4–V5 (normal R-wave progression)

### 3.4 ST Segment Changes

- **Normal**: Isoelectric (within 0.5 mm of baseline in limb leads; within 1 mm in precordial leads)
- **Measured**: End of QRS (J-point) to beginning of T-wave
- **Normal J-point**: May have early repolarization pattern with <1 mm J-point elevation in V2–V4 in young males — this is a normal variant
- **Any elevation >1 mm or depression >0.5 mm** triggers differential and is NOT normal NSR

### 3.5 T-wave Changes

- **Direction**: Concordant with QRS (upright in I, II, V4–V6; inverted in aVR)
- **Amplitude**: <6 mm in limb leads, <10 mm in precordial leads
- **Morphology**: Asymmetric — gradual upslope, rapid downslope. Symmetric peaked T-waves are abnormal.
- **V1**: T-wave may be flat or mildly inverted — normal variant
- **V2–V3**: T-wave inversion in women up to age 50 may be normal variant

### 3.6 QT/QTc Changes

- **QT interval**: Rate-dependent. Measured from QRS onset to T-wave offset in the lead with largest T-wave (usually V5 or V6).
- **QTc (Bazett)**: QT / √(RR in seconds). Normal <440 ms (males), <460 ms (females).
- **Normal NSR QTc**: 380–440 ms. Values outside this range are not NSR abnormalities but separate findings requiring documentation.

### 3.7 Other Features

- **U-wave**: Small, positive deflection after T-wave. Best seen in V2–V3. Amplitude <25% of T-wave. Normal variant; large U-waves suggest hypokalemia.
- **RR regularity**: Coefficient of variation <5% is expected. Strict regularity with no variation at all can paradoxically suggest pacemaker rhythm.

## 4. Differential Diagnosis

### 4.1 Mimics

| Condition | How it mimics NSR | Key differentiating feature |
|-----------|-------------------|----------------------------|
| Accelerated junctional rhythm | Regular narrow QRS, rate 60–100 | P-waves absent, retrograde, or dissociated |
| Low atrial rhythm | Regular P before QRS | P-wave axis <0° (negative II, aVF) |
| Atrial flutter with variable block | Regular-appearing rhythm | Sawtooth flutter waves; atrial rate 250–350 |
| Pacemaker rhythm | Regular, narrow or wide QRS | Pacemaker spike before P or QRS |
| Sinus arrhythmia | Sinus P-waves present | PP variability >10%; rate changes with respiration |

### 4.2 Coexisting Conditions

Normal sinus rhythm does not exclude structural or metabolic pathology. NSR can coexist with:
- STEMI (ST elevation present but rate and rhythm are sinus)
- Bundle branch block (wide QRS but sinus P-waves)
- LVH, RVH (voltage criteria met but rhythm is sinus)
- Long QT, short QT (QTc abnormal but rhythm is sinus)
- Electrolyte abnormalities affecting T-wave or ST segment

The rhythm classification and morphology classification are separate diagnostic domains.

## 5. STAT Classification

| Priority | Criteria |
|----------|----------|
| NOT STAT | Normal sinus rhythm with no concomitant morphologic abnormalities |
| Routine | Standard finding — document and compare to prior ECG |
| Flag for review | If NSR is noted but interval abnormalities, ST changes, or axis deviation also present — those findings drive priority, not the rhythm |

NSR itself is never the source of a STAT alert. The agent must recognize NSR as the baseline and escalate ONLY if morphologic co-findings warrant it.

## 6. Reasoning Complexity Analysis

### 6.1 Reasoning Domains Required

- Rate calculation (RR interval counting or fiducial-derived rate)
- P-wave detection and axis determination
- PR interval measurement
- QRS duration measurement
- Rhythm regularity assessment (PP and RR variance)
- Exclusion of mimics (junctional, low atrial, etc.)

### 6.2 Feature Dependencies

- Accurate P-wave fiducial detection is foundational — all rhythm analysis depends on reliable P onset/offset
- RR interval regularity requires at minimum 5 consecutive beats for statistical confidence
- P-wave axis requires accurate measurement in at least leads I and aVF (or full 12-lead)

### 6.3 Cross-Condition Interactions

NSR is the comparison baseline for every other condition. The agent's NSR classifier must run first in any rhythm classification pipeline. A finding of NSR does NOT close the diagnostic pipeline — morphologic analysis must continue independently.

### 6.4 Reasoning Chain Sketch

```
1. Calculate ventricular rate from RR intervals → 60–100 bpm?
2. Detect P-waves → present before every QRS?
3. Measure P-wave axis → 0–75°? (upright II, negative aVR)
4. Measure PR interval → 120–200 ms, constant?
5. Measure QRS duration → <120 ms?
6. Assess RR regularity → variation <10%?
7. All 6 criteria met → classify NSR
8. Continue to morphologic analysis (ST, T, intervals, axis)
```

### 6.5 Confidence Anchors

- Upright P in II + inverted P in aVR: very high confidence for sinus origin
- Constant PR interval: confirms normal AV conduction
- QRS <120 ms: confirms normal ventricular conduction
- Any deviation from above: do NOT call NSR — specify the abnormality

### 6.6 Difficulty Score

| Dimension | Score (1–5) | Notes |
|-----------|-------------|-------|
| Pattern Recognition | 1 | All criteria are simple measurements; no gestalt required |
| Differential Complexity | 1 | Few true mimics; all easily excluded with systematic criteria |
| Measurement Precision | 2 | P-wave axis and PR interval require accurate fiducials |
| Clinical Integration | 1 | No clinical context needed to identify NSR itself |
| Inter-lead Reasoning | 1 | Lead-by-lead findings are highly consistent and predictable |
| Rare Variant Handling | 1 | Few edge cases; normal variants well characterized |
| **Overall** | **1.0** | **Simplest possible finding — the reference state** |

|-------|--------------------------|-------------------|

| **IT** (Ischemia/Territory) | Not involved | No ST/T abnormalities assessed for this condition |

### Primary Agent
**RRC** — Rate, rhythm regularity, P-wave presence/morphology, and normal conduction intervals are the defining features of a normal sinus rhythm.

### Cross-Domain Hints
No cross-domain hints required — single-domain condition.

### CDS Specific Role
CDS confirms that RRC reports normal sinus rhythm, IT reports no ischemic findings, and MR reports normal morphology and repolarization. Its sole task is to integrate these three normal outputs into a final "Normal ECG" label with high confidence. No conflict resolution is needed.

## 9. Edge Cases and Pitfalls

- **P-wave buried in T-wave at high-normal rates (90–100 bpm)**: At borderline rates, the P-wave of the next beat may partially overlap the preceding T-wave. Use V1 where P is biphasic and more distinguishable. Do not misclassify as no-P rhythm.
- **Sinus rhythm with first-degree AV block**: Rate and P-wave criteria met, but PR >200 ms. This is NOT pure NSR — document "sinus rhythm with first-degree AV block."
- **Normal variant early repolarization**: J-point elevation up to 2 mm in V2–V4 in young males does not violate NSR classification but must be flagged separately to exclude STEMI.
- **Rate at exactly 60 or 100 bpm**: Boundary cases. Apply strict criterion: 60–100 inclusive. <60 = sinus bradycardia; >100 = sinus tachycardia.
- **Lead placement artifact**: Limb lead reversal (e.g., LA/RA swap) will invert P-waves in lead I and aVR, mimicking non-sinus rhythm. Check lead configuration before classifying.
- **Pediatric patients**: Normal rates are higher (100–160 in neonates). NSR criteria are age-adjusted; the rate criterion alone cannot be applied without age context.
- **Athletes**: Resting rates of 40–55 bpm with sinus P-waves = sinus bradycardia, not NSR. Do not apply "athlete" exception without explicit clinical context.
