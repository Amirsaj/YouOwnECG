## 1. Pathophysiology Root Cause

### 1.1 Anatomy of the Right Bundle Branch
The right bundle branch (RBB) is a thin, discrete fascicle that descends along the right side of the interventricular septum before fanning out across the right ventricular endocardium. It carries conduction from the His bundle to the right ventricle (RV). Unlike the left bundle, it has a single, anatomically compact structure with limited collateral blood supply — making it vulnerable to both intrinsic disease and pressure-related disruption.

### 1.2 What "Incomplete" Means
In complete RBBB, conduction through the right bundle is entirely blocked: the right ventricle is depolarized entirely via slow cell-to-cell spread from the left ventricular septum, producing a QRS ≥120ms. In **incomplete RBBB**, conduction delay is partial — some conduction travels through the right bundle but is slowed. The result is the same directional sequence of activation but compressed in time: QRS 110–119ms. The morphological fingerprint (rSR' in V1, wide slurred S in I and V6) is identical to complete RBBB; the QRS simply hasn't crossed the 120ms threshold yet.

### 1.3 Causes and Clinical Spectrum
Incomplete RBBB sits at a spectrum from entirely benign to a harbinger of right-sided pathology:

- **Normal variant**: Found in 2–4% of healthy adults, most commonly young athletes or individuals with thin chest walls. Right ventricular volume load from athletic conditioning can widen the terminal QRS forces without structural disease.
- **Right heart pathology**: Atrial septal defect (ASD), especially ostium secundum, is classically associated with incomplete RBBB because volume overload of the RV slows right-sided conduction. Pulmonary hypertension, pulmonary embolism, and right ventricular hypertrophy (RVH) from any cause can produce the same pattern.
- **Progressive disease**: Incomplete RBBB may represent early Lenegre-Lev disease (fibrotic degeneration of the conduction system). Serial ECGs showing progressive QRS widening are a clinical red flag.
- **Post-surgical**: Common after right heart catheterization, TAVR, or surgical ASD repair due to mechanical trauma to the RBB.

## 2. ECG Presentation — Lead by Lead

### 2.1 Diagnostic Criteria (2025 AHA/ESC Guidelines)
| Criterion | Requirement |
|---|---|
| QRS Duration | 110–119ms (borderline zone; <120ms by definition) |
| Terminal R' in V1/V2 | rSR' or rsR' pattern: small initial r, S descent, terminal R' slur/notch |
| Wide S wave in I, V6 | Broad, slurred terminal S wave (same direction as complete RBBB) |
| QRS Axis | Usually normal; right axis may suggest RVH |
| T waves | Usually concordant (upright) in V1 given incomplete block; discordant T inversion in V1 raises concern |

### 2.2 Lead-by-Lead Manifestation
| Lead | Expected Morphology | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| I | Broad, slurred terminal S wave | Less pronounced than complete RBBB |
| II | Usually unremarkable | Axis-dependent |
| III | May show slight terminal slurring | |
| aVR | Terminal broad R wave (slurred) | Mirror of I |
| aVL | May show slurred S | |
| aVF | Usually normal | Axis-dependent |
| V1 | rSR' or rsR' pattern; R' height variable | KEY LEAD — right precordial terminal R' |
| V2 | rSR' often visible; may mirror V1 | Less pronounced than V1 |
| V3 | Transition zone; may have minimal S slurring | |
| V4 | Usually normal transition | |
| V5 | Mild terminal S slurring | |
| V6 | Broad, slurred terminal S wave | Mirrors lead I |

### 2.3 Key Leads
- **V1**: The definitive diagnostic lead. rSR' morphology (two peaks — initial r from septal activation, S from left ventricular forces, terminal R' from delayed RV activation). In incomplete RBBB, R' amplitude is typically smaller than in complete RBBB.
- **I and V6**: Terminal S wave confirms that the final vector of depolarization is directed rightward (away from I and V6), consistent with delayed RV free wall activation.
- **V5**: Useful to confirm slurred terminal S in left precordial leads.

### 2.4 Beat-by-Beat Considerations
- QRS morphology is consistent beat to beat in sinus rhythm — unlike rate-related (aberrant) RBBB which appears with fast rates and disappears at slower rates.
- If incomplete RBBB appears only at faster heart rates (short-long-short sequences), this is **rate-related aberrancy**, not fixed incomplete RBBB — a critical distinction.
- PR interval: typically normal. If prolonged + incomplete RBBB, consider multifascicular conduction disease.

## 3. Morphology Details

### 3.1 QRS Duration Measurement
Measure QRS from earliest onset in any lead to the last deflection in any lead. Use the widest complex across all 12 leads. For borderline cases (109–121ms), measure in at least 3 leads and average. Digital calipers are preferred.

### 3.2 The rSR' Complex in V1
The three deflections reflect three sequential activation events:
1. **r**: Septal activation (left-to-right through normal septal fibers → toward V1)
2. **S**: Left ventricular free wall activation (large mass, dominates → away from V1)
3. **R'**: Delayed right ventricular free wall activation (slow cell-to-cell spread or delayed RBB conduction → toward V1)

In incomplete RBBB, the R' is present but QRS total is <120ms — the delay is not complete.

### 3.3 Terminal S Wave in I and V6
The terminal S wave is the same delayed rightward vector (RV free wall activation) seen from the left. It is broad and slurred, not sharp. A sharp narrow S wave in V6 is not RBBB — it may be RVH or normal variant.

### 3.4 R' Amplitude
In incomplete RBBB, R' in V1 is typically <5mm. Larger R' amplitudes suggest either complete RBBB or coexisting RVH. R' > R (dominant R' with loss of the initial r) suggests posterior MI + RBBB, not isolated RBBB.

### 3.5 T Wave Polarity
In leads V1–V2, T waves may be upright (unlike complete RBBB where T waves are typically inverted/discordant). This is because the repolarization abnormality of complete block — driven by altered depolarization sequence — is less pronounced in incomplete RBBB. Deeply inverted T waves in V1–V2 in incomplete RBBB are a flag for RV strain or ischemia.

### 3.6 QRS Axis in Incomplete RBBB
Axis is usually normal. Right axis deviation (RAD) accompanying incomplete RBBB elevates suspicion for:
- RVH from pulmonary hypertension or chronic lung disease
- Lateral MI (Q waves in I, aVL)
- Left posterior fascicular block (LPFB) — though this would require ruling out other RAD causes

### 3.7 The ASD Pattern
A classic teaching triad for ASD (ostium secundum):
1. Incomplete RBBB (rSR' V1)
2. Right axis deviation
3. P-pulmonale (tall peaked P waves in II, >2.5mm) or right atrial enlargement

This combination in a young patient without apparent cardiac disease should prompt echocardiogram to rule out ASD.

## 4. Differential Diagnosis

### 4.1 Mimics Table
| Condition | Differentiating Feature |
|---|---|
| Complete RBBB | QRS ≥120ms; otherwise identical morphology |
| Normal rSR' variant (V1) | QRS <110ms; no terminal S in I/V6; R' < r; often seen in children |
| RVH | RAD + dominant R in V1 but typically no rSR' bifid pattern; strain pattern in V1-V2 |
| Rate-related RBBB aberrancy | Only present at faster rates; disappears with slowing |
| Posterior MI | Dominant R in V1 (tall broad R, not bifid rSR'); ST depression V1-V3; inferior Q waves |
| RBBB + LAFB (bifascicular) | Left axis deviation accompanies the rSR' pattern |
| Brugada type 2/3 | rSR' in V1-V2 but ST segment coved/saddle-back; no S in I/V6 |

### 4.2 Coexisting Conditions
- **ASD**: Incomplete RBBB may be the first ECG sign. Atrial arrhythmias (AF, flutter) may coexist from right atrial enlargement.
- **PE**: Acute PE can produce incomplete RBBB from acute RV pressure overload. Coexisting S1Q3T3, sinus tachycardia.
- **Pulmonary hypertension**: Progressive RV pressure load. May see evolving RVH criteria alongside incomplete RBBB.
- **Athletic heart**: Sinus bradycardia, early repolarization, LVH voltage changes may coexist.

## 5. STAT Classification

| Category | Classification | Rationale |
|---|---|---|
| Isolated incomplete RBBB, asymptomatic | NOT STAT | Likely benign; normal variant or stable structural change |
| New incomplete RBBB in acute chest pain | MODERATE ALERT | Rule out PE, acute RV strain, or early RBBB progression |
| Incomplete RBBB + RAD + P-pulmonale | MODERATE ALERT | Suspect ASD or pulmonary hypertension; workup warranted |
| Serial ECG showing progressive QRS widening | MODERATE ALERT | Track toward complete RBBB; may indicate progressive conduction disease |
| Incomplete RBBB + acute anterior MI features | HIGH ALERT | Watch for bifascicular or complete heart block progression |

**Overall STAT status: NOT STAT** — isolated incomplete RBBB does not require emergent intervention but warrants documentation and, in appropriate clinical context, further evaluation.

## 6. Reasoning Complexity Analysis

### 6.1 Pattern Recognition Complexity
The rSR' pattern in V1 is visually distinctive but requires confirmation across multiple leads. The key algorithmic step is QRS duration measurement: the morphological finding is identical to complete RBBB, so the classifier must measure QRS duration accurately and distinguish 110–119ms from ≥120ms.

### 6.2 Measurement Precision Requirement
QRS duration is the single most critical measurement. ECGdeli fiducial detection must accurately detect QRS onset and offset. Borderline cases (115–119ms vs. 120–122ms) require multi-lead averaging. A 5ms difference changes the diagnosis from incomplete to complete RBBB.

### 6.3 Axis Analysis Requirement
Identifying coexisting right axis deviation is important for clinical interpretation (ASD pattern, RVH, LPFB). Axis calculation requires frontal plane lead analysis (I and aVF quadrant method, or hexaxial reference).

### 6.4 Rate-Related Aberrancy Distinction
Algorithm must assess whether RBBB morphology is present at all heart rates (fixed) or only at faster rates (functional). This requires multi-beat analysis, not single-beat pattern matching.

### 6.5 Clinical Context Integration
True diagnostic value of incomplete RBBB emerges from clinical context: age, symptoms, prior ECGs, echo data. The algorithm must flag clinically relevant combinations (incomplete RBBB + RAD in young patient) for escalation.

### 6.6 Difficulty Score

| Dimension | Score (1–5) | Rationale |
|---|---|---|
| Pattern recognition | 2 | rSR' pattern is visually clear; same as complete RBBB |
| Measurement precision | 2 | QRS duration at borderline range is the key challenge |
| Axis analysis | 2 | Standard axis calculation; no advanced computation |
| Differential complexity | 2 | Limited differential; key distinction is QRS duration |
| Clinical integration | 2 | ASD triad, progression tracking adds context |
| Rate-related distinction | 2 | Requires multi-beat rhythm analysis |
| Rarity/recognition challenge | 1 | Very common finding; well-characterized |
| **Overall Difficulty** | **1.8/5** | Common, well-defined, primarily a measurement precision challenge |

| Agent | Role for Incomplete Right Bundle Branch Block | Key Features Used |
|-------|--------------------------|-------------------|
| **RRC** (Rhythm/Rate/Conduction) | Supporting | QRS duration measurement (100-119ms confirming incomplete vs complete RBBB); rate context |
| **IT** (Ischemia/Territory) | Not involved | No ischemia-specific features in isolated incomplete RBBB |
| **MR** (Morphology/Repolarization) | Primary | rSR' or rsR' in V1-V2 with QRS 100-119ms; wide slurred S in I/V6 (may be less prominent than complete RBBB); confirmation that QRS does not meet complete RBBB threshold |
| **CDS** (Cross-Domain Synthesis) | Standard integration only | Integrates MR rSR' morphology with RRC duration to confirm incomplete RBBB; flags if newly present (may indicate acute PE, RV strain, or ASD); differentiates from normal rSR' variant (QRS <100ms) |

### Primary Agent
**MR** — incomplete RBBB is fundamentally a morphology diagnosis (rSR' in V1-V2 with slurred S in I/V6); QRS duration measurement from RRC is a supporting threshold check.

### Cross-Domain Hints
- MR emits: `cross_domain_hint: "rSR' morphology in V1-V2 detected — RRC must confirm QRS 100-119ms to distinguish incomplete RBBB from complete RBBB (≥120ms) or normal rSR' variant (<100ms)"`

### CDS Specific Role
CDS confirms the QRS duration bracket (100-119ms from RRC) against the rSR' morphology (from MR) to render the incomplete RBBB diagnosis. It differentiates this from a normal rSR' variant (QRS <100ms, no wide S in I/V6) and from complete RBBB (QRS ≥120ms). If incomplete RBBB is newly identified, CDS flags for clinical correlation: new incomplete RBBB may indicate acute PE, ASD, or evolving RV strain. Standard integration with no Sgarbossa consideration required.

## 9. Edge Cases and Pitfalls

**Pitfall 1 — Misclassifying complete RBBB as incomplete**: QRS duration measurement error of even 3–5ms changes classification. Always measure in the widest lead. ECGdeli must use global QRS boundaries across all 12 leads simultaneously.

**Pitfall 2 — Missing progressive widening**: A standalone ECG showing incomplete RBBB is benign. The danger is missing serial widening. Algorithm must trigger comparison with prior ECGs when available.

**Pitfall 3 — Normal rSR' variant in V1**: Many normal ECGs show a small rSR' or rsr' in V1 with QRS <110ms. This is NOT incomplete RBBB. Criteria: QRS must be in the 110–119ms range, AND terminal S must be present in I and V6.

**Pitfall 4 — Brugada mimic**: Brugada type 2 and 3 patterns can look like rSR' in V1–V2. Key distinguishers: Brugada shows saddle-back or coved ST elevation after the R'; no terminal S in I/V6; fever, Na-channel blocker drugs as triggers.

**Pitfall 5 — ASD going undetected**: Young patients with incomplete RBBB + RAD are sometimes dismissed as normal variants. The combination is never truly "normal" and warrants echo.

**Pitfall 6 — Posterior MI**: Posterior MI produces a dominant R in V1 that can superficially resemble terminal R' of RBBB. Key: posterior MI has a single broad R wave (not bifid rSR'), ST depression in V1–V3, and typically inferior/lateral Q waves.
