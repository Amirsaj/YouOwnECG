## 1. Pathophysiology Root Cause

### 1.1 What Goes Wrong

- Accelerated Idioventricular Rhythm (AIVR) occurs when a ventricular focus gains enhanced automaticity and fires at a rate faster than the normal ventricular escape rate (40–60 bpm) but slower than the threshold for ventricular tachycardia (≥100 bpm). By definition: rate 60–100 bpm.
- The ventricular focus "accelerates" beyond its intrinsic escape rate — hence "accelerated" — but not to the level that constitutes a tachycardia. This is a rhythm of enhanced automaticity, not reentry.
- The most clinically important context: **reperfusion arrhythmia**. When coronary blood flow is restored (spontaneously, by thrombolysis, or by PCI) to ischemic ventricular myocardium, the metabolic shift during reperfusion — washout of accumulated potassium, calcium overload of injured cells, catecholamine release — transiently destabilizes the resting membrane potential of ventricular myocytes. Enhanced automaticity arises from ventricular Purkinje fibers or myocardium at the reperfusion border zone.
- Other settings:
  - **Acute MI (without reperfusion)**: Periinfarct Purkinje fibers at the ischemic border zone develop enhanced automaticity.
  - **Digitalis toxicity**: Digoxin inhibits Na/K-ATPase → intracellular Na and Ca overload → DADs → triggered automaticity from ventricular foci (classically at rates that produce AIVR range).
  - **Cocaine intoxication**: Catecholamine excess (cocaine blocks norepinephrine reuptake) → adrenergic stimulation of ventricular foci.
  - **Myocarditis**: Inflammatory injury to conduction tissue → focal enhanced automaticity.
  - **Electrolyte disturbances**: Hypokalemia and hypomagnesemia lower firing threshold of ventricular foci.
- AIVR is a benign escape/acceleration phenomenon in the reperfusion context — it does NOT indicate electrical instability. It reflects metabolic recovery of ischemic tissue, not worsening.

### 1.2 Electrical Consequence

- A ventricular focus fires at 60–100 bpm. Because this rate is near the sinus rate, competition between sinus and ventricular foci occurs.
- When the ventricular rate slightly exceeds the sinus rate, the ventricular focus "takes over" — producing a run of AIVR.
- When sinus rate reasserts itself (e.g., brief sinus acceleration), sinus rhythm returns. The ventricular and sinus rates hover near each other, producing characteristic **isorhythmic dissociation** (AV dissociation at similar rates) and **fusion beats**.
- Depolarization originates in ventricular muscle (not the His-Purkinje system in the normal fashion) → abnormal, slow cell-to-cell conduction → wide, aberrant QRS.
- Ventricular activation pattern depends on focus location:
  - Left ventricular origin → RBBB-like morphology (right ventricle activated late via septum).
  - Right ventricular origin → LBBB-like morphology (left ventricle activated late).

### 1.3 Why It Appears on ECG

- **Wide QRS complexes** (>120 ms): Ventricular origin means the impulse spreads through myocardium via gap junctions (slow conduction, ~0.5 m/s) rather than via the fast His-Purkinje network (3–4 m/s). Total ventricular activation takes >120 ms, producing a wide, slurred QRS.
- **Rate 60–100 bpm**: This specific rate window is the defining criterion. Below this = normal escape rhythm; above this = ventricular tachycardia.
- **AV dissociation and fusion beats**: The sinus node continues to fire independently. When a sinus impulse and a ventricular impulse collide in the ventricles simultaneously, the resulting QRS is a hybrid — partially normal (sinus contribution) and partially wide (ventricular contribution) — producing a **fusion beat** that looks narrower and different from the pure AIVR QRS. Fusion beats are hallmark evidence of two competing pacemakers and strongly support AIVR diagnosis over VT (though fusion can occur in VT too).
- **LBBB or RBBB morphology**: Reflects right vs left ventricular origin, respectively. The morphology is consistent throughout a run (same focus firing each time).

## 2. ECG Presentation — Lead by Lead

### 2.1 Diagnostic Criteria (2025 AHA/ESC Guidelines)

| Criterion | Threshold | Notes |
|-----------|-----------|-------|
| QRS duration | >120 ms | Wide, aberrant QRS; reflects ventricular origin |
| Heart rate | 60–100 bpm | Below this = ventricular escape rhythm; above = VT |
| QRS morphology | LBBB-like (RV origin) or RBBB-like (LV origin) | Consistent morphology throughout the run |
| Onset | Gradual (warm-up) or via fusion beat | Distinguished from VT which can onset suddenly |
| AV dissociation | Present (P-waves dissociated from QRS) or isorhythmic dissociation | Sinus continues independently; P-waves may be visible marching through QRS/T waves |
| Fusion beats | Frequently present | Hybrid QRS between pure sinus and pure AIVR morphology; pathognomonic of competing pacemakers |
| Termination | Gradual (cool-down) or via capture beat | Sinus reasserts when its rate exceeds AIVR rate |

### 2.2 Lead-by-Lead Manifestation

| Lead | Expected Finding | Why | Sensitivity |
|------|-----------------|-----|-------------|
| I | Wide QRS; LBBB-like (negative, broad) if RV origin; RBBB-like (broad R with slur or rsR') if LV origin | Lateral lead (0°) — records left-to-right vs right-to-left activation vector | High for morphology characterization |
| II | Wide QRS; P-waves dissociated (may be visible marching through at sinus rate); fusion beats identifiable here | Inferior lead — good for seeing P-wave activity dissociated from QRS | High for AV dissociation detection; good rhythm assessment lead |
| III | Wide QRS; P-waves dissociated | Inferior lead — complements II for AV dissociation assessment | Moderate |
| aVR | Wide QRS; often broad positive (LV origin) or broad negative (RV origin) deflection | Rightward-superior lead — inverts most normal forces | Moderate |
| aVL | Wide QRS; morphology varies with origin | Left lateral lead — records lateral activation differences between LBBB/RBBB morphologies | Moderate |
| aVF | Wide QRS; P-waves dissociated and visible inferiorly | Inferior lead (+90°) — good for P-wave visualization marching through; AV dissociation often clearest here | High for AV dissociation |
| V1 | **Key morphology lead**: LBBB-like (broad negative rS or QS) if RV origin; RBBB-like (rsR' or broad R) if LV origin | V1 directly records right ventricular activation first or late depending on origin | **Highest** — V1 is the primary morphology classification lead for wide QRS rhythms |
| V2 | Similar to V1; supports morphology classification | Adjacent to V1; same principles | High |
| V3-V4 | Transition zone; wide QRS; poor R-wave progression expected | Transitional leads; ventricular origin disrupts normal R-wave progression | Moderate |
| V5-V6 | Wide QRS; LBBB-like (broad monophasic R, no S) if RV origin; RBBB-like (broad S wave) if LV origin | Left lateral precordial leads — record terminal lateral ventricular activation | High for morphology confirmation |

### 2.3 Key Leads

- **V1**: Primary lead for LBBB vs RBBB morphology classification. Determines whether origin is RV (LBBB pattern in V1) or LV (RBBB pattern in V1).
- **Lead II + aVF**: Best leads for identifying dissociated P-waves and fusion beats — the two features that confirm AIVR vs. VT rate-based distinction.
- **Lead I + V5–V6**: Confirm lateral activation pattern; support morphology classification.
- **Long rhythm strip (Lead II)**: AIVR typically occurs in runs — the rate, onset/offset pattern, and presence of fusion beats are best assessed on a continuous rhythm strip.

### 2.4 Beat-by-Beat Considerations

- **Onset (warm-up)**: AIVR often begins with a fusion beat (sinus + ventricular), then transitions into pure wide QRS complexes as the ventricular rate overtakes the sinus rate. Gradual onset (2–3 beats of increasing QRS width) is characteristic and distinguishes from VT (which can onset abruptly).
- **Offset (cool-down)**: AIVR often terminates with a fusion beat or capture beat (a narrow sinus-conducted QRS that interrupts the AIVR run when sinus rate briefly exceeds ventricular rate). The rhythm returns to sinus.
- **Isorhythmic dissociation**: When sinus rate and AIVR rate are nearly identical, P-waves appear to slowly "walk through" the AIVR QRS complexes — the PR relationship changes gradually across beats. The P-wave is sometimes hidden in QRS, sometimes visible just before or after.
- **Fusion beats**: QRS morphology intermediate between sinus and AIVR morphology. Width is narrower than pure AIVR; morphology is neither purely sinus nor purely wide ventricular. Fusion beats confirm that both sinus and ventricular pacemakers are active simultaneously.
- **Capture beats**: A narrow, normal-duration sinus-conducted QRS that appears during an AIVR run — the sinus impulse "captures" the ventricles before the ventricular focus fires. Capture beats confirm AV conduction is intact.

## 3. Morphology Details

### 3.1 QRS Morphology

- **LBBB-like (RV origin, most common in STEMI reperfusion)**: In V1: broad, predominantly negative rS or QS complex. In V5–V6: broad, monophasic R-wave without S-wave notch. In I: positive broad R.
- **RBBB-like (LV origin)**: In V1: rsR' or broad R, or triphasic RSR'. In V5–V6: broad terminal S-wave. In I: broad S-wave.
- QRS is consistent in morphology throughout a pure AIVR run. Beat-to-beat variation suggests fusion (partial sinus capture) rather than a different origin.
- QRS duration typically 120–160 ms. Extremely wide QRS (>160 ms) is more consistent with VT.

### 3.2 P-Waves

- P-waves continue to fire from the SA node at the sinus rate, independently of the ventricular focus.
- P-waves are dissociated from QRS complexes — no fixed PR relationship.
- P-waves may be visible marching through the ECG at a regular (or near-regular) sinus rate, appearing before, inside, or after QRS complexes depending on the isorhythmic relationship.
- P-waves may be hidden within wide QRS complexes or T-waves — careful inspection of all leads required.

### 3.3 PR Interval

- No fixed PR interval — AV dissociation means there is no consistent P-wave-to-QRS relationship.
- When a P-wave happens to precede a QRS by a normal-appearing PR interval, this is coincidental (isorhythmic dissociation) unless followed by a narrow (capture) QRS.

### 3.4 ST Segment and T-Wave

- T-waves are typically opposite in polarity to the dominant QRS deflection (secondary repolarization abnormality) — this is expected and normal for any ventricular paced or wide-complex rhythm.
- In the post-STEMI reperfusion context, the underlying ST-segment changes from the infarct may still be visible in the return sinus beats — but ST analysis during AIVR is unreliable due to the wide QRS distorting the ST baseline.
- ST elevation during AIVR should not be used to judge reperfusion status — assess ST changes in the sinus beats surrounding the AIVR run.

### 3.5 Rate and Rhythm

- Rate: 60–100 bpm. This is the definitional rate range.
  - Below 60 bpm: ventricular escape rhythm (passive escape, SA/AV node failure, not enhanced automaticity).
  - At or above 100 bpm: ventricular tachycardia by definition, regardless of morphology.
- Rhythm: approximately regular during a pure AIVR run. Irregularity occurs at onset/offset (fusion beats, capture beats) and during isorhythmic dissociation transitions.

### 3.6 Axis

- Axis is abnormal (ventricular origin) — typically superior (northwest quadrant) or inferior axis depending on origin.
- RV apical origin: left axis deviation, superior axis.
- LV inferior wall origin: right or normal axis.
- Axis analysis supports morphology characterization but is not required for AIVR diagnosis.

### 3.7 Special Features

- **Fusion beats**: Wide hybrid QRS complexes occurring at onset or offset. Pathognomonic of AIVR when occurring in the 60–100 bpm rate range.
- **Capture beats**: Narrow QRS interrupting AIVR run. Confirms intact AV conduction and that the wide QRS rhythm is ventricular (not aberrant supraventricular conduction).
- **Gradual warm-up and cool-down**: Distinguished from VT (which can appear and terminate abruptly, though VT can also show warm-up).
- **Clinical context is decisive**: In post-STEMI reperfusion, rate 60–100 bpm, wide QRS, starting within minutes of PCI/thrombolysis = AIVR until proven otherwise. No further arrhythmia workup required.

## 4. Differential Diagnosis

### 4.1 Mimics

| Condition | Key Similarity | Key Difference | Distinguishing Feature |
|-----------|---------------|----------------|----------------------|
| Ventricular Tachycardia (VT) | Wide QRS, ventricular origin, AV dissociation possible | VT rate ≥100 bpm; AIVR rate <100 bpm | **Rate is the sole criterion**: if rate ≥100 bpm with wide QRS = VT by definition |
| Ventricular escape rhythm | Wide QRS, ventricular origin | Escape rate <60 bpm (passive escape, not enhanced automaticity); occurs after sinus pause | Rate <60 bpm + preceded by a pause = escape rhythm, not AIVR |
| Accelerated junctional rhythm | Rate 60–100 bpm, may resemble | Junctional rhythm: NARROW QRS (normal His-Purkinje conduction); may have retrograde P-waves | Narrow QRS = junctional (not ventricular); wide QRS = AIVR |
| Sinus rhythm with LBBB/RBBB | Wide QRS | BBB: fixed morphology tied to P-waves with constant PR; atrial rate = ventricular rate; no AV dissociation | P:QRS = 1:1 with fixed PR in BBB; AV dissociation + fusion beats = AIVR |
| Paced rhythm (ventricular pacing) | Wide QRS, LBBB-like | Pacemaker spikes precede QRS; fixed pacing rate; no fusion/capture pattern consistent with AIVR | Pacing spikes visible (especially in leads closest to lead electrode) |
| SVT with aberrant conduction | Wide QRS, fast rate | SVT with aberrancy: rate usually >100 bpm (often >150); P-waves related to QRS or retrograde | Rate, P-wave relationship, and response to vagal maneuvers differentiate |

### 4.2 Coexisting Conditions

- **STEMI (acute)**: The primary diagnosis in the classic AIVR context. AIVR onset within minutes-to-hours of PCI or thrombolysis is a reperfusion marker. ST changes in surrounding sinus beats should be followed for resolution.
- **Complete AV block**: Can coexist — AIVR may be the escape rhythm in complete AV block at an accelerated rate. Distinguish by noting whether P-waves are dissociated due to block (P rate > QRS rate) or due to isorhythmic competition (P rate ≈ QRS rate).
- **Digitalis toxicity**: AIVR coexists with other digoxin arrhythmias (PAT with block, bidirectional VT in severe toxicity, junctional tachycardia). Bidirectional VT (alternating QRS axis beat-to-beat) is pathognomonic of severe digoxin toxicity and must be distinguished from AIVR.
- **Cocaine intoxication**: May produce STEMI + AIVR simultaneously (cocaine-induced coronary spasm with spontaneous reperfusion).

## 5. STAT Classification

| Parameter | Value | Clinical Implication |
|-----------|-------|---------------------|
| STAT Level | **NOT STAT** (in reperfusion context) | In post-STEMI reperfusion: AIVR is a FAVORABLE sign. No treatment required. Do not suppress. |
| STAT Level | **URGENT** (if etiology uncertain or hemodynamic compromise) | If wide QRS rate approaches 100 bpm, if hemodynamic instability present, or if context is unclear — escalate to rule out VT |
| Treatment | None required for AIVR itself in reperfusion | Suppression with antiarrhythmics (lidocaine, amiodarone) is NOT indicated and may be harmful |
| Monitoring | Continuous cardiac monitoring | Watch for transition to VT (rate acceleration ≥100 bpm) — that changes management entirely |
| Clinical Signal | AIVR = reperfusion marker in STEMI | Confirms successful PCI/thrombolysis. Document in the clinical record as a reperfusion sign. |
| Do NOT cardiovert | Electrical cardioversion is inappropriate | AIVR is not hemodynamically significant and will resolve spontaneously |
| Exception | Digitalis toxicity AIVR | If caused by digoxin toxicity, address the toxicity (Digibind for severe cases); AIVR from this cause may require monitoring for degeneration |

## 6. Reasoning Complexity Analysis

### 6.1 Signal Quality Requirements

- QRS morphology must be clearly visible to classify as LBBB vs RBBB type and to identify fusion beats.
- P-wave detection during a wide QRS rhythm is challenging — P-waves may be buried within QRS complexes or T-waves. Signal clarity in between-beat isoelectric segments is required.
- Rate measurement must be precise: a single bpm error near the 100 bpm threshold can result in AIVR vs VT misclassification.

### 6.2 Number of Leads Required

- Minimum: Lead II (rhythm strip, AV dissociation, fusion beats) + V1 (morphology classification).
- Optimal: Full 12-lead for complete morphology characterization and to identify concurrent STEMI findings in surrounding sinus beats.

### 6.3 Cross-Domain Reasoning

- Clinical context is critical and changes the interpretation entirely: AIVR post-PCI = benign reperfusion marker; AIVR in unknown context = must rule out VT.
- Cardiac history (recent STEMI, PCI, thrombolysis) directly informs AIVR probability.
- Drug history (digoxin levels, cocaine exposure) must be integrated.

### 6.4 Temporal Pattern Complexity

- AIVR is a run-based finding: single beats are ambiguous. A run of 3+ consecutive wide QRS complexes at 60–100 bpm with AV dissociation is the diagnostic pattern.
- Onset/offset analysis (fusion and capture beats) requires sequential beat analysis, not single-beat classification.
- Rate stability across the run should be confirmed — gradual rate variation is expected (enhanced automaticity warm-up/cool-down), not abrupt rate jumps.

### 6.5 Differential Complexity

- The VT differential is the most clinically critical: the rate threshold (100 bpm) is the sole criterion separating AIVR from VT. A single miscount of RR intervals could be consequential.
- Fusion beat identification requires recognizing intermediate QRS morphology — this requires template comparison of both sinus QRS and AIVR QRS morphologies.
- Context-dependent interpretation: the same ECG finding means very different things clinically in a post-PCI patient vs. an undifferentiated wide complex tachycardia patient.

### 6.6 Difficulty Score

| Dimension | Score (1–5) | Notes |
|-----------|-------------|-------|
| Signal clarity needed | 3 | P-wave detection during wide QRS rhythm is challenging; fusion beat identification requires clean QRS morphology |
| Number of leads required | 2 | Lead II + V1 minimum; 12-lead preferred for STEMI context and full characterization |
| Cross-domain reasoning | 3 | Clinical context (post-PCI, digitalis, cocaine) directly changes interpretation; rate threshold adjudication required |
| Temporal pattern complexity | 3 | Run analysis required; onset/offset fusion beats add complexity; rate measurement precision near 100 bpm threshold critical |
| Differential complexity | 2 | Rate threshold (100 bpm) is the primary VT differential — simple in principle, but precise rate counting near the boundary is non-trivial |
| Rarity in PTB-XL | 3 | AIVR uncommon in PTB-XL; limited post-PCI recordings; training examples sparse |
| Overall difficulty | **2.5/5** | Moderate: rate precision + fusion beat recognition + clinical context together make this modestly complex despite appearing simple |

### Primary Agent
**RRC** — AIVR is characterized by its rate (40–100 bpm), wide QRS, and AV dissociation with fusion beats; all timing and conduction criteria are RRC-domain.

### Cross-Domain Hints
- RRC → IT: `cross_domain_hint: "slow_wide_complex_rhythm_with_AV_dissociation — check for concurrent or recent STEMI to classify as reperfusion AIVR"`
- IT → CDS: `cross_domain_hint: "STEMI_or_recent_reperfusion_detected — correlate with RRC wide rhythm for AIVR reperfusion marker classification"`

### CDS Specific Role
CDS integrates RRC's AIVR identification (rate, wide QRS, AV dissociation) with IT's detection of concurrent or evolving STEMI to determine whether AIVR represents a reperfusion arrhythmia — a benign and expected finding after successful thrombolysis or PCI. Without the IT context, AIVR could be confused with VT; CDS's cross-domain synthesis is essential to correctly classify urgency.

## 7. RAG Knowledge Requirements

### 7.1 Essential Knowledge Chunks

- AIVR diagnostic criteria: wide QRS >120 ms, rate 60–100 bpm, LBBB or RBBB morphology, AV dissociation, fusion beats
- Rate boundary rules: <60 bpm = ventricular escape; 60–100 bpm = AIVR; ≥100 bpm = VT
- AIVR as reperfusion arrhythmia: timing (within minutes to 2 hours post-reperfusion), clinical significance (favorable sign), management (observe, do not treat)
- Fusion beat definition and identification: hybrid QRS morphology at AIVR onset/offset indicating simultaneous sinus + ventricular activation
- Capture beat definition: narrow sinus-conducted QRS interrupting AIVR run confirming intact AV conduction
- AIVR in digitalis toxicity: mechanism (Na/K-ATPase inhibition → DADs), differentiation from bidirectional VT
- AIVR in cocaine intoxication: catecholamine excess, potential concurrent coronary spasm/STEMI
- 2025 AHA/ESC ventricular arrhythmia guidelines: AIVR classification as non-sustained ventricular arrhythmia not requiring treatment

## 8. Dashboard Visualization Specification

### 8.1 Primary Display Elements

- **Rhythm strip (Lead II + V1 simultaneous)**: 10-second strip with color highlighting of AIVR run vs. sinus beats. Onset/offset fusion and capture beats labeled explicitly.
- **Rate trend**: Beat-to-beat instantaneous rate plot with a horizontal reference line at 100 bpm (VT threshold). AIVR run shaded below 100 bpm line. Any rate approach toward 100 bpm flagged visually.
- **Morphology comparison panel**: Side-by-side averaged QRS templates for (1) preceding sinus beats, (2) AIVR QRS, and (3) fusion beats — allows clinician to visually confirm the three are morphologically distinct.
- **AV dissociation display**: P-wave markers plotted against QRS markers on a timeline to display the dissociated relationship.

### 8.2 Annotations

- AIVR QRS complexes labeled "AIVR".
- Fusion beats labeled "FUSION" with a distinct color.
- Capture beats labeled "CAPTURE".
- P-waves during AV dissociation labeled with a separate marker (not associated with any QRS).
- Rate displayed as: "AIVR Rate: XX bpm" with note "Within AIVR range (60–100 bpm)."
- Clinical context banner (if post-STEMI context detected): "AIVR in post-reperfusion context — consistent with reperfusion arrhythmia. Monitoring recommended."

### 8.3 Pitfall Warnings

## 9. Edge Cases and Pitfalls

- **AIVR vs VT at the 100 bpm boundary**: A run of wide QRS complexes at 98–102 bpm is the most difficult case. A single RR interval measurement error flips the diagnosis. Mitigation: average rate over ≥5 beats; report as "rate XX bpm — at threshold; clinical correlation required" if within ±5 bpm of 100 bpm.
- **AIVR without fusion beats**: Not all AIVR runs show fusion beats (e.g., if sinus rate is significantly slower than AIVR rate). The absence of fusion beats does not exclude AIVR — use rate + morphology + context.
- **AIVR degenerating to VT**: The clinical emergency is if AIVR rate accelerates to ≥100 bpm — this converts from a benign finding to a potentially hemodynamically significant ventricular tachycardia. Continuous monitoring is required for this transition. The algorithm must not "lock in" an AIVR classification and miss VT acceleration.
- **Sinus tachycardia obscuring AIVR**: If the patient's sinus rate is also near 60–100 bpm, isorhythmic dissociation may produce P-waves that appear to march through at a nearly identical rate to the AIVR — making the P-QRS relationship appear near-normal by coincidence. Fusion beats and QRS morphology analysis are required to avoid misclassifying AIVR as sinus rhythm with BBB.
- **Post-cardiac arrest AIVR**: After resuscitation from VF/VT, AIVR may occur as the heart's rhythm recovers — similar mechanism to post-reperfusion AIVR. Benign in this context too, but the clinical situation requires close monitoring.
- **Digitalis toxicity: AIVR vs bidirectional VT**: Bidirectional VT (rate typically >100 bpm, alternating QRS axis) is pathognomonic of severe digoxin toxicity and requires urgent treatment (Digibind). AIVR from digoxin is slower and less ominous. The rate and axis alternation distinguish them — rate measurement and axis analysis per beat are both required.
- **Pediatric AIVR**: In neonates and infants, normal heart rates are higher; AIVR rate ranges are different. PTB-XL is an adult dataset — pediatric AIVR edge cases are not represented and algorithm confidence should be flagged as lower in young patients.
