## 1. Pathophysiology Root Cause

### 1.1 Cellular Mechanism
Magnesium is the most abundant intracellular divalent cation and serves as a critical cofactor for the Na/K-ATPase pump. This pump maintains the resting membrane potential by extruding 3 Na+ in exchange for 2 K+ — the electrogenic activity that sustains the negative resting membrane potential and keeps intracellular K+ high.

When Mg2+ is depleted, Na/K-ATPase activity is impaired. The downstream consequences:
1. Intracellular K+ cannot be maintained → K+ leaks out → secondary hypokalemia
2. Intracellular Na+ rises → Na/Ca2+ exchanger activates → intracellular Ca2+ increases → triggered arrhythmia potential via delayed afterdepolarizations (DADs)

Additionally, Mg2+ directly blocks NMDA receptors and IKr channels. Mg2+ deficiency reduces membrane stabilization → increased excitability of both atrial and ventricular tissue.

### 1.2 Why Hypomagnesemia Mimics Hypokalemia on ECG
Because the primary mechanism is Na/K-ATPase impairment → secondary hypokalemia, the ECG changes of hypomagnesemia are IDENTICAL to hypokalemia. You cannot distinguish isolated hypomagnesemia from hypokalemia on the ECG alone. Lab values are required.

In practice, they almost always coexist — both K+ and Mg2+ are lost together via the kidney (diuretics, alcoholism, Gitelman/Bartter syndromes) or GI tract (diarrhea, malabsorption). Treating hypokalemia alone (without replacing Mg2+) will fail — the kidney will continue wasting K+ because the Na/K-ATPase cannot recapture it without adequate Mg2+.

### 1.3 Arrhythmia Mechanism Specific to Mg2+ Deficiency
Beyond hypokalemia-like changes, Mg2+ has independent anti-arrhythmic properties:
- Mg2+ blocks IKr in a physiologic sense — this is the mechanism of IV Mg2+ treatment for TdP: IV Mg2+ suppresses the IKr-dependent triggered beats that initiate TdP
- IV MgSO4 is the first-line treatment for TdP regardless of the serum Mg2+ level
- This means: even if Mg2+ is measured as normal, IV Mg2+ is still given for TdP — it works pharmacologically by suppressing triggered activity

## 2. ECG Presentation — Lead by Lead

### 2.1 Diagnostic Criteria

| Criterion | Finding | Threshold / Notes |
|---|---|---|
| U wave prominence | U wave > T wave amplitude | Identical to hypokalemia; best V2-V3 |
| T-wave flattening | T amplitude reduced | Progressive with severity |
| QU prolongation | Apparent QT prolongation (QU) | U merges with T; measured to end of U wave |
| ST depression | Horizontal or downsloping | Lateral leads predominant |
| PVCs | Frequent, multifocal | Triggered activity from DADs |
| TdP | Polymorphic VT | Life-threatening; IV Mg2+ is first-line treatment |
| Isolated Mg2+ ECG | Indistinguishable from hypokalemia | Lab values required for definitive diagnosis |

### 2.2 Lead-by-Lead Manifestation

| Lead | Typical Finding | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| I | T-wave flattening, ST depression | Lateral |
| II | T-wave flattening, U wave | Inferior |
| III | Variable | Less prominent |
| aVR | Reciprocal changes | |
| aVL | T flattening, ST depression | Lateral |
| aVF | T flattening, U wave | Inferior |
| V1 | U wave, T changes | |
| V2 | Prominent U wave | PRIMARY diagnostic lead |
| V3 | Prominent U wave | PRIMARY diagnostic lead |
| V4 | U wave, T flattening | |
| V5 | ST depression, U wave | Lateral |
| V6 | ST depression, U wave | Lateral |

### 2.3 Key Leads
Identical to hypokalemia: **V2 and V3** for U wave prominence; **V5-V6, I, aVL** for ST depression and T-wave changes.

No ECG lead or finding distinguishes hypomagnesemia from hypokalemia. The ECG appearance is the same because the mechanism (secondary K+ depletion and Na/K-ATPase impairment) is the same.

### 2.4 Beat-by-Beat Considerations
- U wave follows T wave at 150–250 ms after T peak
- Severe hypomagnesemia: T-U fusion with apparent QT prolongation
- PVCs from triggered activity (DADs) — same mechanism as hypokalemia
- TdP: R-on-T PVC during prolonged repolarization → initiates polymorphic VT
- IV MgSO4 suppresses DAD-triggered PVCs → terminates TdP

## 3. Morphology Details

### 3.1 U Wave
- Morphologically identical to hypokalemia: positive (upright) in V2-V5, amplitude exceeding T wave in symptomatic disease
- U wave prominence is from secondary hypokalemia (Purkinje repolarization prolonged)
- Direct Mg2+ effect on U waves independent of K+: possible but not clinically distinguishable

### 3.2 T Wave
- Progressive flattening
- Inversion in severe disease
- Same progression as hypokalemia

### 3.3 ST Segment
- Horizontal or downsloping ST depression in lateral leads
- Same pattern as hypokalemia

### 3.4 QRS Complex
- Narrow and normal
- No characteristic QRS changes

### 3.5 PR Interval
- May be mildly prolonged (Mg2+ plays a role in AV nodal conduction)
- Not a prominent finding

### 3.6 QTc/QU Interval
- Apparent QTc prolongation reflects QU prolongation (U wave merged with T)
- True QTc may be near-normal; QU is the clinically relevant interval
- QU >500 ms = high TdP risk

### 3.7 Rhythms Associated
- Sinus rhythm initially
- PVCs (bigeminal, multifocal)
- Atrial fibrillation (Mg2+ deficiency is a trigger and perpetuator of AF)
- TdP → VF
- Mg2+ is given post-cardiac surgery prophylactically to reduce AF incidence

## 4. Differential Diagnosis

### 4.1 Mimics

| Condition | Overlapping Feature | Distinguishing Feature |
|---|---|---|
| Hypokalemia | Identical ECG | Lab values only; K+ low; Mg2+ may be normal |
| Normal U waves | Visible U waves in V2-V3 | U < T amplitude; both electrolytes normal |
| Lateral ischemia | ST depression V4-V6 | Territory-specific; troponin rise; no U waves |
| Digoxin effect | T-wave changes | Scooped ST; characteristic morphology; no U wave prominence |
| LVH (strain) | ST depression | Voltage criteria; strain in V5-V6 with asymmetric T inversion |

### 4.2 Coexisting Conditions
- **Hypokalemia:** Virtually always coexists. Both are lost via same mechanism (diuretics, alcoholism, diarrhea). Treating only one is ineffective.
- **Hypocalcemia:** Mg2+ deficiency impairs PTH secretion → secondary hypocalcemia; all three electrolytes may be abnormal simultaneously
- **Alcoholism:** Classic triad: hypomagnesemia + hypokalemia + hypocalcemia + hypophosphatemia
- **Diuretic use:** Loop diuretics (furosemide) and thiazides cause renal Mg2+ and K+ wasting
- **Proton pump inhibitors (PPIs):** Long-term use causes renal Mg2+ wasting via TRPM6 channel downregulation — increasingly recognized as a cause of severe hypomagnesemia
- **Refeeding syndrome:** Rapid insulin secretion with glucose loading drives K+, Mg2+, and phosphate intracellularly → sudden severe depletion of all three

## 5. STAT Classification

| Severity | Mg2+ Level | ECG Finding | Action |
|---|---|---|---|
| Mild | 1.2–1.7 mg/dL | Subtle T flattening, mild U waves | Oral Mg2+ supplement; check K+ |
| Moderate | 0.8–1.2 mg/dL | U > T, QU prolongation | IV MgSO4 2g over 15 min, monitor; replace K+ simultaneously |
| Severe | <0.8 mg/dL | PVCs, QU >500 ms, T-U fusion | STAT — IV MgSO4 infusion, continuous monitoring, K+ repletion |
| Critical | Any with TdP | Polymorphic VT | IV MgSO4 2g bolus IV → terminates TdP; defibrillate if sustained |

## 6. Reasoning Complexity Analysis

### 6.1 ECG Recognition
The ECG findings are identical to hypokalemia. The recognition task is: (1) identify the hypokalemia-pattern ECG (U waves, T flattening, QU prolongation) and (2) always consider hypomagnesemia as a coexisting or primary cause.

### 6.2 The Lab Requirement
Unlike most ECG diagnoses, hypomagnesemia CANNOT be diagnosed by ECG alone. The ECG cannot distinguish it from hypokalemia. The algorithmic rule: any patient with hypokalemia-pattern ECG should have BOTH K+ and Mg2+ checked.

### 6.3 Treatment Priority
The critical clinical insight: IV MgSO4 is given FIRST in TdP, regardless of measured Mg2+ level. This is because:
1. Mg2+ may be depleted intracellularly even with normal serum levels
2. IV Mg2+ pharmacologically suppresses triggered activity (IKr block) even in normomagnesemia
3. TdP management does not wait for lab results

### 6.4 Refractory Hypokalemia Diagnosis
If a patient's hypokalemia does not respond to K+ repletion, hypomagnesemia is the diagnosis until proven otherwise. This is because Mg2+ is required for the Na/K-ATPase to retain K+ intracellularly. Checking Mg2+ in refractory hypokalemia is a clinical mandate.

### 6.5 Prophylactic Use
IV MgSO4 is given prophylactically in:
- Post-cardiac surgery (reduces AF incidence)
- Pre-eclampsia (seizure prophylaxis — different mechanism)
- TdP prophylaxis in drug-induced QT prolongation
This broad use makes Mg2+ the most therapeutically versatile of all electrolytes in cardiology.

### Primary Agent
**MR** — The ECG manifestations of hypomagnesemia (QTc prolongation, U-waves, T-wave flattening) are repolarization changes within MR's domain, mirroring the hypokalemia pattern.

### Cross-Domain Hints
- MR → CDS: `cross_domain_hint: "QTc prolongation with prominent U-waves — pattern consistent with hypomagnesemia or combined hypokalemia/hypomagnesemia; CDS should flag elevated TdP risk and combined electrolyte abnormality"`
- RRC → CDS: `cross_domain_hint: "QTc prolonged with arrhythmia burden (PVCs/VT); if MR confirms U-waves, combined Mg2+/K+ deficiency pattern — CDS should escalate TdP risk rating"`

### CDS Specific Role
CDS integrates hypomagnesemia findings with particular attention to the high rate of co-occurrence with hypokalemia. When MR identifies U-waves and T-wave flattening and RRC measures prolonged QTc with arrhythmia burden, CDS applies the combined electrolyte risk rule: isolated hypomagnesemia ECG pattern is indistinguishable from hypokalemia, but the combination dramatically increases TdP risk beyond what either electrolyte abnormality would produce alone. CDS flags this combined risk explicitly in the final output and recommends urgent electrolyte correction when QTc exceeds 500ms or spontaneous VT/TdP runs are detected.

## 9. Edge Cases and Pitfalls

1. **TdP with normal Mg2+ level:** Do not withhold IV MgSO4 for TdP because serum Mg2+ is normal. Mg2+ acts pharmacologically on IKr regardless of baseline level. Intracellular Mg2+ may be depleted even with normal serum levels.

2. **Refractory hypokalemia:** If K+ does not correct despite aggressive repletion, hypomagnesemia is the cause. Mg2+ must be replete before K+ can be retained.

3. **PPI-induced hypomagnesemia:** Long-term omeprazole/pantoprazole use causes TRPM6 channel downregulation in the gut, reducing Mg2+ absorption. This is increasingly recognized and may be severe (Mg2+ <0.5 mg/dL). The ECG will mimic severe hypokalemia.

4. **Refeeding syndrome:** Malnourished patients given IV dextrose or enteral nutrition develop sudden intracellular shift of K+, Mg2+, and phosphate → acute severe deficiency of all three. ECG monitoring during refeeding initiation is indicated.

5. **Alcoholism triad:** Alcoholic patients in withdrawal or with poor intake commonly have combined hypomagnesemia + hypokalemia + hypocalcemia. All three must be corrected; the ECG alone cannot quantify which deficiency predominates.

6. **Gitelman and Bartter syndromes:** Genetic tubulopathies causing renal K+ and Mg2+ wasting. Patients present in childhood or young adulthood with persistent hypokalemia and hypomagnesemia. ECG shows chronic hypokalemia pattern. May have TdP risk during illness-related exacerbations.

7. **Cardiac surgery:**  Cardiopulmonary bypass causes Mg2+ depletion via hemodilution, hypothermic cardioplegia, and diuresis. Post-bypass Mg2+ <2 mg/dL significantly increases AF risk. Prophylactic MgSO4 is standard in many protocols.
