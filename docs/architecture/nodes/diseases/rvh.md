## 1. Pathophysiology Root Cause

### 1.1 What Goes Wrong (Anatomy/Physiology)
- The right ventricle is a thin-walled, low-pressure chamber that normally pumps against pulmonary vascular resistance of ~1/6 that of the systemic circulation; the LV is ~3× more massive and electrically dominates the QRS
- RVH occurs when chronic pressure overload (pulmonary hypertension, pulmonary stenosis) or volume overload (large ASD, Ebstein anomaly) forces the RV to hypertrophy until its mass is sufficient to alter the sum cardiac electrical vector
- Common causes: pulmonary arterial hypertension (most common in adults), COPD-related cor pulmonale, pulmonary stenosis, tetralogy of Fallot (post-repair), large atrial septal defect, mitral stenosis causing pulmonary hypertension, thromboembolic chronic pulmonary hypertension
- At the cellular level: RV myocytes undergo sarcomere addition in series (concentric hypertrophy for pressure overload), increasing RV wall thickness from a normal ~3 mm to ≥7 mm; this increases RV electrical mass and prolongs RV depolarization

### 1.2 Electrical Consequence
- **Normal balance**: The LV normally dominates the QRS because its mass exceeds the RV by 3:1; the dominant QRS vector points leftward, posteriorly, and inferiorly — producing S-waves in V1 and tall R-waves in V5-V6
- **RVH shifts the balance**: When RV mass increases sufficiently, it produces a rightward, anterior depolarization force that can overcome or cancel the LV's leftward, posterior dominance
- **Depolarization shift**: The mean QRS axis shifts rightward (≥+90°, often ≥+110°); anterior forces increase (taller R in V1) while posterior forces are reduced (smaller R in V5-V6 or deeper S)
- **Repolarization abnormality**: Analogous to the LVH "strain" pattern on the left, RVH produces an RV strain pattern — ST depression and asymmetric T-wave inversion in the right precordial leads (V1-V3) due to subendocardial ischemia of the hypertrophied RV
- **Right atrial pressure**: RVH increases RV end-diastolic pressure, retrograde loading of the RA, and commonly coexists with right atrial enlargement (RAE/P-pulmonale)

### 1.3 Why It Appears on ECG
- V1 and V2 face the anterior RV directly; when RV mass increases, the anterior rightward depolarization vector projects positively onto V1 → dominant R-wave in V1
- Right axis deviation reflects the net QRS vector rotating clockwise (rightward) toward the RV
- Lead I sees the vector moving away (rightward) → small R or S-dominant pattern
- S1S2S3 pattern: deep S-waves in leads I, II, and III indicate a net rightward force with superior displacement; seen in right heart strain, acute PE, and severe COPD
- V5-V6 face the lateral LV and now receive the vector moving away from them (toward the enlarged RV) → deep S-waves
- RV strain (ST depression + T inversion V1-V3) reflects reversed repolarization gradient in the thickened RV wall, identical mechanism to LV strain but in the right-sided leads

## 2. ECG Presentation — Lead by Lead

### 2.1 Diagnostic Criteria (2025 AHA/ESC Guidelines)

| Criterion | Threshold | Source |
|-----------|-----------|--------|
| **R/S ratio in V1** | R/S ≥ 1 (dominant R in V1) | AHA/ACC/HRS 2009; 2022 ESC Pulmonary Hypertension Guidelines |
| **R-wave amplitude in V1** | R(V1) ≥ 7 mm | Chou's; Goldberger |
| **Right axis deviation** | QRS axis ≥ +90° (adults); ≥ +110° more specific | AHA/ACC/HRS 2009 |
| **S-wave in V5 or V6** | S(V5) or S(V6) ≥ 7 mm | AHA/ACC/HRS 2009 |
| **rSR' in V1 with R' ≥ 10 mm** | R' > R — suggests significant RV outflow hypertrophy | Chou's 7th ed |
| **R in V1 + S in V5/V6** | Sum ≥ 10.5 mm | McGinn-White / Marriott criterion |
| **RV strain pattern** | ST depression + asymmetric T-wave inversion V1-V3 | 2022 ESC PH Guidelines |
| **S1S2S3 pattern** | Deep S in leads I, II, and III (≥1.5 mm each) | Acute right heart strain; McGinn-White for PE |
| **P-pulmonale** | P amplitude ≥ 2.5 mm in lead II | Coexistent RAE in >50% of RVH cases |
| **qR pattern in V1** | Q-wave followed by tall R — severe RVH | Severe pulmonary hypertension |

**Note**: ECG sensitivity for RVH is only 25–40% — a normal ECG does NOT exclude even moderate RVH. Specificity is high (~95%) when multiple criteria are met. Echocardiography is the gold standard.

### 2.2 Lead-by-Lead Manifestation

| Lead | Expected Finding | Why (Vector Explanation) | Sensitivity |
|------|-----------------|------------------------|-------------|
| I | Small R or S-dominant (rS or RS); deep S in S1S2S3 pattern | Lead I faces leftward — the rightward shifted QRS axis projects negatively onto Lead I | Moderate |
| II | Normal or tall peaked P (P-pulmonale); possible deep S in S1S2S3 | Lead II faces inferiorly; rightward axis shifts the QRS partially away; RA enlargement produces peaked P | Low for QRS; moderate for P-wave |
| III | Deep S in S1S2S3 pattern; may have small R | Lead III faces inferiorly-rightward; the superior component of the RVH vector projects negatively | Low (part of S1S2S3 only) |
| aVR | May show R or qR pattern (reciprocal to RAD and rightward forces) | aVR faces the right shoulder; rightward QRS vector approaches this lead | Low |
| aVL | Small QRS, deep S, or QS pattern | aVL faces the high left — opposite to rightward RVH vector | Moderate (reciprocal to RAD) |
| aVF | Normal or slightly tall R; may have deep S with marked RAD | aVF faces inferiorly — RVH vector has inferior component in pulmonary hypertension | Low |
| V1 | Dominant R-wave (R/S ≥ 1); tall R ≥ 7 mm; qR in severe RVH; ST depression + T inversion (RV strain) | V1 faces the anterior RV directly — hypertrophied RV projects strongly positive toward V1 | High — most specific single finding |
| V2 | Decreasing R, increasing S; RV strain pattern extending into V2 | V2 is slightly lateral to V1; still anterior but transition begins | Moderate |
| V3 | Transitional; may show rS or RS; end of strain pattern | V3 is the transition zone; RV-LV vector competition | Low-Moderate |
| V4 | Decreasing R relative to normal; deeper-than-expected S | V4 begins facing the LV; reduced LV dominance in severe RVH | Low |
| V5 | Persistent deep S-wave (≥7 mm) instead of tall R | V5 normally shows dominant R; in RVH the net vector moves away → deep S | Moderate-High |
| V6 | Deep S-wave (≥7 mm); R/S ratio <1 | V6 directly faces lateral LV wall; rightward vector shifts amplitude away from V6 | Moderate-High |

### 2.3 Key Leads (Most Diagnostic)
- **V1**: Single most specific lead — dominant R-wave (R/S ≥ 1 or R ≥ 7 mm) combined with RV strain is highly specific
- **V5 and V6**: Deep S-waves ≥ 7 mm are important supporting criteria and reflect the rightward shift of forces away from the lateral leads
- **Lead I and aVF**: Axis determination — small R or deep S in I with upright R in aVF confirms right axis deviation
- **Lead II**: Best lead for P-wave amplitude — peaked P ≥ 2.5 mm = P-pulmonale (coexistent RAE)
- **aVL**: Deep S or QS pattern supports right axis deviation

### 2.4 Beat-by-Beat Considerations
- RVH is a CHRONIC structural finding — ECG changes are constant and present on every beat
- The only exception is ACUTE right heart strain (massive PE, acute pulmonary hypertension): these changes may appear suddenly and evolve over hours — S1Q3T3 pattern, new RBBB, new sinus tachycardia; these are DYNAMIC, not static like chronic RVH
- In COPD, respiratory variation in QRS voltage and axis may occur due to hyperinflation changes with the respiratory cycle, but the overall RVH pattern remains stable
- Beat-to-beat axis variation is NOT expected with stable RVH; if present, consider electrical alternans (pericardial effusion)
- Aggregate voltage measurements across multiple beats are preferred for reliable amplitude assessment

## 3. Morphology Details (What the Agent Must See)

### 3.1 P-wave Changes
- **Morphology**: Tall, peaked, narrow P-wave in lead II (P-pulmonale) when coexistent RAE is present (very common — >50% of RVH cases); P-wave amplitude ≥ 2.5 mm in II
- **Duration change**: Normal or slightly shortened P-wave duration (RA dominates P onset and is enlarged, but the peak is earlier than in LAE)
- **Axis change**: P-wave axis may shift rightward slightly with marked RAE; usually remains within 0° to +75°

### 3.2 PR Interval Changes
- **Duration**: Usually normal; may be slightly prolonged if tricuspid valve disease or RV conduction disease is the cause of RVH
- **Pattern**: Constant; no AV conduction disturbance is intrinsic to RVH itself

### 3.3 QRS Complex Changes
- **Duration**: Narrow to borderline (80-110 ms); if ≥120 ms, may indicate coexistent complete RBBB (common with severe RV pressure overload or RV outflow disease)
- **Morphology**: Dominant R in V1 (rR', RS, R, or qR morphology depending on severity); rSR' with tall R' (≥10 mm) in V1 suggests RV outflow tract hypertrophy; deep persistent S-waves in V5-V6; possible QS in lateral leads with severe disease
- **Amplitude**: Increased in V1 (R-wave); decreased in V5-V6 relative to normal (S-waves deepen, R-waves shorten)
- **Axis**: RIGHT axis deviation ≥ +90° (adults); degrees of +100° to +180° indicate severe RVH or right heart strain; extreme right axis (+180° to -90°) in adults is rarely from RVH alone

### 3.4 ST Segment Changes
- **Direction**: ST depression in V1-V3 (RV strain pattern); ST elevation possible in severe strain (rare, acute RV injury pattern)
- **Morphology**: Downsloping ST depression in V1-V3 in the strain pattern; analogous morphology to LVH strain but mirrored to right-sided leads
- **Measurement point**: J-point + 60-80 ms; persistent downsloping throughout
- **Gender/lead-specific thresholds**: ST depression in V1-V3 is never normal; any persistent depression with T-wave inversion in V1-V3 in the setting of RVH criteria = RV strain

### 3.5 T-wave Changes
- **Direction**: Inverted in V1-V3 with RV strain; may extend to V4 in severe RVH or severe pulmonary hypertension; upright T-waves in V1-V3 in the presence of dominant R = abnormal repolarization direction regardless of strain
- **Amplitude**: Moderate depth of inversion (2-5 mm typically) in RV strain; deep (>5 mm) inversions in V1-V4 raise concern for concomitant ARVC or pulmonary embolism
- **Symmetry**: Asymmetric T-wave inversion in chronic RVH strain; symmetric deep inversions raise concern for acute RV ischemia or acute PE
- **Specific patterns**: T-wave inversion V1-V4 with associated S1Q3T3 in the acute setting — acute right heart strain, PE until proven otherwise

### 3.6 QT/QTc Changes
- **QTc**: May be mildly prolonged (430-470 ms) in severe RVH due to prolonged RV repolarization
- **Clinical significance**: QTc prolongation in RVH is not as well characterized as in LVH; however, RV strain and associated autonomic changes increase arrhythmia risk
- **Torsades de Pointes risk**: Low from RVH alone; increased if QT-prolonging drugs are added

### 3.7 Other Features
- **S1Q3T3 pattern** (McGinn-White): Deep S in lead I, Q-wave in lead III, T-wave inversion in lead III — historically associated with PE but is a non-specific right heart strain marker; may be present in RVH or acute right heart strain
- **Low voltage limb leads with COPD**: Air trapping in COPD reduces QRS amplitude, potentially masking RVH voltage criteria; rightward axis shift with low voltage = COPD pattern
- **Poor R-wave progression in precordials**: COPD-related RVH may produce loss of anterior R-wave forces, mimicking anterior MI (due to lung hyperinflation and downward displacement of the heart)
- **Clockwise rotation**: Persistent rS or QS pattern from V1 through V6 (no transition to dominant R) indicates clockwise rotation, common with COPD and RVH

## 4. Differential Diagnosis

### 4.1 Mimics (What Looks Like This But Isn't)

| Mimic Condition | Shared Features | Distinguishing Features |
|----------------|-----------------|----------------------|
| Posterior STEMI | Dominant R in V1, ST changes V1-V2 | Posterior STEMI has horizontal ST DEPRESSION in V1-V2 with tall, upright T-waves (mirror of posterior ST elevation); R/S ≥ 1 in V1 with UPRIGHT, tall T-wave in V1 = posterior MI until proven otherwise; RVH has ST depression + T INVERSION |
| RBBB | Dominant R' in V1, right axis, wide QRS | RBBB: QRS ≥ 120 ms with classic rSR' in V1 and broad S in I, V5, V6; terminal S-wave in I with RBBB is broad and slurred; RVH: narrow QRS (usually) with dominant R ≥ 7 mm, no rSR' (or rSR' with R' ≥ 10 mm indicates RVH beyond simple RBBB) |
| WPW type A | Dominant R in V1, short PR, delta wave | WPW: short PR <120 ms, delta wave (slurred QRS onset), wide QRS; accessory pathway in left posterior wall produces dominant R in V1 mimicking RVH; ECG pattern is dynamic |
| Normal variant (young adults) | Incomplete rSR' in V1 | Normal rSR' in V1 has R' <5 mm; no right axis deviation; no S in V5-V6 ≥ 7 mm; no P-pulmonale |
| Dextrocardia | Dominant R in V1 if leads reversed | Dextrocardia: all leads appear "mirror image"; R-wave progression reverses (decreasing from V1 to V6); P-wave is inverted in lead I |
| Acute pulmonary embolism | S1Q3T3, right axis, T inversions V1-V3, sinus tachycardia | PE is acute and dynamic; new RBBB pattern; new sinus tachycardia; no prior history; RVH changes are chronic and stable |

### 4.2 Coexisting Conditions
- **RVH + RAE**: Extremely common coexistence (P-pulmonale + RVH criteria) — the two diagnoses share causation (pulmonary hypertension); when both are present, the specificity for pulmonary hypertension increases substantially
- **RVH + RBBB**: RV pressure overload commonly causes RBBB (right bundle is vulnerable to stretch from elevated RV pressure); when RBBB is present, RVH criteria must be modified — R' ≥ 15 mm in V1 (not the usual 7 mm) is needed to suggest RVH on top of RBBB
- **RVH + LVH (biventricular hypertrophy)**: Opposing LV and RV vectors cancel — ECG may appear deceptively normal or show only mild changes; Katz-Wachtel sign (large equiphasic RS complexes in V3-V4); seen with biventricular failure, large VSD
- **RVH + atrial fibrillation**: AF is common in advanced pulmonary hypertension and RVH; rate-controlled AF does not change RVH voltage criteria but may complicate P-wave assessment for RAE
- **COPD masking RVH**: Low limb lead voltage due to air trapping may simultaneously reduce apparent R amplitude in V1 and make standard voltage criteria unreliable

## 5. STAT Classification

| Priority | Criteria |
|----------|----------|
| **STAT** | RVH with NEW ST-T changes beyond baseline, new right axis deviation, or new S1Q3T3 in the setting of dyspnea/hypoxia/hemodynamic compromise — must exclude acute massive PE immediately |
| **Time-sensitive** | Chronic RVH is not acutely time-sensitive; however, identifying new or worsening RVH pattern in a known pulmonary hypertension patient indicates disease progression and warrants urgent specialist review |
| **Clinical action** | New RVH pattern in undifferentiated dyspnea → CT pulmonary angiogram to exclude PE; echocardiography for RV systolic pressure estimation; evaluation for pulmonary hypertension causes; in known chronic RVH, medication review and specialist follow-up |

### 6.1 Reasoning Domains Required to Detect This Condition
- **Voltage measurement**: R-wave amplitude in V1, R-wave and S-wave amplitudes in V5-V6 — requires precise per-lead amplitude extraction
- **Axis calculation**: Right axis deviation (≥+90°) is a primary criterion — requires computing mean frontal QRS axis from limb leads
- **Ratio computation**: R/S ratio in V1 is a key criterion — requires both R and S amplitude in the same lead
- **Morphology analysis**: RV strain pattern recognition (asymmetric ST depression + T inversion V1-V3); differentiation from posterior STEMI pattern (upright T-wave vs inverted) — critical safety-relevant distinction
- **Multi-lead pattern recognition**: S1S2S3 pattern requires simultaneous assessment of I, II, III
- **P-wave analysis**: P-pulmonale detection (amplitude ≥ 2.5 mm lead II) — coexistent RAE assessment
- **Cross-domain reasoning**: Must integrate axis + voltage + morphology + P-wave; must distinguish chronic RVH pattern from acute right heart strain; must flag posterior STEMI mimicry

### 6.2 Feature Dependencies
- **ESSENTIAL features**: R/S ratio in V1 or R amplitude in V1; mean QRS frontal axis; S-wave amplitude in V5 and V6
- **SUPPORTING features**: P-wave amplitude in lead II (P-pulmonale); ST-T morphology V1-V3 (strain); S-wave depths in I, II, III (S1S2S3 pattern); QRS duration (to detect coexistent RBBB)
- **EXCLUSION features**: Dominant R in V1 with UPRIGHT tall T-wave → posterior STEMI must be excluded first; QRS ≥ 120 ms with rSR' → RBBB may be the primary finding
- **Per-beat vs aggregate**: Aggregate voltage measurements across 3-5 beats; axis computed from aggregate; P-wave amplitude from stable beats

### 6.3 Cross-Condition Interactions
- **RVH vs Posterior STEMI**: The most dangerous interaction — both produce dominant R in V1; posterior STEMI is a STEMI equivalent requiring emergent reperfusion; the T-wave direction is the critical differentiator (upright/tall = posterior MI; inverted = RVH strain)
- **RBBB modifies RVH criteria**: RBBB independently produces rSR' and rightward terminal forces in V1; standard RVH amplitude criteria become unreliable without RBBB-adjusted thresholds
- **Acute PE mimics chronic RVH**: New S1Q3T3, new sinus tachycardia, new T-wave inversions V1-V3 = acute right heart strain; indistinguishable from severe chronic RVH without clinical context and ECG history
- **WPW type A**: Accessory pathway with left posterior insertion produces dominant R in V1 with short PR and delta wave — must be excluded before labeling R/S ≥ 1 in V1 as RVH

### 6.4 Reasoning Chain Sketch
**Minimum reasoning chain:**
1. Measure R/S ratio in V1
2. If R/S ≥ 1 (or R ≥ 7 mm), flag possible RVH
3. Check QRS frontal axis — if ≥ +90°, confidence increases substantially
4. Measure S(V5) and S(V6) — if ≥ 7 mm, further confirmation

**Full reasoning chain:**
1. Check QRS duration → if ≥ 120 ms with rSR' in V1, label RBBB and apply modified RVH thresholds
2. Check PR interval and QRS onset for delta wave → exclude WPW
3. Check T-wave direction in V1-V2 → if tall upright T with dominant R, prioritize posterior STEMI over RVH
4. Measure R amplitude in V1 and R/S ratio in V1
5. Calculate mean frontal QRS axis — confirm RAD ≥ +90°
6. Measure S amplitude in V5 and V6 (≥ 7 mm criterion)
7. Assess for S1S2S3 pattern (deep S in I, II, III ≥ 1.5 mm each)
8. Check ST-T in V1-V3 for strain morphology (downsloping ST depression + T inversion, asymmetric)
9. Measure P-wave amplitude in lead II (P-pulmonale criterion ≥ 2.5 mm)
10. Count how many criteria are met → classify as definite/probable/possible RVH
11. Flag clinical urgency: is the pattern new vs chronic? Is the patient hypoxic/tachycardic?

### 6.5 Confidence Anchors
- **HIGH confidence**: R ≥ 7 mm in V1 (or R/S ≥ 1) + right axis ≥ +90° + S ≥ 7 mm in V5/V6 + P-pulmonale = definite RVH
- **MODERATE confidence**: Two of the above criteria met without all three
- **LOW confidence**: Isolated R/S ≥ 1 in V1 without axis deviation in a young patient — could be normal variant or WPW
- **Pathognomonic combination**: qR pattern in V1 + right axis ≥ +110° + RV strain + P-pulmonale = severe pulmonary hypertension with high certainty
- **Features that LOWER confidence**: Low limb lead voltage (COPD masking); RBBB pattern (invalidates standard thresholds); WPW features; posterior lead changes suggesting posterior MI

| Agent | Role for Right Ventricular Hypertrophy | Key Features Used |
|-------|--------------------------|-------------------|
| **RRC** (Rhythm/Rate/Conduction) | Supporting | Right axis deviation >+90° (required supportive criterion); QRS duration (usually <120ms in isolated RVH; ≥120ms suggests concurrent RBBB) |
| **IT** (Ischemia/Territory) | Supporting — must NOT independently classify right precordial T-wave inversions as anterior ischemia | T-wave inversions in V1-V4 detected as a finding; IT must emit cross_domain_hint to CDS before classifying as anterior ischemia, since RVH strain causes identical T-wave inversion pattern |
| **MR** (Morphology/Repolarization) | Primary | R wave ≥7mm in V1; R/S ratio >1 in V1; dominant R wave in V1 (tall monophasic R or qR pattern); S wave persistence in V5-V6 (S >7mm in V6); R/S ratio <1 in V5-V6 (reversal of normal precordial R/S progression); right precordial T-wave inversions V1-V4 (RVH strain pattern); P pulmonale if RAE coexists |
| **CDS** (Cross-Domain Synthesis) | Required — resolves RVH T-wave inversions vs anterior ischemia | Integrates MR RVH voltage criteria (dominant R in V1, R/S >1 in V1) with RRC right axis deviation to confirm RVH; if T-wave inversions in V1-V4 are present and MR confirms RVH morphology, CDS suppresses IT anterior ischemia flag; if T-wave inversions extend beyond V4 or are asymmetric in a non-strain pattern, CDS retains ischemia consideration |

### Primary Agent
**MR** — RVH diagnosis is driven by voltage criteria: dominant R wave in V1 ≥7mm, R/S >1 in V1, S persistence in V5-V6; these are all morphology amplitude measurements in the MR domain.

### Cross-Domain Hints
- MR emits: `cross_domain_hint: "Dominant R wave in V1 (R ≥7mm or R/S >1) with right precordial T-wave inversions V1-V4 detected — RRC must confirm right axis deviation; IT must not classify V1-V4 T-wave inversions as anterior ischemia without CDS adjudication"`
- IT emits: `cross_domain_hint: "T-wave inversions in V1-V4 detected — CDS must determine if this represents RVH right precordial strain (MR dominant R in V1 + RRC right axis deviation) or true anterior ischemia"`

### CDS Specific Role
CDS resolves the most clinically important ambiguity in RVH: right precordial T-wave inversions in V1-V4 are indistinguishable from anterior ischemia (Wellens-type patterns or anterior NSTEMI) on morphology alone. CDS integrates MR dominant R in V1 (R ≥7mm, R/S >1) with RRC right axis deviation >+90°: if both are confirmed, T-wave inversions V1-V4 are classified as RVH strain and the IT anterior ischemia flag is suppressed. If T-wave inversions extend beyond V4 (into V5-V6) or the QRS axis is normal (no RAD), CDS retains the ischemia flag. CDS also differentiates RVH from posterior STEMI (which also shows dominant R in V1 but with upright T-waves and ST depression in V1-V3, not T-wave inversion) using IT posterior territory findings.

## 9. Edge Cases and Pitfalls

- **Posterior STEMI masquerading as RVH**: The most dangerous pitfall. Both produce a dominant R-wave in V1. The T-wave direction is the critical differentiator: posterior STEMI produces TALL UPRIGHT T-waves in V1-V2 (mirror of ST elevation posteriorly), while RVH strain produces T-wave INVERSION in V1-V3. A dominant R in V1 with upright T-waves in the context of acute chest pain is a posterior STEMI until proven otherwise. The system must ALWAYS check T-wave direction in V1 before labeling R/S ≥ 1 as RVH in an undifferentiated presentation.
- **COPD masking RVH voltage**: Low voltage from hyperinflation and increased air-electrode distance reduces QRS amplitudes throughout, potentially masking RVH voltage criteria. A patient with severe COPD may have true RVH by echo but normal R amplitude in V1. The axis change (rightward) and P-pulmonale may be more reliable signs in this context.
- **WPW type A**: Left posterior accessory pathway produces dominant R in V1 with short PR interval and delta wave. The system must check PR interval and QRS onset morphology (slurred delta wave) before interpreting R/S ≥ 1 as RVH.
- **Normal rSR' in V1**: An rSR' with R' < 5 mm in V1 is a normal variant (incomplete RBBB-like pattern) seen in up to 2-3% of healthy adults. Only rSR' with R' ≥ 10 mm qualifies as an RVH criterion.
- **Young athletes**: Right axis deviation is more common in young individuals and athletes as a normal variant (axis up to +110° may be normal in children). Context is critical — P-pulmonale, prominent S in V5-V6, and RV strain are NOT expected in a healthy athlete.
- **Dextrocardia**: Situs inversus produces apparent right axis deviation and dominant R in V1 from the mirror-image cardiac position. Check: is there decreasing R-wave from V1 to V6 (instead of the normal increasing pattern)? Is lead I inverted? If yes, suspect dextrocardia before diagnosing RVH.
- **Biventricular hypertrophy**: In patients with both RVH and LVH (e.g., systemic hypertension + severe COPD, or congenital heart disease), the opposing vectors may cancel, producing an ECG that appears normal or shows only mild changes. Katz-Wachtel sign (large biphasic RS in V2-V4 ≥ 50 mm) is a clue. Echo is essential.
- **RVH criteria in RBBB**: Standard RVH voltage thresholds for V1 do not apply in RBBB, since RBBB itself causes R' dominance. R' ≥ 15 mm in V1 is needed to suggest RVH in the setting of RBBB; right axis deviation persists as a valid criterion.
