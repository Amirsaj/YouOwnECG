## 1. Pathophysiology Root Cause

### 1.1 What Goes Wrong

A PVC arises when an ectopic focus in the ventricular myocardium (below the bundle of His) depolarizes spontaneously before the next expected sinus impulse reaches the ventricles. Three primary mechanisms:

- **Enhanced automaticity**: Ectopic ventricular cell reaches threshold spontaneously — promoted by catecholamines, ischemia, hypokalaemia, hypomagnesaemia, acidosis, hypoxia, mechanical stretch, or structural disease
- **Triggered activity**: Early afterdepolarizations (EADs) during long QT states; delayed afterdepolarizations (DADs) in digoxin toxicity, heart failure with elevated intracellular calcium
- **Reentry**: Micro-reentrant circuits around scar tissue (post-MI fibrosis, cardiomyopathy), conduction velocity heterogeneity

Common structural associations: acute myocardial ischemia/infarction, cardiomyopathy, valvular disease, electrolyte abnormalities, cardiac surgery. Idiopathic PVCs (structurally normal heart) also occur — typically from right ventricular outflow tract (RVOT) or left fascicles.

### 1.2 Electrical Consequence

The ectopic ventricular focus depolarizes the ventricles without using the normal His-Purkinje network. Activation spreads cell-to-cell through working myocardium — a slower, disorganized pathway compared to the specialized conduction system. The result:

- Abnormally wide QRS (>120 ms, often >160 ms)
- Bizarre, notched QRS morphology
- No preceding P-wave from sinus node (the PVC fires independently)
- Discordant T-wave: repolarization must proceed in the opposite direction from abnormal depolarization
- Retrograde conduction to atria and sinus node is usually blocked or ineffective — sinus node is not reset

### 1.3 Why It Appears on ECG

- **Wide bizarre QRS**: spread through non-His-Purkinje path → slow, heterogeneous depolarization → broad, distorted QRS
- **Discordant T-wave**: the terminal portion of ventricular depolarization and repolarization are reversed relative to normal — T-wave points opposite to QRS main deflection (unlike normal concordance in LBBB/RBBB where T-wave direction follows specific rules)
- **No preceding P-wave**: sinus P is absent before PVC (occasionally a sinus P occurs nearby but is unrelated — AV dissociation at that beat)
- **Full compensatory pause**: sinus node is not discharged; the sinus cycle continues undisturbed; the post-PVC beat arrives at the expected time, making the pause = 2 × sinus RR interval

## 2. ECG Presentation — Lead by Lead

### 2.1 Diagnostic Criteria (2025 AHA/ESC Guidelines)

1. Premature complex occurring before the next expected QRS
2. QRS duration >120 ms (typically 140–200 ms)
3. Wide, bizarre, notched QRS morphology
4. No preceding P-wave related to the PVC (sinus P may appear before/within/after but dissociated)
5. Discordant T-wave: T-wave polarity opposite to QRS main deflection
6. Full compensatory pause: (pre-PVC RR) + (post-PVC RR) = 2 × sinus RR
7. Morphology consistent with ventricular origin: LBBB-like pattern = RV origin; RBBB-like pattern = LV origin

### 2.2 Lead-by-Lead Manifestation

| Lead | Finding |
|------|---------|
| I | Wide QRS; morphology depends on PVC origin; concordant or discordant depending on PVC vector |
| II | Wide QRS; T-wave discordant; full compensatory pause visible on rhythm strip |
| III | Wide QRS; morphology variable |
| aVR | Wide QRS; polarity often reversed relative to sinus QRS |
| aVL | Wide QRS; morphology variable with PVC origin |
| aVF | Wide QRS; key for inferior vs superior axis of PVC (helps localize outflow tract vs other origin) |
| V1 | KEY: RBBB-like (broad R in V1) = LV origin; LBBB-like (QS or rS in V1) = RV origin |
| V2 | Wide QRS; transition zone morphology |
| V3 | Wide QRS; transition zone |
| V4 | Wide QRS |
| V5 | Wide QRS; discordant T-wave typically prominent in lateral precordials |
| V6 | Wide QRS; discordant T-wave |

### 2.3 Key Leads (Most Diagnostic)

- **V1**: Most important for morphologic classification (RBBB-like = LV origin; LBBB-like = RV origin)
- **aVF**: Superior vs inferior PVC axis (RVOT PVCs have positive aVF — downward axis; outflow tract origin)
- **Lead II (long rhythm strip)**: Full compensatory pause measurement, pattern recognition (bigeminy, couplets)
- **V1 + I (or V6)**: Combination required to confirm RBBB vs LBBB morphology

### 2.4 Beat-by-Beat Considerations

- PVC bigeminy: alternating sinus–PVC pattern (every other beat is PVC)
- PVC trigeminy: every third beat is PVC
- Couplets: two consecutive PVCs — increased risk significance
- Runs (≥3 consecutive PVCs): non-sustained VT (NSVT) if < 30 seconds; sustained VT if ≥ 30 seconds
- Fusion beats: sinus impulse and PVC simultaneously activate ventricles → intermediate QRS morphology (narrower than pure PVC, different from pure sinus QRS) — proves ventricular origin, distinguishes from aberrant conduction
- Interpolated PVC: PVC sandwiched between two normal sinus beats with no compensatory pause — occurs when the PVC is early enough to allow the sinus impulse to conduct normally afterward; the PR interval of the post-PVC sinus beat may be prolonged (concealed retrograde conduction into AV node)

## 3. Morphology Details

### 3.1 P-wave Changes

- No P-wave precedes the PVC (sinus P is absent from the PVC complex)
- Sinus P may be visible: (a) completely separate from PVC (AV dissociation at that beat); (b) buried within the wide PVC QRS; (c) appearing after the QRS as retrograde P if retrograde VA conduction occurs (uncommon)
- The post-PVC sinus P resumes at the expected time (confirming sinus node was not reset)

### 3.2 PR Interval Changes

- No PR interval for the PVC beat (no conducted P-wave preceding the PVC)
- Post-PVC beat: PR interval normal (sinus conduction resumes undisturbed)
- If interpolated PVC: PR of the next sinus beat may be prolonged due to retrograde concealed conduction into AV node

### 3.3 QRS Complex Changes

- Wide (>120 ms, typically 140–200 ms)
- Bizarre morphology: notched, slurred, multi-peaked
- **LBBB-like PVC (RV origin)**:
  - rS or QS in V1 (predominantly negative)
  - Broad R in V5/V6
  - Common origins: RVOT (inferior axis, positive aVF), RV free wall
- **RBBB-like PVC (LV origin)**:
  - rsR' or R in V1 (predominantly positive, wide)
  - qRS or rS in V5/V6
  - Common origins: LV free wall, papillary muscles, LVOT, left fascicular PVCs
- **Concordance**: all precordial leads showing same-direction QRS deflection (all positive or all negative) — strong evidence for ventricular origin (also used in VT differentiation from SVT with aberrancy)
- **Uniform PVCs**: all PVCs on strip have identical morphology → single ectopic focus (unifocal)
- **Multiform (multifocal) PVCs**: varying PVC morphology → multiple ventricular foci or variable conduction — more concerning

### 3.4 ST Segment Changes

- ST segment of PVC is discordant with QRS (ST elevation when QRS is negative; ST depression when QRS is positive) — this is normal for PVCs; do NOT interpret as primary ischemia within the PVC complex
- ST assessment for ischemia should be performed on the sinus beats only, NOT on PVC complexes
- In acute MI, sinus beats will show ischemic ST changes; PVC complexes will additionally distort the picture

### 3.5 T-wave Changes

- T-wave is always discordant with the main QRS deflection of the PVC (opposite direction)
- This discordance is the expected, physiologic consequence of abnormal ventricular depolarization
- A PVC with concordant T-wave (T pointing same direction as main QRS) is abnormal — primary repolarization abnormality superimposed on the PVC; seen in severe ischemia or electrolyte toxicity

### 3.6 QT/QTc Changes

- QT interval of PVC complex is measured from QRS onset to T-wave end — typically long due to wide QRS and secondary repolarization changes
- QTc correction formulas are not applicable to PVC complexes (designed for normally conducted beats)
- Assess QTc on sinus beats only

### 3.7 Other Features

- **R-on-T phenomenon**: PVC falls on the T-wave of the preceding sinus beat (the vulnerable period of ventricular repolarization) → may trigger polymorphic VT or VF, especially in the setting of acute MI or long QT. Recognized as a short coupling interval between the preceding T-wave peak and the PVC onset.
- **Fusion beat**: Intermediate QRS morphology (between pure sinus and pure PVC) — diagnostic of ventricular origin. Confirms the sinus impulse and PVC were activating the ventricle simultaneously.
- **Capture beat**: During a run of VT/PVC, a normally conducted sinus beat "captures" the ventricle — narrow QRS appears within wide complex run. Confirms AV dissociation (supports VT diagnosis).

## 4. Differential Diagnosis

### 4.1 Mimics

| Condition | Key Differentiator |
|-----------|-------------------|
| Aberrantly conducted PAC (Ashman) | Preceding ectopic P-wave present; incomplete compensatory pause; RBBB pattern most common |
| Ventricular tachycardia | ≥3 consecutive wide beats; AV dissociation; fusion/capture beats; Brugada/Vereckei criteria |
| Bundle branch block + sinus beat | P-wave before every QRS; constant morphology; QRS width typically ≤160 ms |
| SVT with aberrant conduction | Regular rhythm, narrow complexes elsewhere; P-waves present; rate typically >150 bpm |
| Pre-excited beat (WPW + delta wave) | Delta wave on sinus beats; PR short; slurred QRS onset; specific morphology pattern |
| Pacemaker-stimulated beat | Pacing spike before wide QRS; patient history of pacemaker |

### 4.2 Coexisting Conditions

- **Acute MI**: PVCs in the setting of STEMI/NSTEMI carry increased risk of VF. Frequent PVCs or runs post-MI are warning arrhythmias — continuous monitoring mandatory. The underlying ischemia is the primary problem.
- **Cardiomyopathy (ischemic or dilated)**: High PVC burden (>10–15% of beats) can cause or worsen PVC-induced cardiomyopathy — clinically significant.
- **Long QT syndrome**: EAD-triggered PVCs with R-on-T → TdP. PVCs in this context are a warning sign.
- **Hypokalemia/hypomagnesemia**: Common treatable cause of PVCs — electrolytes are a mandatory part of evaluation.
- **Digitalis toxicity**: PVC bigeminy is a classic toxic manifestation.

## 5. STAT Classification

**STAT level depends on clinical context:**

**NOT STAT — isolated, infrequent PVCs in structurally normal heart:**
- Unifocal, rare PVCs in asymptomatic patient without cardiac history
- Document, assess electrolytes and medications

**STAT-2 (Urgent) when:**
- Frequent PVCs (>6/min), bigeminy, or trigeminy — continuous monitoring, electrolyte assessment
- PVC couplets — increased VT risk
- Non-sustained VT (≥3 consecutive PVCs): urgent cardiology evaluation
- High PVC burden (>10% of beats) in known cardiomyopathy

**STAT-1 (Immediate) when:**
- PVCs in the setting of acute STEMI/NSTEMI — continuous monitoring, antiarrhythmic therapy if VT/VF occurs
- R-on-T phenomenon: high-risk warning; continuous monitoring, treat underlying cause (ischemia, long QT, electrolytes)
- PVC degenerating into sustained VT or VF: immediate cardioversion/defibrillation
- Polymorphic VT following PVC: defibrillation + underlying cause treatment

## 6. Reasoning Complexity Analysis

### 6.1 Reasoning Domains Required

1. QRS width measurement and classification
2. P-wave dissociation analysis (is there a related P before the wide QRS?)
3. T-wave discordance assessment
4. Compensatory pause calculation (full vs incomplete)
5. Morphologic classification (LBBB-like vs RBBB-like, axis)
6. Pattern recognition (unifocal vs multifocal, bigeminy, couplets, R-on-T)
7. Fusion beat and interpolated PVC recognition
8. Risk stratification (clinical context integration: ischemia, cardiomyopathy, QT)

### 6.2 Feature Dependencies

- Compensatory pause measurement requires accurate RR interval measurement across at least 3 beats surrounding the PVC
- Fusion beat recognition requires both sinus and PVC morphology templates established first
- R-on-T detection requires QT interval and T-wave peak timing from preceding sinus beats
- Multifocal determination requires morphology comparison across multiple PVC complexes

### 6.3 Cross-Condition Interactions

- Wide complex differential is the central PVC challenge: PVC vs aberrant PAC vs VT vs pre-excitation
- In acute MI: PVC + ST elevation in sinus beats = STEMI with warning arrhythmia
- PVC + long QT (sinus beats): R-on-T risk elevated → TdP pathway
- PVC bigeminy slowing effective ventricular rate: hemodynamic impact may occur if the bigeminal PVCs are poorly perfusing beats
- PVC during SVT: difficult to assess — the wide beat could be aberrant SVT or PVC

### 6.4 Reasoning Chain Sketch

```
Wide QRS (>120 ms), premature?
  └── Yes → Is there a P-wave related to this beat?
        ├── No P present → PVC (or VT if ≥3 consecutive)
        │     ├── Full compensatory pause? → confirms PVC
        │     ├── Fusion beat present? → confirms ventricular origin
        │     ├── Morphology: LBBB-like → RV origin; RBBB-like → LV origin
        │     └── R-on-T? → flag high-risk
        └── Ectopic P before wide QRS → aberrant PAC (Ashman)
              └── PR interval measurable? → PAC with aberrancy

  └── Is this isolated or run of ≥3?
        ├── Isolated or couplet → PVC
        └── ≥3 consecutive → VT protocol (Brugada/Vereckei criteria)
```

### 6.5 Confidence Anchors

- High confidence PVC: wide QRS + no preceding P + discordant T + full compensatory pause
- High confidence PVC (additional): fusion beat present, or post-pause sinus beat with normal morphology
- Uncertainty trigger: wide QRS with partially visible P-wave → PAC with aberrancy vs PVC; look at PR interval and compensatory pause
- Uncertainty trigger: multiple consecutive wide beats → do not call PVC; apply VT differentiation protocol
- High-risk flag: short coupling interval (R-on-T), STEMI context, QTc >500 ms on sinus beats

### 6.6 Difficulty Score

| Dimension | Score |
|-----------|-------|
| Signal clarity needed | 3/5 — wide QRS is usually prominent; P-wave search in noisy signal and T-wave overlap adds difficulty |
| Number of leads required | 3/5 — V1 essential for LBBB/RBBB classification; lead II for pause; multi-lead for origin localization |
| Cross-domain reasoning | 3/5 — morphology + timing + clinical context (ischemia, QT) integration required |
| Temporal pattern complexity | 3/5 — bigeminy, trigeminy, couplets, runs all require multi-beat analysis |
| Differential complexity | 3/5 — wide complex differential (aberrant PAC, VT, pre-excitation) is critical |
| Rarity in PTB-XL | 2/5 — PVCs are common and well-labeled in PTB-XL |
| **Overall difficulty** | **2.5/5** |

## 6A. Agent Assignment

**Architecture:** Option E — 3 Parallel Phase 1 Specialists + 1 Sequential CDS (Node 2.1 Gate Passed 2026-03-27)

| Agent | Role for PVC | Key Features Used |
|-------|--------------------------|-------------------|
| **RRC** (Rhythm/Rate/Conduction) | Primary | Premature wide QRS (>120 ms) without preceding P-wave, full compensatory pause (post-PVC RR = 2× sinus RR), coupling interval, beat frequency, bigeminy/trigeminy pattern |
| **IT** (Ischemia/Territory) | Supporting | ST/T wave morphology of PVC beat (discordant T-wave expected; concordant T-wave or ST elevation may indicate ischemic substrate) |
| **MR** (Morphology/Repolarization) | Supporting | PVC QRS morphology: LBBB pattern = right ventricular origin; RBBB pattern = left ventricular origin; QRS axis determines specific site of origin |
| **CDS** (Cross-Domain Synthesis) | Required — resolves PVC origin and detects R-on-T | Integrates RRC timing + MR morphology to estimate PVC origin site; detects R-on-T phenomenon when coupling interval ≤ QTc; flags ischemic substrate if IT reports concordant changes |

### Primary Agent
**RRC** — PVC diagnosis is established by premature wide QRS without preceding P-wave and full compensatory pause; all timing criteria are RRC-domain.

### Cross-Domain Hints
- RRC → MR: `cross_domain_hint: "premature_wide_QRS — classify LBBB vs RBBB morphology to determine ventricular origin"`
- RRC → IT: `cross_domain_hint: "wide_premature_beat — evaluate ST/T discordance to distinguish expected vs ischemic pattern"`
- MR → CDS: `cross_domain_hint: "PVC_morphology_classified — integrate with coupling interval for R-on-T risk and origin localization"`

### CDS Specific Role
CDS synthesizes RRC's coupling interval measurement with MR's PVC morphology classification to produce an estimated origin (RVOT, LVOT, or scar-related). When coupling interval is short relative to QTc, CDS flags R-on-T risk and increases urgency. If IT reports concordant ST changes in the PVC beat (rather than expected discordant T-waves), CDS raises ischemic substrate as a contributing mechanism.

## 9. Edge Cases and Pitfalls

1. **PVC vs aberrantly conducted PAC (Ashman)**: The most important wide complex differential in supraventricular context. Key rule: always search for a P-wave before every wide QRS. A preceding ectopic P-wave (even buried in the prior T-wave) points to PAC with aberrancy. An incomplete compensatory pause points to PAC. Full compensatory pause points to PVC.

2. **PVC vs VT**: A single PVC is isolated; ≥3 consecutive wide beats = VT. Never call isolated PVCs VT, and never dismiss 3+ consecutive wide beats as PVCs. The distinction is binary and clinically non-negotiable.

3. **PVC in LBBB**: Patient with pre-existing LBBB can have PVCs. The PVC in an LBBB patient may appear RBBB-like (LV origin) — different from baseline LBBB. Comparison with baseline ECG required.

4. **Pacemaker spikes**: Paced beats are wide and may resemble PVCs. Look for a pacing spike immediately before the wide QRS. Patient history of pacemaker should be captured.

5. **R-on-T underrecognition**: R-on-T is a short-coupling interval PVC that is easily missed if only a few beats are shown. Requires measurement of QT interval on sinus beats and coupling interval of PVC.

6. **PVC-induced cardiomyopathy**: Not visible on a single 12-lead ECG, but a high PVC burden (>10–15%) detected on Holter is a clinically actionable finding. The ECG snapshot alone cannot establish burden — this is a Holter finding.

7. **Multifocal PVCs in electrolyte abnormality**: Changing PVC morphologies suggest multiple ventricular foci — typically associated with hypokalemia, hypomagnesemia, or acute ischemia. Immediately check electrolytes and troponin.

8. **Post-extrasystolic T-wave change**: The sinus beat immediately following a PVC may show T-wave inversion or ST changes (post-extrasystolic T-wave change) — this is a normal electrophysiologic phenomenon and should NOT be interpreted as ischemia.
