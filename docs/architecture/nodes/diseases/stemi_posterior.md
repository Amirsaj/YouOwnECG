## 1. Pathophysiology Root Cause

### 1.1 What Goes Wrong (Anatomy/Physiology)
- Acute thrombotic occlusion of the posterior descending artery (PDA), which is supplied by the RCA in ~85% of patients (right-dominant circulation) or the LCx in ~15% (left-dominant circulation). In co-dominant circulation (~7%), both RCA and LCx contribute posterior branches.
- The posterior (inferobasal) wall of the left ventricle is the territory at risk. This wall lies against the vertebral column/diaphragm posteriorly, directly opposite the anterior wall. It is the most posteriorly situated myocardial surface, positioned farthest from any standard precordial electrode.
- Posterior STEMI rarely occurs in isolation — it accompanies inferior STEMI (inferoposterior MI) in 15-20% of inferior STEMIs and accompanies lateral STEMI (posterolateral MI) when the LCx is the culprit. Isolated posterior MI accounts for ~3-11% of all acute MIs and is the most commonly MISSED STEMI territory.
- At the cellular level, transmural ischemia develops in the posterior wall. ATP depletion opens K_ATP channels, shortening the action potential in ischemic cells. The intracellular K+ leak and Na+/Ca2+ accumulation creates a voltage gradient between ischemic posterior tissue and healthy anterior tissue — the injury current. This current flows from posterior (injured) toward anterior (healthy), creating a posteriorly directed injury vector.

### 1.2 Electrical Consequence
- The injury current vector points directly posteriorly (away from the sternum, toward the back). This is the fundamental problem: NO standard 12-lead electrode sits over the posterior wall.
- The standard precordial leads V1-V3 sit on the anterior chest wall, facing the opposite direction from the posterior injury vector. They record the mirror image of the posterior injury — ST depression instead of ST elevation, tall R-waves instead of Q-waves, and upright T-waves instead of T-wave inversions.
- This mirror-image phenomenon is the electrocardiographic equivalent of seeing a reflection in a mirror — everything is reversed. The ST depression in V1-V3 is the reciprocal of posterior ST elevation, just as aVL depression is the reciprocal of inferior ST elevation.
- If posterior leads V7-V9 are placed on the patient's back (V7 at the posterior axillary line, V8 at the tip of the left scapula, V9 at the left paraspinal border — all at the V6 horizontal level), they directly overlie the posterior wall and record true ST elevation >=0.5 mm.
- Over time, the posterior wall becomes electrically silent (necrosis). The anteriorly directed depolarization is no longer opposed by posterior forces, resulting in a dominant R-wave in V1 (the mirror image of a posterior Q-wave — the "window theory" in reverse).

### 1.3 Why It Appears on ECG
- The injury vector points posteriorly. Leads V1-V3 have their positive poles facing anteriorly — directly opposite to the injury vector. They therefore record the negative projection of the posterior ST elevation as ST depression.
- V1 is closest to the septum and most directly opposite the posterior wall. It shows the most pronounced reciprocal changes: ST depression, tall R-wave, and upright T-wave.
- V2-V3 are positioned slightly leftward and capture the posterolateral extension. Their changes are similar to V1 but may diminish as V3 transitions toward the anterior territory.
- The inferior leads (II, III, aVF) show ST elevation when the posteroinferior territory is involved (inferoposterior MI), which is the most common presentation. Isolated posterior MI without inferior involvement (pure posterior MI from an isolated posterolateral branch) is less common and harder to diagnose.
- The lateral leads (I, aVL, V5-V6) may show ST elevation if the posterolateral territory is involved (LCx occlusion with posterolateral branches).

## 2. ECG Presentation — Lead by Lead

### 2.1 Diagnostic Criteria (2025 AHA/ESC Guidelines)

| Criterion | Threshold | Source |
|-----------|-----------|--------|
| ST depression V1-V3 | Horizontal or downsloping ST depression >=0.5 mm in >=2 of V1-V3 (reciprocal to posterior elevation) | 2023 ESC STEMI Guidelines, reaffirmed 2025 |
| Tall R-wave in V1 | R/S ratio >1 in V1 (mirror of posterior Q-wave); R-wave >=6 mm or R-wave duration >=40 ms | 2025 AHA Universal MI Definition update |
| Posterior leads V7-V9 | ST elevation >=0.5 mm (>=1 mm in men <40 yr) in >=1 posterior lead | ESC 2023; lower threshold than standard leads due to electrode distance from injury zone |
| Upright T-waves V1-V2 | Prominent upright T-waves in V1-V2 (mirror of posterior T-wave inversion), especially if T-waves are normally inverted or isoelectric in V1 | Nikus et al., 2024 |
| Concomitant inferior ST elevation | ST elevation >=1 mm in >=2 of II, III, aVF (inferoposterior MI) | Present in 60-80% of posterior STEMI |
| Concomitant lateral ST elevation | ST elevation in V5-V6, I, aVL (posterolateral MI via LCx) | More common in left-dominant circulation |

### 2.3 Key Leads (Most Diagnostic)
- **Primary diagnostic leads**: V1-V3 — the reciprocal changes here (ST depression, tall R-wave, upright T-wave) are the cornerstone of posterior STEMI diagnosis on standard 12-lead ECG. V1 is the single most sensitive standard lead.
- **Most sensitive supplementary leads**: V7-V9 (posterior leads) — direct ST elevation >=0.5 mm. These are NOT routinely recorded but should be obtained whenever posterior MI is suspected. V7-V9 convert "suspected" posterior MI to "confirmed."
- **Culprit artery differentiation**: If concurrent inferior ST elevation (II, III, aVF) with III > II → RCA inferoposterior MI. If concurrent lateral ST elevation (I, aVL, V5-V6) → LCx posterolateral MI.
- **Most commonly missed pattern**: Isolated ST depression in V1-V3 without obvious inferior or lateral ST elevation — this represents isolated posterior STEMI and is the most frequently missed acute MI territory. The depression is often misinterpreted as "anterior ischemia" or "subendocardial ischemia."
- **Critical differentiating feature**: The ST depression in V1-V3 from posterior STEMI is horizontal or downsloping, NOT upsloping. Upsloping ST depression is more consistent with rate-related changes or demand ischemia.

### 2.4 Beat-by-Beat Considerations
- The ST depression in V1-V3 is typically constant across beats in established posterior STEMI, not intermittent.
- In the hyperacute phase, the earliest change may be a subtle increase in R-wave amplitude in V1-V2 and/or subtle T-wave changes (becoming more upright) before frank ST depression develops.
- If atrial fibrillation develops (more common with LCx occlusion affecting left atrial branches), beat-to-beat variation in ST levels may occur due to varying R-R intervals — measure ST in beats with moderate cycle lengths.
- Post-PVC beats may show exaggerated ST changes; exclude PVCs from ST measurement.
- Serial ECGs are critical: the R-wave in V1 may progressively grow taller over hours as posterior necrosis develops — tracking R-wave amplitude in V1 across serial ECGs can document evolving posterior MI.

## 3. Morphology Details (What the Agent Must See)

### 3.1 P-wave Changes
- Morphology: Usually normal unless there is concomitant atrial ischemia. If LCx occlusion involves left atrial branches, P-wave morphology may change (widened, notched P-wave in II suggesting left atrial abnormality from acute ischemia).
- Duration change: Normal in most cases. If atrial fibrillation develops (LCx territory), P-waves are replaced by fibrillatory baseline.
- Axis change: Normal unless junctional rhythm develops (uncommon in isolated posterior MI; more common in inferoposterior MI with AV node ischemia).

### 3.2 PR Interval Changes
- Duration: Usually normal in isolated posterior MI. May be prolonged in inferoposterior MI (AV node ischemia from RCA involvement) — occurs in 10-15% of inferoposterior STEMI.
- Pattern: Wenckebach (Mobitz Type I) may develop with RCA-dominant inferoposterior MI. This is less common with LCx-dominant posterolateral MI because the AV node artery usually arises from the RCA.
- PR depression: Not expected. If present, consider concurrent pericarditis or atrial infarction.

### 3.3 QRS Complex Changes
- Duration: Narrow (<120 ms) in uncomplicated posterior STEMI. Widening may occur with massive infarction involving the septum or with concurrent bundle branch disease.
- Morphology: The hallmark is a tall R-wave in V1 (mirror of posterior Q-wave). R/S ratio >1 in V1 develops as necrosis progresses. The R-wave may be >6 mm and >=40 ms in duration. In V1, the R-wave is typically broad-based (not the rSR' of RBBB). A Q-wave may develop in V6 if the posterolateral territory is involved.
- Amplitude: R-wave amplitude increases in V1-V2 as the posterior wall becomes electrically silent (loss of posterior depolarization force that normally opposes the anterior forces, unmasking a dominant anterior R-wave). Conversely, V7-V9 (if recorded) would show Q-wave development.
- Axis: Usually unchanged from baseline. If concurrent LAFB from septal ischemia occurs, left axis deviation may appear, but this is more characteristic of LAD territory.

### 3.4 ST Segment Changes
- Direction: Depression in V1-V3 (reciprocal to posterior elevation); elevation in V7-V9 if recorded. If inferoposterior, concurrent elevation in II, III, aVF. If posterolateral, concurrent elevation in I, aVL, V5-V6.
- Morphology: The ST depression in V1-V3 is typically horizontal or downsloping — NOT concave-up (which would suggest benign early repolarization). The "mirror image" concept means that if you could flip V1-V3 upside down, you would see the convex ST elevation of an acute STEMI.
- Measurement point: J-point measurement is standard. Measure ST depression at J-point relative to the TP baseline (or PR segment). The threshold for V1-V3 depression is >=0.5 mm. For V7-V9 elevation, measure at J-point with threshold >=0.5 mm (>=1 mm in men <40).
- Gender/lead-specific thresholds: The >=0.5 mm threshold for posterior leads (V7-V9) applies to both sexes (lower than the 1 mm threshold for standard leads because the posterior electrodes are farther from the heart, attenuating the signal by ~50%).

### 3.5 T-wave Changes
- Direction: In V1-V3, the T-waves become upright and prominent — this is the mirror image of posterior T-wave inversion. In patients where T-waves in V1 are normally inverted (common in women and some men), the appearance of upright T-waves in V1 is a hyperacute equivalent for posterior MI.
- Amplitude: The upright T-waves in V1-V2 may reach 5-8 mm — disproportionately tall for these leads. This "hyperacute mirror T-wave" in V1-V2 may be the earliest sign of posterior injury.
- Symmetry: The mirror T-waves in V1-V2 are typically broad-based and symmetric (reflecting the symmetric hyperacute T-waves of the posterior injury zone).
- Specific patterns: Wellens-type changes do NOT apply to posterior MI (those are LAD territory). The key T-wave pattern for posterior MI evolution is: upright/tall T in V1-V2 (hyperacute mirror) → persistent upright T with ST depression (acute) → T-wave normalization with R-wave growth in V1 (evolving) → chronic tall R in V1 with normal ST-T (completed posterior MI).

### 3.6 QT/QTc Changes
- QTc may prolong during acute ischemia (440-500 ms range) due to inhomogeneous repolarization across the ischemic border zone.
- Not specific to posterior STEMI — occurs with any acute transmural ischemia.
- Torsades de Pointes risk is present but not specifically elevated for posterior MI compared to other STEMI territories. Monitor electrolytes (K+, Mg2+) regardless.

### 3.7 Other Features
- **Tall R-wave in V1 is the signature chronic finding**: R/S ratio >1 in V1 with R-wave duration >=40 ms. This is the posterior MI equivalent of a pathologic Q-wave — it develops as the posterior wall becomes electrically silent. It persists as a permanent marker of prior posterior MI.
- **Posterior leads V7-V9**: ST elevation >=0.5 mm confirms the diagnosis. These leads should be obtained in any patient with unexplained ST depression in V1-V3 during acute chest pain. V7 is placed at the posterior axillary line, V8 at the scapular tip, V9 at the left paraspinal border — all at the level of V6.
- **Right-sided leads**: If inferior STEMI coexists, V4R should also be obtained to evaluate for concurrent RV infarction (proximal RCA occlusion).
- **Osborn waves / U-waves**: Not expected features of posterior STEMI. If present, evaluate for hypothermia or electrolyte derangements as comorbidities.

## 4. Differential Diagnosis

### 4.1 Mimics (What Looks Like This But Isn't)

| Mimic Condition | Shared Features | Distinguishing Features |
|----------------|-----------------|----------------------|
| Right ventricular hypertrophy (RVH) | Tall R-wave in V1 (R/S >1), right axis deviation | RVH has RAD, P-pulmonale (peaked P in II), RV strain pattern (T-wave inversion in V1-V3 — NOT upright), persistent across serial ECGs (chronic, not acute). No ST depression. Clinical context: COPD, pulmonary hypertension, congenital heart disease. |
| WPW (Type A — left-sided accessory pathway) | Tall R-wave in V1, may mimic posterior Q-wave mirror | WPW shows short PR interval (<120 ms), delta wave (slurred upstroke of QRS), widened QRS. The tall R in V1 is from the delta wave, not from posterior injury. No ST depression pattern consistent with ischemia. |
| Incomplete RBBB or RBBB | rSR' pattern in V1 may create tall R-wave | RBBB has characteristic rSR' morphology (not a smooth tall R), wide S-wave in I and V6, QRS >=120 ms for complete RBBB. No horizontal ST depression in V2-V3 (RBBB produces discordant ST-T changes that differ from posterior MI pattern). |
| Normal variant dominant R in V1 | Some individuals have R/S >1 in V1 as a normal variant | Stable on serial ECGs, no ST depression, no acute symptoms, normal T-wave morphology. More common in young adults and thin-chested individuals. |
| Anterior subendocardial ischemia (demand ischemia) | ST depression V1-V3 | Subendocardial ischemia produces diffuse ST depression (not limited to V1-V3), typically in V3-V6, II, aVF as well. Depression is usually maximal in V4-V6, not V1-V2. No tall R-wave development. Upsloping rather than horizontal depression. Often rate-related and resolves with rate control. |
| Hypertrophic cardiomyopathy (HCM) | Deep septal Q-waves, tall R-wave in V1 possible | HCM produces deep narrow Q-waves in lateral leads (I, aVL, V5-V6) from septal hypertrophy, may have tall R in V1 from posterior-to-anterior septal depolarization. LVH voltage criteria present. No acute ST depression pattern. |
| Duchenne muscular dystrophy | Tall R in V1, deep Q-waves in lateral leads | Pediatric/young male patient, progressive cardiomyopathy pattern, clinical context is key. |

### 4.2 Coexisting Conditions
- **Posterior STEMI + Inferior STEMI (inferoposterior MI)**: This is the most common presentation — 60-80% of posterior STEMIs accompany inferior STEMI. The inferior ST elevation in II, III, aVF makes the diagnosis easier because it alerts the clinician to look for posterior extension. The V1-V3 ST depression is then correctly interpreted as posterior injury rather than "reciprocal anterior changes."
- **Posterior STEMI + Lateral STEMI (posterolateral MI)**: When LCx is the culprit, both the posterior and lateral walls are affected. ST elevation appears in V5-V6, I, aVL (lateral territory) alongside the V1-V3 depression (posterior territory). This combination strongly suggests LCx occlusion.
- **Posterior STEMI + LBBB**: LBBB alters V1 morphology (produces QS or rS in V1 normally). In the setting of LBBB, the tall R-wave criterion for posterior MI is unreliable. Apply modified Sgarbossa criteria — specifically, concordant ST depression in V1-V3 exceeding expected discordant changes (ST/S ratio >0.25 in V1-V3) suggests posterior injury.
- **Posterior STEMI + RV Infarction**: Proximal RCA occlusion may produce a triad: inferior STEMI + posterior extension + RV infarction. This is the most hemodynamically devastating combination. V1 may show competing forces: RV injury (elevation) vs posterior reciprocal (depression) — the net result depends on which signal dominates. ST elevation in V1 favoring RV involvement, or ST depression favoring dominant posterior injury.
- **Posterior STEMI + Atrial Fibrillation**: LCx occlusion may trigger AF (left atrial ischemia). AF makes ST measurement more challenging — use beats with moderate R-R intervals.

## 5. STAT Classification

| Priority | Criteria |
|----------|----------|
| **STAT** | YES — posterior STEMI is a STAT condition. It represents acute coronary occlusion with ongoing transmural myocardial necrosis. It is the MOST COMMONLY MISSED STEMI territory because the standard 12-lead ECG shows only reciprocal changes, not direct ST elevation. Automated detection is critical to prevent missed diagnosis. |
| **Time-sensitive** | Door-to-balloon time goal: <90 minutes for primary PCI, <120 minutes if transfer required. The challenge is that posterior STEMI is frequently diagnosed LATE because the ECG findings (ST depression in V1-V3) are misinterpreted as "non-STEMI" or "anterior subendocardial ischemia." The system must flag isolated V1-V3 depression as a potential posterior STEMI and recommend posterior leads. Every minute of diagnostic delay adds to reperfusion delay. |
| **Clinical action** | 1) Recognize ST depression pattern in V1-V3 as potential posterior STEMI — do NOT dismiss as "nonspecific" or "subendocardial." 2) Obtain posterior leads V7-V9 immediately. 3) If V7-V9 confirm ST elevation >=0.5 mm, activate cath lab. 4) If inferior STEMI coexists, obtain V4R to assess RV involvement. 5) Aspirin 325 mg chewed + P2Y12 inhibitor loading + heparin. 6) If RV involvement confirmed, avoid nitroglycerin and diuretics; give IV fluids for hypotension. 7) If isolated posterior STEMI without inferior involvement, cath lab activation is still indicated — do NOT wait for "more ECG evidence." |

### 6.1 Reasoning Domains Required to Detect This Condition
- **Lead-group correlation (PRIMARY)**: The core of posterior STEMI detection is recognizing a specific pattern of ST depression in V1-V3 that is distinct from anterior subendocardial ischemia. This requires comparing ST depression distribution (maximal in V1-V2 for posterior MI vs maximal in V4-V6 for diffuse subendocardial ischemia) and correlating with the presence or absence of inferior/lateral ST elevation.
- **Morphology analysis**: Tall R-wave detection in V1 (R/S ratio calculation), ST depression morphology (horizontal/downsloping vs upsloping), T-wave directionality in V1-V2 (upright and prominent vs normal).
- **Cross-domain reasoning REQUIRED**: The system must integrate multiple observations: (1) ST depression in V1-V3 [ischemia domain], (2) R-wave amplitude in V1 [morphology domain], (3) T-wave polarity in V1-V2 [morphology domain], (4) presence/absence of inferior ST elevation [lead-group correlation], (5) presence/absence of lateral ST elevation [lead-group correlation]. No single domain can make the diagnosis.
- **Sequential reasoning**: IF V1-V3 ST depression is detected THEN assess depression morphology (horizontal/downsloping vs upsloping) THEN check R/S ratio in V1 THEN check for concurrent inferior ST elevation (II, III, aVF) THEN check for concurrent lateral ST elevation (V5-V6, I, aVL) THEN classify: inferoposterior vs posterolateral vs isolated posterior THEN recommend posterior leads V7-V9.
- **Interval/rate analysis**: Standard monitoring for PR prolongation (AV block risk if inferoposterior MI from RCA), QTc prolongation. Not the primary diagnostic domain.
- **"Mirror image" reasoning (UNIQUE to this condition)**: The agent must understand that ST depression in V1-V3 can represent EITHER (a) anterior subendocardial ischemia (demand-type) or (b) posterior STEMI (reciprocal mirror). This disambiguation requires higher-order reasoning about the clinical significance of lead distribution and morphology features.

### 6.2 Feature Dependencies
- **ESSENTIAL features**: ST-segment amplitude at J-point in V1, V2, V3 (depression values); ST-segment morphology in V1-V3 (horizontal/downsloping vs upsloping); R-wave amplitude and duration in V1; R/S ratio in V1; T-wave polarity and amplitude in V1, V2.
- **SUPPORTING features**: ST-segment amplitude in II, III, aVF (concurrent inferior elevation); ST-segment amplitude in I, aVL, V5, V6 (concurrent lateral elevation or aVL reciprocal depression); R-wave amplitude in V2 (progressive R-wave growth); comparison of V1-V3 depression depth vs V4-V6 depression depth (posterior MI = maximal V1-V2; subendocardial = maximal V4-V5).
- **EXCLUSIONARY features**: Diffuse ST depression across V1-V6 with maximum in V4-V5 and without territorial localization (suggests subendocardial ischemia, not posterior STEMI). Short PR interval + delta wave + tall R in V1 (WPW Type A, not posterior MI). RAD + RV strain pattern + peaked P-waves (RVH, not posterior MI). rSR' morphology in V1 (RBBB, not posterior MI).
- **Per-beat analysis needed**: ST amplitude stability (constant = STEMI; intermittent or rate-dependent = demand ischemia or vasospasm). R-wave amplitude trending across serial ECGs (progressive growth = evolving posterior necrosis).

### 6.3 Cross-Condition Interactions
- **Posterior STEMI complicates anterior ischemia interpretation**: The most dangerous cross-condition interaction. Isolated ST depression in V1-V3 can be misclassified as anterior subendocardial ischemia (NSTEMI equivalent) instead of posterior STEMI. This misclassification leads to delayed reperfusion. The system MUST have a dedicated pathway: "V1-V3 ST depression in acute chest pain → evaluate for posterior STEMI before concluding NSTEMI."
- **Posterior STEMI affects inferior STEMI characterization**: When posterior extension coexists with inferior STEMI, the V1-V3 depression deepens beyond what would be expected from reciprocal changes alone. The system should flag "deeper-than-expected V1-V3 depression" in the context of inferior STEMI as likely posterior extension.
- **RVH can mask posterior STEMI**: Pre-existing RVH already produces tall R-waves in V1. New posterior MI on top of pre-existing RVH may be harder to detect because the R-wave is already dominant. Serial comparison (new R-wave growth or new ST depression) becomes critical.
- **LBBB alters V1 morphology**: LBBB normally produces QS or rS in V1. The development of a tall R-wave in V1 in the setting of LBBB is abnormal and may suggest posterior MI, but Sgarbossa criteria must be applied as the primary STEMI detection framework.
- **Concurrent RV infarction creates competing V1 forces**: RV injury produces ST elevation in V1 while posterior injury produces ST depression in V1. The net V1 ST change depends on which signal dominates. This is a unique diagnostic challenge for proximal RCA occlusion with both RV and posterior involvement.

### 6.4 Reasoning Chain Sketch

**Minimum reasoning chain (fast path to high confidence):**
1. Detect horizontal/downsloping ST depression >=0.5 mm in >=2 of V1, V2, V3
2. Confirm R/S ratio >1 in V1 OR prominent upright T-wave in V1 (where normally inverted)
3. Confirm absence of diffuse V1-V6 depression (rules out subendocardial ischemia)
4. -> POSTERIOR STEMI suspected, flag STAT, recommend V7-V9

**Full reasoning chain (complete evidence assembly):**
1. Detect ST depression in V1-V3 — characterize morphology (horizontal/downsloping)
2. Measure R-wave amplitude and R/S ratio in V1 — check for tall R (R/S >1)
3. Assess T-wave polarity in V1-V2 — upright and prominent?
4. Check distribution: is depression maximal in V1-V2 (posterior) vs V4-V5 (subendocardial)?
5. Check for concurrent inferior ST elevation in II, III, aVF → inferoposterior MI
6. Check for concurrent lateral ST elevation in I, aVL, V5-V6 → posterolateral MI
7. Exclude mimics: short PR + delta wave (WPW)? RAD + peaked P (RVH)? rSR' (RBBB)?
8. If inferoposterior, compare III vs II → culprit artery (RCA vs LCx)
9. If inferoposterior, check V1 for ST elevation (may indicate competing RV + posterior forces)
10. Assess PR interval for AV block risk (if RCA territory)
11. Compile: territory (posterior +/- inferior +/- lateral +/- RV), culprit vessel, acuity
12. Generate confidence score, flag STAT, recommend V7-V9 and V4R as appropriate

### 6.5 Confidence Anchors
- **HIGH confidence features**: Horizontal/downsloping ST depression in V1-V3 (maximal V1-V2) WITH tall R-wave (R/S >1 in V1) WITH concurrent inferior ST elevation (specificity >90%). ST elevation in V7-V9 >=0.5 mm if recorded (near-pathognomonic).
- **LOWER confidence if absent**: No tall R-wave in V1 (may be early in infarct evolution, before necrosis develops — ST depression alone is less specific). No concurrent inferior or lateral ST elevation (isolated posterior MI is harder to distinguish from subendocardial ischemia). Upsloping ST depression in V1-V3 (more consistent with demand ischemia).
- **Pathognomonic combination**: ST depression V1-V3 (horizontal, maximal V1-V2) + tall R in V1 (R/S >1) + inferior ST elevation (II, III, aVF) + ST elevation in V7-V9. Near-100% specific.
- **Classification thresholds**:
  - *Possible*: ST depression >=0.5 mm in V1-V3 (horizontal/downsloping), no concurrent inferior or lateral elevation, no tall R-wave yet, clinical context of acute chest pain
  - *Probable*: ST depression in V1-V3 with R/S ratio approaching or >1 in V1, OR concurrent inferior/lateral ST elevation, upright T-waves in V1-V2
  - *Definite*: All of the above plus ST elevation >=0.5 mm in V7-V9, or classic inferoposterior pattern with deep V1-V3 depression + tall R in V1 + inferior elevation

| Rarity in PTB-XL | 4 | Isolated posterior MI is uncommon in PTB-XL. Inferoposterior MI is more commonly labeled as "inferior MI" without explicit posterior annotation. May require re-review of V1-V3 patterns in inferior MI cohort to identify posterior extension cases. |
| **Overall difficulty** | **3.8** | **High — the most commonly missed STEMI territory. Diagnosis by reciprocal/mirror changes rather than direct signs makes this fundamentally harder than other STEMI territories. Cross-domain reasoning and differential complexity are the highest of any single-territory MI.** |

## 6A. Agent Assignment

**Architecture:** Option E — 3 Parallel Phase 1 Specialists + 1 Sequential CDS (Node 2.1 Gate Passed 2026-03-27)

| Agent | Role for Posterior STEMI | Key Features Used |
|-------|--------------------------|-------------------|
| **RRC** (Rhythm/Rate/Conduction) | Supporting | Heart rate, rhythm, QRS duration; RBBB detection (must be flagged — dominant R in V1 from RBBB mimics posterior MI) |
| **IT** (Ischemia/Territory) | Primary | Horizontal ST depression ≥0.5 mm in V1–V3 (reciprocal/mirror of posterior injury), tall upright T-waves in V1–V3 (reciprocal of posterior hyperacute T), ST elevation ≥0.5 mm in V7–V9 if posterior leads available, coronary territory assignment (RCA posterior descending artery or LCx posterolateral); emits cross_domain_hint for CDS |
| **MR** (Morphology/Repolarization) | Supporting | R/S ratio in V1 (dominant R ≥1 as sign of posterior MI), R-wave amplitude progression V1–V3, QRS morphology to distinguish RBBB pattern from posterior R-wave, assessment of WPW Type A morphology |
| **CDS** (Cross-Domain Synthesis) | Required — resolves posterior vs RVH vs RBBB morphology in V1 | Integrates IT's ST depression + MR's R/S ratio + RRC's RBBB flag to determine if dominant R in V1 is from posterior MI, RVH, RBBB, WPW, or normal variant; applies mirror-image test to confirm ST depression as posterior STEMI equivalent |

### Primary Agent
**IT** — posterior STEMI is detected via reciprocal ST depression in V1–V3 representing the mirror image of posterior wall injury, which is the IT agent's ischemia/territory domain; however, the reciprocal nature of the key signals makes CDS integration essential.

### Cross-Domain Hints
- IT emits `cross_domain_hint: "Horizontal ST depression V1–V3 with dominant R-wave — posterior STEMI possible; MR R/S ratio and RRC QRS morphology required to exclude RBBB/RVH"` when horizontal ST depression ≥0.5 mm is detected in V1–V3.
- MR emits `cross_domain_hint: "Dominant R in V1 (R/S ≥1) detected — IT must evaluate for posterior ST depression to determine if this represents posterior MI vs RBBB vs RVH"` when dominant R-wave is identified in V1.

### CDS Specific Role
CDS performs the critical three-way morphology resolution for dominant R-wave in V1: it receives RRC's conduction analysis (RBBB present/absent), IT's ST depression pattern (horizontal depth, lead distribution), and MR's R-wave amplitude metrics. CDS applies the following logic: if RBBB is absent AND R/S ≥1 in V1 AND horizontal ST depression ≥0.5 mm in V1–V3, posterior STEMI is confirmed. If RBBB is present, the dominant R is attributed to RBBB and the ST changes are re-evaluated under Sgarbossa logic. If RVH criteria are met from MR, CDS flags the competing diagnosis.

## 9. Edge Cases and Pitfalls

- **The "missed STEMI" problem**: Posterior STEMI is the most commonly missed acute MI territory because the standard 12-lead ECG shows only reciprocal changes. Studies report that isolated posterior MI is initially missed in up to 30-50% of cases. The system must have a dedicated alert pathway for V1-V3 ST depression that explicitly flags posterior STEMI as a differential — not just "anterior ST depression noted."
- **Isolated posterior MI without inferior or lateral changes**: This occurs when a small posterolateral branch of the LCx (or a small PDA) is occluded without involving the main inferior or lateral territory. The ONLY findings are in V1-V3. This is the hardest variant to diagnose and the one most often misclassified as NSTEMI/subendocardial ischemia. The differentiating features are: depression maximal in V1-V2 (not V4-V5), horizontal/downsloping morphology, tall R-wave, and upright T-waves.
- **V1-V2 electrode placement errors**: V1-V2 placed too high (above the 4th intercostal space) can alter R-wave amplitude and create artifactual changes that mimic posterior MI. The system should note that electrode placement quality affects confidence in tall R-wave interpretation.
- **Pre-existing tall R-wave in V1**: Patients with pre-existing RVH, WPW Type A, or normal variant dominant R in V1 may have tall R-waves at baseline. New posterior MI adds ST depression and morphological changes to the pre-existing tall R. Serial comparison with prior ECGs is essential. The system should flag "new ST depression in V1-V3 with pre-existing tall R" as high suspicion for posterior MI.
- **Posterior STEMI masked by concurrent RV infarction**: In proximal RCA occlusion, the V1 lead receives competing signals — ST elevation from RV injury and ST depression from posterior reciprocal changes. The V1 ST segment may appear isoelectric or only mildly abnormal, masking both conditions. The system should flag: "Inferior STEMI with isoelectric V1 — consider competing posterior and RV forces; obtain both V4R and V7-V9."
- **Female patients**: Women more frequently present with atypical STEMI patterns and may have lower-magnitude ST changes. The baseline T-wave inversion in V1 (more common in women) means that an upright T-wave in V1 is more significant and should raise suspicion for posterior injury.
- **Elderly patients with baseline ST-T abnormalities**: Pre-existing LVH strain, digoxin effect, or chronic ischemic changes can mask the acute posterior MI pattern. The system should compare with prior ECGs when available and flag new changes.
- **Posterior MI evolving to tall R-wave over hours**: In the hyperacute phase, the R-wave in V1 may not yet be dominant (R/S <1). The earliest sign may be isolated horizontal ST depression with subtle T-wave changes. Serial ECGs (every 15-30 minutes) may reveal progressive R-wave growth, confirming evolving posterior necrosis.
- **Rate-related ST depression mimicking posterior MI**: Tachycardia-induced ST depression is typically upsloping and diffuse (V3-V6 predominant). The system should flag the morphological difference: posterior MI produces horizontal/downsloping depression maximal in V1-V2, while rate-related depression is upsloping and maximal in V4-V5.
- **Low-voltage ECG**: In patients with obesity, COPD, or pericardial effusion, the already-attenuated posterior reciprocal signals may fall below detection threshold. Lower the ST depression threshold to 0.3 mm if diffuse low voltage is detected, with a "low voltage caveat" annotation.
