## 1. Pathophysiology Root Cause

### 1.1 What Goes Wrong (Anatomy/Physiology)
- Severe stenosis (typically >70%) of the proximal left anterior descending (LAD) artery, usually before the first septal perforator and/or first diagonal branch
- The patient has experienced a recent episode of transient LAD occlusion — a plaque rupture or vasospasm that temporarily halted flow, then spontaneously reperfused (or was relieved by endogenous thrombolysis / vasodilation)
- During the occlusion, the anterior and septal myocardium supplied by the LAD undergoes subepicardial ischemia, affecting the outer layer of the ventricular wall first (because subepicardial tissue is farthest from the lumen and most vulnerable to supply interruption)
- After reperfusion restores flow, the myocardium is stunned but still viable — no transmural necrosis has occurred yet. The ischemic insult leaves a repolarization abnormality that persists in the pain-free state
- This is a "pre-infarction syndrome" — the critical stenosis remains, and without intervention the LAD will re-occlude. Studies show that without catheterization, 75% of these patients develop anterior STEMI within days to weeks (mean time to infarction: 8.5 days in the original de Zwaan series)
- The myocardium is in a window of salvageability: tissue is alive, enzymes are minimal or normal, but the substrate for complete infarction is established

### 1.2 Electrical Consequence
- The transient ischemic episode disrupts the normal repolarization sequence of the anterior and septal myocardium
- Normal repolarization proceeds from epicardium to endocardium (opposite to depolarization). The subepicardial ischemia reverses this: the damaged epicardial cells repolarize later, shifting the repolarization vector
- The T-wave vector, normally directed anteriorly and leftward (concordant with the QRS in precordial leads), reverses direction — it points posteriorly and rightward, away from the ischemic anterior wall
- This produces T-wave inversions in the leads overlying the anterior wall (V2-V3 primarily, extending to V1-V6 in more extensive disease)
- Critically, there is NO ST elevation at the time of ECG recording because the patient is pain-free and the artery has transiently reperfused. The repolarization abnormality is a "memory" of the ischemic insult
- The QRS complex remains normal because depolarization pathways are intact — there is no necrosis (no Q waves) and no ongoing injury current (no ST elevation)
- Phase 3 of the action potential (repolarization) is selectively affected; Phase 0 (depolarization) is preserved

### 1.3 Why It Appears on ECG
- Leads V2-V3 face the anterior septum directly — the LAD territory. The reversed repolarization vector (now pointing away from these leads) produces T-wave inversions or biphasic T-waves in these leads
- The two morphological patterns reflect different degrees/distributions of repolarization disturbance:
  - **Type A (biphasic)**: The initial positive T-wave component reflects relatively preserved endocardial repolarization; the terminal negative deflection reflects delayed epicardial repolarization. This indicates partial repolarization disruption
  - **Type B (deep symmetric inversion)**: Complete reversal of the repolarization sequence across the full wall thickness. The symmetric shape indicates uniform slowing of repolarization (unlike the asymmetric T inversions of LVH strain, which reflect a gradient)
- Leads I and aVL may show changes if the LAD supplies the high lateral wall (wrapped LAD or large diagonal involvement)
- Inferior leads (II, III, aVF) are typically unaffected because the inferior wall has separate blood supply (RCA or LCx)
- aVR, facing the cavity from the right shoulder, may show a reciprocal upright T-wave (mirror image of the precordial inversions), but this is not a primary diagnostic feature

## 2. ECG Presentation — Lead by Lead

### 2.1 Diagnostic Criteria (2025 AHA/ESC Guidelines)

| Criterion | Threshold | Source |
|-----------|-----------|--------|
| Biphasic T-waves (Type A) or deep symmetric T-wave inversions (Type B) | Present in V2 and V3 (minimum); may extend V1-V6 | de Zwaan et al. 1982; Rhinehardt et al. 2002 |
| Isoelectric or minimally elevated/depressed ST segment | ST elevation <1mm at J-point | ESC 2023 ACS Guidelines |
| No pathological Q waves | Q-wave duration <40ms, depth <25% of R-wave | AHA 2022 STEMI Guidelines (exclusion criterion) |
| Preserved R-wave progression | R-wave amplitude not diminished in precordial leads | de Zwaan criteria |
| ECG recorded during pain-free interval | Changes present AFTER resolution of chest pain | Original Wellens criteria (critical context) |
| Minimal or no troponin elevation | Troponin may be mildly elevated but NOT in typical MI range | Clinical context criterion |
| History of recent anginal chest pain | Pain has resolved at time of ECG | Clinical context criterion |

### 2.3 Key Leads (Most Diagnostic)
- **V2 and V3 are the essential diagnostic leads** — Wellens cannot be diagnosed without changes in at least these two leads
- **V1 through V4** form the core diagnostic territory; V2-V3 are always affected, V1 and V4 are frequently affected
- Extension to V5-V6 and/or I, aVL indicates more proximal LAD stenosis (before first diagonal) or a wrapped LAD — this carries higher risk because more myocardium is at jeopardy
- **Earliest changes appear in V2-V3** and may precede extension to other leads
- **No true reciprocal changes** — this is a repolarization abnormality, not an injury current. Unlike STEMI where ST elevation in one territory produces reciprocal ST depression in the opposite territory, Wellens T-wave changes are confined to leads overlying the affected myocardium

### 2.4 Beat-by-Beat Considerations
- Wellens pattern is **constant across all beats** in a given ECG recording — it does not vary beat-to-beat
- The pattern **evolves over time** (hours to days): many patients show Type A initially that evolves to Type B over hours, then slowly normalizes over weeks if no re-occlusion occurs
- The pattern may **recur** with recurrent anginal episodes — each pain-free interval may reproduce or deepen the T-wave changes
- Rate-independence: the Wellens pattern persists regardless of heart rate (unlike rate-dependent repolarization changes)
- **Serial ECGs are diagnostic**: demonstrating evolution from normal T-waves → pain episode → Wellens pattern on pain-free ECG is the classic presentation

## 3. Morphology Details (What the Agent Must See)

### 3.1 P-wave Changes
- **Morphology**: Normal sinus P-waves. No primary P-wave abnormality from Wellens syndrome itself
- **Duration**: Normal (80-120ms)
- **Axis**: Normal (0 to +75 degrees)
- Note: Coexisting conditions (hypertension causing LAE) may alter P-waves, but this is not part of Wellens

### 3.2 PR Interval Changes
- **Duration**: Normal (120-200ms)
- **Pattern**: Constant; no AV conduction disturbance from Wellens
- The LAD does not supply the AV node (supplied by AV nodal artery, usually from RCA), so AV conduction is unaffected

### 3.3 QRS Complex Changes
- **Duration**: Normal (<120ms) — this is a critical diagnostic requirement. Wide QRS (BBB) makes Wellens pattern unreliable
- **Morphology**: Normal. No pathological Q waves (absence of Q waves confirms viable myocardium — this is what makes urgent intervention meaningful)
- **Amplitude**: Normal R-wave progression V1-V6. Preserved R-waves confirm no prior transmural infarction
- **Axis**: Normal (-30 to +90 degrees). No axis deviation from Wellens itself
- **Key negative finding**: If pathological Q waves are present in V2-V3, the diagnosis shifts from Wellens (pre-infarction) to completed anterior MI with repolarization changes

### 3.4 ST Segment Changes
- **Direction**: Isoelectric or minimal deviation (<1mm elevation or depression at J-point)
- **Morphology**: Isoelectric. NOT elevated (which would indicate acute injury), NOT significantly depressed (which would indicate active ischemia)
- **Measurement point**: J-point
- **Critical distinction**: The ABSENCE of significant ST deviation is a diagnostic criterion. If there is >1mm ST elevation, the patient may be in acute STEMI, not Wellens. If recorded during chest pain, ST elevation/depression represents acute ischemia, not Wellens
- **Gender/lead-specific**: Standard J-point thresholds apply (the point is that ST should be near baseline)

### 3.5 T-wave Changes
- **Type A (Biphasic)**:
  - Direction: Initial upright component followed by terminal inversion
  - The biphasic morphology is specific — it is NOT a simple notched T-wave. The initial positive deflection smoothly transitions to a negative terminal component
  - Amplitude: Variable; the terminal negative component is typically >1mm deep
  - Symmetry: The negative component is relatively symmetric
  - Duration of the negative component is usually >50% of total T-wave duration
- **Type B (Deep Symmetric Inversions)**:
  - Direction: Fully inverted
  - Amplitude: Deep — typically >2mm, often >5mm in V2-V3 (can reach 10-15mm in profound cases)
  - **Symmetry: SYMMETRIC** — this is a critical distinguishing feature. Both limbs of the inverted T-wave have equal or near-equal slopes. This contrasts with the ASYMMETRIC inversions of LVH strain (gradual downslope, sharp upslope)
  - The nadir of the T-wave typically falls near the midpoint of the T-wave duration
- **Both types**: T-wave changes are present in V2-V3 at minimum

### 3.6 QT/QTc Changes
- QT interval may be mildly prolonged due to delayed repolarization in the ischemic territory
- QTc prolongation is typically modest (<500ms) and is a secondary finding, not diagnostic
- No Torsades de Pointes risk specifically from Wellens pattern
- The prolongation reflects the regional repolarization delay that produces the T-wave morphology changes

### 3.7 Other Features
- **No U-waves** characteristic of Wellens (U-waves suggest hypokalemia or LVH, not ischemic repolarization change)
- **No delta waves** (would indicate WPW, a separate condition)
- **No epsilon waves** (would indicate ARVC)
- **J-point**: At baseline (no J-point elevation that would suggest early repolarization or Brugada)
- **Heart rate**: Typically normal sinus rate. Tachycardia suggests ongoing pain/distress (and the ECG may not show classic Wellens in that state)

## 4. Differential Diagnosis

### 4.1 Mimics (What Looks Like This But Isn't)

| Mimic Condition | Shared Features | Distinguishing Features |
|----------------|-----------------|----------------------|
| **Post-tachycardia T-wave changes ("cardiac memory")** | Deep symmetric T-wave inversions in precordial leads; normal QRS; no ST elevation | History of recent tachycardia (SVT, AF with RVR); changes resolve within hours to days; no anginal history; T-wave vector mirrors the direction of the preceding wide-complex QRS during tachycardia |
| **CNS event T-waves ("cerebral T-waves")** | Deep, wide, symmetric T-wave inversions; can be very deep (>10mm); precordial and limb leads | Typically diffuse (not confined to V2-V3); associated neurological symptoms (SAH, stroke, raised ICP); QT often markedly prolonged (>500ms); often accompanied by large U-waves |
| **Myocarditis** | T-wave inversions in precordial leads; troponin elevation; chest pain history | More diffuse T-wave changes (not confined to LAD territory); may have PR depression; troponin significantly elevated; often preceded by viral illness; MRI shows patchy enhancement |
| **Hypertrophic cardiomyopathy (HCM)** | Deep T-wave inversions in precordial leads; can be >10mm (apical HCM) | T-inversions are ASYMMETRIC (strain pattern); high voltage QRS (LVH criteria met); often has abnormal Q-waves in lateral leads (septal hypertrophy); echocardiographic wall thickness >15mm |
| **LVH with strain pattern** | T-wave inversions in lateral/precordial leads | ASYMMETRIC T-wave inversions (slow downslope, rapid upstroke); meets voltage criteria for LVH (Sokolow-Lyon, Cornell); ST depression is downsloping, not isoelectric; primarily in V5-V6, I, aVL (not V2-V3 predominant) |
| **Right ventricular strain (acute PE)** | T-wave inversions in V1-V4; right precordial changes | Associated with sinus tachycardia, S1Q3T3, RBBB; T-inversions in V1-V3 + III (right heart leads); clinical context of dyspnea, pleuritic pain; no anginal pain pattern |
| **Persistent juvenile T-wave pattern** | T-wave inversions in V1-V3 | Patient is typically young female; T-inversions are long-standing and stable on serial ECGs; shallow (<2mm); no anginal history; not new or dynamic |
| **Takotsubo cardiomyopathy** | Deep T-wave inversions in precordial leads after chest pain; wall motion abnormality | T-inversions typically more widespread (V1-V6 + limb leads); often preceded by emotional/physical stress; QT markedly prolonged; wall motion abnormality extends beyond single coronary territory; coronaries are normal on catheterization |
| **Post-MI repolarization changes** | Deep T-wave inversions; precordial leads | Pathological Q-waves present (Wellens has NO Q-waves); history of completed MI; troponin was significantly elevated; changes are persistent (weeks to months) |

### 4.2 Coexisting Conditions
- **Wellens + hypertension/LVH**: LVH strain pattern (asymmetric T inversions in V5-V6) may coexist with Wellens (symmetric T inversions in V2-V3). Key: compare symmetry of T-waves and lead distribution
- **Wellens + prior inferior MI**: Old inferior Q-waves (II, III, aVF) do not affect the diagnosis of Wellens in precordial leads, but suggest multi-vessel disease
- **Wellens + LBBB**: LBBB disrupts normal repolarization in V1-V3, making Wellens pattern UNRELIABLE. Expected discordant T-waves in LBBB can mask or mimic Wellens. Diagnosis requires angiographic correlation
- **Wellens + rate-related changes**: Tachycardia-associated repolarization changes (rate-dependent T-wave inversions) can mimic Wellens. Serial ECGs at different heart rates help distinguish
- **Wellens + digitalis therapy**: Digoxin causes characteristic ST/T changes (scooped ST depression, "Salvador Dali mustache") that can obscure Wellens T-wave morphology

## 5. STAT Classification

| Priority | Criteria |
|----------|----------|
| **STAT** | YES — Wellens syndrome indicates critical proximal LAD stenosis with imminent risk of anterior STEMI. Without intervention, 75% of patients develop extensive anterior MI within days to weeks. The myocardium is still viable — this is the intervention window. |
| **Time-sensitive** | URGENT — cardiology consultation and catheterization within hours, not days. However, this is NOT the same as a "code STEMI" activation (which requires minutes). Wellens patients are hemodynamically stable and pain-free at time of diagnosis. Target: catheterization within 24 hours. |
| **Clinical action** | 1. **DO NOT perform stress testing** — exercise or pharmacological stress testing is CONTRAINDICATED (can precipitate complete LAD occlusion and anterior STEMI on the treadmill). 2. Admit to monitored bed (telemetry). 3. Urgent cardiology consultation for catheterization. 4. Anticoagulation and antiplatelet therapy per ACS protocol. 5. Serial ECGs and troponins. 6. Avoid discharge — these patients are frequently misdiagnosed as "non-cardiac chest pain" due to normal enzymes and pain-free presentation. |

## 6. Reasoning Complexity Analysis (Feeds Into Node 2.1 — Agent Architecture Research)

### 6.1 Reasoning Domains Required to Detect This Condition
- **Morphology analysis (primary)**: T-wave shape classification — must distinguish biphasic (Type A) from deep symmetric (Type B) from asymmetric strain pattern from normal variant
- **Ischemia pattern recognition**: Understanding that this pattern represents repolarization abnormality in the LAD territory; requires knowledge of coronary anatomy to ECG lead mapping
- **Lead-group correlation**: Must verify that changes are in V2-V3 (± extension) — the correct anatomical territory for LAD disease
- **Negative criterion verification**: Must confirm ABSENCE of ST elevation, ABSENCE of pathological Q waves, ABSENCE of significant troponin elevation. These negative findings are as important as the positive T-wave findings
- **Cross-domain reasoning: YES** — requires BOTH morphology expertise (T-wave shape analysis: symmetric vs asymmetric, biphasic pattern recognition) AND ischemia territory knowledge (LAD territory mapping, STEMI-equivalent pattern recognition)
- **Sequential reasoning: YES** — "IF T-wave inversions in V2-V3 AND symmetric AND no Q-waves AND no significant ST elevation THEN Wellens" — this is a multi-step conditional chain
- **Clinical context reasoning**: Ideally requires knowledge that patient is pain-free at time of ECG (this may not be available to the algorithm from ECG signal alone — requires metadata)

### 6.2 Feature Dependencies

**ESSENTIAL features (must be computed by SDA-1):**
- T-wave polarity per lead (positive / negative / biphasic)
- T-wave morphology classification per lead (symmetric vs asymmetric inversion; biphasic initial-up-terminal-down vs initial-down-terminal-up)
- T-wave amplitude (depth of inversion in mm/mV)
- T-wave symmetry ratio (ascending limb slope vs descending limb slope)
- ST segment level at J-point per lead (must be near-isoelectric)
- Q-wave presence/absence and duration per lead
- R-wave amplitude progression V1-V6
- QRS duration

**SUPPORTING features (increase confidence):**
- T-wave inversion depth gradient across leads (V2 deepest → V6 shallowest supports Wellens)
- QTc interval (mild prolongation expected)
- Heart rate (normal sinus rhythm expected)
- P-wave morphology (normal expected)
- Lead-group concordance analysis (changes confined to LAD territory)

**EXCLUSIONARY features (if abnormal, reconsider diagnosis):**
- ST elevation >1mm in V2-V3 → suggests active STEMI, not Wellens
- Pathological Q waves in V2-V4 → suggests completed MI with repolarization changes
- QRS duration >120ms (BBB) → Wellens pattern unreliable
- Diffuse T-wave inversions in non-LAD territory leads → suggests alternative diagnosis (CNS, Takotsubo)
- Asymmetric T-wave inversions with high voltage → suggests LVH strain

**Per-beat vs aggregate:**
- Per-beat: T-wave morphology should be consistent beat-to-beat (if variable, consider artifact or post-tachycardia memory)
- Aggregate: Lead-group analysis is inherently multi-lead/aggregate

### 6.3 Cross-Condition Interactions
- **Wellens affects interpretation of subsequent ECGs**: If a known Wellens patient presents with chest pain and ST elevation, it confirms the predicted progression to STEMI — this accelerates the diagnosis
- **LBBB masks Wellens**: Pre-existing LBBB produces expected discordant T-wave changes in V1-V3 that mimic or obscure Wellens. Cannot diagnose Wellens in the presence of LBBB without serial comparison
- **LVH confounds Wellens**: LVH strain produces T-wave inversions, but these are asymmetric and typically in V5-V6/I/aVL. If LVH coexists, the agent must differentiate strain inversions (lateral leads, asymmetric) from Wellens inversions (V2-V3, symmetric)
- **Wellens vs de Winter**: Both represent critical LAD disease but are mutually exclusive patterns. De Winter shows upsloping ST depression with tall peaked T-waves (during active occlusion). Wellens shows T-wave inversions with isoelectric ST (after reperfusion). They may occur in the SAME patient at different time points
- **Wellens → STEMI evolution**: Wellens can evolve into frank anterior STEMI. The agent should flag if a prior ECG showed Wellens and the current ECG shows ST elevation — this is the predicted natural history
- **Differential with post-reperfusion changes**: After PCI for STEMI, persistent T-wave inversions in the infarct territory are EXPECTED and benign. Wellens must be distinguished from post-reperfusion T-wave inversions by clinical context (no prior PCI in Wellens)

### 6.4 Reasoning Chain Sketch

**Minimum reasoning chain (high confidence):**
1. Detect T-wave inversions or biphasic T-waves in V2 and V3
2. Classify T-wave morphology: symmetric inversion (Type B) or biphasic with initial upright then terminal inversion (Type A)
3. Confirm ST segment is isoelectric (no significant elevation or depression at J-point)
4. Confirm no pathological Q waves in V1-V4
5. Confirm QRS is narrow (<120ms)
6. → **Wellens pattern identified, STAT flag**

**Full reasoning chain (complete evidence assembly):**
1. Detect T-wave polarity in all 12 leads
2. Identify T-wave inversions or biphasic T-waves in V2 and V3
3. Assess T-wave morphology: Type A (biphasic: initial up, terminal down) or Type B (deep, symmetric, fully inverted)
4. Measure T-wave symmetry ratio in V2-V3 (symmetric = Wellens; asymmetric = LVH strain)
5. Measure T-wave depth in V2-V3 (>2mm supports; >5mm strong support)
6. Check extension: V1, V4, V5, V6, I, aVL — more extension = more proximal LAD stenosis
7. Measure ST segment at J-point in V1-V6: must be <1mm elevation
8. Check for pathological Q waves in V1-V4: must be absent
9. Verify R-wave progression: must be preserved (no loss of R-wave height)
10. Check QRS duration: must be <120ms (narrow)
11. Assess heart rate and rhythm: normal sinus expected
12. Rule out mimics: check for LVH voltage criteria (if met, T inversions may be strain); check for diffuse T inversions beyond LAD territory (suggests Takotsubo, CNS)
13. Calculate confidence score based on criteria fulfillment
14. → **Classification: Possible / Probable / Definite Wellens + Type A or B + STAT flag**

### 6.5 Confidence Anchors

**HIGH confidence features:**
- Deep (>5mm) symmetric T-wave inversions in V2 AND V3 with isoelectric ST segment
- Biphasic T-waves (initial up, terminal down) in V2 AND V3 — this pattern is highly specific
- Normal QRS with no Q-waves AND preserved R-wave progression
- Changes confined to LAD territory (V1-V6, ± I, aVL) with normal inferior leads

**Features that LOWER confidence:**
- Asymmetric T-wave inversions (raises LVH strain possibility)
- T-wave inversions only in V2 or only in V3 (not both) — below diagnostic threshold
- Significant ST elevation (>1mm) — suggests active STEMI, not Wellens
- Pathological Q-waves — suggests completed MI
- Wide QRS (BBB) — Wellens pattern unreliable
- T-wave inversions extending to inferior leads — suggests non-LAD etiology
- Very high voltage QRS (LVH criteria met) — strain pattern may explain the findings

**Pathognomonic combination (near-100% specific):**
- Biphasic T-wave (Type A) in V2-V3 with initial positive and terminal negative component + isoelectric ST + no Q waves + narrow QRS — this specific biphasic morphology in these specific leads has very high specificity for critical LAD stenosis

**Classification thresholds:**
- **Possible Wellens**: T-wave inversions in V2 or V3 (one lead) + isoelectric ST + no Q-waves (sensitivity-oriented, low specificity)
- **Probable Wellens**: T-wave inversions or biphasic T-waves in V2 AND V3 + isoelectric ST + no Q-waves + narrow QRS (balanced)
- **Definite Wellens**: Symmetric T-wave inversions >2mm or classic biphasic T-waves in V2 AND V3 + isoelectric ST + no Q-waves + narrow QRS + preserved R-waves + no LVH voltage criteria + changes confined to LAD territory (high specificity)

### 6.6 Difficulty Score

| Dimension | Score (1-5) | Notes |
|-----------|-------------|-------|
| Signal clarity needed | 3 | T-wave morphology analysis requires clean signal — baseline wander or noise can obscure the biphasic pattern or make symmetric inversions appear asymmetric. High-pass filter artifacts can distort T-waves. |
| Number of leads required | 2 | Minimum 2 leads (V2, V3) are diagnostic; but confirmation requires all precordial leads + limb leads to assess territory and rule out mimics. Can be suspected from V1-V4 subset. |

## 6A. Agent Assignment

**Architecture:** Option E — 3 Parallel Phase 1 Specialists + 1 Sequential CDS (Node 2.1 Gate Passed 2026-03-27)

| Agent | Role for Wellens Syndrome | Key Features Used |
|-------|--------------------------|-------------------|
| **RRC** (Rhythm/Rate/Conduction) | Supporting | Heart rate and rhythm (sinus required; tachycardia would invalidate Wellens — post-tachycardia T-wave changes are a mimic), QRS duration (must be narrow — WPW or BBB are differential mimics) |
| **IT** (Ischemia/Territory) | Primary | Biphasic T-waves (Type A: terminal inversion in V2–V3) or deep symmetric T-wave inversion (Type B: in V2–V3), absence of ST elevation ≥1 mm (critical negative criterion), absence of pathological Q-waves (critical negative criterion), minimal or no ST depression, LAD territory assignment |
| **MR** (Morphology/Repolarization) | Supporting | T-wave morphology classification — symmetric vs asymmetric inversion, T-wave depth measurement, biphasic terminal negativity morphology (Type A), uniform deep inversion (Type B); distinguishes from LVH strain (asymmetric) and CNS T-waves (widely inverted, very deep) |
| **CDS** (Cross-Domain Synthesis) | Required — confirms negative criteria across all agents | Integrates IT's ischemic T-wave pattern with MR's morphology classification and RRC's rhythm/QRS findings; confirms all three negative criteria are met (no STE, no Q-waves, narrow QRS); generates final Wellens Type A vs Type B classification |

### Primary Agent
**IT** — Wellens syndrome is an ischemic T-wave pattern representing critical LAD stenosis with reperfusion, detected primarily by characteristic T-wave changes in V2–V3 in the ischemia/territory domain.

### Cross-Domain Hints
No cross-domain hints required from Phase 1 agents — the pattern is within IT's primary domain with MR providing T-wave morphology support. CDS performs the critical absence confirmation across all three agents.

### CDS Specific Role
CDS integrates IT's T-wave pattern detection with MR's morphology classification and RRC's rhythm findings to confirm Wellens syndrome. The critical CDS function is validating the three negative criteria that must be absent: no ST elevation (IT), no pathological Q-waves (IT), and no wide QRS or preexcitation (RRC). CDS then classifies the pattern as Type A (biphasic, MR confirmation) or Type B (symmetric deep inversion, MR confirmation) and generates the final diagnosis with clinical urgency flag (unstable LAD disease — do not stress test).

## 9. Edge Cases and Pitfalls

### Atypical Presentations
- **Wellens in a single lead (V2 or V3 alone)**: May represent very focal disease or early/evolving pattern. Lower confidence but should still trigger monitoring and serial ECGs
- **Wellens extending to V1-V6 + I + aVL**: Indicates very proximal LAD stenosis (before S1 and D1 branches) or a dominant LAD that wraps around the apex. Higher myocardial jeopardy — more urgent
- **Type A evolving to Type B within hours**: This is the natural progression and confirms the diagnosis. Serial ECG comparison is highly valuable
- **"Pseudonormalization"**: If a patient with prior Wellens ECG presents with chest pain and now has UPRIGHT T-waves in V2-V3, this is NOT normal — the T-waves have "pseudonormalized" due to acute transmural ischemia. This is equivalent to STEMI presentation in the context of known Wellens

### Age/Sex/Demographic Variations
- **Young women**: May have persistent juvenile T-wave inversions in V1-V3 that mimic Wellens. Key differentiator: persistent juvenile pattern is stable over years, shallow (<2mm), and not associated with anginal symptoms
- **Elderly patients**: T-wave inversions may be blunted by age-related repolarization changes. Wellens should still be suspected if the pattern is new on serial comparison
- **Athletes**: Athletic heart can produce T-wave inversions in V1-V4 (training-related repolarization changes). Context critical — athletes with Wellens-like pattern and exertional chest pain need catheterization regardless

### Technical Artifacts That Mimic This Condition
- **High-pass filter artifact (0.5 Hz)**: Can distort ST-T segment, creating artificial T-wave inversion or biphasic appearance. Ensure standard 0.05 Hz high-pass filter for diagnostic ECGs
- **Baseline wander**: Can make isoelectric ST appear elevated or depressed, and can distort T-wave morphology. Preprocessing pipeline must correct baseline wander before Wellens assessment
- **Lead reversal (LA-RA swap)**: Swapping left and right arm leads can alter precordial T-wave morphology. However, V2-V3 are relatively unaffected by limb lead reversal (they have independent chest electrodes)
- **Poor electrode contact on V2/V3**: Produces noisy signal that can mimic T-wave morphology changes. Quality assessment should flag low SNR in key leads

### When This Condition Is Masked by Another
- **LBBB masking Wellens**: LBBB produces expected discordant T-wave inversions in V1-V3. Wellens CANNOT be reliably diagnosed in the presence of LBBB. If clinical suspicion is high, proceed directly to catheterization regardless of ECG
- **Paced rhythm masking Wellens**: RV pacing produces LBBB-like QRS and secondary repolarization changes that mask Wellens. Same limitation as LBBB
- **LVH strain overriding Wellens**: If LVH strain pattern is present in V5-V6 and T-wave inversions are also present in V2-V3, the V2-V3 inversions may be dismissed as "strain." Key: strain inversions are asymmetric (V5-V6); if V2-V3 inversions are symmetric and deeper, Wellens should be considered as coexisting
- **Wellens after fibrinolytic therapy / PCI**: Post-reperfusion T-wave inversions are expected and benign. Wellens should NOT be diagnosed if the patient has recently undergone reperfusion therapy. If T-wave inversions RECUR after initial resolution post-PCI, this suggests in-stent restenosis, not primary Wellens
