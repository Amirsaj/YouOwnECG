## 1. Pathophysiology Root Cause

### 1.1 Inferior Wall Anatomy and Arterial Supply
The inferior wall of the left ventricle (diaphragmatic surface) is supplied predominantly by the right coronary artery (RCA) in 85% of individuals (right-dominant circulation). In left-dominant individuals (~8%) and co-dominant cases (~7%), the left circumflex artery (LCx) supplies the inferior wall via the posterior descending artery (PDA).

The inferior wall encompasses the diaphragmatic surface from base to apex, including the inferior interventricular septum in its basal portions. The RCA also supplies the right ventricle via RV marginal branches, and the AV node via the AV nodal artery (typically an RCA branch in right-dominant anatomy). This anatomical relationship explains the frequent co-occurrence of inferior MI with AV conduction abnormalities and right ventricular infarction.

### 1.2 Scar Formation and Electrical Silence
Complete occlusion of the RCA or LCx with failure of timely reperfusion causes transmural necrosis of the inferior wall. Over the subsequent weeks to months, dead myocytes are replaced by fibrous scar tissue that is electrically silent. The scar cannot contribute to ventricular depolarization, and the recording electrodes overlying the inferior wall (II, III, aVF) detect only the unopposed electrical forces moving away from them — generating pathological Q waves in the inferior leads.

### 1.3 Chronic Phase Characteristics
In the chronic phase (months to years after the acute event), the acute injury pattern (ST elevation, hyperacute T-waves) has fully resolved. The persistent electrocardiographic signature consists of:
1. Pathological Q waves in II, III, and/or aVF
2. Possible mild persistent T-wave inversions in inferior leads
3. Normal or near-normal ST segments

The right ventricular free wall may also have sustained infarction during the acute event, but RV infarction scar is rarely visible on the standard 12-lead ECG in the chronic phase. RV wall thinning and dysfunction is assessed by imaging.

## 2. ECG Presentation — Lead by Lead

### 2.1 Diagnostic Criteria (2025 AHA/ESC Guidelines)

Per the Fourth Universal Definition of MI and 2022 ESC Chronic Coronary Syndrome guidelines:

**Pathological Q-wave criteria for inferior MI** (must be present in ≥2 contiguous inferior leads):
- **Duration ≥40ms (≥1 small square)** in leads II, III, or aVF
- **Depth ≥25% of the amplitude of the following R wave** in the same lead
- **QS complex** in inferior leads (no R wave at all)

**Critical exclusion**: An isolated Q wave in lead III alone is NOT pathological. Lead III is highly sensitive to respiratory variation and position (the heart's electrical axis shifts with inspiration, which can eliminate the R-wave in III, producing a pseudo-Q or QS). The Q must appear in ≥2 of the three inferior leads (any combination of II + III, III + aVF, or all three) to be considered pathological.

**Supplementary finding**: Q in aVF without Q in III can be an isolated finding of uncertain significance and should not be confidently labelled as inferior MI without supporting history or imaging.

### 2.2 Lead-by-Lead Manifestation

| Lead | Finding in Chronic Inferior MI | Significance |
|------|--------------------------------|--------------|
| I | Usually normal | Not inferior territory |
| II | Pathological Q (≥40ms or ≥25% R) or QS | Core inferior lead — most reliable |
| III | Pathological Q or QS | Core inferior lead — Q in III alone is NOT diagnostic |
| aVR | Normal | Reciprocal opposite; usually upright R |
| aVL | May show mild ST-T changes | Reciprocal lead to inferior; baseline may show high ST |
| aVF | Pathological Q or QS | Core inferior lead — high specificity |
| V1 | Normal or RV changes if prior RV MI | Not standard inferior territory |
| V2 | Normal | Not inferior territory |
| V3 | Normal | Not inferior territory |
| V4 | Normal; check for apex involvement | If apical, Q in V4 possible |
| V5 | Normal | Not inferior territory |
| V6 | Normal | Not inferior territory |

**Reciprocal relationship**: Leads aVL and I are reciprocal to the inferior leads. In acute inferior MI, ST elevation in II/III/aVF is accompanied by ST depression in aVL. In chronic inferior MI, the ST relationship has normalized, but the Q waves remain.

### 2.3 Key Leads
- **II + aVF**: The most reliable pair for confirming inferior MI (both limb leads, respiratory variation less likely to affect both simultaneously)
- **III**: Most sensitive for inferior MI Q-waves but least specific (normal variant Q common)
- **Minimum requirement**: Q meeting criteria in ≥2 of {II, III, aVF}
- **Supporting feature**: Reduced R-wave amplitude in inferior leads relative to prior ECG

### 2.4 Beat-by-Beat Considerations
- Inferior Q-wave measurement should be performed during normal sinus rhythm when possible; VPBs may show Q-wave changes not present in sinus beats
- Q-wave depth in III may vary beat to beat with respiration — measure in normal-depth beats, not at peak inspiration
- In AF, QRS morphology per beat may vary; consistent Q-wave pattern across multiple beats is more reliable
- Rate does not substantially affect Q-wave morphology in the inferior leads

## 3. Morphology Details

### 3.1 Q Wave in Lead III — Special Case
Lead III is the most problematic inferior lead for Q-wave interpretation:
- A Q wave in III can be a normal variant, appearing in virtually all healthy individuals at some respiratory phases
- Inspiratory shift of the heart makes the mean QRS axis more vertical, which can project the initial vector negatively onto lead III
- Normal Q in III: narrow (<40ms), depth variable, may disappear with held expiration
- Pathological Q in III: wide (≥40ms), persists across respiratory phases, accompanied by Q in II or aVF

**Clinical rule**: Always insist on Q in ≥2 inferior leads before diagnosing inferior MI. A sole Q in III is a normal variant unless proven otherwise.

### 3.2 Q Wave in aVF — Reliability
aVF Q waves are more reliable than lead III alone. aVF points directly inferiorly (at +90°) and is less affected by respiratory variation than III. An aVF Q meeting criteria (≥40ms or ≥25% R-wave) in conjunction with any Q in II or III is strong evidence of inferior scar.

### 3.3 Q Wave in Lead II — Significance
Lead II Q waves meeting criteria are highly specific for inferior MI. Lead II points at +60°; a Q in II requires a broad initial vector pointed away from +60°, which in the absence of other causes (LAHB, WPW) is almost always inferior scar.

### 3.4 T-Wave Changes in Chronic Phase
- Mild T-wave inversion in II, III, aVF may persist chronically (chronic repolarization abnormality)
- Deep persistent T-wave inversions in inferior leads months after MI: consider peri-infarct ischemia or aneurysm
- Upright T-waves in inferior leads with Q waves = stable chronic healed inferior MI

### 3.5 ST Segment in Chronic Phase
- Should be isoelectric or near-isoelectric in chronic phase
- Persistent inferior ST elevation (especially with Q waves and LV aneurysm shape on echo) = inferior LV aneurysm — rare but described
- New inferior ST elevation on chronic inferior Q-wave background = acute re-infarction — urgent

### 3.6 RV Involvement Markers (Chronic)
- In acute inferior MI, right precordial leads (V3R, V4R) show ST elevation if RV is involved
- In chronic phase, RV infarction ECG markers typically normalize
- Residual Q in V4R in the chronic phase suggests prior RV infarction (modest sensitivity)
- RV dysfunction after chronic inferior MI is better assessed with echo

### 3.7 AV Conduction
- AV node is supplied by the AV nodal artery (usually RCA) — acute inferior MI commonly causes transient AV block (often resolves within days)
- In chronic inferior MI: complete AV block from acute phase usually resolves; persistent PR prolongation or Mobitz I is possible if AV node ischemia was severe
- Bundle of His and left bundle branch are not typically in inferior MI territory — fascicular blocks are not a characteristic sequela

## 4. Differential Diagnosis

### 4.1 Mimics
| Mimic | Distinguishing Feature |
|-------|------------------------|
| Normal variant Q in III | Q in III ONLY; disappears with expiration; <40ms; no clinical history |
| LAHB (Left Anterior Fascicular Block) | Small q in I, aVL; rS in II, III, aVF (S wave dominant, not QS); left axis deviation; no wide Q |
| WPW with inferior negative delta | Short PR; delta wave visible; pseudo-Q waves inferior from pre-excitation |
| Acute inferior STEMI | ST elevation present — active event, not chronic; must not confuse with old Q + new ST elevation |
| COPD/chronic lung disease | S1Q3T3 pattern (S in I, Q in III, T inversion in III); not full inferior criteria; Q in III alone |
| Hyperkalemia | Widened QRS; may simulate Q waves from QRS broadening; tall peaked T-waves; biochemistry |
| Cardiac amyloid | Pseudo-infarction pattern inferior; low voltage; clinical context |
| Pulmonary embolism (acute) | S1Q3T3 pattern; right heart strain; sinus tachycardia; not chronic scar pattern |

### 4.2 Coexisting Conditions
- **Prior inferior MI + new inferior STEMI**: The existing Q waves may mask the full degree of ST elevation. The ST elevation is the key acute indicator — even modest ST elevation in the setting of pre-existing inferior Q waves is an acute emergency.
- **Prior inferior MI + RV infarction**: During the acute event, RV MI may have occurred simultaneously. Chronically, this may leave residual RV dysfunction visible on echo but the ECG is less helpful.
- **Prior inferior MI + LBBB**: LBBB masks Q-wave interpretation. In LBBB, the inferior leads may show rS pattern (no Q expected). Prior inferior MI in LBBB requires imaging.
- **Prior inferior MI + posterior MI**: RCA occlusion can cause both inferior and posterior MI simultaneously. In the chronic phase, look for tall R in V1-V2 (posterior Q-wave equivalent) co-existing with inferior Q waves.

## 6. Reasoning Complexity Analysis

### 6.1 Q-Wave III Exclusion Logic
The most important inferential step in inferior MI diagnosis: the system must detect when only lead III carries a Q wave and explicitly withhold the inferior MI diagnosis. This requires lead-specific logic rather than simple "Q wave in any inferior lead" detection. Lead III isolation requires active reasoning about the two-lead rule.

### 6.2 Respiratory Variant Handling
Standard 12-lead ECGs are acquired at a fixed moment without controlled respiration. Lead III Q-wave amplitude may vary by recording phase. The system should note when Q in III is the sole abnormal finding and flag it as "possible normal variant — Q in III only."

### 6.3 Acute on Chronic Recognition
An inferior MI patient who re-occludes the RCA territory presents with new ST elevation on a background of pre-existing Q waves. The pre-existing Q waves may cause an examiner to under-interpret the ST changes ("old changes"). The system must flag any new ST elevation regardless of pre-existing Q-wave status.

### 6.4 Posterior MI Co-occurrence
Inferior MI from RCA is commonly accompanied by posterior MI (RCA courses to posterior wall). In the chronic phase, concurrent posterior MI manifests as tall R waves in V1-V2. The system should look for this pairing and report it as "inferior + posterior MI pattern."

### 6.5 AV Nodal Artery Territory
Chronic inferior MI patients may have pre-existing first-degree or second-degree (Mobitz I) AV block from chronic AV nodal ischemia. This should be noted in the arrhythmia assessment as a chronic sequela rather than a new finding.

### 6.6 Difficulty Score

| Dimension | Score (1–5) | Rationale |
|-----------|-------------|-----------|
| Q-Wave Identification | 2 | Clear criteria; majority of cases straightforward |
| Lead III Exclusion Logic | 3 | Must apply 2-lead rule and isolate lead III cases |
| Respiratory Artifact Handling | 2 | Flag lone lead III Q; manageable logic |
| Acute on Chronic Detection | 3 | ST elevation detection on Q-wave background |
| Posterior MI Co-detection | 2 | R-wave in V1-V2 is a secondary check |

| Agent | Role for Old Inferior MI | Key Features Used |
|-------|--------------------------|-------------------|
| **RRC** (Rhythm/Rate/Conduction) | Supporting | Left axis deviation resulting from inferior wall scar (loss of inferior forces); baseline rhythm; any arrhythmia |
| **IT** (Ischemia/Territory) | Primary | Pathological Q-waves in II, III, aVF (≥40 ms duration or ≥25% of R-wave amplitude); Q in III allowed if also in II and aVF; RCA/LCx territory assignment; no concurrent ST elevation (distinguishes old from acute) |
| **MR** (Morphology/Repolarization) | Not involved | No morphology features specific to old inferior MI beyond Q-waves |
| **CDS** (Cross-Domain Synthesis) | Standard integration only | Integrates Q-wave territory (IT) with axis deviation (RRC) to confirm old inferior MI; distinguishes pathological from physiological Q-waves in inferior leads (Q in III alone is normal) |

### Primary Agent
**IT** — Old inferior MI is defined by pathological Q-waves in II/III/aVF assigned to the RCA or LCx territory; Q-wave criteria and coronary territory mapping are IT-domain functions.

### Cross-Domain Hints
- IT → RRC: `cross_domain_hint: "inferior_Q_waves_detected — check for left axis deviation from inferior wall voltage loss"`

### CDS Specific Role
CDS integrates IT's inferior Q-wave findings with RRC's axis measurement. The key CDS task is differentiating pathological inferior Q-waves (present in ≥2 of II/III/aVF, meeting duration/depth criteria) from physiological Q in lead III alone (normal variant, axis-dependent). CDS applies the rule: Q in III alone without Q in II or aVF = normal variant; Q in II and III and aVF = old inferior MI. Concurrent left axis deviation from RRC strengthens the old inferior MI classification.

## 9. Edge Cases and Pitfalls

**1. Q in lead III alone — the most common false positive.** An isolated Q wave in lead III is one of the most over-diagnosed "abnormalities" in ECG interpretation. Lead III is oriented at +120° from lead I and is exquisitely sensitive to respiratory variation. During deep inspiration, the diaphragm flattens and rotates the heart, producing a more vertical axis that can project the initial QRS vector negatively onto lead III. Always require Q in ≥2 inferior leads (including II or aVF) before labeling inferior MI.

**2. LAFB producing rS in inferior leads.** Left anterior fascicular block (LAFB) changes the initial ventricular activation direction, producing small initial R waves followed by deep S waves in inferior leads (rS pattern). This is NOT a Q wave. True pathological Q waves in inferior leads with LAFB are possible (inferior MI + LAFB) but the two conditions can coexist from RCA disease (RCA can supply anterior fascicle in some individuals).

**3. New inferior ST elevation on chronic inferior Q-wave background.** Patients with prior inferior MI may present with re-occlusion of the RCA (stent thrombosis, progression of disease) and develop new inferior STEMI. The pre-existing Q waves may reduce the clinician's or system's index of suspicion. Any new ST elevation ≥0.5mm in ≥2 inferior leads must be treated as acute until proven otherwise.

**4. Inferior MI masking posterior MI.** The RCA in right-dominant anatomy supplies the posterior descending artery (PDA), which perfuses the posterior (inferior-basal) left ventricular wall. RCA occlusion can cause both inferior and posterior MI. Posterior MI manifests as tall R waves in V1-V2 (mirror image of posterior Q waves). This finding may be missed if attention is focused only on the inferior leads.

**5. WPW with inferior pathway.** Accessory pathways with inferior pre-excitation produce negative delta waves in II, III, aVF that create pseudo-Q waves exactly mimicking inferior MI. The short PR interval (<120ms) and the presence of delta waves in other leads are the discriminators. If PR is normal and no delta is seen elsewhere, WPW is less likely.

**6. Pulmonary embolism S1Q3T3 mimicry.** Large PE produces the S1Q3T3 pattern: S wave in lead I, Q wave in lead III, T-wave inversion in lead III. This involves only lead III and does not produce Q waves in lead II or aVF, which is the key distinguishing feature. Additionally, the acute clinical setting (hypoxia, tachycardia, right heart strain on echo) is very different from chronic inferior MI.

**7. Right ventricular infarction co-assessment.** In the acute phase, RV MI accompanying inferior MI requires right-sided leads. In the chronic phase, standard ECG has low sensitivity for prior RV MI. If a patient with prior inferior MI presents with signs of right heart failure, echo is the appropriate assessment tool rather than ECG.
