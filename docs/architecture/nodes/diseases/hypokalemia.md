## 1. Pathophysiology Root Cause

### 1.1 Cellular Mechanism

Potassium is the primary intracellular cation. The resting membrane potential (RMP) is determined by the K+ equilibrium potential via the Nernst equation. When extracellular K+ falls, the K+ equilibrium potential becomes MORE negative (hyperpolarized), making the RMP more negative. This increases the distance between RMP and the threshold potential, paradoxically increasing membrane excitability via triggered and automaticity mechanisms.

The primary ECG effect, however, arises from repolarization: phase 3 repolarization depends on rapid K+ efflux through IKr and IKs channels. With low extracellular K+, outward K+ current is paradoxically reduced (inward rectification), prolonging the action potential duration (APD). This prolonged repolarization is directly reflected as QU prolongation on the surface ECG.

### 1.2 Ionic Current Changes

- **IKr and IKs (rapid/slow delayed rectifier):** Reduced outward current → prolonged phase 3
- **Na/K-ATPase:** Slowed pump activity → intracellular Na+ rises → secondary Na/Ca exchanger activation → increased intracellular Ca2+
- **Increased intracellular Ca2+:** Promotes delayed afterdepolarizations (DADs) → triggered arrhythmias
- **U wave generation:** Likely from prolonged repolarization of His-Purkinje fibers (M cells or Purkinje tissue repolarize last, creating a second positive deflection after the T wave)

### 1.3 Progressive Severity and ECG Correlation

K+ 3.0–3.5 mEq/L: Subtle T-wave flattening, early U-wave prominence
K+ 2.5–3.0 mEq/L: U waves exceed T wave amplitude in V2-V3, apparent QT prolongation (QU prolongation)
K+ <2.5 mEq/L: ST depression, T-U fusion, PVCs, high TdP risk — STAT threshold
K+ <2.0 mEq/L: Risk of sustained VT/VF

## 2. ECG Presentation — Lead by Lead

### 2.1 Diagnostic Criteria


| Criterion                | Finding                           | Threshold / Notes                           |
| ------------------------ | --------------------------------- | ------------------------------------------- |
| U wave prominence        | U wave > T wave amplitude         | Most sensitive finding; best in V2–V3       |
| T-wave flattening        | T amplitude reduced               | Early finding; may invert in severe cases   |
| Apparent QT prolongation | QU interval prolonged             | U wave merges with T; true QT may be normal |
| QU interval              | >500 ms                           | High TdP risk threshold                     |
| ST depression            | Horizontal or downsloping         | Lateral leads predominant                   |
| PVCs                     | Frequent or multifocal            | Increasing arrhythmia burden with severity  |
| TdP                      | Polymorphic VT with rotating axis | End-stage, life-threatening                 |


### 2.2 Lead-by-Lead Manifestation


| Lead | Typical Finding                  | Notes                    |
| ---- | -------------------------------- | ------------------------ |
| I    | T-wave flattening, ST depression | Lateral involvement      |
| II   | T-wave flattening, U wave        | Inferior, U wave visible |
| III  | Variable, less prominent         | Less reliable            |
| aVR  | Reciprocal changes               | Often upright U in aVR   |
| aVL  | T flattening, ST depression      | Lateral                  |
| aVF  | T flattening, U wave             | Inferior                 |
| V1   | U wave, T-wave changes           | Often U wave visible     |
| V2   | Prominent U wave (most visible)  | PRIMARY diagnostic lead  |
| V3   | Prominent U wave                 | PRIMARY diagnostic lead  |
| V4   | U wave, T flattening             | Good sensitivity         |
| V5   | ST depression, U wave            | Lateral                  |
| V6   | ST depression, U wave            | Lateral                  |


### 2.3 Key Leads

**V2 and V3** are the primary leads for U wave detection. The U wave is largest in V2-V3 because the normal T-U complex is largest in these leads and the underlying Purkinje repolarization gradient is most visible in the precordial orientation.

**V5-V6 and I** show ST depression and T-wave changes predominantly when lateral ischemic-pattern changes develop.

The diagnostic maneuver: measure the amplitude of the U wave vs. the T wave in V2 or V3. U > T = significant hypokalemia until proven otherwise.

### 2.4 Beat-by-Beat Considerations

- U wave follows the T wave at approximately 150–250 ms after T peak
- In severe hypokalemia, T and U waves fuse — the apparent T wave is actually a T-U complex
- Measuring QT to the end of the fused T-U complex overestimates true QT; the true QT ends at the nadir between T and U (if distinguishable)
- PVCs arising from triggered activity (DADs) occur on the descending T wave slope
- R-on-T phenomenon risk in hypokalemia: PVC falling during prolonged repolarization → VF

## 3. Morphology Details

### 3.1 U Wave Morphology

- Polarity: Positive (upright) in V2-V5 normally; abnormally prominent in hypokalemia
- Normal amplitude: <1 mm or <25% of preceding T wave
- Hypokalemia threshold: U ≥ T amplitude
- Duration: 150–200 ms
- Configuration: Smooth, rounded dome; broader than T wave

### 3.2 T Wave Morphology

- Flattening is progressive with K+ decline
- May invert in severe hypokalemia (V4-V6)
- T wave flattening precedes overt U wave prominence in mild-moderate hypokalemia

### 3.3 ST Segment

- Horizontal or downsloping ST depression in V4-V6, I, aVL
- Not primarily ischemic — electrolyte-mediated repolarization abnormality
- Distinguishing from ischemia: hypokalemia ST depression is diffuse lateral; ischemia tends to be territory-specific

### 3.4 PR Interval

- May be slightly prolonged in severe hypokalemia
- Less prominent than in hyperkalemia

### 3.5 QRS Complex

- Generally narrow and normal
- Severe hypokalemia: very rarely QRS widening
- Wide QRS in the setting of hypokalemia → consider co-existing hypermagnesemia or drug toxicity

### 3.6 QT/QU Interval Measurement

- True QT: measure to end of T wave before U wave begins (use lead with clear T-U separation)
- QU interval: measured to end of U wave — this is the clinically dangerous interval for TdP risk
- QTc (Bazett): QT / √(RR in seconds)
- QU >500 ms = high arrhythmia risk

### 3.7 Rhythm Abnormalities

- Sinus rhythm initially preserved
- PVCs: early, often bigeminal or multifocal
- PACs: increased atrial ectopy
- Atrial fibrillation: hypokalemia is a common precipitant of AF
- TdP: polymorphic VT with rotating QRS axis, triggered by PVC-on-T
- VF: terminal arrhythmia

## 4. Differential Diagnosis

### 4.1 Mimics


| Condition                | Overlapping Feature             | Distinguishing Feature                                               |
| ------------------------ | ------------------------------- | -------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| Hypomagnesemia           | Identical ECG, U waves, TdP     | Lab values only; Mg deficiency usually coexists                      |
| Normal variant U waves   | Prominent U waves in V2-V3      | U < T amplitude; K+ normal                                           |
| Ischemia (LAD)           | ST depression, T changes        | ST depression territory-specific; no U waves                         |
| LVH                      | T-wave inversion, ST depression | Voltage criteria met; strain pattern; no U waves                     |
| Digoxin effect           | T-wave changes, ST scooping     | Characteristic "scooped" morphology; no U waves                      |
| Bradycardia (slow rates) | Visible U waves                 | U waves more prominent at slow rates in normal patients; K+ normal   |
| Long QT syndrome         | Prolonged QT interval           | T-wave morphology abnormal; QTc corrected for rate; no U-wave fusion |


### 4.2 Coexisting Conditions

- **Hypomagnesemia:** Synergistic; Mg2+ deficiency impairs Na/K-ATPase → perpetuates hypokalemia; both must be corrected for TdP treatment
- **Metabolic alkalosis:** Common cause of hypokalemia (K+/H+ exchange across cell membrane)
- **Digoxin use:** Hypokalemia dramatically potentiates digoxin toxicity at therapeutic levels
- **Diuretic use:** Loop and thiazide diuretics → K+ and Mg2+ wasting
- **Atrial fibrillation:** Both cause and consequence; new AF may reflect K+ derangement

## 6. Reasoning Complexity Analysis

### 6.1 Pattern Recognition Complexity

Moderate. U wave identification requires distinguishing from the T wave and P wave of the next beat. At fast rates, the U wave may be buried in the P wave, making identification difficult.

### 6.2 Measurement Challenges

The primary challenge is QT vs. QU measurement. When T and U waves fuse, the automated QT measurement algorithms typically measure to the end of the U wave, falsely prolonging the measured QTc. The clinician must determine whether prolonged QT is truly prolonged T or a T-U fusion artifact.

### 6.3 Multi-Variable Interaction

Hypokalemia + hypomagnesemia → synergistic TdP risk. Must consider both electrolytes simultaneously. Hypokalemia in the setting of digoxin use → dramatically increased toxicity risk at therapeutic digoxin levels.

### 6.4 Treatment-ECG Feedback Loop

Serial ECG monitoring during K+ repletion is clinically important. Progressive normalization of U waves, T waves, and QU interval confirms adequate repletion. Failure to normalize suggests ongoing losses or coexisting hypomagnesemia.

### 6.5 False Positives and Clinical Traps

- Slow heart rate → prominent U waves even in normokalemia
- Misidentifying the U wave as a P wave → artificially shortening measured PR interval
- Measuring QU interval as QT → inappropriate diagnosis of LQT syndrome
- Assuming TdP is only from QT prolongation — may be QU

### 6.6 Difficulty Score


| Dimension                   | Score (1–5) | Rationale                                       |
| --------------------------- | ----------- | ----------------------------------------------- |
| Pattern recognition         | 2           | U waves are visually distinctive once known     |
| Measurement precision       | 3           | QT vs QU fusion is genuinely difficult          |
| Differential diagnosis      | 2           | Limited close mimics with this specific pattern |
| Multi-condition interaction | 3           | Hypomagnesemia co-existence is critical         |
| Severity stratification     | 2           | K+ level drives stratification; ECG confirms    |

| **RRC** (Rhythm/Rate/Conduction)   | Supporting                                                                     | QTc measurement (prolonged due to U-wave fusion), arrhythmia surveillance (PVCs, TdP risk context)                                                         |

| **MR** (Morphology/Repolarization) | Primary                                                                        | Prominent U-waves (>1mm or taller than T-wave), T-U fusion, T-wave flattening/inversion, diffuse ST depression, QTc prolongation from U-wave incorporation |
| **CDS** (Cross-Domain Synthesis)   | Required — resolves IT ST depression vs MR U-wave/T-flattening; flags TdP risk | Integrates MR U-wave pattern + IT ST depression distribution + RRC QTc measurement → hypokalemia diagnosis and risk stratification                         |


### Primary Agent

**MR** — Hypokalemia's hallmark findings (prominent U-waves, T-U fusion, T-wave flattening, ST depression) are all morphology and repolarization features directly within MR's domain.

### Cross-Domain Hints

- MR → IT: `cross_domain_hint: "Diffuse ST depression present across multiple non-territorial leads — confirm IT has not classified as ischemia; distribution and U-wave context indicate hypokalemia pattern"`
- MR → CDS: `cross_domain_hint: "U-waves prominent with T-U fusion; QTc appears prolonged due to U-wave incorporation — CDS should flag TdP risk if effective QT-U interval >500ms"`
- RRC → CDS: `cross_domain_hint: "QTc prolonged; if U-waves confirmed by MR, combined electrolyte pattern increases TdP risk — CDS should integrate and flag"`

### CDS Specific Role

CDS resolves the primary diagnostic conflict for hypokalemia: diffuse ST depression flagged by IT must be distinguished from ischemic ST depression. CDS applies the rule that ST depression co-occurring with prominent U-waves, T-wave flattening, and T-U fusion in a non-territorial distribution indicates hypokalemia rather than ischemia. CDS additionally integrates QTc prolongation (RRC measurement) with U-wave findings (MR) to calculate the effective QT-U interval and stratify TdP risk, particularly flagging cases where the combined QT-U interval exceeds 500ms or where concurrent hypomagnesemia pattern is suggested by the morphology.

### 7.1 Essential Knowledge Chunks

- Nernst equation and K+ equilibrium potential physiology
- U wave generation mechanism (Purkinje/M cell repolarization)
- QT vs. QU measurement methodology
- Hypokalemia-hypomagnesemia synergy and treatment implications
- TdP recognition and immediate management (IV Mg2+)
- Digoxin toxicity potentiation by hypokalemia
- Loop and thiazide diuretic electrolyte effects

### 7.2 Reference Values

- Normal K+: 3.5–5.0 mEq/L
- STAT threshold: K+ <2.5 mEq/L or QU >500 ms
- Normal U wave: <1 mm or <25% of T wave amplitude
- Normal QTc: 350–450 ms (male), 350–460 ms (female)
- TdP risk threshold: QU >500 ms

## 9. Edge Cases and Pitfalls

1. **U wave mistaken for P wave:** At fast rates, the U wave of one beat may appear to be the P wave of the next, especially in leads with small P waves. This can lead to incorrect PR interval measurement and missed U wave pathology.
2. **Bradycardia amplifies U waves:** Sinus bradycardia with normal K+ can produce visible U waves. Always check K+ level before attributing prominent U waves to hypokalemia.
3. **T-U fusion at fast rates:** As heart rate increases, U wave merges into T wave more readily, making U wave identification difficult. Slowing the rate (carotid massage, vagal maneuvers — only in stable patients) can unmask T-U separation.
4. **Hypokalemia masking hyperkalemia ECG changes:** In a patient with renal failure who develops vomiting, concurrent hypokalemia can partially normalize hyperkalemia ECG findings (peaked T waves), masking the true K+ level.
5. **TdP degenerating to VF:** TdP is not always self-terminating. Prolonged TdP → VF. Defibrillation is required for sustained polymorphic VT with hemodynamic compromise.
6. **Repletion-induced arrhythmia:** Rapid IV K+ administration can cause bradycardia, heart block, or VF. Repletion rate should not exceed 20 mEq/hour in peripheral vein, 40 mEq/hour via central line with continuous monitoring.
7. **Magnesium repletion priority:** IV magnesium sulfate should be given simultaneously with K+ in any patient with TdP or QU >500 ms, regardless of measured Mg2+ level. Hypomagnesemia prevents K+ retention.
