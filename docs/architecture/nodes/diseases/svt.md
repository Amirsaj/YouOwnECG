## 1. Pathophysiology Root Cause

### 1.1 What Goes Wrong

SVT is a broad term encompassing any rapid tachycardia originating above the bundle of His. In clinical ER practice, "SVT" most commonly refers to two reentrant mechanisms:

**AVNRT (Atrioventricular Nodal Reentrant Tachycardia) — 60-70% of SVT:**
The AV node contains two functionally distinct pathways: a fast pathway (short refractory period) and a slow pathway (long refractory period). Normally the fast pathway conducts antegrade impulses preferentially. A critically timed premature atrial beat finds the fast pathway still refractory, conducts down the slow pathway, and if the fast pathway has recovered by then, re-enters retrogradely — establishing a circular reentrant loop entirely within or perinodal to the AV node.

**AVRT (Atrioventricular Reentrant Tachycardia) — 30% of SVT:**
An accessory pathway (bundle of Kent in WPW syndrome or a concealed pathway) forms a second electrical bridge between atria and ventricles outside the AV node. Reentrant circuit uses the AV node antegradely and the accessory pathway retrogradely (orthodromic AVRT — narrow complex, most common) or vice versa (antidromic AVRT — wide complex, dangerous if WPW).

### 1.2 Electrical Consequence

In AVNRT: The reentrant loop is compact within the AV node. Atrial activation (retrograde) occurs nearly simultaneously with ventricular activation, producing a P-wave that is buried within or immediately adjacent to the QRS complex.

In AVRT: The circuit includes the entire ventricle (antegrade AV node → His-Purkinje → ventricles → accessory pathway → atria retrograde). The atria are activated after ventricular depolarization, so retrograde P-waves appear after the QRS with a measurable RP' interval.

### 1.3 Why It Appears on ECG

The circuit determines the timing of retrograde P-waves relative to QRS:
- AVNRT: retrograde P simultaneously with or just after QRS → pseudo-R' in V1 (terminal QRS deformity), pseudo-S waves in II/III/aVF
- Typical AVNRT: RP' < 70 ms (P buried in QRS or immediately after)
- AVRT: RP' > 70 ms (retrograde P visible between QRS and T-wave)
- Rate driven by the circuit length and refractoriness of the two limbs

## 2. ECG Presentation — Lead by Lead

### 2.1 Diagnostic Criteria (2025 AHA/ESC Guidelines)

1. Heart rate 150–250 bpm (most commonly 160–200 bpm)
2. Regular RR intervals (strict regularity)
3. Narrow QRS ≤120 ms (unless aberrant conduction or antidromic AVRT)
4. P-waves: retrograde, negative in II/III/aVF, positive in aVR
5. RP' interval: <70 ms suggests AVNRT; >70 ms suggests AVRT or atypical AVNRT
6. Pseudo-R' in V1 or pseudo-S in inferior leads = AVNRT signature
7. Abrupt onset and offset (distinguishes from sinus tachycardia)
8. Vagal maneuvers or adenosine: abrupt termination is diagnostic

### 2.2 Lead-by-Lead Manifestation

| Lead | Finding |
|------|---------|
| I | Narrow QRS, typically no visible P-wave; subtle terminal notching if AVNRT |
| II | Retrograde P-wave: deeply negative or buried in QRS; pseudo-S wave in AVNRT |
| III | Retrograde P-wave: deeply negative or buried; pseudo-S wave in AVNRT |
| aVR | Retrograde P-wave: positive (upright) — diagnostic of retrograde atrial activation |
| aVL | P-wave variable; may be isoelectric or slightly negative |
| aVF | Retrograde P-wave: deeply negative or buried; pseudo-S wave in AVNRT |
| V1 | KEY: pseudo-R' wave (terminal positive deflection) in AVNRT = retrograde P superimposed on QRS end; AVRT may show discrete P after QRS |
| V2 | Narrow QRS; P-wave if visible is negative or isoelectric |
| V3 | Narrow QRS; minimal P-wave change |
| V4 | Narrow QRS; P-wave usually not identifiable |
| V5 | Narrow QRS; P-wave usually not identifiable |
| V6 | Narrow QRS; P-wave usually not identifiable |

### 2.3 Key Leads (Most Diagnostic)

- **V1**: Pseudo-R' (terminal rightward deflection) — most reliable AVNRT marker. Compare with patient's baseline ECG if available.
- **II, III, aVF**: Pseudo-S waves in AVNRT; discrete retrograde P after QRS in AVRT
- **aVR**: Upright P-wave confirms retrograde atrial activation (retrograde = inverted in inferior leads = upright in aVR)

### 2.4 Beat-by-Beat Considerations

- Onset: abrupt — look for initiating PAC (if captured on strip)
- During tachycardia: strictly regular, no variation in RR
- Offset: abrupt termination with adenosine or vagal maneuvers; occasionally terminates with a P-wave (retrograde P) visible before return of sinus rhythm
- Post-termination: brief sinus pause may occur before return of sinus rhythm (normal)
- Rate-related ST depression may appear (do not interpret as ischemia during active tachycardia)

## 3. Morphology Details

### 3.1 P-wave Changes

Retrograde P-waves are the defining morphologic feature:
- Negative (inverted) in leads II, III, aVF (depolarization moving away from inferior leads — opposite of normal sinus)
- Positive in aVR (depolarization moving toward aVR axis)
- Location relative to QRS determines subtype:
  - Buried in QRS (RP' < 20 ms): typical AVNRT — P not visible as separate wave
  - Immediately after QRS (RP' 20–70 ms): typical AVNRT — pseudo-R' in V1, pseudo-S in II/III/aVF
  - Clearly after QRS (RP' > 70 ms, RP' < PR): AVRT or atypical AVNRT

### 3.2 PR Interval Changes

- No true PR interval measurable during tachycardia (retrograde P not a sinus P)
- RP' interval: the retrograde P to next QRS interval
- RP' < ½ RR: short RP tachycardia (typical AVNRT, AVRT)
- RP' > ½ RR: long RP tachycardia (atypical AVNRT "fast-slow," PJRT, atrial tachycardia)

### 3.3 QRS Complex Changes

- Narrow QRS ≤120 ms in orthodromic AVRT and AVNRT (normal His-Purkinje conduction)
- Wide QRS ≥120 ms if:
  - Aberrant conduction (rate-related bundle branch block — usually RBBB pattern)
  - Antidromic AVRT (antegrade conduction via accessory pathway — fully pre-excited, maximally wide QRS)
- QRS alternans (alternating QRS amplitude beat-to-beat): seen in up to 25% of AVRT — pathognomonic when present

### 3.4 ST Segment Changes

- Rate-related ST depression (horizontal or downsloping) is common during rapid SVT
- Do not diagnose ischemia during active tachycardia — reassess post-conversion
- True ischemia (persistent ST changes post-conversion) must be separately evaluated

### 3.5 T-wave Changes

- T-waves may be inverted or flattened during tachycardia (rate effect)
- Retrograde P-wave superimposed on T-wave in AVRT alters T-wave morphology — compare with sinus rhythm T-wave

### 3.6 QT/QTc Changes

- QT interval shortens at rapid rates (normal physiologic response)
- Corrected QTc may be difficult to calculate during SVT; assess post-conversion

### 3.7 Other Features

- QRS alternans: beat-to-beat alternation in QRS amplitude (most common in AVRT, rate >200 bpm)
- Delta waves absent during tachycardia in orthodromic AVRT (accessory pathway conducting retrogradely only)
- Delta waves present before tachycardia onset = WPW — critical flag for antidromic risk

## 4. Differential Diagnosis

### 4.1 Mimics

| Condition | Key Differentiator |
|-----------|-------------------|
| Atrial Flutter | Rate usually 150 bpm (2:1 block); sawtooth flutter waves in II/III/aVF/V1; F-waves visible if rate varies |
| Sinus Tachycardia | Gradual onset/offset; upright P before each QRS; rate usually <150 bpm at rest |
| Atrial Tachycardia | P-wave morphology distinct (not retrograde); RP' > PR; may have isoelectric baseline between P-waves |
| Junctional Tachycardia | Rate usually lower (100-130 bpm); AV dissociation possible; no reentrant mechanism |
| Antidromic AVRT | Wide complex, delta wave pattern, extremely rapid — differentiate from VT |

### 4.2 Coexisting Conditions

- WPW syndrome: may coexist with AVRT; delta waves visible in sinus rhythm. AF with WPW is a separate, life-threatening condition requiring immediate attention.
- Aberrant conduction: bundle branch block during SVT mimics VT — if uncertain, treat as VT
- Structural heart disease: AVNRT may occur without structural disease; AVRT with accessory pathway

## 5. STAT Classification

**STAT-2 (Urgent, not immediate) for hemodynamically stable SVT:**
- Initiate vagal maneuvers first (Valsalva — modified supine leg-raise technique preferred)
- IV adenosine 6 mg rapid push if vagal fails; repeat 12 mg x2 if needed
- Document rhythm strip before and during adenosine

**STAT-1 (Immediate) when any of the following:**
- Hemodynamic instability (hypotension, chest pain, altered consciousness, pulmonary edema) → synchronized DC cardioversion
- Wide complex SVT that cannot confidently be distinguished from VT → treat as VT
- Pre-excited SVT (WPW + AF or antidromic AVRT) → avoid AV nodal blocking agents (adenosine, beta-blockers, calcium channel blockers, digoxin — may accelerate accessory pathway conduction and trigger VF)
- Rate >220 bpm in context of WPW → STAT-1

## 6. Reasoning Complexity Analysis

### 6.1 Reasoning Domains Required

1. Rate measurement and regularity assessment
2. QRS width classification (narrow vs wide)
3. P-wave detection in noise-prone, overlapping morphology (pseudo-R', pseudo-S identification)
4. RP' interval measurement and classification
5. Subtype differentiation (AVNRT vs AVRT)
6. WPW risk stratification (delta wave history, antidromic recognition)
7. Flutter exclusion at 150 bpm

### 6.2 Feature Dependencies

- RP' interval measurement requires accurate QRS end and P-wave onset detection
- Pseudo-R' in V1 requires comparison against patient baseline (ECG history dependency)
- QRS alternans detection requires multi-beat amplitude trend analysis
- Aberrant conduction assessment requires rate-dependent bundle branch refractoriness modeling

### 6.3 Cross-Condition Interactions

- SVT + WPW: orthodromic AVRT may degenerate to AF with rapid accessory pathway conduction → VF
- SVT during inferior MI: adenosine may cause transient complete AV block — use with caution
- SVT in structural heart disease: higher likelihood of aberrant conduction mimicking VT
- Atrial flutter with 2:1 block at 150 bpm: most dangerous SVT mimic — must be systematically excluded

### 6.4 Reasoning Chain Sketch

```
Rate ≥ 150 bpm, regular?
  └── Yes → QRS narrow or wide?
        ├── Narrow: P-wave location relative to QRS?
        │     ├── P buried / RP' < 70 ms → AVNRT (check pseudo-R' V1, pseudo-S II/III/aVF)
        │     ├── P after QRS / RP' 70-150 ms → AVRT (check QRS alternans)
        │     └── RP' > 150 ms → long RP tachycardia (atypical AVNRT, AT, PJRT)
        └── Wide: accessory pathway? aberrant conduction? → escalate to VT protocol
  └── Rate ~150 bpm → MUST exclude atrial flutter (2:1)
        └── Look for sawtooth pattern in II/III/aVF/V1 — adenosine may unmask flutter waves
```

### 6.5 Confidence Anchors

- High confidence AVNRT: narrow QRS + pseudo-R' V1 + pseudo-S inferior + RP' < 70 ms + abrupt termination with adenosine
- High confidence AVRT: narrow QRS + RP' 70–150 ms + QRS alternans + delta waves in sinus rhythm
- Uncertainty trigger: rate exactly 150 bpm → flutter exclusion required before any other conclusion
- Uncertainty trigger: wide complex → VT protocol invoked, SVT subtyping deferred

### 6.6 Difficulty Score

| Dimension | Score |
|-----------|-------|
| Signal clarity needed | 4/5 — pseudo-R' and pseudo-S detection require high-quality signal |
| Number of leads required | 4/5 — V1 + inferior leads + aVR all needed for subtype |
| Cross-domain reasoning | 3/5 — rate, morphology, P-wave timing, WPW risk stratification |
| Temporal pattern complexity | 2/5 — regular rhythm, but onset/offset analysis adds complexity |
| Differential complexity | 3/5 — flutter at 150 bpm is critical pitfall; wide complex escalation |
| Rarity in PTB-XL | 3/5 — SVT present but subtype labeling may be coarse |
| **Overall difficulty** | **3.0/5** |

## 6A. Agent Assignment

**Architecture:** Option E — 3 Parallel Phase 1 Specialists + 1 Sequential CDS (Node 2.1 Gate Passed 2026-03-27)

| Agent | Role for SVT | Key Features Used |
|-------|--------------------------|-------------------|
| **RRC** (Rhythm/Rate/Conduction) | Primary | Regular narrow QRS tachycardia (QRS <120 ms) at 150–250 bpm, retrograde P-wave location (RP relationship: short RP = AVNRT/AVRT; long RP = atypical AVNRT or AT), P-wave presence/absence in ST segment, abrupt onset/offset if visible, regular RR intervals |
| **IT** (Ischemia/Territory) | Not involved | — |
| **MR** (Morphology/Repolarization) | Supporting | QRS morphology during tachycardia — narrow (confirms supraventricular) vs wide (aberrant conduction — triggers VT differentiation pathway); pseudo-R' in V1 (retrograde P buried in QRS, typical AVNRT); pseudo-S in inferior leads (retrograde P after QRS) |
| **CDS** (Cross-Domain Synthesis) | Required — resolves aberrant SVT vs VT if QRS is wide | If MR detects wide QRS during RRC's tachycardia detection, CDS applies VT differentiation algorithm (Brugada criteria, axis, AV dissociation from RRC); for narrow-complex SVT, CDS performs standard integration and RP-based subtype classification (AVNRT vs AVRT vs AT) |

### Primary Agent
**RRC** — SVT is a rhythm diagnosis defined by regular narrow-complex tachycardia with specific P-wave and RP relationships, which is the RRC agent's core domain.

### Cross-Domain Hints
- MR emits `cross_domain_hint: "Wide QRS detected during regular tachycardia — aberrant SVT vs VT differentiation required; forward QRS morphology to CDS for Brugada algorithm"` when QRS ≥120 ms is found during a regular tachycardia.

### CDS Specific Role
CDS performs two functions: for narrow-complex SVT, CDS integrates RRC's RP interval measurement with MR's pseudo-R'/pseudo-S morphology findings to subtype the SVT (AVNRT: very short RP, pseudo-R' in V1; AVRT: short RP, visible retrograde P; AT: long RP, discrete P before QRS). For wide-complex SVT (aberrant conduction), CDS applies the full VT differentiation algorithm using RRC's AV relationship data and MR's morphology analysis, generating a VT vs SVT+aberrancy determination with confidence score.

## 9. Edge Cases and Pitfalls

1. **Atrial flutter at 150 bpm**: The most dangerous SVT mimic. Flutter waves may be subtle or buried in QRS at 2:1 conduction. Always scrutinize II/III/aVF/V1 for sawtooth. Adenosine may slow rate transiently, unmasking flutter waves — diagnostic.

2. **WPW + AF**: Not SVT, but often presents as irregular wide-complex tachycardia. Adenosine is contraindicated. May degenerate to VF. Requires immediate cardioversion if unstable, IV procainamide or ibutilide if stable.

3. **Antidromic AVRT**: Wide complex, delta-wave morphology throughout QRS. Easily mistaken for VT. History of WPW and absence of fusion beats/AV dissociation may help but cardioversion is safest approach.

4. **Rate-related aberrant conduction**: SVT with RBBB aberrancy looks like VT. Classic Brugada criteria or Vereckei algorithm required. Default to treating as VT.

5. **Pseudo-R' baseline confusion**: Patient may have true RBBB or incomplete RBBB at baseline. Always compare current ECG to prior if available.

6. **Post-adenosine assessment**: Adenosine has a 10–15 second window. Rhythm strip must be running. Termination confirms SVT; slowing with visible flutter waves → flutter; no effect at 12 mg → consider VT or AT.

7. **Vagal maneuver technique**: Modified Valsalva (supine, legs elevated) has 43% success rate vs 17% standard technique. Document in reasoning.

8. **Pregnancy**: SVT is more common during pregnancy. Adenosine is safe. Avoid verapamil (hypotension risk).
